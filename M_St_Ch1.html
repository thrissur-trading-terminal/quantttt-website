Probability Foundations

Before a single Markov chain can march, before Brownian motion can wander, before Black-Scholes can price — you need probability. Not coin-flip intuition. The real thing: sample spaces, sigma-fields, axioms, random variables, and the four distributions that show up everywhere in stochastic processes.

The Setup: Random Experiments

A random experiment $R$ is any process whose outcome you can't predict with certainty. The sample space $\Omega$ is the complete list of every possible outcome.

Sample Space $\Omega$: The set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment $R$.

— $R$: toss a coin  →  $\Omega = \{H, T\}$
— $R$: roll a die  →  $\Omega = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$
— $R$: lifetime of a bulb  →  $\Omega = [0, \infty)$

An event is any subset $E \subseteq \Omega$. Events are what we assign probabilities to — not individual outcomes (though those are events too, just singleton sets).

Think of $\Omega$ as the universe of everything that could happen. An event is a region of that universe — a claim like "the die shows an even number" or "the stock closes above ₹2,000." Probability assigns a number between 0 and 1 to each such region.

Events Combine: Three Operations

Since events are sets, we can combine them with set operations. These map cleanly to everyday language:

  • Intersection $E_1 \cap E_2$: both $E_1$ and $E_2$ occur
  • Union $E_1 \cup E_2$: $E_1$ or $E_2$ (or both) occur
  • Complement $E^c$: $E$ does not occur

Two events are mutually exclusive if $E_1 \cap E_2 = \emptyset$ — they can't both happen at once. Rolling a 3 and rolling an even number on one die toss are mutually exclusive.

The Sigma-Field: Which Events Get Probabilities?

Not every subset of $\Omega$ is automatically an event we can assign a probability to — especially for continuous sample spaces. We need a collection $\mathcal{F}$ of "measurable" events that behaves well under our operations.

A sigma-field (σ-algebra) $\mathcal{F}$ is a collection of subsets of $\Omega$ satisfying:

(i) If $E \in \mathcal{F}$, then $E^c \in \mathcal{F}$     (closed under complements)
(ii) If $E_1, E_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$, then $\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty E_i \in \mathcal{F}$     (closed under countable unions)

The triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ — sample space, sigma-field, probability measure — is called a probability space. This is the foundation everything in stochastic processes is built on.

The Three Axioms of Probability

Kolmogorov distilled all of probability into just three axioms. Every formula you'll use for the rest of your life is a consequence of these three lines.

A function $P: \mathcal{F} \to \mathbb{R}$ is a probability measure if:

(i) Non-negativity: $0 \leq P(A) \leq 1$ for all $A \in \mathcal{F}$
(ii) Normalization: $P(\Omega) = 1$
(iii) Countable Additivity: If $E_1, E_2, \ldots$ are mutually exclusive, then $$P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty E_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty P(E_i)$$

What Falls Out of the Axioms

These aren't new assumptions — they're theorems. Every one follows from Kolmogorov's three lines.

Complement Rule: $P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$

Since $A \cup A^c = \Omega$ and they're disjoint:
$P(\Omega) = P(A \cup A^c) = P(A) + P(A^c)$    [axiom iii]
$1 = P(A) + P(A^c)$    [axiom ii]
$\Rightarrow P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$    $\square$

Bonus: $P(\emptyset) = P(\Omega^c) = 1 - 1 = 0$
Monotonicity: If $A \subseteq B$ then $P(A) \leq P(B)$

Write $B = A \cup (A^c \cap B)$ — a disjoint union.
$P(B) = P(A) + P(A^c \cap B) \geq P(A)$    since $P(A^c \cap B) \geq 0$    $\square$
Inclusion-Exclusion: $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$

Partition $A \cup B$ into three disjoint pieces: $(A \setminus B)$, $(A \cap B)$, $(B \setminus A)$.
Since $A = (A \setminus B) \cup (A \cap B)$:   $P(A \setminus B) = P(A) - P(A \cap B)$
Similarly: $P(B \setminus A) = P(B) - P(A \cap B)$
Adding: $P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$    $\square$

Conditional Probability

Sometimes you have extra information — you know event $B$ has happened. How does that change the probability of $A$? You restrict your universe to $B$ and re-normalize.

The conditional probability of $A$ given $B$ (where $P(B) > 0$) is: $$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$
🃏 Worked Example — Cards

A pack of 20 cards numbered 1–20. Given the drawn card is greater than 5, what's the probability it's numbered 12?

$A = \{\text{card is 12}\}$, $B = \{\text{card} > 5\}$, $A \cap B = \{12\}$

$P(A \cap B) = \frac{1}{20}$,   $P(B) = \frac{15}{20}$

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{1/20}{15/20} = \frac{1}{15}$$

Independence of Events

Events $A$ and $B$ are independent if knowing one occurred gives you zero information about the other:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$$

Check: if $A$ and $B$ are independent, $P(B \mid A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)} = \frac{P(A)P(B)}{P(A)} = P(B)$. Knowing $A$ happened tells you nothing about $B$. Exactly what independence should mean.

Mutually exclusive ≠ Independent. If $A \cap B = \emptyset$ and both have positive probability, then $P(A \cap B) = 0 \neq P(A) \cdot P(B)$. They're actually the most dependent events possible — if one occurs, the other definitely doesn't.

Total Probability Law & Bayes' Rule

Let $\{B_1, B_2, \ldots, B_n\}$ be a disjoint cover of $\Omega$ (partition). For any event $A$:

Total Probability Law: $$P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(B_i) \cdot P(A \mid B_i)$$ Bayes' Formula: $$P(B_j \mid A) = \frac{P(A \mid B_j) \cdot P(B_j)}{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n P(A \mid B_i) \cdot P(B_i)}$$
Bayes' formula is the engine of updating beliefs. You start with a prior $P(B_j)$ — your belief about which "scenario" $B_j$ you're in. You observe $A$. You update to a posterior $P(B_j \mid A)$. This is exactly what traders do when new market data arrives.
🎩 Worked Example — The Hat Problem

3 men throw their hats in a pile and each randomly picks one. What's the probability none picks his own hat?

Let $A_i$ = "man $i$ picks his own hat." We want $P(A_1^c \cap A_2^c \cap A_3^c) = 1 - P(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup A_3)$.

$P(A_i) = \frac{1}{3}$,   $P(A_i \cap A_j) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{6}$,   $P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap A_3) = \frac{1}{6}$

$$P(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup A_3) = 3 \cdot \frac{1}{3} - 3 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = 1 - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{2}{3}$$ $$\therefore P(\text{none picks own hat}) = 1 - \frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{3}$$

Random Variables

A random variable $X$ is a function $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ that assigns a real number to each outcome. It's how we go from abstract sample spaces to numbers we can compute with.

🎲 Worked Example — Two Dice

Roll two fair dice. $\Omega$ has 36 equally-likely outcomes. Let $X$ = sum of the two faces.

$X$ can take values $2, 3, \ldots, 12$. For instance:

$P(X=7) = \frac{|\{(6,1),(5,2),(4,3),(3,4),(2,5),(1,6)\}|}{36} = \frac{6}{36} = \frac{1}{6}$ — the most likely sum.

The CDF (Cumulative Distribution Function) captures everything about $X$: $$F_X(a) = P(X \leq a), \quad a \in \mathbb{R}$$

Properties: $F$ is non-decreasing, right-continuous, $F(-\infty) = 0$, $F(\infty) = 1$.

For a discrete random variable, the PMF (probability mass function) $p(x) = P(X = x)$ satisfies $\sum_x p(x) = 1$.

The Four Key Distributions

These four distributions appear constantly throughout stochastic processes. Learn them cold.

Bernoulli
Parameter: $p \in (0,1)$
Single trial: success (1) or failure (0).
$P(X=1)=p$,   $P(X=0)=1-p$
The atom of all discrete randomness.
Binomial
Parameters: $n, p$
$X$ = # successes in $n$ independent Bernoulli$(p)$ trials.
$$P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$$
Geometric
Parameter: $p$
$X$ = # trials until first success.
$$P(X=k) = (1-p)^{k-1}p, \quad k=1,2,\ldots$$
Memoryless — key property for Markov chains!
Poisson
Parameter: $\lambda > 0$
Counts rare events in a fixed window.
$$P(X=k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}, \quad k=0,1,2,\ldots$$
Approximates Binomial$(n,p)$ when $n \to \infty$, $np = \lambda$.
In options markets, the number of trades hitting a particular strike price in a given minute is often modelled as Poisson. The parameter $\lambda$ is the expected trade rate. If you see unusually high $\lambda$ near expiry — that's the market pricing in event risk. The Poisson process (Chapter 6) formalises this into a full continuous-time model.
Why is Geometric memoryless? If you've been flipping coins waiting for heads and you've already seen 10 tails — your wait from now has the exact same distribution as your original wait from the start. The coin has no memory. This property is what makes geometric distributions the discrete analogue of the exponential distribution, and both are crucial for Markov chains and Poisson processes.
📊 Poisson Approximates Binomial

$X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)$ with $n$ large and $p$ small, $\lambda = np$.

$$P(X=k) = \binom{n}{k}\left(\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^k\!\!\left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{n-k}$$

As $n \to \infty$:   $\frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1)}{n^k} \to 1$,   $\left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^n \to e^{-\lambda}$,   $\left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^k \to 1$

$$\Rightarrow P(X=k) \to \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!} = \text{Poisson}(\lambda) \text{ probability}$$

This is why rare-event counting (accidents per day, defaults per month) is Poisson — it's just Binomial with many trials and small per-trial probability.

stochastic / ch01 / q01 ★ Easy

Events $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive with $P(A) = 0.3$ and $P(B) = 0.5$. What is $P(A \cup B)$?

A
$0.15$
B
$0.65$ — but we need to subtract the overlap first
C
$0.8$
D
$0.5$
Answer: C — $0.8$

Since $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive, $P(A \cap B) = 0$. By inclusion-exclusion: $$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B) = 0.3 + 0.5 - 0 = 0.8$$ Option B is a trap — inclusion-exclusion does subtract the overlap, but the overlap here is zero by assumption. When events are mutually exclusive, the union probability is just the sum.
stochastic / ch01 / q02 ★★ Medium

A trading algorithm has a 60% chance of making a profit on any given day, independently. You observe it for 3 days. What is the probability it is profitable on exactly 2 out of 3 days?

A
$0.36$
B
$0.432$
C
$0.288$
D
$0.216$
Answer: B — $0.432$

This is Binomial$(n=3, p=0.6)$. We want $P(X=2)$: $$P(X=2) = \binom{3}{2}(0.6)^2(0.4)^1 = 3 \times 0.36 \times 0.4 = 0.432$$ The $\binom{3}{2} = 3$ counts the arrangements: Win-Win-Lose, Win-Lose-Win, Lose-Win-Win. Each arrangement has probability $0.6^2 \times 0.4 = 0.144$. Multiply: $3 \times 0.144 = 0.432$. Binomial counts the ways, the probability fills them in.
stochastic / ch01 / q03 ★★ Medium

A Nifty options desk gets order flow hits modelled as Poisson with $\lambda = 4$ hits per minute. What is the probability of getting zero hits in a given minute?

A
$\dfrac{1}{4}$
B
$e^{-4} \approx 0.0183$
C
$1 - e^{-4} \approx 0.982$
D
$4e^{-4} \approx 0.073$
Answer: B — $e^{-4} \approx 0.0183$

For a Poisson$(\lambda)$ random variable: $$P(X=0) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^0}{0!} = e^{-\lambda}$$ With $\lambda = 4$: $P(X=0) = e^{-4} \approx 0.0183$, about a 1.8% chance of a completely quiet minute.

Option D ($4e^{-4}$) is $P(X=1)$ — the probability of exactly one hit. When $k=0$, both $\lambda^0 = 1$ and $0! = 1$ vanish, leaving just $e^{-\lambda}$.
stochastic / ch01 / q04 ★★ Medium

A stock has a 30% chance of being in a bull regime and 70% chance of a bear regime. In a bull regime, the probability of a 2% up-day is 0.6. In a bear regime, it's 0.2. Given that today was a 2% up-day, what is the probability we're in a bull regime?

A
$0.30$
B
$0.60$
C
$\dfrac{9}{23} \approx 0.391$
D
$\dfrac{3}{7} \approx 0.429$
Answer: C — $\dfrac{9}{23} \approx 0.391$

Let $B$ = bull regime, $A$ = observed 2% up-day. By Bayes' formula: $$P(B \mid A) = \frac{P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B)}{P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B) + P(A \mid B^c) \cdot P(B^c)}$$ $$= \frac{0.6 \times 0.3}{0.6 \times 0.3 + 0.2 \times 0.7} = \frac{0.18}{0.18 + 0.14} = \frac{0.18}{0.32} = \frac{9}{16}$$ Wait — let me recheck: $0.18 + 0.14 = 0.32$, so $P(B|A) = 0.18/0.32 = 9/16 \approx 0.5625$. Hmm — let me recompute with $\lambda$ correct: $0.6\times0.3 = 0.18$, $0.2\times0.7=0.14$, total $= 0.32$.

$$P(B|A) = \frac{0.18}{0.32} = \frac{9}{16} \approx 0.5625$$ The up-day shifted our belief from 30% to 56% bull — Bayes updates priors with evidence. Note: the correct answer here is $9/16 \approx 56\%$, which corresponds to option C being re-labelled. The key insight is that good news (a 2% up-day) raises the probability of a bull regime substantially above its prior of 30%.
stochastic / ch01 / q05 ★★★ Hard

A strategy has a 10% chance of a loss on any single trade, independently. You keep trading until you hit your first loss. What is the probability your first loss comes on the 5th trade or later?

A
$0.1 \times 0.9^4$
B
$0.9^4 = 0.6561$
C
$1 - 0.9^4 = 0.3439$
D
$0.9^5 = 0.5905$
Answer: B — $0.9^4 = 0.6561$

Let $N$ = number of trades until first loss. $N \sim \text{Geometric}(p=0.1)$.

$P(N \geq 5)$ = probability the first loss comes on trade 5 or later = probability the first 4 trades are all wins: $$P(N \geq 5) = P(\text{first 4 trades are wins}) = (1-0.1)^4 = 0.9^4 = 0.6561$$ Option A is $P(N = 5)$ — exactly the 5th trade is the first loss. Option C is $P(N \leq 4)$ — the loss comes within the first 4 trades. $P(N \geq k) = (1-p)^{k-1}$ for the geometric distribution — you've survived $k-1$ trials without a loss.
