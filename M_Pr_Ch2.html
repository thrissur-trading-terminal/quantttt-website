Random Experiments & Classical Probability

Probability wasn't invented in a vacuum. It was born out of gambling disputes, refined by genius, and eventually formalised into something powerful enough to price options on Nifty. Let's trace that journey — and build the foundations properly.

A Brief, Glorious History

17th century: Blaise Pascal and Pierre de Fermat exchange letters about a gambling problem — who gets what share of the prize money if a game is interrupted? Two mathematicians, a dice dispute, and a few letters. That's all it took to birth probability theory.

18th century: Pierre-Simon Laplace formalises it. He proposes that the probability of an event is the ratio of favourable outcomes to the total number of outcomes. Clean. Elegant. And — as we'll discover in Chapter 3 — not the whole story.

Laplace's definition is where most of us first meet probability. It's also where most textbooks stop — which is fine for a coin flip, but dangerously incomplete the moment you try to price an option or model a stock price.

Laplace's Classical Definition: $$P(\text{event}) = \frac{\text{number of favourable outcomes}}{\text{total number of possible outcomes}}$$ This is valid when: (1) the sample space is finite, and (2) all outcomes are equally likely. Violate either condition, and you need something more powerful.

What Makes an Experiment "Random"?

Before we can compute probabilities, we need something to be uncertain about. That something is a random experiment.

A random experiment is an action or process that satisfies three conditions:
1. It can be repeated any number of times under the same setup.
2. The set of all possible outcomes — the sample space $\Omega$ — is known in advance.
3. The actual outcome on any given trial is unknown until it occurs.

That third condition is the soul of it. You know what could happen. You don't know what will happen. The gap between possibility and certainty is exactly where probability lives.

📋 Examples of Random Experiments

• Tossing a coin → $\Omega = \{H, T\}$

• Throwing a die → $\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$

• Tossing a coin twice → $\Omega = \{HH, HT, TH, TT\}$

• Drawing a card from a deck → $\Omega$ = all 52 cards

• Observing whether a Nifty call expires ITM, ATM, or OTM → $\Omega = \{\text{ITM, ATM, OTM}\}$

With Replacement vs Without Replacement

Here's a subtle distinction that matters a great deal — in probability theory and in trading alike. Suppose a box contains $m$ white balls and $n$ black balls. You draw one ball, note its colour, and then you face a choice.

With replacement: You put the ball back before the next draw. The box is always identical at the start of each trial. The sample space never changes. This experiment can be repeated indefinitely under truly identical conditions — this is the gold standard of a random experiment.

Without replacement: The ball is not returned. Each draw changes what remains in the box. The sample space shrinks. Eventually the box is empty. This experiment has a finite lifespan — it cannot be repeated infinitely, because the balls are finite.

In quantitative finance, bootstrapping — a core technique in risk management — is sampling with replacement from historical returns. You draw a random trading day from the past, record its return, put it back, and draw again. This lets you simulate thousands of possible market scenarios from a finite historical dataset. The NSE's VaR calculations rely on exactly this kind of thinking.

Events and the Laplace Formula in Practice

An event is any subset of $\Omega$. Laplace's formula lets us assign probabilities to events — as long as all elementary outcomes are equally likely. Let's see it work on a die and on a deck of cards.

🎲 Die Roll — Various Events

Sample space: $\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$

• Getting a 6: $P(\{6\}) = \frac{1}{6}$

• Getting a number greater than 3: $P(\{4,5,6\}) = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$

• Getting an even number: $P(\{2,4,6\}) = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$

Cards are richer. Let's work through a problem that requires careful counting — the kind you'll see in quant interviews.

🃏 Card Problems — Laplace in Action

Getting a spade: 13 spades in 52 cards. $P = \frac{13}{52} = \frac{1}{4}$

Getting an ace: 4 aces in 52 cards. $P = \frac{4}{52} = \frac{1}{13}$

Getting a red queen OR a black card with face value 2–10:

— Red queens: Queen of Hearts + Queen of Diamonds = 2 cards

— Black cards (Clubs + Spades) with values 2 through 10: 9 values × 2 suits = 18 cards

— These sets are disjoint (red vs black — no overlap).

— Favourable outcomes: $2 + 18 = 20$

$$P = \frac{20}{52} = \frac{5}{13} \approx 38.5\%$$
When combining events with "or", always check whether the groups overlap. Here, "red queens" and "black 2–10" are disjoint — different colours, no card can belong to both. So simple addition works. When they do} overlap, simple addition double-counts. The fix is the inclusion-exclusion principle — we'll cover it precisely in Chapter 4.

Four Definitions You Must Own

These four concepts appear everywhere in probability — in problems, in proofs, and in exam questions designed to trip people up. Learn them cold.

① Exhaustive Events
All possible outcomes — the complete picture
A set of events is exhaustive if together they cover the entire sample space $\Omega$. At least one of them must occur in any trial. In a coin toss, $\{H, T\}$ is exhaustive — something must happen. In a die roll, $\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$ is exhaustive. You cannot get a 7.
② Elementary Events
The atoms — indivisible outcomes
An elementary event is a single, indivisible outcome from one trial. It cannot be decomposed further. In a coin toss: $H$ and $T$ are the two elementary events. In tossing a coin and rolling a die together, the 12 outcomes $\{H1, H2, H3, H4, H5, H6, T1, T2, T3, T4, T5, T6\}$ are all elementary events. Every event is a collection of elementary events.
③ Equally Likely Events
When no outcome has an unfair advantage
Events are equally likely if, given all relevant evidence, there is no reason to prefer one outcome over another. This is the hidden assumption in Laplace's formula. For a fair coin tossed 3 times, the 8 outcomes $\{HHH, HHT, HTH, HTT, THH, THT, TTH, TTT\}$ are equally likely. But if you reframe the experiment as "count the number of heads," the outcomes $\{0, 1, 2, 3\}$ are not equally likely — 1 head can occur in 3 ways, 3 heads in only 1. Same experiment, different description, and equal likelihood vanishes.
④ Mutually Exclusive Events
When two events cannot co-exist
Two events $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive if the occurrence of one prevents the occurrence of the other. Formally: $A \cap B = \emptyset$. Drawing a single card from a standard deck, the events "getting a club," "getting a spade," "getting a heart," and "getting a diamond" are mutually exclusive — one card, one suit. You cannot draw a card that is simultaneously a club and a spade. Generalising: $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ are mutually exclusive if $A_i \cap A_j = \emptyset$ for all $i \neq j$.
Mutually exclusive ≠ Independent. This is one of the most common and costly confusions in probability. Mutually exclusive means the events cannot both happen: $P(A \cap B) = 0$. Independent means knowing one happened tells you nothing about the other: $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$. If two events are mutually exclusive and both have positive probability — they are actually dependent. If $A$ happens, you instantly know $B$ didn't. That's the opposite of independence. We'll treat this rigorously in Chapter 5.

One Last Thought Before Chapter 3

Everything we've done here assumes the sample space is finite and outcomes are equally likely. Laplace's formula is elegant precisely because of those assumptions. But what happens when $\Omega$ is infinite — like the set of all possible Nifty closing prices? You can't count outcomes. The ratio $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ is undefined.

That's not a flaw in probability theory. It's an invitation. In Chapter 3, we'll see exactly why the classical definition breaks down — and meet the more powerful framework that replaces it: probability measure.

The move from "counting outcomes" to "measuring sets" is one of the great conceptual leaps in mathematics. It's also what separates traders who use probability as a vague hand-wave from quants who wield it as a precision instrument. Chapter 3 is where that leap happens.

A bag has 5 red and 3 blue marbles. You draw one marble, note its colour, and put it back before drawing again. Which of the following is true?

A
The sample space changes after each draw
B
This experiment cannot be repeated infinitely
C
The setup is identical for every trial — this is sampling with replacement
D
The probability of drawing red changes on each draw
Answer: C

Because the marble is returned after each draw, the bag is always identical: 5 red, 3 blue. The sample space $\Omega = \{\text{Red, Blue}\}$ and the probabilities $P(\text{Red}) = \frac{5}{8}$, $P(\text{Blue}) = \frac{3}{8}$ stay constant across every single draw.

This is the defining property of sampling with replacement — truly repeatable under identical conditions. Options A and D describe what happens without replacement. Option B is wrong — with replacement, you can draw forever.
probability / ch02 / q02 ★★ Medium

A fair coin is tossed 3 times. Riya claims: "The sample space is {0, 1, 2, 3} — the number of heads — and all four outcomes are equally likely." Is she correct?

A
Yes — there are 4 outcomes, all equally likely by symmetry of the coin
B
No — the sample space is valid, but the outcomes are not equally likely
C
No — the sample space itself is wrong; it must have 8 outcomes
D
Yes — for a fair coin, any representation of the sample space is equally likely
Answer: B

The sample space $\{0, 1, 2, 3\}$ is perfectly valid — it lists all possible values of "number of heads." But equally likely? No.

Count how many of the 8 elementary outcomes produce each value:
— 0 heads (TTT): 1 way
— 1 head (HTT, THT, TTH): 3 ways
— 2 heads (HHT, HTH, THH): 3 ways
— 3 heads (HHH): 1 way

So $P(0)=P(3)=\frac{1}{8}$ and $P(1)=P(2)=\frac{3}{8}$. Very unequal. Equal likelihood lives at the level of elementary events — the 8 raw sequences — not derived summaries like "number of heads."
probability / ch02 / q03 ★★ Medium

One card is drawn at random from a standard 52-card deck. What is the probability of drawing a red queen OR a black card with face value between 2 and 10 (inclusive)?

A
$\dfrac{4}{52} = \dfrac{1}{13}$
B
$\dfrac{18}{52} = \dfrac{9}{26}$
C
$\dfrac{20}{52} = \dfrac{5}{13}$
D
$\dfrac{22}{52} = \dfrac{11}{26}$
Answer: C — $\dfrac{20}{52} = \dfrac{5}{13}$

Count each group carefully:

Red queens: Queen of Hearts + Queen of Diamonds = 2 cards

Black cards with values 2–10: Black suits are Clubs and Spades. Each has 9 cards (2,3,4,5,6,7,8,9,10). That's $9 \times 2 = \mathbf{18}$ cards.

Are the groups disjoint? Yes — one is red, the other is black. Zero overlap.

$$\text{Favourable} = 2 + 18 = 20 \qquad P = \frac{20}{52} = \frac{5}{13}$$ Option A counts only queens (all 4, not just red). Option B forgets the red queens. Option D — check your sets before adding.
