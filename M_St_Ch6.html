Poisson Processes

The Bernoulli process discretised time. The Poisson process lets time flow continuously — and everything clicks into place. Inter-arrivals become exponential, cumulative times become Erlang, counts become Poisson. This is the most important arrival model in all of applied probability.

From BRP to PCP: The Key Idea

Take a BRP$(p)$ and shrink the slot size to $\Delta t$. To keep the arrival rate constant at $\lambda$ events per unit time, set $p = \lambda \Delta t$. Now let $\Delta t \to 0$. The resulting continuous-time process is the Poisson Counting Process PCP$(\lambda)$.

The Poisson process is what you get when you take infinitely many infinitesimally small Bernoulli slots, each with vanishingly small probability $\lambda \Delta t$ of an arrival. The magic is that this limit is well-defined and has beautiful properties — and it's the natural model whenever arrivals are rare, random, and independent.

Deriving the Poisson PMF from ODEs

Let $P_n(t) = P(N(t) = n)$. In a small interval $[t, t+\Delta t]$, at most one arrival occurs, with probability $\lambda \Delta t$. This gives the recurrence:

$$P_n(t+\Delta t) = P_n(t)(1-\lambda\Delta t) + P_{n-1}(t)(\lambda\Delta t), \quad n \ge 1$$

Rearranging and taking $\Delta t \to 0$:

Coupled ODEs: $$\frac{dP_n(t)}{dt} = -\lambda P_n(t) + \lambda P_{n-1}(t), \quad n \ge 1$$ $$\frac{dP_0(t)}{dt} = -\lambda P_0(t), \quad n = 0$$ Solve: From the $n=0$ equation with $P_0(0) = 1$: $P_0(t) = e^{-\lambda t}$.

For $n=1$: $\frac{dP_1}{dt} = -\lambda P_1 + \lambda e^{-\lambda t}$. Multiply by integrating factor $e^{\lambda t}$: $$\frac{d}{dt}(e^{\lambda t} P_1) = \lambda \implies e^{\lambda t} P_1(t) = \lambda t \implies P_1(t) = \lambda t\, e^{-\lambda t}$$ Continuing by induction: $$\boxed{P_n(t) = \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^n}{n!}} \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad N(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t) \quad \square$$

Three Defining Properties

A process $\{N(t), t \ge 0\}$ is a Poisson Counting Process PCP$(\lambda)$ if:

(1) Initialization: $N(0) = 0$ almost surely.

(2) Independent Increments: For $0 \le t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_k$, the increments $N(t_1)-N(t_0),\; N(t_2)-N(t_1),\; \ldots$ are mutually independent.

(3) Stationary Increments: $N(t+s) - N(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda s)$ for all $t \ge 0, s > 0$ — the distribution depends only on the length $s$, not the starting time $t$.
Every option trader's best friend: on a liquid index like Nifty 50, trades arrive as approximately a PCP. The rate $\lambda$ spikes around FOMC announcements, RBI policy days, or expiry. The independent increments property means past trade counts don't predict future counts — each window is a fresh start. This is why order flow is modelled as Poisson before adding autocorrelation (Hawkes processes).

Inter-Arrival Times are Exponential

Let $T_1$ = time of first arrival. Then:

$$P(T_1 > t) = P(N(t) = 0) = e^{-\lambda t} \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad T_1 \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda)$$
Inter-arrivals are iid Exponential$(\lambda)$:
$$P(T_1 > t,\; T_2 > s) = P(N(t)=0) \cdot P(N(t+s)-N(t)=0) = e^{-\lambda t} \cdot e^{-\lambda s} = P(T_1>t) \cdot P(T_2>s)$$ So $T_1 \perp\!\!\!\perp T_2$, and by the same argument $T_1, T_2, T_3, \ldots$ are iid Exp$(\lambda)$.

$k$-th Arrival Time: Erlang (Gamma) Distribution

$S_k = T_1 + T_2 + \cdots + T_k$ — the time of the $k$-th arrival. Since $T_i$ are iid Exp$(\lambda)$:

$$f_{S_k}(s) = \frac{\lambda e^{-\lambda s}(\lambda s)^{k-1}}{(k-1)!}, \quad s \ge 0 \qquad S_k \sim \text{Gamma}(k, \lambda) = k\text{-Erlang}(\lambda)$$

The duality from BRP carries over:

$$\{N(t) \ge k\} = \{S_k \le t\} \qquad \{N(t) = k\} = \{S_k \le t < S_{k+1}\}$$

Conditional Distribution of Arrival Times

Given that exactly one arrival occurred in $[0, s]$, when did it arrive?

$$P(T_1 \le t \mid N(s) = 1) = \frac{P(N(t)=1,\; N(s)-N(t)=0)}{P(N(s)=1)}$$ $$= \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t) \cdot e^{-\lambda(s-t)}}{\lambda s\, e^{-\lambda s}} = \frac{t}{s}$$ So $T_1 \mid N(s)=1 \sim \text{Uniform}(0, s)$. The arrival is uniformly distributed over the interval — no position is more likely than another.
Given $N(s) = 2$ (two arrivals in $[0,s]$), the two arrival times are distributed as the order statistics of two independent Uniform$(0,s)$ random variables. This elegant result — Poisson arrivals are "uniformly scattered" given the count — is one of the deepest properties of the Poisson process.

Splitting and Merging

Splitting: Take PCP$(\lambda)$ and independently retain each arrival with probability $p$. Then: $$\text{Retained} \sim \text{PCP}(p\lambda) \qquad \text{Discarded} \sim \text{PCP}((1-p)\lambda) \qquad \text{(independent)}$$ Merging: Two independent PCP$(\lambda_1)$ and PCP$(\lambda_2)$: $$\text{Merged process} \sim \text{PCP}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)$$ Rates simply add — no inclusion-exclusion needed (unlike BRP), because simultaneous arrivals have probability zero in continuous time.
💡 Worked Example — Three Light Bulbs

Three bulbs fail independently: $T_i \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$. Find the expected time until all three have failed.

Model as three independent PCP$(\lambda)$ processes merged. The first failure comes from PCP$(3\lambda)$, so $T_A \sim \text{Exp}(3\lambda)$, $E[T_A] = \frac{1}{3\lambda}$.

After the first failure, two bulbs remain — a PCP$(2\lambda)$. By the memoryless property, $T_B \sim \text{Exp}(2\lambda)$, $E[T_B] = \frac{1}{2\lambda}$.

After the second failure, one bulb remains. $T_C \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, $E[T_C] = \frac{1}{\lambda}$.

Total expected time: $W = T_A + T_B + T_C$, so:

$$E[W] = \frac{1}{3\lambda} + \frac{1}{2\lambda} + \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{11}{6\lambda}$$

The memoryless property of exponential is the key — after each failure, the remaining system starts fresh.

🚗 Worked Example — Crossing a Busy Road

Cars arrive as PCP$(\lambda)$. You cross when you find a gap of length $\tau$ with no car. $N$ = number of cars seen before crossing.

$\{N = n\}$ means the first $n$ inter-arrival times are less than $\tau$ (you can't cross) and the $(n+1)$-th is at least $\tau$ (you cross). Each $T_i \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, so:

$$P(T_i < \tau) = 1 - e^{-\lambda\tau} \quad P(T_i \ge \tau) = e^{-\lambda\tau}$$

So $N \sim \text{Geometric}(e^{-\lambda\tau})$ and by Wald's Lemma:

$$E[N] = \frac{1 - e^{-\lambda\tau}}{e^{-\lambda\tau}} = e^{\lambda\tau} - 1$$

If $\lambda = 1$ car/min and you need $\tau = 2$ min gap: $E[N] = e^2 - 1 \approx 6.4$ cars to watch before crossing.

Wald's Lemma

When you sum a random number of iid random variables, how do you find the expected total?

Wald's Lemma: Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be iid with $E[X] < \infty$. Let $N \ge 1$ be an independent random variable with $E[N] < \infty$. Then: $$E\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^N X_i\right] = E[N] \cdot E[X]$$
Condition on $N$: $E[S_N \mid N=n] = E[X_1 + \cdots + X_n] = nE[X]$. So $E[S_N \mid N] = N \cdot E[X]$. By the Tower Property: $E[S_N] = E[N \cdot E[X]] = E[N] \cdot E[X]$. $\square$
Wald's Lemma is the foundation of risk-neutral pricing under random stopping times. A strategy that trades $N$ times, where $N$ is random and each trade has expected PnL $\mu$, has total expected PnL $E[N] \cdot \mu$ — provided $N$ is independent of trade outcomes. When $N$ depends on outcomes (e.g., "keep trading until up ₹X"), the lemma no longer applies and optional stopping theorems (Chapter 11) are needed.

Trades on Nifty 50 arrive as PCP$(\lambda = 5)$ per second. What is the probability of zero trades in a 2-second window?

A
$e^{-5}$
B
$e^{-10}$
C
$5e^{-5}$
D
$1 - e^{-10}$
Answer: B — $e^{-10}$

$N(2) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda \cdot t) = \text{Poisson}(5 \times 2) = \text{Poisson}(10)$. So: $$P(N(2) = 0) = \frac{e^{-10}(10)^0}{0!} = e^{-10} \approx 0.0000454$$ Option A uses $\lambda t = 5$ instead of $\lambda t = 10$ — confusing $\lambda$ with $\lambda t$. Option D is the CDF complement — probability of at least one trade. Always compute $\lambda t$ first: the Poisson parameter scales with the window length.
stochastic / ch06 / q02 ★★ Medium

Orders arrive as PCP$(4)$ per minute. Each order is independently a market order with probability $0.3$, limit order otherwise. What is the arrival rate of market orders?

A
PCP$(0.3)$
B
PCP$(1.2)$
C
PCP$(4)$
D
PCP$(2.8)$
Answer: B — PCP$(1.2)$

By the splitting theorem: market orders $\sim$ PCP$(p\lambda) = $ PCP$(0.3 \times 4) = $ PCP$(1.2)$. Limit orders $\sim$ PCP$(0.7 \times 4) = $ PCP$(2.8)$. The two streams are independent. Splitting a Poisson process always produces Poisson subprocesses — this is why the Poisson process is so tractable.
stochastic / ch06 / q03 ★★ Medium

Two independent PCP processes — retail orders at rate $\lambda_1 = 3$ and algorithmic orders at rate $\lambda_2 = 7$ — are merged. What is the expected time to the first merged arrival?

A
$1/3$ minutes
B
$1/7$ minutes
C
$1/10$ minutes
D
$1/4$ minutes
Answer: C — $1/10$ minutes

Merged process $\sim$ PCP$(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2) = $ PCP$(10)$. First arrival $\sim$ Exp$(10)$, so $E[T_1] = 1/10$ minutes = 6 seconds.

This is the minimum of two independent exponentials: $\min(\text{Exp}(3), \text{Exp}(7)) \sim \text{Exp}(10)$. When merging, rates add. The combined system processes arrivals faster — the first event from either source comes much sooner than from either alone.
stochastic / ch06 / q04 ★★ Medium

Given that exactly one trade occurred in a 10-second window in a PCP$(\lambda)$ process, the conditional distribution of the trade time is:

A
Exponential$(\lambda)$
B
Exponential$(1/10)$
C
Uniform$(0, 10)$
D
Poisson$(\lambda \cdot 10)$
Answer: C — Uniform$(0, 10)$

We showed: $P(T_1 \le t \mid N(s) = 1) = t/s$. This is the CDF of Uniform$(0, s)$. With $s = 10$, the trade time is Uniform$(0, 10)$.

Given a fixed count, Poisson arrivals scatter uniformly — no part of the window is more likely than another. This "uniformly scattered" property is unique to the Poisson process.
stochastic / ch06 / q05 ★★★ Hard

A strategy executes a random number $N$ of trades, where $N \sim \text{Geometric}(0.1)$ (i.e., $E[N] = 10$). Each trade has an independent PnL with mean $\mu = 500$ and standard deviation $\sigma = 2000$. By Wald's Lemma, the expected total PnL is:

A
500
B
5000 only if trades are normal
C
5000, regardless of the PnL distribution
D
Cannot be determined without the full distribution of $N$
Answer: C — 5000, regardless of distribution

By Wald's Lemma: $E[S_N] = E[N] \cdot E[X] = 10 \times 500 = 5000$.

The key conditions: (i) $X_i$ are iid, (ii) $N$ is independent of the $X_i$'s, (iii) both $E[N]$ and $E[X]$ are finite. No normality assumption needed — Wald's Lemma works for any distribution satisfying these conditions.

Wald's Lemma is powerful precisely because it only needs means — not the full distributions. The total expected PnL is always (expected trades) × (expected PnL per trade), as long as $N$ doesn't depend on individual trade outcomes.
