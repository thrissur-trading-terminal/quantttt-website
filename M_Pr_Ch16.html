The Poisson Process

We have studied the Poisson distribution as a formula. Now we derive it from physical assumptions about how random events arrive over time. The Poisson process is a complete probabilistic model of arrivals — and it unifies three distributions: Poisson (counts), Exponential (first waiting time), and Gamma ($k$-th waiting time).

What the Poisson Process Models

Any process where discrete events arrive randomly over time or space: phone calls at a call centre, cars at a toll plaza, insurance claims in a year, typos on a page, orders arriving at an exchange.

A Poisson Process with rate $\lambda > 0$ satisfies three assumptions:

A1. Independence: The number of arrivals in non-overlapping time intervals are independent.

A2. Stationarity: $P(\text{exactly 1 arrival in } [t, t+\Delta t]) = \lambda\Delta t$ for any $t$.

A3. Rare doubles: $P(\text{more than 1 arrival in } [t, t+\Delta t]) = O(\Delta t)$, meaning this probability divided by $\Delta t$ goes to 0 as $\Delta t \to 0$.
In algorithmic trading on NSE, order arrivals from multiple market participants follow a Poisson process under normal conditions. $\lambda$ is the order arrival rate (orders/second). The Poisson process assumption underlies every limit order book model, execution quality analysis, and market impact estimation used in Indian HFT.

Deriving the Poisson PMF from First Principles

Let $p_x(t) = P(X_t = x)$ = probability of exactly $x$ arrivals in time $t$. Initial conditions: $p_0(0) = 1$, $p_x(0) = 0$ for $x > 0$.

Step 1 — Differential equation for $p_0(t)$:

In $[0, t+\Delta t]$, zero arrivals requires zero in $[0,t]$ AND zero in $[t, t+\Delta t]$: $$p_0(t+\Delta t) = p_0(t)\cdot(1 - \lambda\Delta t - O(\Delta t))$$ Rearranging and dividing by $\Delta t$, then taking $\Delta t \to 0$: $$p_0'(t) = -\lambda p_0(t) \implies \frac{p_0'(t)}{p_0(t)} = -\lambda$$ Integrating: $\log p_0(t) = -\lambda t + C$. At $t=0$: $C=0$. Therefore: $$\boxed{p_0(t) = e^{-\lambda t}}$$
Step 2 — Differential equation for $p_x(t)$, $x \geq 1$:

Exactly $x$ arrivals in $[0, t+\Delta t]$ happens two ways: — $x$ arrivals in $[0,t]$, then 0 in $[t,t+\Delta t]$, or — $x-1$ arrivals in $[0,t]$, then 1 in $[t,t+\Delta t]$ $$p_x(t+\Delta t) = p_x(t)(1-\lambda\Delta t) + p_{x-1}(t)\cdot\lambda\Delta t + O(\Delta t)$$ Taking $\Delta t \to 0$: $$p_x'(t) = -\lambda p_x(t) + \lambda p_{x-1}(t) \qquad \cdots (*)$$
Step 3 — Solve recursively:

For $x=1$: $p_1'(t) + \lambda p_1(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}$. Multiply by integrating factor $e^{\lambda t}$: $$\frac{d}{dt}[e^{\lambda t}p_1(t)] = \lambda \implies e^{\lambda t}p_1(t) = \lambda t + C$$ At $t=0$: $p_1(0) = 0 \Rightarrow C=0$. So $p_1(t) = e^{-\lambda t}\lambda t = e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^1}{1!}$.

For $x=2$: $p_2'(t) + \lambda p_2(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}\lambda t$. Same method: $$\frac{d}{dt}[e^{\lambda t}p_2(t)] = \lambda^2 t \implies e^{\lambda t}p_2(t) = \frac{\lambda^2 t^2}{2} \implies p_2(t) = e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^2}{2!}$$
By induction: $p_x(t) = e^{-\lambda t}\dfrac{(\lambda t)^x}{x!}$. $\square$

Conclusion: $X_t \sim \text{Poi}(\lambda t)$.

Worked Examples

📋 Insurance Claims

An insurer covers 5000 persons. Historical rate: 10 claims per 10000 persons/year, so $\lambda = 0.001$ claims/person/year. With $t = 5000$ persons, $\lambda t = 5$.

$$P(\text{3 claims}) = e^{-5}\frac{5^3}{3!} = e^{-5} \times \frac{125}{6} \approx e^{-5} \times 20.83$$
📖 Book Errors

500 pages, 300 errors → rate $\lambda = 300/500 = 0.6$ errors/page. For $t = 10$ pages: $\lambda t = 6$.

$$P(\text{0 errors in 10 pages}) = e^{-6}\frac{6^0}{0!} = e^{-6} \approx 0.00248$$

Poisson → Exponential → Gamma: The Waiting Time Trinity

If arrivals follow a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$:

Waiting time for the 1st arrival: $W_1 \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$

Waiting time for the $k$th arrival: $W_k \sim \Gamma(\lambda, k)$
First arrival: $P(W_1 \leq t) = P(\text{at least 1 arrival in } [0,t]) = 1 - P(\text{0 arrivals}) = 1 - e^{-\lambda t}$. Differentiating: $f_{W_1}(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}$ — the Exp$(\lambda)$ PDF. $\square$

$k$th arrival: $F_{W_k}(t) = P(W_k \leq t) = P(\text{at least } k \text{ arrivals in } [0,t]) = 1 - \sum_{i=0}^{k-1}e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^i}{i!}$. Differentiating (via the product rule telescoping): $$f_{W_k}(t) = \frac{d}{dt}\left[-e^{-\lambda t} - \sum_{i=1}^{k-1}e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^i}{i!}\right]$$ Each term $\frac{d}{dt}\left[e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^i}{i!}\right] = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^{i-1}}{(i-1)!} - \lambda e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^i}{i!}$ — a telescoping difference. Summing: everything cancels except the last positive term: $$f_{W_k}(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}\frac{(\lambda t)^{k-1}}{(k-1)!} = \frac{\lambda^k}{\Gamma(k)}e^{-\lambda t}t^{k-1} \qquad \square$$ This is the $\Gamma(\lambda, k)$ PDF.
This is not a coincidence — it's the fundamental link between three distributions:

The Poisson counts events in a fixed time. The Exponential times the first event. The Gamma$(k)$ times the $k$th event. They are all faces of the same process, viewed from different angles.

Equivalently: since $W_k = T_1 + T_2 + \cdots + T_k$ where $T_i$ are iid Exp$(\lambda)$ inter-arrival times, and the sum of $k$ independent Exp$(\lambda)$ is $\Gamma(\lambda, k)$ — this is the reproductive property of the Gamma in action.
On NSE, if large institutional orders arrive at rate $\lambda = 3$/second (Poisson), then: the time until the next large order is Exp(3) (mean = 1/3 second). The time until the 10th large order is $\Gamma(3, 10)$ (mean = 10/3 ≈ 3.3 seconds). This is exactly the model used to estimate optimal VWAP execution windows.

Orders arrive at an exchange at rate $\lambda = 4$ per second. What is the expected number of orders in 3 seconds?

A
$4$
B
$12$
C
$4/3$
D
$3$
Answer: B — $12$

$X_t \sim \text{Poi}(\lambda t)$ with $\lambda = 4$, $t = 3$. So $\lambda t = 12$ and $E(X_3) = 12$. The Poisson process scales linearly in time — double the time, double the expected count.

Same exchange, $\lambda = 4$/sec. What is $P(\text{0 orders in the next 0.5 seconds})$?

A
$e^{-4}$
B
$e^{-2}$
C
$e^{-0.5}$
D
$1 - e^{-2}$
Answer: B — $e^{-2}$

$\lambda t = 4 \times 0.5 = 2$. $P(X=0) = e^{-2}\frac{2^0}{0!} = e^{-2} \approx 0.135$. About 13.5% chance of a 0.5-second quiet period — not unusual at rate 4. Option D is $P(\text{at least 1 arrival})$ — the complement.

If arrivals are Poisson with rate $\lambda$, what is the distribution of the waiting time until the first arrival?

A
Poisson($\lambda$)
B
Exp($\lambda$) — derived from $P(W_1 \leq t) = 1 - e^{-\lambda t}$
C
Uniform$(0, 1/\lambda)$
D
$\Gamma(\lambda, 2)$
Answer: B — Exp($\lambda$)

$P(W_1 \leq t) = 1 - e^{-\lambda t}$. Differentiating: $f_{W_1}(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}$ — the Exp($\lambda$) PDF. Mean waiting time = $1/\lambda$. If orders arrive at 4/sec, expect to wait 0.25 seconds for the next one.

Large Nifty block orders arrive at rate $\lambda = 3$/min. What is the expected time until the 5th block order, and what is its distribution?

A
Exp(15); mean = $1/15$ min
B
$\Gamma(3, 5)$; mean = $5/3$ min
C
Poi(15); mean = 15 orders
D
$\Gamma(3, 3)$; mean = $1$ min
Answer: B — $\Gamma(3, 5)$, mean $= 5/3$ min

Waiting time for the $k$th arrival in a Poisson($\lambda$) process follows $\Gamma(\lambda, k)$. Here $\lambda=3$, $k=5$: $W_5 \sim \Gamma(3, 5)$. Mean $= k/\lambda = 5/3 \approx 1.67$ minutes. This equals $5 \times \frac{1}{\lambda}$ — five independent Exp(3) inter-arrival times.
probability / ch16 / q05★★★ Hard

An insurance company has claim rate $\lambda = 0.002$ per policy per year. They insure 8000 policies. What is the probability of exactly 20 claims in a year?

A
$e^{-8000} \cdot \frac{8000^{20}}{20!}$
B
$\binom{8000}{20}(0.002)^{20}(0.998)^{7980}$ — Binomial exact
C
$e^{-16}\frac{16^{20}}{20!}$ — Poisson with $\lambda t = 0.002 \times 8000 = 16$
D
$e^{-20}\frac{20^{16}}{16!}$
Answer: C — $e^{-16}\frac{16^{20}}{20!}$

$\lambda t = 0.002 \times 8000 = 16$. The number of claims follows Poi(16). $P(X=20) = e^{-16}\frac{16^{20}}{20!}$. Option B (Binomial) is technically correct but computationally impractical — this is exactly the regime where Poisson approximates Binomial (large $n$, small $p$, moderate $np$). The Poisson process formulation gives the exact model, not just an approximation.
