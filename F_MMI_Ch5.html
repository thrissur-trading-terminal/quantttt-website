5.1 The Complete Formula Reference

Before reading the live book, here is every calculation from this module collected in one place — with its source, its Python implementation in the dashboard, and what it tells you.

MIDPRICE Ch. 1 · Cartea p.16
$$P_{mid} = \frac{1}{2}(P_t^a + P_t^b)$$

Neutral geometric centre. No volume information. Used as the baseline reference for all other calculations.

Market Maker
Starting point for quote placement — post at $P_{mid} \pm \delta^*$
Liquidity Trader
Benchmark for impact cost: VWAP$_{exec}$ vs $P_{mid}$
MICROPRICE (Eq. 3.3) Ch. 2 · Cartea p.46
$$S_t^* = \frac{V_t^b}{V_t^b + V_t^a}P_t^a + \frac{V_t^a}{V_t^b + V_t^a}P_t^b$$

Volume-weighted mid using Level 1 quantities. Incorporates touch asymmetry. Leads price by 1–3 ticks on average.

Market Maker
Short-term direction signal at the touch — shade quotes toward microprice
Liquidity Trader
More accurate entry benchmark than midprice for timing aggressive orders
SPREAD IN BASIS POINTS Ch. 2 · Cartea p.71
$$\text{Spread}_{bps} = \frac{P_t^a - P_t^b}{P_{mid}} \times 10{,}000$$

Normalised crossing cost. Comparable across instruments and time. Minimum hurdle rate for any aggressive trade.

Market Maker
Profit opportunity when spread widens above typical — post inside spread aggressively
Liquidity Trader
Half-spread bps must be smaller than signal strength (urgency) in bps
VOLUME IMBALANCE Ch. 2 · Cartea p.295
$$\rho_t = \frac{V_t^b - V_t^a}{V_t^b + V_t^a} \in [-1, +1]$$

Full 50-level directional signal. Predictive power over future price direction and MO arrival rates. The most actionable single number from the book.

Market Maker
Skew quotes: positive $\rho_t$ → shade ask upward, tighten bid
Liquidity Trader
$\rho_t > +0.3$ signals good time to sell into buyers; $< -0.3$ good to buy into sellers
FILL PROBABILITY & OPTIMAL DEPTH (Eq. 2.3) Ch. 3 · Cartea p.29
$$P(\delta) = e^{-\kappa\delta} \qquad \delta^{*} = \frac{1}{\kappa} \qquad P(\delta^*) = e^{-1} \approx 36.8\%$$

Fill probability decays exponentially with depth. Optimal depth = reciprocal of liquidity parameter. Fill probability at optimum is always $e^{-1}$.

Market Maker
Post at $\delta^* = 1/\kappa$ — the unique profit-maximising depth. Never post deeper
Liquidity Trader
Use to understand where MMs are likely resting — depth clusters near $1/\kappa$ from mid
LIQUIDITY DISCOUNT & MARKET IMPACT Ch. 4 · Cartea pp.24–25
$$S_1 = \mu - \gamma\sigma^2\frac{i}{n+1} \qquad \text{Impact} = P_{mid} - \text{VWAP}_{exec}$$

Model estimate (Grossman–Miller) and empirical estimate (book walk) of execution cost. Grows with order size, volatility, and risk aversion; falls with MM competition.

Market Maker
Impact = revenue from absorbing LT flow. More competition (higher $n$) compresses this margin
Liquidity Trader
Minimise impact by slicing orders, timing execution, and choosing when $\rho_t$ favours her direction

5.2 A Complete Book Reading — Step by Step

We now apply every formula to the live NIFTY26MAYFUT snapshot from 29 April 2026 at 12:34:20 IST in a structured 8-step reading. This is the exact sequence a trained desk analyst runs through when a new snapshot arrives.

1
Establish the Baseline
Best Bid Rs.24,442.80 · Best Ask Rs.24,444.90. Compute midprice and spread.
$P_{mid} = \frac{24442.80 + 24444.90}{2} = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.85$  ·  Spread = Rs.2.10 = 42 ticks = 0.86 bps
2
Compute the Microprice
Touch quantities: Bid L1 = 130 lots, Ask L1 = 195 lots. Ask-heavy at the touch.
$S_t^* = \frac{130}{325} \times 24444.90 + \frac{195}{325} \times 24442.80 = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.64$  ·  Microprice Rs.0.21 below mid → mild downward lean at touch
3
Read the Full-Book Imbalance
TBQ = 5,28,125 · TSQ = 2,20,415. Full 50-level aggregate signal.
$\rho_t = \frac{528125 - 220415}{528125 + 220415} = +0.411$  ·  Strongly bullish book · Bid side 2.4× heavier than ask
4
Reconcile Steps 2 and 3
Microprice says mild downward lean at the touch; full-book imbalance says strongly bullish. These are not contradictory — they speak different time horizons.
Touch (L1): ask-heavy → price may dip 1–2 ticks short-term  ·  Full book: bid-heavy → price likely higher in next 1–5 minutes  ·  Net read: short-term dip into structural bid support
5
Locate the Structural Walls
Scan for levels with unusually high qty or low order count (iceberg pattern). Two standouts: Bid L8 and Ask L18.
Bid L8: Rs.24,440.00 · 7,475 lots · 22 orders → Avg 340 lots/order · Institutional support wall
Ask L18: Rs.24,450.00 · 12,415 lots · 116 orders → Avg 107 lots/order · Fragmented retail resistance
6
Estimate Market Maker Optimal Posting Zone
Assume $\kappa = 0.20$ (typical mid-session NIFTY Futures). Optimal depth $\delta^* = 1/0.20 = $ Rs.5.00 from mid.
MM bid zone: $24443.85 - 5.00 = $ Rs.24,438.85 (near L15–16 of bid book)
MM ask zone: $24443.85 + 5.00 = $ Rs.24,448.85 (near L7–8 of ask book)  ·  Fill probability: 36.8%
7
Compute Impact Cost for a Hypothetical 500-lot Sell
Walk the bid book: L1 (130 lots @ Rs.24,442.80) → L2 (195 lots @ Rs.24,442.50) → … until 500 lots filled.
VWAP$_{exec}$ ≈ Rs.24,441.85  ·  Impact = Rs.24,443.85 − Rs.24,441.85 = Rs.2.00/lot = 0.82 bps
Total cost on 500 lots: Rs.1,000 · Per lot-of-25: Rs.50
8
Form the Overall Assessment
Synthesise all signals into an actionable view for both market participants.
For a Market Maker: Book strongly bullish in aggregate. Post bid closer to mid than usual (tighten bid), widen ask by 1–2 ticks. The Rs.24,440 wall provides a natural stop for inventory risk.

For a Liquidity Trader selling: Rs.2.00/lot impact is acceptable if urgency exceeds Rs.2.00 + Rs.1.05 half-spread = Rs.3.05. Strong buy imbalance (+41%) means good absorption — sell now while buyers are plentiful, before imbalance reverses.

5.4 The Code — Key Calculation Functions

Every formula above lives in the compute_metrics() function in dashboard.py. Here is the exact implementation, with formula references:

def compute_metrics(msg): bp = np.array(msg.bidprice) # 50 bid prices ap = np.array(msg.askprice) # 50 ask prices bq = np.array(msg.bidqty) # 50 bid quantities aq = np.array(msg.askqty) # 50 ask quantities bo = np.array(msg.bidordn) # 50 bid order counts ao = np.array(msg.askordn) # 50 ask order counts # ── Ch.1: Midprice & Spread ────────────────────────────── best_bid = bp[0] best_ask = ap[0] mid = (best_bid + best_ask) / 2 # Cartea p.16 spread = best_ask - best_bid spread_bps = (spread / mid) * 10000 # Cartea p.71 # ── Ch.2: Microprice (Eq. 3.3) ────────────────────────── micro = (bq[0] * best_ask + aq[0] * best_bid) \ / (bq[0] + aq[0]) # Cartea Eq.(3.3) # ── Ch.2: Volume Imbalance ─────────────────────────────── tbq = msg.tbq # Total bid qty (all 50 levels) tsq = msg.tsq # Total ask qty (all 50 levels) imbalance = (tbq - tsq) / (tbq + tsq) # Cartea p.295, ρ_t ∈ [-1,+1] # ── Ch.2: Level Imbalance (top 10) ────────────────────── level_imb = (bq[:10] - aq[:10]) \ / (bq[:10] + aq[:10] + 1e-9) # ε guard for zero depth # ── Cumulative depth ───────────────────────────────────── cum_bq = np.cumsum(bq) # CumBid_k cum_aq = np.cumsum(aq) # CumAsk_k return dict( mid=mid, spread=spread, spread_bps=spread_bps, micro=micro, tbq=tbq, tsq=tsq, imbalance=imbalance, level_imb=level_imb, cum_bq=cum_bq, cum_aq=cum_aq, bp=bp, ap=ap, bq=bq, aq=aq, bo=bo, ao=ao, )

5.5 What This Module Has Not Covered

This module covers the foundational static models. Several important extensions exist in Cartea et al. (2015) and the broader literature:

  • Dynamic inventory management — the full Avellaneda–Stoikov model (Chapters 10 of Cartea) where the MM's optimal depth changes continuously as her inventory accumulates risk
  • Optimal liquidation — how a large order should be sliced across time (TWAP/VWAP strategies) to minimise market impact, with order imbalance as a state variable (Chapter 12 of Cartea)
  • Informed trading and Kyle's lambda — the price impact parameter $\lambda$ from Kyle (1985), estimating how much price moves per unit of order flow
  • High-frequency alpha signals — using order flow, imbalance, and microprice to build short-term predictive models (the full Chapter 12 treatment)
  • Cross-asset microstructure — how Futures and Options books interact, lead-lag relationships between NIFTY Futures and ATM options order flow
For NSE-specific microstructure research: the SEBI Academic Research Programme has published several papers on NIFTY order book dynamics. The NSE's own EMERGE platform provides tick data access for academic researchers. The Fyers TBT WebSocket used in this module's dashboard is one of the few retail-accessible feeds providing the full 50-level book — most retail platforms cap at 5 levels.
Final Exam — All Chapters 4 questions
In the live NIFTY26MAYFUT snapshot, the microprice (Rs.24,443.64) is below the midprice (Rs.24,443.85), yet volume imbalance $\rho_t = +0.411$ is strongly positive. A market maker uses the microprice for 1-second quoting and $\rho_t$ for 5-minute inventory skewing. Which of the following correctly describes her combined strategy?
A Post symmetrically around the midprice — contradictory signals cancel each other out
B Post ask slightly below midprice (toward microprice) for the immediate tick, while skewing the inventory position long over the next 5 minutes in response to the +41% imbalance — using both signals on their respective time horizons
C Ignore the microprice since the full-book signal is more reliable — always use $\rho_t$
D Pull all quotes — conflicting signals mean the book is too uncertain to quote
Explanation: The key insight from Chapter 2 is that microprice and volume imbalance operate on different time horizons — they are not contradictory. The microprice (Level 1 only, ask-heavy touch) gives a 1-tick short-term lean downward. The full-book $\rho_t = +0.411$ gives a 1–5 minute bullish signal. A sophisticated MM uses both simultaneously: she sets her immediate ask quote slightly below mid (toward microprice) to be more competitive at the touch, while allowing her bid-side inventory to build (skewing long) because the medium-term book is bullish. Options A and D treat the signals as contradictory — they are not. Option C discards the microprice entirely, losing a valuable short-term signal.
A market maker with $\kappa = 0.20$ is posting at depth Rs.5.00. Suddenly, a large participant places 15,000 lots on the ask side at Rs.24,448 (near the MM's ask posting zone), raising $\kappa$ locally to 0.40 on the ask side. What should the MM do?
A Keep posting at Rs.5.00 depth — $\kappa$ changes don't affect the optimal strategy
B Move her ask limit order to the new optimal depth of Rs.2.50 (= $1/0.40$) — post closer to mid on the ask side to get ahead of the dense queue, while keeping the bid at Rs.5.00 depth
C Move both bid and ask to Rs.2.50 depth symmetrically
D Cancel the ask order entirely — too much competition
Explanation: The optimal depth formula $\delta^* = 1/\kappa$ applies independently to each side. The new ask-side $\kappa = 0.40$ gives $\delta^{*,+} = 1/0.40 = \text{Rs.}2.50$ — half the original depth. The MM should move her ask order closer to mid to get better queue position ahead of the dense order flow at Rs.24,448. The bid side is unchanged ($\kappa^- = 0.20$ still, $\delta^{*,-} = \text{Rs.}5.00$). Option C over-adjusts — the bid side liquidity didn't change. Option D is too conservative; a denser book on the ask side means more MO flow hitting ask orders, which actually increases expected fills even at a shallower depth.
The order count heatmap shows Ask Level 20 (Rs.24,450.00) has 12,415 lots across 116 orders. Ask Level 4 (Rs.24,445.00) has 3,965 lots across 10 orders. A liquidity trader needs to buy 4,000 lots. From a market impact perspective, which statement is correct?
A Level 20 is more expensive to cross because it has more total lots
B The trader should cross Level 4 first (3,965 lots at Rs.24,445.00), then the book will likely refresh before she needs to reach Level 20 — and Level 20 may partially cancel under buying pressure since many of its 116 small orders are fragile retail quotes
C Both levels are equivalent since their total notional is similar
D Level 20 is cheaper to cross because it has more participants sharing the risk
Explanation: This is an application of the order count / iceberg analysis from Chapter 1. Level 4 (Rs.24,445.00) has 3,965 lots across only 10 orders = 397 lots/order — large, concentrated orders that won't cancel easily under normal conditions. Level 20 (Rs.24,450.00) has 12,415 lots across 116 orders = 107 lots/order — highly fragmented retail quotes that are much more likely to cancel if they see a large buy order approaching (adverse selection from the quote-poster's side). The trader should buy aggressively through Level 4, which will likely stay put. By the time she needs Level 20's liquidity, many of those 116 small orders may have cancelled, making the level cheaper or thinner than it appears. Option A incorrectly assumes total lots = impact. Option D misreads order count as "shared risk" — each of the 116 orders at Level 20 is an individual participant who can cancel independently.
Using the compute_metrics() function in dashboard.py: the level_imbalance for Level 8 ($I_8$) is computed as $(V_{bid,8} - V_{ask,8}) / (V_{bid,8} + V_{ask,8} + \varepsilon)$ where $\varepsilon = 10^{-9}$. Level 8 has bid qty 7,475 and ask qty 65. A student says "the epsilon makes the result inaccurate — we should remove it." Is the student correct?
A Yes — the epsilon introduces a small error and should be removed for accuracy
B No — the epsilon is a division-by-zero guard for levels where both bid and ask qty are zero (which can occur in illiquid instruments); at Level 8 the denominator is 7,540 so the epsilon $10^{-9}$ changes the result by less than $10^{-13}$ — completely negligible
C Yes — epsilon should be replaced with the tick size Rs.0.05 for better numerical stability
D It depends — epsilon is only needed for the bid side, not the ask side
Explanation: At Level 8: $I_8 = (7475 - 65) / (7475 + 65 + 10^{-9}) = 7410 / 7540.000000001 \approx 0.98274$. Without epsilon: $7410/7540 \approx 0.98274$ — identical to 13 decimal places. The epsilon is $10^{-9}$, the denominator is $7540$ — the relative error introduced is $10^{-9}/7540 \approx 10^{-13}$, completely undetectable. The epsilon exists solely to prevent a division-by-zero NaN when both bid and ask qty are zero at a level (which happens on very illiquid instruments or at deep levels where neither side has resting orders). Removing it is risky — it would cause a runtime crash on those inputs. Option C suggests Rs.0.05 as an epsilon, which would actually introduce a meaningful error: $0.05/0 = \infty$ is avoided, but $0.05$ as additive epsilon for a denominator of 7,540 is still negligible — though using a dimensionless $10^{-9}$ is cleaner. Option D is wrong: the epsilon applies to the denominator $(V_{bid} + V_{ask})$ symmetrically.
