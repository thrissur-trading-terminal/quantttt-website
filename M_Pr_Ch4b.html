Geometric Probability

When the sample space is continuous — a line, a square, a triangle — probability becomes area. This chapter is where analysis and geometry shake hands, and the results are often beautifully surprising.

The Big Idea

In classical probability, we count outcomes. But what happens when there are uncountably many? Two points chosen randomly from $[0,1]$ — you can't count that. Instead, we measure. Probability becomes the ratio of a favourable area to the total area of the sample space.

This is the continuous version of the Laplace formula. Instead of $\frac{\text{favourable outcomes}}{\text{total outcomes}}$, we use $\frac{\text{favourable area}}{\text{total area}}$. The axioms still hold — we've just swapped counting for measuring.

Problem 1 — The Three Segments

Two points are chosen randomly from the unit interval $[0,1]$. They divide $[0,1]$ into three segments. Find the probability that all three segments have length $\geq \frac{1}{4}$.

Let the two points be $x$ and $y$ with $x < y$. The three segments have lengths $x$, $y-x$, and $1-y$. So $\Omega = \{(x,y) \mid 0 \leq x < y \leq 1\}$ — the upper triangle of the unit square.

The shaded triangle △ABC is the favourable region. Ω is the large triangle (half the unit square).

Setting up the constraints. All three segments $\geq \frac{1}{4}$ means:

$$x \geq \frac{1}{4}, \quad y - x \geq \frac{1}{4}, \quad 1-y \geq \frac{1}{4}$$ which simplifies to: $x \geq \frac{1}{4}$, $y \geq x + \frac{1}{4}$, $y \leq \frac{3}{4}$.

Finding the vertices of △ABC. The feasible region is a triangle with vertices:

$$A = \left(\frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{2}\right), \quad B = \left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{3}{4}\right), \quad C = \left(\frac{1}{4}, \frac{3}{4}\right)$$

Computing areas.

$$\text{Area}(\Delta ABC) = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{4} \times \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{32}$$ $$\text{Area}(\Omega) = \frac{1}{2} \times 1 \times 1 = \frac{1}{2}$$

Result:

$$\boxed{P(\text{all segments} \geq \tfrac{1}{4}) = \frac{1/32}{1/2} = \frac{1}{16}}$$
The sample space $\Omega$ here is the triangle $\{(x,y) \mid 0 \leq x < y \leq 1\}$, not the full unit square. We assumed $x < y$ at the start. Its area is $\frac{1}{2}$, not $1$. Always identify $\Omega$ precisely before computing — the denominator is the total area of $\Omega$, not necessarily $1$.

Problem 2 — Sum and Product Constraints

Two points $x, y$ are chosen randomly from $[0,1]$. Find the probability that their sum $\leq 1$ and product $\leq \frac{2}{9}$.

Here $x$ and $y$ are both chosen independently from $[0,1]$, so $\Omega$ is the full unit square with area $1$. We need the area of the region satisfying both $x + y \leq 1$ and $xy \leq \frac{2}{9}$.

The favourable region is decomposed into triangles and a curvilinear strip under the hyperbola $xy = \frac{2}{9}$.

The favourable region (satisfying both inequalities) is decomposed as: $\triangle DEF + \square AMFE + \triangle GBH + \int_{1/3}^{2/3} \frac{2}{9x}\,dx$

$$= \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{3}\cdot\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3}\cdot\frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{3}\cdot\frac{1}{3} + \frac{2}{9}\int_{1/3}^{2/3}\frac{1}{x}\,dx$$ $$= \frac{1}{9} + \frac{2}{9} + \frac{2}{9}\left(\log\frac{2}{3} - \log\frac{1}{3}\right)$$ $$= \frac{3}{9} + \frac{2}{9}\log 2$$ Since $\text{Area}(\Omega) = 1$: $$\boxed{P(x+y \leq 1 \text{ and } xy \leq \tfrac{2}{9}) = \frac{3}{9} + \frac{2}{9}\log 2}$$ Numerically: $\approx 0.333 + 0.154 \approx 0.487$.
The integral $\int_{1/3}^{2/3} \frac{2}{9x}\,dx$ computes the area under the hyperbola $xy = \frac{2}{9}$, i.e., $y = \frac{2}{9x}$, between $x = \frac{1}{3}$ and $x = \frac{2}{3}$. This is where $\log$ enters a probability problem — a beautiful and slightly unexpected result from a purely geometric setup.
Geometric probability appears in options trading when modelling the joint distribution of two correlated underlying prices. The "feasible region" in $(S_1, S_2)$ space — where both options are in-the-money simultaneously — is exactly this kind of constrained geometric area. The probability of a spread strategy paying off is literally the area of such a region divided by the total measure of the sample space.

Two points are picked from $[0,1]$. What is the sample space $\Omega$ if we label them $x < y$?

A
The full unit square $[0,1]^2$, with area 1
B
The upper triangle $\{(x,y) \mid 0 \leq x < y \leq 1\}$, with area $\frac{1}{2}$
C
The unit interval $[0,1]$, with length 1
D
The lower triangle $\{(x,y) \mid 0 \leq y < x \leq 1\}$, with area $\frac{1}{2}$
Answer: B

When we fix $x < y$, we restrict to the upper triangle above the diagonal $y = x$. This has area $\frac{1}{2}$ — half the unit square. This is the critical denominator in all geometric probability calculations on this problem. Always identify $\Omega$ and its measure before computing anything.
probability / ch04b / q02 ★★ Medium

In Problem 1, the favourable region △ABC has vertices at $(\frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{2})$, $(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{3}{4})$, $(\frac{1}{4}, \frac{3}{4})$. What is $P(\text{all 3 segments} \geq \frac{1}{4})$?

A
$\dfrac{1}{32}$
B
$\dfrac{1}{8}$
C
$\dfrac{1}{16}$
D
$\dfrac{1}{4}$
Answer: C — $\dfrac{1}{16}$

Area of △ABC: base = $\frac{3}{4} - \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$, height = $\frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{4}$. So area $= \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{4} \times \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{32}$.

Area of $\Omega = \frac{1}{2}$ (the upper triangle).

$$P = \frac{1/32}{1/2} = \frac{1}{16}$$ $\frac{1}{32}$ is the favourable area, not the probability — don't forget to divide by $\text{Area}(\Omega) = \frac{1}{2}$.
probability / ch04b / q03 ★★★ Hard

In Problem 2, the answer is $\frac{3}{9} + \frac{2}{9}\log 2$. Why does $\log$ appear in a geometric probability problem?

A
It's a coincidence — log appears because we used logarithmic coordinates
B
Because the sample space has logarithmic measure
C
Because integrating $\frac{1}{x}$ — the height under the hyperbola $xy = \frac{2}{9}$ — produces $\log x$
D
Because the constraint $x + y \leq 1$ is logarithmic in nature
Answer: C

The hyperbola $xy = \frac{2}{9}$ can be written as $y = \frac{2}{9x}$. The area under this curve from $x = \frac{1}{3}$ to $x = \frac{2}{3}$ is: $$\int_{1/3}^{2/3} \frac{2}{9x}\,dx = \frac{2}{9}\left[\log x\right]_{1/3}^{2/3} = \frac{2}{9}\log 2$$ The $\log$ is a direct consequence of the hyperbola's shape — it's the antiderivative of $\frac{1}{x}$. This is a recurring theme: constraints involving products of variables produce logarithms via integration.
