The $t$ and $F$ Distributions

The $t$ and $F$ distributions are not postulated — they are derived. This chapter builds them from the ground up using the Jacobian method: $t_n$ as the ratio of a standard Normal to a scaled $\chi^2$, and $F_{m,n}$ as the ratio of two independent $\chi^2$ variables. Four warm-up examples sharpen the technique first.

Warm-Up: Ratios via Jacobian

$X\sim\text{Exp}(1)$, $Y\sim\text{Exp}(2)$ independent. PDF of $U=X/Y$.

Case 1 — $V=X$: $Y=V/U$, $X=V$. $J = \begin{vmatrix}0&1\\-v/u^2&1/u\end{vmatrix} = v/u^2$. Integrating $v$: the integral $\int_0^\infty v\,e^{-v(1+2/u)}dv$ is the second moment of Exp$(1+2/u)$, which equals $1/(1+2/u)^2$.

$$f_U(u) = \frac{2}{u^2}\cdot\frac{u^2}{(u+2)^2} = \frac{2}{(u+2)^2}, \quad u>0$$

Case 2 — $V=Y$: $X=UV$, $Y=V$. $J=|v|=v$. Same answer. ✓

$X\sim\Gamma(\lambda,\alpha)$, $Y\sim\Gamma(\lambda,\beta)$ independent. PDF of $U=X/Y$.

$V=Y$: $X=UV$, $Y=V$, $J=v$. Joint: $f(u,v) \propto e^{-\lambda v(u+1)}(uv)^{\alpha-1}v^{\beta-1}\cdot v$. Integrating $v\in(0,\infty)$ via Gamma integral:

$$f_U(u) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}\cdot\frac{u^{\alpha-1}}{(u+1)^{\alpha+\beta}}, \quad u>0 \implies U\sim\beta_2(\alpha,\beta)$$
Same $X,Y$. PDF of $U = X/(X+Y)$.

$U\in(0,1)$ since $X,Y>0$. Set $V=X+Y$: $X=UV$, $Y=V(1-U)$. Jacobian:

$$J = \begin{vmatrix}v & u \\ -v & 1-u\end{vmatrix} = v(1-u)+uv = v \geq 0$$

Joint: $f(u,v) \propto e^{-\lambda v}(uv)^{\alpha-1}(v(1-u))^{\beta-1}\cdot v = e^{-\lambda v}\,u^{\alpha-1}(1-u)^{\beta-1}v^{\alpha+\beta-1}$. Integrating $v$ via Gamma:

$$f_U(u) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}u^{\alpha-1}(1-u)^{\beta-1}, \quad 0So $\Gamma(\lambda,\alpha)/(\Gamma(\lambda,\alpha)+\Gamma(\lambda,\beta))\sim\beta_1(\alpha,\beta)$. Elegant: the Gamma rate $\lambda$ cancels completely.

The $t$-Distribution: Derived from First Principles

Fisher's $t_n$ distribution: If $X\sim N(0,1)$ and $Y\sim\chi^2_n$ are independent, then: $$t_n = \frac{X}{\sqrt{Y/n}}$$ has the $t$-distribution with $n$ degrees of freedom.
Set $Z = X/\sqrt{Y/n}$ (the quantity of interest), $W = Y$ (auxiliary). Inverse: $X = Z\sqrt{W/n}$, $Y=W$. $$J = \begin{vmatrix}\sqrt{W/n} & Z/(2\sqrt{Wn}) \\ 0 & 1\end{vmatrix} = \sqrt{W/n} = \sqrt{w/n}$$ Joint PDF ($z\in\mathbb{R}$, $w>0$): $$f(w,z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-z^2w/(2n)}\cdot\frac{1}{2^{n/2}\Gamma(n/2)}e^{-w/2}w^{n/2-1}\cdot\sqrt{w/n}$$ $$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\cdot 2^{n/2}\Gamma(n/2)\sqrt{n}}\,e^{-w(1+z^2/n)/2}\,w^{(n+1)/2-1}$$ Integrate $w$ via Gamma integral $\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda w}w^{k-1}dw = \Gamma(k)/\lambda^k$ with $k=(n+1)/2$, $\lambda=(1+z^2/n)/2$: $$f(z) = \frac{\Gamma\!\left(\frac{n+1}{2}\right)}{\Gamma\!\left(\frac{n}{2}\right)\sqrt{\pi n}}\left(1+\frac{z^2}{n}\right)^{-(n+1)/2}$$ Since $\sqrt{\pi} = \Gamma(\frac{1}{2})$, this can be written: $$\boxed{f(z) = \frac{1}{B\!\left(\frac{n}{2},\frac{1}{2}\right)\sqrt{n}}\left(1+\frac{z^2}{n}\right)^{-(n+1)/2}, \quad -\infty < z < \infty}$$
Key properties:

1. Symmetric around 0; $E(t_n) = 0$ (for $n>1$).
2. Heavier tails than $N(0,1)$ — the $\chi^2$ denominator adds randomness that spreads the distribution.
3. As $n\to\infty$: $t_n\to N(0,1)$. For $n\geq 25$, essentially identical to standard Normal.

The shape looks like a flattened bell curve — same centre, wider tails. $t_{10}$ has noticeably heavier tails than $N(0,1)$; $t_{25}$ is nearly indistinguishable.

The $F$-Distribution: Derived from First Principles

Snedecor's $F_{m,n}$ distribution: If $X\sim\chi^2_m$ and $Y\sim\chi^2_n$ are independent, then: $$F_{m,n} = \frac{X/m}{Y/n}$$ has the $F$-distribution with $m$ and $n$ degrees of freedom.
Key insight — reduce to a known result: $$F_{m,n} = \frac{X/m}{Y/n} = \frac{n}{m}\cdot\frac{X/2}{Y/2}$$ Since $X\sim\chi^2_m = \Gamma(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{m}{2})$ and $Y\sim\chi^2_n = \Gamma(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{n}{2})$ (same rate $\lambda=\frac{1}{2}$), from the warm-up result: $(X/2)/(Y/2) = X/Y\sim\beta_2(m/2,n/2)$.

So $F_{m,n} = \frac{n}{m}\cdot X_{\beta_2}$ where $X_{\beta_2}\sim\beta_2(m/2,n/2)$.

Apply the 1D change of variable $Z = \frac{n}{m}X$, i.e. $X=\frac{m}{n}Z$, $dx/dz=m/n$: $$f(z) = \frac{1}{B(m/2,n/2)}\cdot\left(\frac{m}{n}z\right)^{m/2-1}\cdot\frac{1}{\left(1+\frac{m}{n}z\right)^{(m+n)/2}}\cdot\frac{m}{n}$$ $$\boxed{f(z) = \frac{(m/n)^{m/2}}{B(m/2,n/2)}\cdot\frac{z^{m/2-1}}{\left(1+\frac{m}{n}z\right)^{(m+n)/2}}, \quad z>0}$$
Connection between $t$ and $F$: $$t_n^2 = \frac{[N(0,1)]^2}{\chi^2_n/n} = \frac{\chi^2_1/1}{\chi^2_n/n} = F_{1,n}$$ Squaring a $t_n$ variable gives an $F_{1,n}$ variable.
Both distributions are everywhere in Indian quantitative finance. When testing whether a regression coefficient (e.g., beta of a Nifty stock) is statistically significant, the $t$-statistic is used. When comparing the explanatory power of two models of options volatility, the $F$-statistic is used. The SEBI BANKNIFTY manipulation analysis (Jane Street case, Jan 2024) involved testing whether observed price impact was statistically anomalous — exactly the kind of hypothesis test built on these distributions.

$X\sim N(0,1)$ and $Y\sim\chi^2_{16}$ are independent. $t = X/\sqrt{Y/16}$ has which distribution?

A
$N(0,1)$
B
$t_{16}$ — $t$ with 16 degrees of freedom
C
$F_{1,16}$
D
$\chi^2_{17}$
Answer: B — $t_{16}$

By definition: $N(0,1)/\sqrt{\chi^2_n/n}\sim t_n$. Here $n=16$. The degrees of freedom of the $\chi^2$ denominator become the degrees of freedom of the resulting $t$. For large $n$ (like 16), this is already fairly close to $N(0,1)$ but has heavier tails.
probability / ch21 / q02★★ Medium

$X\sim\Gamma(\lambda,\alpha)$ and $Y\sim\Gamma(\lambda,\beta)$ independent. The distribution of $X/(X+Y)$ is:

A
$\beta_2(\alpha,\beta)$ on $(0,\infty)$
B
$\beta_1(\alpha,\beta)$ on $(0,1)$ — the rate $\lambda$ cancels
C
$\Gamma(\lambda,\alpha+\beta)$
D
Uniform$(0,1)$ — by the Probability Integral Transform
Answer: B — $\beta_1(\alpha,\beta)$ on $(0,1)$

With $U=X/(X+Y)$, $V=X+Y$: $J=v$, and integrating $v$ gives $f_U(u) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}u^{\alpha-1}(1-u)^{\beta-1}$. The Gamma rate $\lambda$ cancels entirely. This is a beautiful result: $X/(X+Y)$ loses all information about the scale parameter.
probability / ch21 / q03★★ Medium

If $T\sim t_{25}$, what is the distribution of $T^2$?

A
$t_{50}$ — squaring doubles the degrees of freedom
B
$\chi^2_{25}$ — squaring a $t$ gives chi-squared
C
$F_{1,25}$ — since $t_n^2 = F_{1,n}$
D
$N(0,1)^2$ — approximately, for large $n$
Answer: C — $F_{1,25}$

$t_n = N(0,1)/\sqrt{\chi^2_n/n}$, so $t_n^2 = [N(0,1)]^2/(\chi^2_n/n) = \chi^2_1/1\div\chi^2_n/n = F_{1,n}$. Squaring a $t_n$ variable gives $F_{1,n}$. This is why two-tailed $t$-tests and one-tailed $F$-tests on a single coefficient give equivalent results.

In deriving the $t$-distribution PDF, the $w$-integral becomes $\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda w}w^{k-1}dw$ with $\lambda = (1+z^2/n)/2$ and $k=(n+1)/2$. What does this evaluate to?

A
$\Gamma(k)$ — the $\lambda$ is absorbed
B
$\Gamma(k)/\lambda^k$ — standard Gamma integral result
C
$e^{-\lambda}\Gamma(k)$
D
$k!\,/\lambda^k$
Answer: B — $\Gamma(k)/\lambda^k$

Standard Gamma integral: $\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda w}w^{k-1}dw = \Gamma(k)/\lambda^k$. This is the same integral used throughout the course. With $\lambda=(1+z^2/n)/2$ and $k=(n+1)/2$: the result is $\Gamma((n+1)/2)/((1+z^2/n)/2)^{(n+1)/2}$, which produces the $(1+z^2/n)^{-(n+1)/2}$ factor in the $t$-PDF.
probability / ch21 / q05★★★ Hard

$X\sim\chi^2_4$ and $Y\sim\chi^2_{20}$ independent. The ratio $F=(X/4)/(Y/20)$ follows $F_{4,20}$. What intermediate distribution does $X/Y$ follow, before the scaling $n/m = 5$?

A
$\beta_1(2,10)$ on $(0,1)$
B
$\beta_2(2,10)$ on $(0,\infty)$
C
$\Gamma(\frac{1}{2}, 12)$ — sum of the shape parameters
D
$F_{4,20}$ directly — no intermediate step needed
Answer: B — $\beta_2(2,10)$

$\chi^2_4 = \Gamma(\frac{1}{2},2)$ and $\chi^2_{20}=\Gamma(\frac{1}{2},10)$, both with the same rate $\lambda=\frac{1}{2}$. By the ratio-of-Gammas result: $X/Y\sim\beta_2(m/2,n/2) = \beta_2(2,10)$. Then $F_{4,20} = (20/4)\cdot(X/Y) = 5\cdot\beta_2(2,10)$, and the 1D change of variable $z=5x$ produces the F-PDF.
