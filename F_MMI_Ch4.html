Liquidity Traders & Price Impact

The market maker posts and waits. The liquidity trader must transact — now or soon. This chapter covers market impact, the liquidity discount, urgency, and the adverse selection that connects both sides of the book.

4.1 Who Is a Liquidity Trader?

In Chapters 2 and 3 we took the market maker's perspective — passive, quoting, waiting to be filled. Now we flip sides. A liquidity trader (LT) is any participant who needs to transact for reasons external to the book itself: hedging an options position, deploying capital into an index fund, rebalancing a portfolio, executing a program trade.

The liquidity trader's problem is fundamentally different from the market maker's. She cannot wait indefinitely — she has an urgency that forces her to cross the spread and accept whatever the book offers. The question is not where to post, but how much her trade will cost, and whether that cost is worth paying now versus waiting for better conditions.

A liquidity trader is defined by having an exogenous reason to trade — not informational advantage, not market-making profit, but an external need. Cartea et al. (2015) model this as a liquidity shock: a trader arrives with a position of size $i$ that she must unwind. The cost she pays to do so is the liquidity discount — the gap between the efficient price and her actual execution price.
Cartea, Jaimungal & Penalva (2015), Chapter 2, Section 2.1.1 — Market Making and Section 2.1.2 — Trading Costs, pp. 20–25. The Grossman & Miller (1988) framework.

4.2 The Liquidity Discount

In the Grossman–Miller (1988) framework, an LT wants to sell $i > 0$ units immediately. There are $n$ market makers willing to absorb those units, but only at a discount below the efficient price $\mu$ — because they are taking on price risk by holding the LT's position.

The equilibrium execution price for the LT's trade is:

$$S_1 = \mu - \gamma\sigma^2 \frac{i}{n+1}$$
Cartea et al. (2015), Ch. 2, §2.1.1, p. 24 — Grossman–Miller equilibrium price

Where:

  • $\mu$ = efficient (fair) price of the asset — the price that would prevail with no frictions
  • $\gamma > 0$ = risk aversion coefficient of the market makers
  • $\sigma^2$ = variance of the asset's returns (price uncertainty)
  • $i > 0$ = size of the LT's sell order (number of units)
  • $n$ = number of competing market makers
  • $\gamma\sigma^2 \frac{i}{n+1}$ = the liquidity discount — the price concession the LT must offer
The liquidity discount has a beautiful structure: it grows with $i$ (larger order = bigger discount), grows with $\sigma^2$ (riskier asset = bigger discount because MMs demand more compensation), grows with $\gamma$ (more risk-averse MMs = bigger discount), and shrinks with $n$ (more competition among MMs = smaller discount, because they compete to absorb the flow). The limit as $n \to \infty$ gives $S_1 \to \mu$ — perfect competition eliminates the discount entirely, recovering the efficient price.
WORKED EXAMPLE — LIQUIDITY DISCOUNT NSE:NIFTY26MAYFUT · 29 Apr 2026

A fund manager needs to sell 500 lots of NIFTY26MAYFUT immediately. The current efficient price is $\mu = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.85$ (the midprice). Suppose there are $n = 4$ active market makers, each with risk aversion $\gamma = 0.002$, and daily NIFTY return variance $\sigma^2 = 0.0004$ (corresponding to roughly 2% daily vol on a Rs.24,000 index).

Step 1 — Compute the liquidity discount

$$\text{Discount} = \gamma\sigma^2 \frac{i}{n+1} = 0.002 \times 0.0004 \times \frac{500}{5} = 0.002 \times 0.0004 \times 100 = \text{Rs.}0.00008$$

This is tiny — as expected for NIFTY Futures, which is one of the most liquid instruments on the planet. The discount effectively vanishes for a 500-lot order. Let us instead use a more realistic parameterisation for Indian single-stock Futures where $\gamma = 0.5$ and $\sigma^2 = 0.0025$:

$$\text{Discount} = 0.5 \times 0.0025 \times \frac{500}{5} = 0.5 \times 0.0025 \times 100 = \text{Rs.}0.125 \text{ per unit}$$

Step 2 — Execution price

$$S_1 = \mu - \text{Discount} = 24{,}443.85 - 0.125 = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.725$$

Execution price = Rs.24,443.725 vs efficient price Rs.24,443.85
Discount = Rs.0.125 per unit = Rs.3.125 per lot (lot size 25) = Rs.1,562.50 total for 500 lots. This is the direct model estimate. The actual cost in the live book depends on how the 500 lots walk through the visible depth — which we calculate next.

4.3 Market Impact — Walking the Book

The Grossman–Miller model gives an aggregate estimate. In practice, we can compute the exact market impact from the visible order book by simulating the trade execution level by level. This is called walking the book.

For a market sell order of size $Q$, the execution proceeds as follows: consume the best bid entirely, then move to the next level, and so on until $Q$ lots are filled. The volume-weighted average price (VWAP) of execution is:

$$\text{VWAP}_\text{exec} = \frac{\sum_{k=1}^{K} P_{bid,k} \cdot q_k^*}{\sum_{k=1}^{K} q_k^*}$$
Cartea et al. (2015), Ch. 2, §2.1.2 — Trading Costs, pp. 24–25

Where:

  • $K$ = number of levels consumed (the order walks through $K$ bid levels)
  • $P_{bid,k}$ = price at the $k$-th bid level
  • $q_k^*$ = quantity filled at the $k$-th level ($= V_{bid,k}$ for fully consumed levels, $\leq V_{bid,K}$ for the last partial level)

The market impact cost is the difference between the midprice and the VWAP of execution:

$$\text{Impact Cost} = P_{mid} - \text{VWAP}_\text{exec}$$

The static simulation is a lower bound on real execution cost; live impact is typically 20–50% higher on large orders due to book dynamics and adverse selection.

4.4 Urgency — Should You Trade Now?

A rational liquidity trader compares the cost of executing now against the benefit of executing at all. Cartea et al. (2015) formalise this through an urgency parameter — the cash-equivalent utility gain from completing the trade. The LT trades only if:

$$\text{Urgency} > \text{Execution Cost}$$
Cartea et al. (2015), Ch. 2, §2.3.2 — Price-Sensitive Liquidity Traders, p. 36

If the cost to execute (impact + spread) exceeds the urgency, the rational response is to wait, use a patient limit order strategy, or slice the order across time to reduce the footprint on the book. The urgency framework creates a natural threshold — below it, the liquidity trader becomes a patient limit order poster; above it, she becomes an aggressive market order sender.

Think of urgency as the deadline pressure on a trade. A fund with T+2 settlement obligations and a delta hedge expiring today has very high urgency — the cost of not trading exceeds any realistic market impact. A quant desk rebalancing a statistical arbitrage portfolio has low urgency — it can wait hours for the right price. The same book, the same spread, the same impact cost — but completely different decisions depending on urgency.
WORKED EXAMPLE — URGENCY vs EXECUTION COST NIFTY Straddle Delta Hedge · Expiry Day

A TTT short-straddle position on NIFTY26MAYFUT requires a delta hedge of 200 lots on expiry day. The position will lose Rs.0.50 per lot per minute if left unhedged (gamma risk). The current NIFTY Futures book shows an impact cost of Rs.1.80 per lot for a 200-lot market sell order.

Urgency calculation

Time remaining to expiry: 45 minutes.
Total unhedged loss if not trading now: $200 \times 0.50 \times 45 = \text{Rs.}4{,}500$
Per-lot urgency: $\text{Rs.}4{,}500 / 200 = \text{Rs.}22.50$ per lot

Compare to execution cost

Execution cost: Rs.1.80 per lot (market impact only)
Spread cost: Rs.2.10 / 2 = Rs.1.05 per lot (half-spread for one-way trip)
Total execution cost: Rs.1.80 + Rs.1.05 = Rs.2.85 per lot

Decision

$$\text{Urgency (Rs.22.50)} \gg \text{Execution Cost (Rs.2.85)}$$

Trade immediately — urgency exceeds cost by 7.9×
The Rs.22.50/lot urgency crushes the Rs.2.85/lot execution cost. Waiting to find a better price risks accumulating far larger gamma losses. This is the canonical case where the LT must be the aggressive side — patience here is not a virtue, it is a liability.

4.5 Adverse Selection — The Connection to Market Making

We now have all the pieces to understand the most important concept in market microstructure: adverse selection. This is the risk that the market maker faces when her limit order is filled — not by a benign liquidity trader, but by an informed trader who knows which direction price is about to move.

Cartea et al. (2015) model this through order toxicity. The probability that any given incoming MO is from an informed (toxic) trader is $\alpha$. An informed buyer knows the true asset value $V_H > \mu$; an informed seller knows $V_L < \mu$. The MM, knowing that some fraction $\alpha$ of her counterparties are informed, must widen her spread to recover the expected losses from those informed trades:

$$a_t = \mu_t + \Delta_{a,t} = \mu_t + \frac{1}{1 + \frac{1-\alpha}{\alpha} \cdot \frac{1/2}{p_t}}(V_H - \mu_t)$$ $$b_t = \mu_t - \Delta_{b,t} = \mu_t - \frac{1}{1 + \frac{1-\alpha}{\alpha} \cdot \frac{1/2}{1-p_t}}(\mu_t - V_L)$$
Cartea et al. (2015), Ch. 2, §2.3.1 — Price Dynamics, p. 36

Where:

  • $a_t, b_t$ = ask and bid prices posted by the MM at time $t$
  • $\mu_t = \mathbb{E}[v \mid \mathcal{F}_t]$ = MM's current estimate of the asset's true value given all observed order flow
  • $\alpha$ = prevalence of informed (toxic) traders — fraction of MOs from informed participants
  • $p_t = \mathbb{P}(v = V_H \mid \mathcal{F}_t)$ = MM's current probability that the asset is worth $V_H$
  • $V_H, V_L$ = high and low possible asset values (informed traders' private information)
  • $\Delta_{a,t}, \Delta_{b,t}$ = ask and bid half-spreads, which increase with toxicity $\alpha$
Cartea et al. (2015), Chapter 2, Section 2.3.1 — Price Dynamics, p. 36: the bid-ask prices are derived by updating the prior distribution of asset values using Bayes' rule on observed order flow. As MMs observe more buy orders, $p_t$ increases (buy pressure is bullish signal), which widens the ask spread and narrows the bid spread.

The key insight: the MM widens her spread not out of greed, but out of necessity. Every informed trade she accepts costs her the full informational advantage of the informed trader. The spread is the only mechanism she has to recover those losses across the larger volume of uninformed (liquidity) trades.

Imagine a poker table where 30% of players are card-counters (informed traders) and 70% are tourists playing for fun (liquidity traders). You are the house, taking a fixed percentage from each pot. If card-counters only play when they have an edge, your house cut must be large enough to recover what you lose to the card-counters from what you earn from the tourists. The bid-ask spread is the house cut. Toxicity $\alpha$ is the fraction of card-counters at the table.

4.6 The View from Both Sides of the Book

We now have the full picture. Here is how the same order book data is used completely differently depending on which side of the trade you are on:

Situation Market Maker's lens Liquidity Trader's lens
Large bid wall at L8 (7,475 lots, Rs.24,440) Signals institutional support — shade bid quotes upward, post tighter on ask side Reduces impact cost if selling — more depth to absorb my order before price falls
Ask level 18: 12,415 lots at Rs.24,450 across 116 orders Fragmented retail at round number — easy to jump with one better price; low adverse selection risk here Large resistance level — a buy order walking through this will pay Rs.24,450 for 12,415 lots
Volume imbalance +41% (TBQ dominates) Bullish signal — widen ask half-spread $\Delta_a$ to capture more upside, tighten bid Good time to sell into the bid — lots of buyers waiting to absorb supply at good prices
Spread 42 ticks (Rs.2.10) Unusually wide — aggressive quoting opportunity; post inside the spread for better fills High crossing cost — Rs.1.05/lot half-spread must be justified by urgency before sending MO
Microprice below midprice (ask-heavy touch) Short-term downward lean at touch — shade ask quotes slightly downward Slight selling pressure at touch — a buy MO may move price up less than usual
On NSE expiry days (Thursday for weekly NIFTY options), adverse selection spikes dramatically. Informed institutional players who know their net delta positions make large, urgent trades in Futures to hedge. The fraction of toxic order flow $\alpha$ can spike from 20–30% on normal days to 50–70% in the last 30 minutes before expiry. This is why NIFTY Futures spreads widen measurably on Thursday afternoons — market makers are not panicking, they are correctly pricing the higher adversity of the order flow.
Practice Problems 4 questions
In the Grossman–Miller framework, a liquidity trader wants to sell 1,000 lots. Currently $n=4$ market makers are active, each with $\gamma = 0.3$ and $\sigma^2 = 0.002$. The efficient price is Rs.24,443.85. What is the execution price?
A $S_1 = 24443.85 - 0.3 \times 0.002 \times 1000 = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.25$
B $S_1 = 24443.85 - 0.3 \times 0.002 \times \frac{1000}{5} = 24443.85 - 0.12 = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.73$
C $S_1 = 24443.85 + 0.3 \times 0.002 \times \frac{1000}{5} = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.97$ (premium, not discount)
D $S_1 = 24443.85 - 0.3 \times 0.002 \times \frac{1000}{4} = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.70$
Explanation: The formula is $S_1 = \mu - \gamma\sigma^2 \frac{i}{n+1}$. With $n=4$ MMs, the denominator is $n+1=5$, not $n=4$ (Option D trap). Discount $= 0.3 \times 0.002 \times (1000/5) = 0.3 \times 0.002 \times 200 = 0.12$. So $S_1 = 24443.85 - 0.12 = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.73$. Option A forgets to divide by $n+1$. Option C has the wrong sign — selling always produces a discount below $\mu$, never a premium.
Using the NIFTY26MAYFUT bid book from Chapter 1, a market sell order of 8,000 lots is sent. The top 8 bid levels have qtys: 130, 195, 65, 65, 390, 455, 520, 7,475. After consuming those 8 levels, how many lots remain unfilled, and what does the order do next?
A 0 lots remain — the top 8 levels sum to exactly 8,000 lots
B 8,000 lots remain — a 8,000-lot order cannot be filled from the visible book
C 705 lots remain unfilled after consuming levels 1–8 (total 7,295 lots); the order walks to level 9 and beyond until fully filled or cancelled
D 5,705 lots remain — the order can only fill at level 8 since that is where the large wall is
Explanation: Sum of bid qty levels 1–8: $130+195+65+65+390+455+520+7{,}475 = 9{,}295$ lots... wait, let me recount: $130+195 = 325$, $+65 = 390$, $+65 = 455$, $+390 = 845$, $+455 = 1{,}300$, $+520 = 1{,}820$, $+7{,}475 = 9{,}295$. So actually levels 1–8 sum to 9,295 lots — more than the 8,000-lot order. The order is fully filled within the first 8 levels. It consumes levels 1–7 (1,820 lots) and then takes $8{,}000 - 1{,}820 = 6{,}180$ lots from the 7,475-lot wall at level 8, leaving 1,295 lots still resting at Rs.24,440. A quant desk wants to buy 300 lots of NIFTY26MAYFUT. The impact cost is estimated at Rs.0.80/lot and the half-spread is Rs.1.05/lot, giving total execution cost Rs.1.85/lot. The desk's signal predicts a Rs.5.00/lot gain over the next 2 minutes. Should they trade using a market order or a patient limit order?
A Market order — urgency (Rs.5.00 signal value) exceeds execution cost (Rs.1.85) by 2.7×; waiting with a limit order risks missing the move entirely
B Limit order — always post patiently to avoid paying the spread
C Do not trade — the signal value (Rs.5.00) is too small relative to NIFTY's volatility
D Wait for the spread to narrow to 1 tick before trading
Explanation: Urgency = Rs.5.00/lot signal value. Execution cost = Rs.1.85/lot. Since urgency (Rs.5.00) $\gg$ execution cost (Rs.1.85), the urgency threshold is clearly exceeded — trade with a market order. Net expected gain after costs: $5.00 - 1.85 = \text{Rs.}3.15/\text{lot}$, or Rs.945 total for 300 lots. Option B (always use limit orders) ignores urgency entirely — a limit order might not fill if price moves away, and the Rs.5.00 signal has a 2-minute window. Option D (wait for spread to narrow) could mean waiting indefinitely, losing the alpha signal.
NIFTY is 30 minutes from weekly expiry. A market maker observes that 70% of incoming MOs in the last 5 minutes have been buys (strongly directional flow). According to the adverse selection model, what should she do with her quotes?
A Keep quotes unchanged — the MM should always quote tight to maximise fill rate
B Widen both bid and ask symmetrically by the same amount
C Raise $\mu_t$ (update her midprice estimate upward given the buy flow), widen the ask half-spread $\Delta_{a,t}$ significantly, and tighten the bid half-spread $\Delta_{b,t}$ — she expects informed buying and needs to recover adverse selection losses on sells while eagerly buying
D Pull all quotes entirely — never quote when flow is directional
Explanation: From Cartea et al. p.36: as the MM observes more buy orders, $p_t$ increases (probability of high asset value goes up), which causes $\mu_t$ to rise (Bayesian update), $\Delta_{a,t}$ to widen (she is selling into informed buyers — expensive), and $\Delta_{b,t}$ to narrow (she wants to buy at the updated lower true value). This is the adverse selection adjustment: the ask spread widens to protect against the informed buying pressure, while the bid narrows to accumulate inventory at what she now believes is a depressed price. Option B (symmetric widening) fails to use the directional signal — it protects one side unnecessarily. Option D is too aggressive; pulling all quotes is only rational if the book is completely toxic, not merely directional.
