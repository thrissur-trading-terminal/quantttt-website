The Characteristic Function Machine

Heston's real genius was not the model itself — it was recognising that his model has a closed-form characteristic function. That one fact turns an analytically intractable problem into a numerical integration that runs in microseconds.

Why the PDE Approach Breaks Down

In Chapter 1, BSM gave us a closed-form option price because the stock price distribution at maturity is lognormal — a fully characterised distribution with a known density. Computing $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[(S_T - K)^+]$ was a straightforward Gaussian integral, and the answer was $N(d_1)$ and $N(d_2)$.

In Heston, the stock price $S_T$ is not lognormally distributed. It is the endpoint of a process whose diffusion coefficient $\sqrt{v_t}$ is itself a random process. The marginal distribution of $S_T$ has no simple closed form — it is a complicated mixture of lognormals, weighted by the random path of $v_t$. You cannot write it down as a density and integrate it analytically.

The BSM PDE route also fails. In BSM, there is one state variable ($S$), and the PDE has one solution. In Heston, there are two state variables ($S$ and $v$), making the PDE two-dimensional. Two-dimensional PDEs generally do not have closed-form solutions. Solving them numerically requires a grid over $(S, v, t)$, which is slower by orders of magnitude.

So how did Heston price options in his 1993 paper? He took a completely different route. Instead of trying to find the distribution of $S_T$ directly, he computed its characteristic function — and then inverted that to get prices.

Think of the characteristic function as a different way of encoding a probability distribution. The density and the characteristic function carry exactly the same information — they are Fourier transform pairs. But sometimes one is tractable when the other is not. Heston's insight was that the characteristic function of $\ln S_T$, conditioned on $v_0$, satisfies a system of ODEs that can be solved in closed form — even though the density of $S_T$ cannot be written down explicitly.

What Is a Characteristic Function?

Let $X$ be a real-valued random variable. Its characteristic function is:

$$\phi_X(u) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[e^{iuX}\right], \quad u \in \mathbb{R}$$

where $i = \sqrt{-1}$ is the imaginary unit. The characteristic function is a complex-valued function of a real argument $u$.

Characteristic function. For a random variable $X$ with distribution $\mu$, the characteristic function is: $$\phi_X(u) = \mathbb{E}[e^{iuX}] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{iux} \, d\mu(x)$$ If $X$ has a density $f_X(x)$, then $\phi_X(u) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{iux} f_X(x)\,dx$ — precisely the Fourier transform of $f_X$ evaluated at $-u$. The CF always exists (the integrand has modulus 1), and it uniquely determines the distribution of $X$.

Three properties make the CF powerful for our purposes:

Uniqueness. Two distributions have the same CF if and only if they are identical. So knowing $\phi_X$ is the same as knowing the distribution of $X$.

Inversion. Given $\phi_X(u)$, we can recover the CDF of $X$ via the Gil-Pelaez inversion formula (which we will use in Chapter 4 to turn $\phi$ into option prices):

$$F_X(x) = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{-iux}\phi_X(u)}{iu}\right]du$$

Affine transforms. If $Y = aX + b$, then $\phi_Y(u) = e^{iub}\phi_X(au)$. If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, $\phi_{X+Y}(u) = \phi_X(u)\phi_Y(u)$. These rules make CFs easy to manipulate algebraically.

A useful sanity check: if $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then: $$\phi_X(u) = \mathbb{E}[e^{iuX}] = e^{i\mu u - \sigma^2 u^2/2}$$ This is the moment generating function (MGF) evaluated at $iu$. The CF always exists because $|e^{iuX}| = 1$ — unlike the MGF, which can blow up. For option pricing, we need the CF of $\ln S_T$ under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$.

The Feynman-Kac Theorem — Connecting PDEs to Expectations

The Feynman-Kac theorem is the bridge between stochastic processes and PDEs. It says: the solution to a certain class of linear PDE can be written as a conditional expectation under a corresponding stochastic process — and vice versa.

Feynman-Kac (informal statement). If $u(t, x)$ satisfies: $$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \mu(x)\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2(x)\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} - r\,u = 0$$ with terminal condition $u(T, x) = g(x)$, then: $$u(t, x) = e^{-r(T-t)}\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[g(X_T)\,|\,X_t = x\right]$$ where $dX_s = \mu(X_s)\,ds + \sigma(X_s)\,dW_s$.

We used this implicitly in Chapter 1 — the BSM PDE is exactly the Feynman-Kac equation for the discounted expectation of the payoff under $\mathbb{Q}$. In Heston, the same logic applies, but now we have two state variables: $\ln S_t$ and $v_t$. The Feynman-Kac PDE is two-dimensional.

Here is the key move. Instead of trying to solve the PDE for the option price $V(S, v, t)$ directly, we solve it for the characteristic function of $\ln S_T$:

$$\phi(\tau; u) \equiv \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{iu\ln S_T}\,\Big|\,S_t,\, v_t\right], \quad \tau = T - t$$

This object also satisfies a Feynman-Kac PDE — but with a critical difference: we can make an educated guess about its functional form (an ansatz) that reduces the PDE to a system of ordinary differential equations, which we can then solve explicitly.

The PDE That the CF Satisfies

Under the Heston dynamics:

$$dS_t = rS_t\,dt + \sqrt{v_t}\,S_t\,dW_t^S, \qquad dv_t = \kappa(\theta - v_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^v$$

Let $x_t = \ln S_t$. By Itô's lemma:

$$dx_t = \left(r - \frac{v_t}{2}\right)dt + \sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^S$$

The characteristic function $\phi(\tau; u) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[e^{iux_T} \mid x_t, v_t]$ satisfies, by Feynman-Kac applied to the pair $(x_t, v_t)$:

$$-\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial \tau} + \left(r - \frac{v}{2}\right)\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + \kappa(\theta - v)\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial v} + \frac{1}{2}v\frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} + \rho\xi v\frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x\,\partial v} + \frac{1}{2}\xi^2 v\frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial v^2} = 0$$

with initial condition $\phi(0; u) = e^{iux}$ (at $\tau = 0$, i.e. $t = T$, we know $x_T$ exactly).

This is a two-dimensional linear PDE with variable coefficients — formidable on its face. But it has a special structure: all terms are either constant or linear in $v$. This is what "affine" means in the Heston model, and it is what makes the next step work.

The Affine Ansatz — A Brilliant Guess

We guess that the characteristic function has the exponential-affine form:

The Affine Ansatz
$$\phi(\tau; u) = \exp\!\Big[C(\tau; u) + D(\tau; u)\cdot v + iu\cdot x\Big]$$

where $C(\tau; u)$ and $D(\tau; u)$ are complex-valued functions of time-to-maturity $\tau$ and frequency $u$, with initial conditions $C(0; u) = 0$ and $D(0; u) = 0$ (so that $\phi(0; u) = e^{iux}$ as required).

Why guess this form? Because it is exponential-linear in both $x$ and $v$. Substituting into the PDE, every term is proportional to $\phi$ itself — the exponential factors cancel, and the two-dimensional PDE collapses into two ODEs: one for $D(\tau; u)$ and one for $C(\tau; u)$. The PDE has been completely separated.

The ansatz is not a lucky guess — it follows from the theory of affine processes. A process $(x_t, v_t)$ is affine if the coefficients of its SDE are linear in the state vector. Heston is affine in $(x_t, v_t)$, and it is a theorem that the CF of any affine process is exponential-affine in the state vector. Heston knew this. The ansatz is not a trick; it is the correct functional form, guaranteed by the structure of the model.

Substituting the Ansatz — The Riccati Equation

Substituting $\phi = e^{C + Dv + iux}$ into the PDE and cancelling the common $\phi$ factor:

Derivation — Reducing the PDE to Two ODEs
Step 1 — Compute partial derivatives of the ansatz

From $\phi = e^{C(\tau) + D(\tau)v + iux}$:

$$\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial \tau} = (C' + D'v)\phi, \quad \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} = iu\,\phi, \quad \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial v} = D\,\phi$$ $$\frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x^2} = (iu)^2\phi = -u^2\phi, \quad \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial x\,\partial v} = iu\cdot D\,\phi, \quad \frac{\partial^2 \phi}{\partial v^2} = D^2\phi$$
Step 2 — Substitute into the PDE and cancel $\phi$
$$-(C' + D'v) + \left(r - \frac{v}{2}\right)iu + \kappa(\theta - v)D + \frac{1}{2}v(-u^2) + \rho\xi v(iuD) + \frac{1}{2}\xi^2 v D^2 = 0$$
Step 3 — Separate terms by powers of $v$

Group all terms independent of $v$ (the "$v^0$" terms) and all terms proportional to $v$ (the "$v^1$" terms) separately:

$$v^0: \quad -C' + riu + \kappa\theta D = 0$$ $$v^1: \quad -D' - \frac{iu}{2} - \kappa D - \frac{u^2}{2} + i\rho\xi u D + \frac{1}{2}\xi^2 D^2 = 0$$
Step 4 — Rearrange into standard form

The $v^0$ equation gives the ODE for $C$:

$$\frac{dC}{d\tau} = riu + \kappa\theta D$$

The $v^1$ equation gives the ODE for $D$:

$$\frac{dD}{d\tau} = \frac{1}{2}\xi^2 D^2 + (i\rho\xi u - \kappa)D + \left(-\frac{u^2}{2} - \frac{iu}{2}\right)$$

The ODE for $D$ is a Riccati equation — a first-order ODE that is quadratic in $D$. Riccati equations appear throughout physics and engineering; the quadratic $\xi^2 D^2/2$ term is what makes it Riccati rather than linear. Fortunately, Riccati equations with constant coefficients can always be solved in closed form.

Solving the ODEs — Closed-Form Expressions

Define the discriminant $d$ (a complex number, a function of $u$):

$$d \equiv d(u) = \sqrt{(\kappa - i\rho\xi u)^2 + \xi^2(u^2 + iu)}$$
$d$ is the square root of a complex number, and complex square roots have two branches. The standard choice selects the branch with $\mathrm{Re}(d) \geq 0$. This choice affects the numerical evaluation of $C(\tau; u)$ through a logarithm that appears below. At large $|\tau|$, the standard branch can cause the logarithm to accumulate phase jumps — the famous branch cut problem. We will return to this at the end of the chapter. For now, accept $d$ as a well-defined complex constant for each $u$.

Also define the ratio:

$$g \equiv g(u) = \frac{\kappa - i\rho\xi u - d}{\kappa - i\rho\xi u + d}$$

With these definitions, the Riccati ODE for $D$ has the explicit solution:

Solution for D(τ; u)
$$D(\tau; u) = \frac{\kappa - i\rho\xi u - d}{\xi^2}\cdot\frac{1 - e^{-d\tau}}{1 - g\,e^{-d\tau}}$$

Substituting this into the ODE $dC/d\tau = riu + \kappa\theta D$ and integrating from $0$ to $\tau$:

Solution for C(τ; u)
$$C(\tau; u) = riu\tau + \frac{\kappa\theta}{\xi^2}\!\left[(\kappa - i\rho\xi u - d)\,\tau - 2\ln\!\left(\frac{1 - g\,e^{-d\tau}}{1 - g}\right)\right]$$

Both formulas can be verified by direct differentiation and confirmed to satisfy the initial conditions $C(0; u) = D(0; u) = 0$.

Worked Example 1 · Evaluating d and g at u = 0 Sanity check

Any valid CF must satisfy $\phi(\tau; 0) = \mathbb{E}[e^{0}] = 1$ for all $\tau$. Verify that the Heston CF formula gives $\phi = 1$ when $u = 0$.

Evaluate d at u = 0
$$d(0) = \sqrt{(\kappa - 0)^2 + \xi^2(0 + 0)} = \sqrt{\kappa^2} = \kappa$$
Evaluate g at u = 0
$$g(0) = \frac{\kappa - 0 - \kappa}{\kappa - 0 + \kappa} = \frac{0}{2\kappa} = 0$$
Evaluate D at u = 0
$$D(\tau; 0) = \frac{\kappa - 0 - \kappa}{\xi^2}\cdot\frac{1 - e^{-\kappa\tau}}{1 - 0} = \frac{0}{\xi^2}\cdot(1 - e^{-\kappa\tau}) = 0$$
Evaluate C at u = 0
$$C(\tau; 0) = 0 + \frac{\kappa\theta}{\xi^2}\left[(\kappa - 0 - \kappa)\tau - 2\ln\!\left(\frac{1 - 0}{1 - 0}\right)\right] = \frac{\kappa\theta}{\xi^2}\left[0 - 2\ln(1)\right] = 0$$
Result: When $u = 0$: $C = 0$, $D = 0$, and $iu \cdot x = 0$. So $\phi(\tau; 0) = e^{0 + 0 + 0} = 1$ for all $\tau$ and all $x$, $v$. The normalisation condition is satisfied. This is a necessary (though not sufficient) check that the formula is correct.

The Complete Heston Characteristic Function

Assembling all the pieces, the characteristic function of $\ln S_T$ under $\mathbb{Q}$, conditioned on $(S_t, v_t)$ at time $t$ with $\tau = T - t$, is:

The Heston Characteristic Function — Complete Formula
$$\boxed{\phi_T(u) = \exp\!\Big[C(\tau; u) + D(\tau; u)\cdot v_t + iu\ln S_t\Big]}$$ where: $$d = \sqrt{(\kappa - i\rho\xi u)^2 + \xi^2(u^2 + iu)}$$ $$g = \frac{\kappa - i\rho\xi u - d}{\kappa - i\rho\xi u + d}$$ $$D(\tau; u) = \frac{\kappa - i\rho\xi u - d}{\xi^2}\cdot\frac{1 - e^{-d\tau}}{1 - g\,e^{-d\tau}}$$ $$C(\tau; u) = riu\tau + \frac{\kappa\theta}{\xi^2}\!\left[(\kappa - i\rho\xi u - d)\tau - 2\ln\!\left(\frac{1-g\,e^{-d\tau}}{1-g}\right)\right]$$

This is a complex-valued function of the real variable $u$. Given a set of Heston parameters $(\kappa, \theta, \xi, \rho, v_0)$ and market inputs $(S_0, r, T)$, it can be evaluated at any $u$ in a few microseconds. The payoff — computing option prices from this CF — is the subject of Chapter 4.

The Branch Cut Problem — When the Formula Breaks

The logarithm in $C(\tau; u)$ is of a complex number: $\ln\!\left(\frac{1-ge^{-d\tau}}{1-g}\right)$. The complex logarithm is multi-valued. Every time the argument crosses the negative real axis, its principal branch jumps by $2\pi i$ — a branch cut discontinuity.

For short maturities $\tau$, the argument of the log stays well away from the negative real axis and there is no problem. But as $\tau$ increases — long-dated options or when $d$ has a large imaginary part — the argument of the logarithm can wind around the origin, accumulating phase jumps. The numerical evaluation of $C$ will then be discontinuous and incorrect.

The symptom: the numerically computed CF $\phi_T(u)$ will have visible discontinuities as a function of $u$, and the resulting option price will be wrong by an amount related to $2\pi$ phase errors.

There is a well-known alternative formulation due to Kahl and Jäckel (2005) that avoids the branch cut problem entirely by rotating the formula. Instead of using $d = \sqrt{(\kappa - i\rho\xi u)^2 + \xi^2(u^2+iu)}$, they use $d^* = \sqrt{(\kappa - i\rho\xi u)^2 + \xi^2(u^2+iu)}$ with the sign convention that $\mathrm{Re}(d^*) \geq 0$ always enforced at each $u$, and rewrite the logarithm using $\ln(1/g)$ instead of $\ln(g)$. The resulting formula is mathematically identical but numerically stable for all maturities. Any production implementation should use the Kahl-Jäckel form.
Worked Example 2 · Evaluating the CF at a Specific u Numerical walkthrough

Evaluate $\phi_T(u)$ at $u = 1$ for the following Heston parameters: $\kappa = 2$, $\theta = 0.04$, $\xi = 0.3$, $\rho = -0.7$, $v_0 = 0.04$, with $S_0 = 100$, $r = 0.05$, $\tau = 0.5$. Compute $d$, $g$, $D$, $C$, and $\phi$.

Compute d (complex)
$$\kappa - i\rho\xi u = 2 - i(-0.7)(0.3)(1) = 2 + 0.21i$$ $$u^2 + iu = 1 + i$$ $$d = \sqrt{(2 + 0.21i)^2 + (0.09)(1+i)}$$ $$= \sqrt{4 + 0.84i - 0.0441 + 0.09 + 0.09i}$$ $$= \sqrt{4.0459 + 0.93i}$$

To compute $\sqrt{4.0459 + 0.93i}$: modulus $= \sqrt{4.0459^2 + 0.93^2} = \sqrt{16.370 + 0.865} = \sqrt{17.235} \approx 4.151$. Argument $= \arctan(0.93/4.0459) \approx \arctan(0.2299) \approx 0.2257$ rad. So $d \approx \sqrt{4.151}\,e^{i \cdot 0.1129} \approx 2.037\,(\cos 0.1129 + i\sin 0.1129) \approx 2.037(0.9937 + 0.1128i) \approx 2.024 + 0.230i$.

Compute g
$$g = \frac{(2 + 0.21i) - (2.024 + 0.230i)}{(2 + 0.21i) + (2.024 + 0.230i)} = \frac{-0.024 - 0.020i}{4.024 + 0.440i}$$

Numerically: $|$numerator$| \approx 0.0312$, $|$denominator$| \approx 4.048$, so $|g| \approx 0.0077$ — very small, meaning $g \approx 0$ for this parameter set. The formula is stable here.

Compute D and C

Since $|g| \approx 0.0077 \ll 1$ and $e^{-d\tau} = e^{-(2.024+0.230i)(0.5)} = e^{-1.012 - 0.115i} \approx e^{-1.012}(\cos(-0.115) + i\sin(-0.115)) \approx 0.3636(0.9934 - 0.1148i) \approx 0.3612 - 0.0417i$:

$$D \approx \frac{-0.024-0.020i}{0.09}\cdot\frac{1-(0.3612-0.0417i)}{1 - 0} \approx \frac{-0.267-0.222i}{1}\cdot(0.639+0.042i) \approx -0.180 - 0.153i$$
$$C \approx (0.05)(i)(1)(0.5) + \frac{(2)(0.04)}{0.09}\left[(-0.024-0.020i)(0.5) - 2\ln(1)\right]$$ $$\approx 0.025i + 0.889\left[-0.012 - 0.010i\right] \approx -0.0107 + 0.0161i$$
Assemble the CF
$$\phi_T(1) = \exp\!\left[(-0.0107 + 0.0161i) + (-0.180-0.153i)(0.04) + i(1)\ln(100)\right]$$ $$= \exp\!\left[-0.0107 + 0.0161i - 0.00720 - 0.00612i + 4.6052i\right]$$ $$= \exp\!\left[-0.01790 + 4.615i\right]$$
Result: $\phi_T(1) \approx e^{-0.01790} \cdot e^{4.615i} \approx 0.9823\,(\cos(4.615) + i\sin(4.615)) \approx 0.9823(-0.1367 - 0.9906i) \approx -0.134 - 0.973i$.

The CF is a complex number with modulus $\approx 0.982$ (close to 1, as expected for small arguments) and a large phase arising from the $iu\ln S_0 = 4.605i$ term. Actual implementation uses double-precision complex arithmetic and evaluates this at hundreds of values of $u$ simultaneously via vectorised code.

Why This Is Remarkable

Let's pause to appreciate what just happened. We have a model — Heston — whose stock price distribution at maturity has no closed-form density. You cannot write $f_{S_T}(s)$ explicitly. And yet we have a completely explicit, algebraically closed formula for the characteristic function of $\ln S_T$.

The characteristic function carries all the same information as the density. So in a mathematically precise sense, we know everything about the distribution of $S_T$ — we just know it in Fourier space rather than in direct space. And in Fourier space, option pricing is a matter of numerical integration, which modern computers do extremely fast.

Compare this to the alternatives. Monte Carlo simulation requires thousands of paths and minutes of computation for complex instruments. Finite-difference PDE solvers require careful grid design and are slow for multi-dimensional state spaces. The CF approach — sometimes called the semi-analytical approach — gives option prices in milliseconds, making it suitable for real-time calibration and live trading.

On an NSE options desk, Heston is re-calibrated throughout the trading day as the NIFTY option chain updates tick by tick. The calibration loop — compute model prices, compare to market, update parameters — runs dozens of times per minute. This is only feasible because the CF formula turns each model-price computation into a one-dimensional numerical integral rather than a Monte Carlo simulation. The entire quantitative options market in India relies on exactly this formula, running on servers in Powai and BKC, thousands of times per day.

The Heston model has no closed-form probability density for $S_T$. Yet it has a closed-form characteristic function $\phi_T(u)$. A junior quant asks: "If we can't write down the density, how do we know the full distribution?" What is the correct answer?

A
We don't — the CF only gives moments, not the full distribution.
B
We approximate the distribution as lognormal with matching mean and variance from the CF.
C
The CF uniquely determines the full distribution — it is the Fourier transform of the density, and the two carry identical information.
D
The CF only works for symmetric distributions, so Heston requires a correction for the skew.
Answer: C.

The characteristic function and the probability density (or CDF) carry exactly the same information — they are related by the Fourier transform, and the transform is invertible. The Gil-Pelaez inversion formula recovers the CDF from the CF via a single integral. Knowing $\phi_T(u)$ for all $u \in \mathbb{R}$ is mathematically equivalent to knowing the full distribution of $\ln S_T$.

Option A confuses the CF with the moment generating function. The CF gives all moments too (via derivatives at $u=0$), but it gives much more — the full distribution. Option B (lognormal approximation) is a computational shortcut sometimes used in practice but is not what we are doing with the Heston CF. Option D is simply false — the CF is defined for any distribution, symmetric or not.
finance / option-pricing / ch03 / q02 ★★ Conceptual

The affine ansatz $\phi = e^{C(\tau) + D(\tau)v + iux}$ is substituted into the two-dimensional Feynman-Kac PDE for the characteristic function. What is the key structural property of the Heston model that makes this substitution reduce the PDE to two ODEs?

A
The Heston model has constant drift, so all time-derivative terms vanish after substitution.
B
The ansatz turns the PDE into a polynomial in $u$, and matching coefficients gives the ODEs.
C
The Heston model is affine in $(x, v)$ — all PDE coefficients are at most linear in $v$ — so after substitution, the PDE separates by powers of $v$ into two independent ODEs.
D
The exponential form of the ansatz allows the PDE to be factored into a product of two independent equations.
Answer: C.

After substituting the ansatz and cancelling the common $\phi$ factor, the left-hand side of the PDE becomes a polynomial in $v$. Since all the original PDE coefficients are at most linear in $v$ (this is the affine property), the result contains only $v^0$ and $v^1$ terms — no higher powers. Setting the coefficient of $v^0$ to zero gives the ODE for $C$; setting the coefficient of $v^1$ to zero gives the (Riccati) ODE for $D$. The two equations are then solved sequentially: first $D$, then $C$ by integration.

Option A is wrong — the drift is not constant in Heston (the variance drift $\kappa(\theta-v)$ depends on $v$). Option B is wrong — the separation is by powers of $v$, not powers of $u$. Option D describes the form of the ansatz but not the mechanism by which it decouples the PDE.
finance / option-pricing / ch03 / q03 ★★★ Computational / Conceptual

A quant implements the Heston CF formula and finds that for short maturities ($T = 1$ month) the computed option prices are accurate, but for long maturities ($T = 3$ years) they are wildly wrong — sometimes negative. She has checked her algebra multiple times. The most likely cause is:

A
A sign error in the Riccati ODE — the $\xi^2 D^2/2$ term should be negative.
B
The Feller condition is violated, causing negative variance at long maturities.
C
A branch cut discontinuity in the complex logarithm inside $C(\tau; u)$, causing phase jumps of $2\pi i$ that accumulate at large $\tau$.
D
The Gil-Pelaez inversion integral diverges for long maturities because the CF decays too slowly.
Answer: C.

This is one of the most common bugs in Heston implementations. The formula for $C(\tau; u)$ contains the term $\ln\!\left(\frac{1-ge^{-d\tau}}{1-g}\right)$, where the argument is a complex number. As $\tau$ increases, the argument of the logarithm rotates in the complex plane. Each time it crosses the negative real axis, the principal-branch logarithm jumps by $2\pi i$. These jumps propagate through the exponential into the CF, causing the computed $\phi_T(u)$ to be wrong by a factor of $e^{2k\pi i} = 1$ for integer $k$ — but the intermediate values are wrong, and the numerical integration that produces option prices accumulates these errors, producing prices that can be negative or absurdly large.

The fix is the Kahl-Jäckel (2005) rotation: rewrite the log as $\ln(g^{-1}(e^{d\tau} - g))$, track the argument continuously using $\mathrm{arg}(z)$ (not the principal branch), or use the alternative $D$ formulation with $d$ replaced by $-d$. Option B is a separate (real) problem that manifests differently. Options A and D are red herrings.
