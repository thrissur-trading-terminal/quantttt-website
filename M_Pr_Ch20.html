Functions of Two Random Variables

The Jacobian method extends the change-of-variable technique to two dimensions. Introduce an auxiliary variable, compute the $2\times 2$ Jacobian determinant, get the joint PDF, then marginalise. Four worked examples derive the Rayleigh, Cauchy, $\chi^2_{(2)}$, and Laplace distributions from scratch.

Recap: Rayleigh Distribution

$X\sim\text{Exp}(1)$, find PDF of $Y=\sqrt{X}$

Since $X>0$, $h(x)=\sqrt{x}$ is monotone increasing. $h^{-1}(y)=y^2$, $|dx/dy|=2y$.

$$g(y) = e^{-y^2}\cdot 2y = 2ye^{-y^2}, \quad y>0$$

This is the Rayleigh distribution. General form ($X\sim\text{Exp}(1/\alpha)$):

$$g(y) = \frac{2y}{\alpha}e^{-y^2/\alpha}, \quad y>0,\; \alpha>0$$

The 2D Jacobian Theorem

Let $(X,Y)$ have joint PDF $f(x,y)$. Define $U=h_1(X,Y)$ and $V=h_2(X,Y)$ with unique inverse $x=g_1(u,v)$, $y=g_2(u,v)$.

The Jacobian is: $$J = \left|\det\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\[6pt] \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial v}\end{pmatrix}\right|$$ Then the joint PDF of $(U,V)$ is: $$g(u,v) = f(g_1(u,v),\; g_2(u,v))\cdot J$$ Marginal: $f_U(u) = \int g(u,v)\,dv$.
This is the 2D analogue of the 1D formula $g(y) = f(h^{-1}(y))|dx/dy|$. The $|dx/dy|$ becomes the Jacobian determinant. The one-to-one requirement replaces monotonicity. Same structure, one dimension up.

Example 1 — Gamma Sum via Jacobian

$X\sim\Gamma(\lambda,\alpha)$, $Y\sim\Gamma(\lambda,\beta)$ independent. Find PDF of $U=X+Y$.

Step 1 — auxiliary variable: $U=X+Y$, $V=X$. Inverse: $x=v$, $y=u-v$.

Step 2 — Jacobian: $J = \begin{vmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 1 & -1\end{vmatrix} = |{-1}| = 1$

Step 3 — joint PDF:

$$g(u,v) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)}e^{-\lambda v}v^{\alpha-1}\cdot\frac{\lambda^\beta}{\Gamma(\beta)}e^{-\lambda(u-v)}(u-v)^{\beta-1}\cdot 1, \quad 0 < v < u$$

Step 4 — marginalise: Substitute $z=v/u$:

$$f_U(u) = \frac{\lambda^{\alpha+\beta}}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}e^{-\lambda u}u^{\alpha+\beta-1}\underbrace{\int_0^1 z^{\alpha-1}(1-z)^{\beta-1}dz}_{B(\alpha,\beta) = \Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)/\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}$$ $$= \frac{\lambda^{\alpha+\beta}}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}e^{-\lambda u}u^{\alpha+\beta-1} \implies U\sim\Gamma(\lambda,\alpha+\beta) \qquad \square$$

The Jacobian method gives the same reproductive property we proved via MGF — by a completely different route.

Example 2 — $X^2+Y^2$ from $N(0,1)$ iid

$X,Y$ iid $N(0,1)$. Find PDF of $Z=X^2+Y^2$.

Polar coordinates: $X=R\cos\theta$, $Y=R\sin\theta$, $R\in[0,\infty)$, $\theta\in[0,2\pi)$.

Jacobian: $J = \begin{vmatrix}\cos\theta & -R\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & R\cos\theta\end{vmatrix} = R\cos^2\theta + R\sin^2\theta = R$

Joint PDF in $(R,\theta)$:

$$g(R,\theta) = \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-(R^2\cos^2\theta+R^2\sin^2\theta)/2}\cdot R = \frac{R}{2\pi}e^{-R^2/2}$$

Marginal of $R$: integrate $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$: $f(R) = Re^{-R^2/2}$ (Rayleigh).

Apply 1D change of variable $Z=R^2$: $dR/dZ = 1/(2\sqrt{Z})$: $$f(z) = \sqrt{z}\,e^{-z/2}\cdot\frac{1}{2\sqrt{z}} = \frac{1}{2}e^{-z/2} \qquad (z>0)$$ This is $\chi^2_{(2)} = \Gamma(\frac{1}{2},1)$. ✓

Example 3 — Ratio $X/Y$ of iid Normals = Cauchy

$X,Y$ iid $N(0,1)$. Find PDF of $U=X/Y$.

Auxiliary: $U=X/Y$, $V=Y$. Inverse: $X=UV$, $Y=V$.

Jacobian: $J = \begin{vmatrix}v & u \\ 0 & 1\end{vmatrix} = |v|$

Joint PDF: $g(u,v) = \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-(u^2v^2+v^2)/2}\cdot|v| = \frac{|v|}{2\pi}e^{-v^2(u^2+1)/2}$

Marginalise over $v\in(-\infty,\infty)$: using symmetry ($|v|$ is even in $v$):

$$f_U(u) = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\infty v\,e^{-v^2(u^2+1)/2}\,dv$$ Substitute $z = v^2(u^2+1)/2$: $\int_0^\infty e^{-z}dz = 1$: $$f_U(u) = \frac{1}{\pi}\cdot\frac{1}{1+u^2}, \quad -\infty < u < \infty$$

This is the Cauchy$(1,0)$ distribution. The ratio of two independent standard Normals is Cauchy. ✓

Example 4 — Sum and Difference of iid Exp(1)

$X,Y$ iid $\text{Exp}(1)$. Find PDFs of $Z=X+Y$ and $W=X-Y$.

Setup: $Z=X+Y$, $W=X-Y$. Inverse: $X=(Z+W)/2$, $Y=(Z-W)/2$. $J=1/2$.

Joint PDF ($z>0$, $w\in(-z,z)$ since $X,Y\geq 0$):

$$g(z,w) = e^{-(z+w)/2}\cdot e^{-(z-w)/2}\cdot\frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2}e^{-z}$$

Marginal of $Z$: integrate $w\in(-z,z)$:

$$f_Z(z) = \frac{1}{2}e^{-z}\cdot 2z = ze^{-z} \implies Z\sim\Gamma(1,2) \qquad \checkmark$$

Marginal of $W$: two cases:

$$f_W(w) = \frac{1}{2}\int_{|w|}^\infty e^{-z}\,dz = \frac{1}{2}e^{-|w|}, \quad w\in(-\infty,\infty)$$

This is the Laplace (double exponential) distribution! $X-Y$ where $X,Y$ are iid Exp(1) is Laplace. ✓

The ratio distribution result (Example 3) explains why the $t$-statistic has heavy tails. A $t$ distribution arises as a ratio involving Normal and $\chi^2$ variables — and heavy-tailed ratio distributions are generic. In NSE options: the ratio of two risk factors (e.g., two correlated underlying moves) often has Cauchy-like tails, which is why simple Normal assumptions systematically underestimate tail risk in spread options.

$U = X+Y$, $V = X$. What is the Jacobian $J$?

A
$J = 2$
B
$J = 1$
C
$J = 0$
D
$J = -1$ — the determinant is $-1$
Answer: B — $J=1$

Inverse: $x=v$, $y=u-v$. The Jacobian matrix is $\begin{pmatrix}\partial x/\partial u & \partial x/\partial v \\ \partial y/\partial u & \partial y/\partial v\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 1 & -1\end{pmatrix}$. Det $= 0(-1)-1(1)=-1$. $J = |-1| = 1$. The absolute value is taken, so $J=1$. Option D is the determinant before taking the absolute value.
probability / ch20 / q02★★ Medium

$X,Y$ iid $N(0,1)$. The distribution of $X/Y$ is:

A
$N(0,2)$ — ratio of two Normals is Normal
B
Cauchy$(1,0)$ — derived via Jacobian method
C
$\chi^2_{(2)}$ — related to the sum of squares
D
Uniform$(−1,1)$ — bounded between $-1$ and $1$
Answer: B — Cauchy$(1,0)$

The Jacobian calculation gives $f_U(u) = \frac{1}{\pi(1+u^2)}$. This has no finite mean and no finite variance — even though each of $X$ and $Y$ has mean 0 and variance 1. The ratio operation introduces catastrophic tail heaviness. This is why ratios of financial quantities (price-to-earnings, leverage ratios) can behave very differently from their components.
probability / ch20 / q03★★ Medium

Why do we introduce an auxiliary variable $V=h_2(X,Y)$ when finding the PDF of $U=h_1(X,Y)$?

A
To make the problem harder and more interesting
B
Because $U$ alone cannot have a PDF — it needs a partner variable
C
To create a 2D transformation $(x,y)\to(u,v)$ with a well-defined Jacobian, then marginalise over $v$
D
$V$ must equal $Y$ always — it cannot be anything else
Answer: C

To use the Jacobian theorem, we need a bijective map from $(x,y)$ to some 2D space $(u,v)$. The auxiliary $V$ is chosen for mathematical convenience — simple inverse, easy integration. After finding the joint PDF $g(u,v)$, we integrate out $v$ to get the marginal $f_U(u)$. $V$ is a mathematical tool, not an object of interest.

$X,Y$ iid $\text{Exp}(1)$. The distribution of $W=X-Y$ is:

A
Exp(1) — difference of exponentials is exponential
B
$\Gamma(1,2)$ — same as the sum
C
Laplace: $f(w) = \frac{1}{2}e^{-|w|}$, $w\in\mathbb{R}$
D
$N(0,2)$ — differences of exponentials approach Normal
Answer: C — Laplace

From the joint $g(z,w) = \frac{1}{2}e^{-z}$, marginalising over $z\in(|w|,\infty)$: $f_W(w) = \frac{1}{2}e^{-|w|}$. The difference of two iid Exponentials is the Laplace (double exponential) distribution — symmetric around 0 with exponential tails on both sides. This is a non-obvious result that falls out cleanly from the Jacobian method.
probability / ch20 / q05★★★ Hard

For $X=R\cos\theta$, $Y=R\sin\theta$ (polar coordinates), what is the Jacobian $J$?

A
$J = 1$ — polar transformation is area-preserving
B
$J = R^2$ — volume element in 2D polar
C
$J = R$ — from $\det\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -R\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & R\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$
D
$J = \sin\theta\cos\theta$
Answer: C — $J=R$

$\det\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -R\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & R\cos\theta\end{pmatrix} = R\cos^2\theta + R\sin^2\theta = R$. This is why the area element in polar coordinates is $r\,dr\,d\theta$ rather than $dr\,d\theta$. The Jacobian $R$ appears explicitly in the PDF calculation: $g(R,\theta) = \frac{R}{2\pi}e^{-R^2/2}$, which after integrating $\theta$ gives the Rayleigh distribution $f(R) = Re^{-R^2/2}$.
