Conditional Probability

New information changes everything. Conditional probability is the formal machinery for updating what we know when partial information arrives — the heartbeat of inference, Bayesian reasoning, and derivatives pricing under a changed measure.

Why We Need It

A bag contains 10 white balls and 20 red balls. Two balls are picked without replacement. What is the probability the 2nd ball is red?

You can't answer this without knowing what happened first. If the 1st ball was white, the bag now has (9W, 20R) — 20 red out of 29. If the 1st ball was red, the bag is (10W, 19R) — 19 red out of 29. The probability of the 2nd event depends on the 1st. Conditional probability is the tool that handles exactly this.

The Definition

Let $A$ and $B$ be events with $P(B) > 0$. The conditional probability of $A$ given $B$ is: $$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$ We write this as $P(A|B)$ — "probability of $A$, given that $B$ has occurred."
Think of it geometrically. $B$ has occurred — so the entire sample space has shrunk to just $B$. We're now asking: within this smaller world (just $B$), what fraction of it is also $A$? That fraction is $\frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$. We zoom into $B$ and ask how much of $B$ overlaps with $A$.

Counting Perspective

In a finite sample space, conditional probability has an elegant counting interpretation:

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{\#(A \cap B) / \#\Omega}{\#(B) / \#\Omega} = \frac{\#(A \cap B)}{\#(B)}$$

Given that $B$ occurred, we restrict our attention to the $\#(B)$ outcomes in $B$, and ask how many of those also lie in $A$.

Worked Example — Balls Without Replacement

🎱 Bag: 10 White + 20 Red, Two draws without replacement

Question (i): $P(\text{2nd ball is Red} \mid \text{1st ball is White})$

Using the definition: $P(A|B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$

$$P(\text{2nd Red} \mid \text{1st White}) = \frac{P(\text{1st White} \cap \text{2nd Red})}{P(\text{1st White})} = \frac{\frac{10}{30} \cdot \frac{20}{29}}{\frac{10}{30}} = \frac{20}{29}$$

Question (ii): $P(\text{2nd ball is Red} \mid \text{1st ball is Red})$

$$P(\text{2nd Red} \mid \text{1st Red}) = \frac{P(\text{1st Red} \cap \text{2nd Red})}{P(\text{1st Red})} = \frac{\frac{20}{30} \cdot \frac{19}{29}}{\frac{20}{30}} = \frac{19}{29}$$

As expected — if the first ball was red, there are only 19 red balls left out of 29 remaining.

Is $P(\cdot \mid B)$ a Valid Probability Measure?

This is a beautiful structural question. We've defined $P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$ — but does this new function satisfy the three probability axioms? It had better, otherwise we've built something inconsistent.

For any fixed event $B$ with $P(B) > 0$, the function $A \mapsto P(A \mid B)$ is a valid probability measure on $(\Omega, \mathcal{S})$.
We verify all three axioms:

Axiom 1 — Non-negativity: $P(A \mid B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \geq 0$ since both numerator and denominator are non-negative. ✓

Axiom 2 — Total measure: $$P(\Omega \mid B) = \frac{P(\Omega \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(B)}{P(B)} = 1 \checkmark$$

Axiom 3 — Countable additivity: Let $A_1, A_2, \ldots$ be mutually disjoint. Then: $$P\!\left(\bigcup_i A_i \;\Big|\; B\right) = \frac{P\!\left(\left(\bigcup_i A_i\right) \cap B\right)}{P(B)} = \frac{P\!\left(\bigcup_i (A_i \cap B)\right)}{P(B)}$$ Since $A_i$ are disjoint, so are $A_i \cap B$. Therefore by countable additivity of $P$: $$= \frac{\sum_i P(A_i \cap B)}{P(B)} = \sum_i \frac{P(A_i \cap B)}{P(B)} = \sum_i P(A_i \mid B) \checkmark$$ All three axioms hold. $P(\cdot \mid B)$ is a valid probability measure. $\square$

The Multiplication Law

Rearranging the definition gives us a tool for computing joint probabilities:

Multiplication Law: $$P(A \cap B) = P(A \mid B) \cdot P(B)$$ Extended to three events: $$P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A \mid B \cap C) \cdot P(B \mid C) \cdot P(C)$$ Extended to $n$ events (chain rule): $$P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = P(A_1) \cdot P(A_2 \mid A_1) \cdot P(A_3 \mid A_1 \cap A_2) \cdots P(A_n \mid A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_{n-1})$$
The chain rule is how you read a probability tree from left to right — multiply along each branch. Each factor conditions on everything that came before it. In the balls example: $P(\text{1st White} \cap \text{2nd Red}) = P(\text{1st White}) \times P(\text{2nd Red} \mid \text{1st White}) = \frac{10}{30} \times \frac{20}{29}$.

Independence

Two events $A$ and $B$ are independent if their simultaneous occurrence has the same probability as the product of their individual probabilities: $$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$$ Equivalently (when $P(B) > 0$): $P(A \mid B) = P(A)$ — knowing $B$ occurred gives you zero new information about $A$.
Independence ≠ Mutually Exclusive. In fact, if $A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive with positive probabilities, they are maximally dependent — knowing $B$ occurred tells you with certainty that $A$ did not. Mutual exclusivity means $P(A \cap B) = 0$, while independence requires $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B) > 0$.
🎱 Same Bag — Now WITH Replacement

10 White + 20 Red. Pick a ball, record its colour, put it back, then pick again.

$P(\text{2nd Red} \mid \text{1st White}) = \dfrac{P(\text{1st White} \cap \text{2nd Red})}{P(\text{1st White})}$

With replacement, draws are independent: $P(\text{1st White} \cap \text{2nd Red}) = P(\text{1st White}) \times P(\text{2nd Red}) = \dfrac{10}{30} \times \dfrac{20}{30}$

$$P(\text{2nd Red} \mid \text{1st White}) = \frac{\frac{10}{30} \times \frac{20}{30}}{\frac{10}{30}} = \frac{20}{30}$$

The condition on the 1st ball cancels out entirely — with replacement, the two draws are independent, and the colour of the 1st ball carries no information about the 2nd.

Independence is the central assumption in Black-Scholes — daily log-returns are assumed independent. This is why the model breaks during crises: when stocks crash, correlations spike and independence collapses. Understanding conditional probability is the first step toward understanding why simple models fail and what replaces them (copulas, stochastic correlation, regime-switching models).

$P(A) = 0.4$, $P(B) = 0.5$, $P(A \cap B) = 0.2$. What is $P(A \mid B)$?

A
$0.4$
B
$0.4$ — and also $P(A)$, so $A$ and $B$ are independent
C
$0.2$
D
$0.8$
Answer: B

$P(A \mid B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \dfrac{0.2}{0.5} = 0.4$.

Notice that $P(A \mid B) = 0.4 = P(A)$. This means $A$ and $B$ are independent — knowing $B$ occurred gives you no new information about $A$. This is also confirmed by $P(A \cap B) = 0.2 = P(A) \cdot P(B) = 0.4 \times 0.5$.
probability / ch05 / q02 ★★ Medium

A bag has 5 red and 3 blue balls. Two are drawn without replacement. Using the multiplication law, find $P(\text{both red})$.

A
$\dfrac{25}{64}$
B
$\dfrac{20}{56} = \dfrac{5}{14}$
C
$\dfrac{5}{8}$
D
$\dfrac{10}{64}$
Answer: B — $\dfrac{5}{14}$

Using the multiplication law: $$P(\text{both red}) = P(\text{1st red}) \times P(\text{2nd red} \mid \text{1st red}) = \frac{5}{8} \times \frac{4}{7} = \frac{20}{56} = \frac{5}{14}$$ After picking one red ball, only 4 red remain out of 7 total. Note: $\frac{25}{64}$ would be the answer if draws were with replacement (independent), but here they're not.
probability / ch05 / q03 ★★ Medium

$A$ and $B$ are mutually exclusive events, each with positive probability. Are they independent?

A
Yes — mutually exclusive events always have independent outcomes
B
No — mutually exclusive events with positive probabilities are always dependent
C
It depends on the specific values of $P(A)$ and $P(B)$
D
Only if $P(A) = P(B)$
Answer: B

Mutually exclusive means $P(A \cap B) = 0$. Independence requires $P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$. Since both $P(A) > 0$ and $P(B) > 0$, we have $P(A) \cdot P(B) > 0 \neq 0 = P(A \cap B)$.

They cannot be independent. In fact they are maximally dependent — if $B$ occurs you know for certain $A$ did not, so $P(A \mid B) = 0 \neq P(A)$.
probability / ch05 / q04 ★★★ Hard

On a given trading day, let $A$ = "Nifty closes up" and $B$ = "India VIX closes down." A quant observes that $P(A) = 0.55$, $P(B) = 0.60$, and $P(A \cap B) = 0.33$. Are these events independent? And what is $P(A \mid B)$?

A
Dependent; $P(A \mid B) = 0.55$
B
Independent; $P(A \mid B) = 0.55$
C
Dependent; $P(A \mid B) = 0.33$
D
Independent; $P(A \mid B) = 0.60$
Answer: B

$P(A \mid B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \dfrac{0.33}{0.60} = 0.55 = P(A)$.

Since $P(A \mid B) = P(A)$, knowing that VIX fell gives you zero extra information about whether Nifty closed up. The events are independent.

Also check: $P(A) \cdot P(B) = 0.55 \times 0.60 = 0.33 = P(A \cap B)$. ✓ Independence confirmed both ways. In practice this would be an empirical finding worth stress-testing — true independence in financial markets is rare.
