More Discrete Distributions

Uniform, Geometric, Negative Binomial, Hypergeometric — four distributions with vivid real-world interpretations. This chapter also contains one of the most beautiful results in elementary probability: the memoryless property of the Geometric distribution.

Discrete Uniform Distribution

If $\Omega = \{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\}$ and $P(X = x_i) = \dfrac{1}{n}$ for all $i$, then $X$ is uniformly distributed on $\Omega$. Every outcome is equally likely.

The Bernoulli$(p=\frac{1}{2})$ is the uniform distribution on $\{0,1\}$ — a fair coin. A fair die is uniform on $\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$. A die is called unbiased if $P(X=1)=P(X=2)=\cdots=P(X=6)=\frac{1}{6}$. Uniformity is the formal definition of "fair."

Geometric Distribution

$X$ takes values $1, 2, 3, \ldots$ with PMF: $$P(X=k) = p\,q^{k-1}, \quad k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots, \quad q = 1-p, \quad 0 < p < 1$$ Interpretation: $X$ = number of coin tosses until the first Head. The first $k-1$ tosses are Tails (probability $q^{k-1}$) and the $k$th toss is Heads (probability $p$).
Valid PMF? Sum over all $k$: $$\sum_{k=1}^\infty pq^{k-1} = p(1 + q + q^2 + \cdots) = p \cdot \frac{1}{1-q} = p \cdot \frac{1}{p} = 1 \qquad \checkmark$$ The probabilities sum to 1 via the geometric series formula.

The Memoryless Property

This is what makes the Geometric distribution unique among discrete distributions. Having already waited $k$ times with no success doesn't change the distribution of future waiting time.

Memoryless Property: Let $X \sim \text{Geo}(p)$ and let $Y$ = number of additional tosses needed after $k$ failures. Then: $$P(Y = t \mid X > k) = P(X = t) \quad \text{for all } t \geq 1$$ The past is completely irrelevant. Starting fresh is statistically identical to continuing.
Step 1: Find $P(X > k)$. This is the probability that at least $k+1$ tosses are needed: $$P(X > k) = \sum_{i=k}^\infty pq^i = pq^k \cdot \frac{1}{1-q} = q^k$$ Step 2: Compute $P(Y > t \mid X > k)$ using the conditional probability definition. Note that $Y > t$ given $X > k$ means $X - k > t$, i.e., $X > k+t$: $$P(Y > t \mid X > k) = \frac{P(X > k+t \cap X > k)}{P(X > k)} = \frac{P(X > k+t)}{P(X > k)} = \frac{q^{k+t}}{q^k} = q^t$$ Step 3: Recover $P(Y = t \mid X > k)$: $$P(Y = t \mid X > k) = P(Y \geq t-1 \mid X>k) - P(Y \geq t \mid X>k) = q^{t-1} - q^t = q^{t-1}(1-q) = pq^{t-1} = P(X=t) \qquad \square$$
Think of it this way. You've been waiting at a bus stop for 20 minutes and no bus has come. The memoryless property says: the distribution of your remaining wait time is exactly the same as if you had just arrived. The bus has no memory of how long you've been waiting. This is either deeply reassuring or deeply frustrating, depending on your mood.
🎲 Bonus Result — Uniform Conditional Distribution

Let $X, Y$ be independent Geo($p$). What is $P(X = k \mid X+Y = m)$?

$X+Y = m$ can occur as $(X=1,Y=m{-}1), (X=2,Y=m{-}2), \ldots, (X=m{-}1,Y=1)$, giving $m-1$ cases. Each has probability:

$$P(X=j)\cdot P(Y=m-j) = pq^{j-1} \cdot pq^{m-j-1} = p^2 q^{m-2}$$

All cases have the same probability! So $P(X+Y=m) = (m-1)p^2 q^{m-2}$, and:

$$P(X=k \mid X+Y=m) = \frac{p^2 q^{m-2}}{(m-1)p^2 q^{m-2}} = \frac{1}{m-1}$$

The conditional distribution of $X$ given $X+Y=m$ is uniform on $\{1,2,\ldots,m-1\}$ — regardless of $p$. Two geometric waiting times, conditioned on their sum, spread out uniformly.

Negative Binomial Distribution

$X$ takes values $0, 1, 2, \ldots$ with PMF: $$P(X=n) = \binom{n+k-1}{k-1} p^k q^n, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$ where $k \geq 1$ is a positive integer and $0 < p < 1$. We write $X \sim \text{NB}(k,p)$.

Interpretation: $X$ = number of failures before the $k$th success. Run trials until $k$ heads have occurred; $X$ counts how many tails appeared along the way.
Why this formula? If there are $n$ failures before the $k$th success, the total number of trials is $n+k$. The last trial must be a success (probability $p$). Among the first $n+k-1$ trials, exactly $k-1$ are successes — chosen in $\binom{n+k-1}{k-1}$ ways — with probability $p^{k-1}q^n$. Multiply: $\binom{n+k-1}{k-1}p^{k-1}q^n \cdot p = \binom{n+k-1}{k-1}p^k q^n$.
Valid PMF? Using the negative binomial series identity: $$\binom{n+k-1}{k-1} = \binom{n+k-1}{n} = (-1)^n\binom{-k}{n}$$ So $P(X=n) = \binom{-k}{n}p^k(-q)^n$. Summing: $$\sum_{n=0}^\infty \binom{-k}{n}p^k(-q)^n = p^k(1-q)^{-k} = p^k \cdot p^{-k} = 1 \qquad \checkmark$$ The sum telescopes to 1 via the generalised binomial theorem $(1+x)^{-k} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \binom{-k}{n}x^n$.
A factory needs 100 non-defective items. Each item is non-defective with probability $p$. The number of defective items produced before getting 100 good ones is NB$(100, p)$. This is exactly the quality control model used in batch manufacturing — the negative binomial is the natural distribution for "how much waste before we get what we need."

Hypergeometric Distribution

A box contains $N$ balls: $M$ white and $N-M$ black. Draw $n$ balls without replacement. Let $X$ = number of white balls drawn. Then: $$P(X=x) = \frac{\dbinom{M}{x}\dbinom{N-M}{n-x}}{\dbinom{N}{n}}, \quad x = 0, 1, \ldots, \min(n, M)$$ We write $X \sim \text{Hyper}(N, M, n)$.
Choose $x$ whites from the $M$ available: $\binom{M}{x}$ ways. Choose the remaining $n-x$ balls from the $N-M$ blacks: $\binom{N-M}{n-x}$ ways. Total favourable outcomes: numerator. Total ways to choose $n$ from $N$: denominator. Classic counting argument.
Valid PMF? Consider the identity $(1+a)^N = (1+a)^M (1+a)^{N-M}$. The coefficient of $a^n$ on the left is $\binom{N}{n}$. The coefficient of $a^n$ on the right is $\displaystyle\sum_{x=0}^n \binom{M}{x}\binom{N-M}{n-x}$ (convolution of coefficients). Therefore: $$\sum_{x=0}^n \binom{M}{x}\binom{N-M}{n-x} = \binom{N}{n}$$ Dividing both sides by $\binom{N}{n}$: the PMF sums to 1. $\square$
Hypergeometric vs Binomial: If you draw with replacement, the number of whites follows Binomial$(n, M/N)$. Without replacement: Hypergeometric. For large $N$ relative to $n$, the two become nearly identical — each draw barely changes the composition of the box. But for small $N$, the without-replacement structure matters significantly.

$X \sim \text{Geo}(0.4)$. What is $P(X = 3)$?

A
$0.4^3 = 0.064$
B
$0.6^3 = 0.216$
C
$0.4 \times 0.6^2 = 0.144$
D
$3 \times 0.4 \times 0.6^2 = 0.432$
Answer: C — $0.144$

$P(X=k) = pq^{k-1}$. For $k=3$: $P(X=3) = 0.4 \times (0.6)^2 = 0.4 \times 0.36 = 0.144$. The first 2 tosses are Tails ($q^2$), the 3rd is Heads ($p$). Option A uses $p^k$ (wrong). Option D has the Binomial formula — not geometric.

You have been flipping a coin for 10 rounds with no Heads. $p = 0.3$. What is the probability you need exactly 2 more flips to get the first Head?

A
$0.3 \times 0.7^{11}$ — must account for the 10 previous failures
B
$0.3 \times 0.7^1 = 0.21$ — same as $P(X=2)$ from scratch
C
$0.7^{10} \times 0.3 \times 0.7 = 0.7^{11} \times 0.3$
D
$0$ — after 10 failures the distribution changes
Answer: B — $0.21$

The memoryless property: $P(Y=t \mid X>k) = P(X=t)$. The 10 previous failures are completely irrelevant. You need 2 more flips (1 Tail then 1 Head) from a fresh start: $P(X=2) = 0.3 \times 0.7 = 0.21$. The geometric distribution has no memory of the past.
probability / ch09 / q03★★ Medium

$X \sim \text{NB}(3, 0.5)$ (3 successes required). What does $P(X=2)$ represent?

A
Probability of exactly 2 successes in 3 trials
B
Probability of exactly 2 failures before the 3rd success
C
Probability that the 3rd success occurs on trial 2
D
Probability of exactly 3 failures in 2 trials
Answer: B

By definition, $X$ in NB$(k,p)$ counts failures before the $k$th success. So $P(X=2)$ = probability of exactly 2 failures before the 3rd success. This means the experiment ends after exactly $2+3=5$ trials, with the 5th being the 3rd success. $P(X=2) = \binom{4}{2}(0.5)^3(0.5)^2 = 6/32 = 3/16$.

An NSE index has 50 stocks. 20 are from the IT sector. An analyst randomly picks 5 stocks. What is $P(X=2)$, where $X$ = number of IT stocks picked?

A
$\dbinom{5}{2}(0.4)^2(0.6)^3$ — Binomial with $p=20/50$
B
$\dfrac{\dbinom{20}{2}\dbinom{30}{3}}{\dbinom{50}{5}}$ — Hypergeometric
C
$\dfrac{20}{50} \times \dfrac{19}{49} \times \dfrac{30}{48} \times \dfrac{29}{47} \times \dfrac{28}{46}$
D
$\dbinom{20}{2} / \dbinom{50}{5}$
Answer: B — Hypergeometric

This is sampling without replacement from a finite population — exactly the Hypergeometric setting. $N=50$, $M=20$ (IT stocks), $n=5$ draws, $x=2$: $$P(X=2) = \frac{\binom{20}{2}\binom{30}{3}}{\binom{50}{5}} = \frac{190 \times 4060}{2118760} \approx 0.364$$ Option A (Binomial) would be correct only if stocks were replaced after each draw. Option C is $P(\text{IT, IT, non-IT, non-IT, non-IT in that order})$ — one specific sequence, not all arrangements.

$X$ and $Y$ are independent Geo($p$) and $X+Y=10$. What is $P(X=3 \mid X+Y=10)$?

A
$P(X=3) = p(1-p)^2$ — conditioning has no effect
B
Depends on $p$
C
$\dfrac{1}{9}$ — uniform on $\{1,2,\ldots,9\}$
D
$\dfrac{p(1-p)^2}{P(X+Y=10)}$ — cannot simplify further
Answer: C — $\dfrac{1}{9}$

From the worked example above: when $X, Y \sim \text{Geo}(p)$ are independent, $P(X=k \mid X+Y=m) = \dfrac{1}{m-1}$ for all $k \in \{1,\ldots,m-1\}$, regardless of $p$. With $m=10$: $P(X=3 \mid X+Y=10) = \dfrac{1}{9}$. The $p$'s cancel completely — every value of $X$ from 1 to 9 is equally likely given the sum is 10.
