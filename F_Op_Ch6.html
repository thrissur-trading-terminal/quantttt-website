The Carr-Madan FFT Method

The semi-closed form prices one option in a millisecond. What if you need to price four thousand options simultaneously — an entire implied volatility surface — and refresh it every second? That is the problem Carr and Madan solved in 1999, and the answer is the Fast Fourier Transform.

The Surface Pricing Problem

Everything we have built so far — the Heston CF, the Gil-Pelaez inversion, the 128-point Gauss-Legendre rule — prices one option at one strike efficiently. That is enough for a single hedge or a single risk check. It is not enough for calibration or market-making.

Consider what calibration requires: you need model prices at perhaps 40 strikes across 6 maturities — 240 options — evaluated hundreds of times inside an optimisation loop. With the semi-closed form, each option is a separate GL integration. For a single maturity with $M$ strikes, the cost is $O(M \cdot N)$ where $N = 128$ is the number of quadrature nodes. That is $M \times 128$ characteristic function evaluations per maturity.

The Carr-Madan insight is simple but powerful: for a fixed maturity, the option price as a function of log-strike $k = \ln K$ has a Fourier representation that can be inverted all at once. Instead of pricing each strike separately, you compute $N$ values of the Heston CF on a frequency grid, apply the FFT, and recover prices at $N$ strikes simultaneously. The cost drops from $O(MN)$ to $O(N \log N)$ — and since $N$ is the number of strikes you want, this is essentially free once you have the CF.

On a NIFTY options desk, the full implied vol surface has roughly 20–30 strikes per weekly expiry across 5–8 active maturities. Refreshing this every few seconds requires real-time surface pricing. The FFT approach — one CF evaluation pass per maturity, outputting all strikes simultaneously — is what makes live surface management computationally feasible. The semi-closed form is kept as a backup for pricing at arbitrary off-grid strikes (e.g. for exotic hedges), but the surface itself runs on FFT.
Complexity comparison. Suppose you want prices at $M$ strikes for a fixed maturity. Semi-closed form (Ch4+Ch5): $O(M \cdot N_{\text{GL}})$ CF evaluations — each strike is priced independently with its own GL sum. Carr-Madan FFT: $O(N \log N)$ total — one FFT of size $N \geq M$ delivers all $M$ prices at once. For $M = 50$ strikes and $N_\text{GL} = 128$: semi-closed needs $6{,}400$ CF calls; FFT with $N = 4096$ needs $\approx 50{,}000$ operations but only $4{,}096$ CF evaluations — and most of those operations are additions and multiplications, not expensive CF calls.

The Damping Trick — Making the Transform Exist

Write the call price as a function of log-strike $k = \ln K$:

$$C_T(k) = e^{-rT}\,\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[(S_T - e^k)^+\right]$$

We want to take the Fourier transform of $C_T(k)$ with respect to $k$. Here is the problem: as $k \to -\infty$ (very low strike, deep ITM), the call approaches the stock price $S_0$ — it never goes to zero. So $C_T(k)$ is not integrable on $(-\infty, \infty)$ and its Fourier transform does not exist.

The fix is to multiply by a damping factor $e^{\alpha k}$ with $\alpha > 0$ before transforming. Define:

$$c_T(k) = e^{\alpha k}\,C_T(k)$$

Now check the limits. As $k \to -\infty$: $e^{\alpha k} \to 0$ faster than $C_T(k) \to S_0$, so $c_T(k) \to 0$. As $k \to +\infty$: $C_T(k) \to 0$ (deep OTM call is worthless), so $c_T(k) \to 0$ as well. The damped function $c_T(k) \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$ and its Fourier transform exists:

$$\psi_T(v) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{ivk}\,c_T(k)\,dk = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{ivk}\,e^{\alpha k}\,C_T(k)\,dk$$

To recover the original call price we invert and undo the damping:

$$C_T(k) = e^{-\alpha k}\,\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-ivk}\,\psi_T(v)\,dv$$
Why $e^{\alpha k}$ specifically? A call price as a function of log-strike $k$ looks like a hockey stick shifted into Fourier space — it is flat on the left (deep ITM) and decays to zero on the right (deep OTM). The $e^{\alpha k}$ factor kills the flat left side by tilting it downward. This is the same Esscher transform idea we saw in Chapter 4 when changing from $\mathbb{Q}$ to $\mathbb{Q}^S$: multiplying by an exponential in $k$-space is a shift in $v$-space, which is exactly what we will see in the final formula.

Deriving the Carr-Madan Formula

We now derive an explicit expression for $\psi_T(v)$ in terms of the Heston characteristic function. The derivation has one clever step — a Fubini swap — and then it is algebra.

Derivation — $\psi_T(v)$ in Terms of $\phi_T$
Step 1 — Expand $C_T(k)$ as an expectation over the density

Write $s = \ln S_T$ and let $f(s)$ denote the risk-neutral density of $\ln S_T$. Then $(S_T - e^k)^+ = \int_k^\infty (e^s - e^k)f(s)\,ds$ (the payoff integrates over all log-price realisations above $k$):

$$\psi_T(v) = e^{-rT}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{(iv+\alpha)k}\int_k^{\infty}(e^s - e^k)f(s)\,ds\,dk$$
Step 2 — Swap order of integration (Fubini)

Since $\alpha > 0$ ensures absolute integrability, we can exchange the $dk$ and $ds$ integrals. The region of integration is $\{(k,s) : k < s\}$, so after swapping:

$$\psi_T(v) = e^{-rT}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(s)\int_{-\infty}^{s} e^{(iv+\alpha)k}(e^s - e^k)\,dk\,ds$$
Step 3 — Evaluate the inner integral over $k$

Split $(e^s - e^k) = e^s - e^k$ and integrate each term from $-\infty$ to $s$:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{s} e^{(iv+\alpha)k} e^s\,dk = e^s \cdot \frac{e^{(iv+\alpha)s}}{iv+\alpha}$$
$$\int_{-\infty}^{s} e^{(iv+\alpha)k} e^k\,dk = \int_{-\infty}^s e^{(iv+\alpha+1)k}\,dk = \frac{e^{(iv+\alpha+1)s}}{iv+\alpha+1}$$

Subtracting and factoring out $e^{(iv+\alpha+1)s}$:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{s} e^{(iv+\alpha)k}(e^s - e^k)\,dk = e^{(iv+\alpha+1)s}\left(\frac{1}{iv+\alpha} - \frac{1}{iv+\alpha+1}\right) = \frac{e^{(iv+\alpha+1)s}}{(iv+\alpha)(iv+\alpha+1)}$$
Step 4 — Substitute back and recognise the characteristic function

Plugging the inner integral result back into $\psi_T(v)$:

$$\psi_T(v) = \frac{e^{-rT}}{(iv+\alpha)(iv+\alpha+1)}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{(iv+\alpha+1)s}f(s)\,ds$$

The remaining integral is $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[e^{(iv+\alpha+1)\ln S_T}]$. Writing $iv + \alpha + 1 = i(v - (\alpha+1)i)$:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{(iv+\alpha+1)s}f(s)\,ds = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{i(v-(\alpha+1)i)\ln S_T}\right] = \phi_T(v - (\alpha+1)i)$$
Step 5 — Simplify the denominator

Expand $(iv + \alpha)(iv + \alpha + 1)$:

$$(iv+\alpha)(iv+\alpha+1) = (iv)^2 + (2\alpha+1)(iv) + \alpha(\alpha+1) = -v^2 + i(2\alpha+1)v + \alpha^2+\alpha$$
The Carr-Madan Formula
$$\boxed{\psi_T(v) = \frac{e^{-rT}\,\phi_T\!\left(v - (\alpha+1)i\right)}{\alpha^2 + \alpha - v^2 + i(2\alpha+1)v}}$$

$\psi_T(v)$ is the Fourier transform of the damped call price. It is expressed entirely in terms of the Heston CF $\phi_T$, evaluated at the complex argument $v - (\alpha+1)i$.

Notice the denominator $\alpha^2 + \alpha - v^2 + i(2\alpha+1)v$. At $v = 0$ this equals $\alpha^2 + \alpha = \alpha(\alpha+1) > 0$ — no singularity. At $v \neq 0$ it stays nonzero as long as $\alpha > 0$. The formula is well-defined everywhere on the real $v$-axis. The numerator involves $\phi_T$ evaluated at a complex frequency $v - (\alpha+1)i$ — this is exactly the kind of imaginary shift we handled in Chapter 4 when deriving $\phi_T^{(1)}(u) = \phi_T(u-i)/\phi_T(-i)$. The Heston CF is analytic in a strip around the real axis, so this evaluation is valid as long as $\alpha+1$ lies inside that strip.

Discretizing for the FFT

We need to evaluate the inverse Fourier transform:

$$C_T(k) = \frac{e^{-\alpha k}}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-ivk}\,\psi_T(v)\,dv$$

Three steps: truncate the frequency integral, lay down a uniform grid, and recognise the result as a Discrete Fourier Transform.

Frequency grid. Truncate at $v_\text{max} = \eta N$ and discretize with spacing $\eta$:

$$v_j = \eta(j-1), \quad j = 1, 2, \ldots, N$$

Log-strike grid. The DFT output naturally lives on a uniform log-strike grid. The key constraint is the DFT duality relation: frequency spacing $\eta$ and log-strike spacing $\lambda$ must satisfy $\lambda = 2\pi/(N\eta)$. Centre the log-strike grid at zero by setting $b = N\lambda/2 = \pi/\eta$:

$$k_u = -b + \lambda(u-1), \quad u = 1, 2, \ldots, N, \qquad K_u = S_0\,e^{k_u}$$

The discretized inversion formula at log-strike $k_u$ becomes:

$$C_T(k_u) \approx \frac{e^{-\alpha k_u}}{\pi}\,\mathrm{Re}\!\left[\sum_{j=1}^{N} e^{-iv_j k_u}\,\psi_T(v_j)\,w_j\,\eta\right]$$

where $w_j$ are quadrature weights. Substituting $v_j = \eta(j-1)$ and $k_u = -b + \lambda(u-1)$:

$$e^{-iv_j k_u} = e^{-i\eta(j-1)(-b + \lambda(u-1))} = e^{i\eta(j-1)b}\cdot e^{-i\eta\lambda(j-1)(u-1)}$$

Using $\eta\lambda = 2\pi/N$, the second factor becomes $e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{N}(j-1)(u-1)}$ — this is the DFT kernel. The sum is therefore exactly a DFT:

$$\sum_{j=1}^{N} e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{N}(j-1)(u-1)} \underbrace{\left[e^{i\eta(j-1)b}\,\psi_T(v_j)\,w_j\,\eta\right]}_{x_j}$$

Compute $x_j$ for all $j$, apply the inverse FFT, take the real part, and multiply by $e^{-\alpha k_u}/\pi$ — you get all $N$ option prices at once.

Why Simpson Weights — Not Gauss-Legendre

In Chapter 5 we established that Gauss-Legendre is far superior to Simpson's rule for the Heston probability integrals. So why are we using Simpson weights here?

Because the FFT requires a uniform grid. The GL nodes are non-uniform — they are roots of Legendre polynomials, clustered toward the endpoints of $[-1,1]$. If we used GL nodes in the frequency sum above, the $e^{-i\frac{2\pi}{N}(j-1)(u-1)}$ factor would no longer be a DFT kernel, and the FFT trick would not apply. We would be back to computing each strike separately — exactly what we were trying to avoid.

On the uniform frequency grid $v_j = \eta(j-1)$, the best quadrature is Simpson's rule:

$$w_1 = w_N = \frac{1}{3}, \qquad w_j = \frac{4}{3} \;\text{($j$ odd, }j \neq 1, N\text{)}, \qquad w_j = \frac{2}{3} \;\text{($j$ even)}$$

Simpson's rule has $O(\eta^4)$ error on a uniform grid — not spectral, but four orders of magnitude better than the trapezoidal rule, and good enough for option pricing when $\eta = 0.25$. The error in the final option price from the quadrature discretisation is then $O(\eta^4)$, which for $\eta = 0.25$ is on the order of $10^{-3}$ relative error — acceptable for calibration, where market data noise is typically larger than this.

The FFT method has two distinct sources of error: (1) truncation of the frequency integral at $v_\text{max} = \eta N$, and (2) discretisation error from the uniform grid with Simpson weights. In the semi-closed form, both were negligible (the GL quadrature error is $O(e^{-cN}) \approx 10^{-15}$). In the FFT method, the discretisation error $O(\eta^4)$ dominates. For $\eta = 0.25$ and typical Heston parameters, the resulting option price error is around 1–5 basis points — well within bid-ask spread for calibration purposes, but not suitable for risk-neutral derivative pricing where you need machine precision.

The Complete Algorithm

Carr-Madan FFT — Full Pricing Algorithm
1
Choose parameters: $\alpha = 1.5$, $\eta = 0.25$, $N = 4096$. Compute $\lambda = 2\pi/(N\eta)$ and $b = \pi/\eta$. The log-strike grid spans $[-b, b]$; the strike grid spans $[S_0 e^{-b}, S_0 e^b]$.
2
Build frequency grid: $v_j = \eta(j-1)$ for $j = 1, \ldots, N$.
3
Evaluate the Carr-Madan transform: For each $j$, compute $\phi_T(v_j - (\alpha+1)i)$ using the Heston CF from Chapter 3, then: $$\psi_j = \frac{e^{-rT}\,\phi_T(v_j - (\alpha+1)i)}{\alpha^2 + \alpha - v_j^2 + i(2\alpha+1)v_j}$$
4
Apply Simpson weights and phase factor: For each $j$: $$x_j = e^{iv_j b}\,\psi_j\,w_j\,\eta$$ where $w_j$ are the Simpson weights and $e^{iv_j b}$ is the phase correction for the shifted log-strike grid.
5
Apply inverse FFT: Compute $\tilde{C} = \mathrm{IFFT}(x)$ (or equivalently $\mathrm{FFT}$ with conjugate — depends on sign convention). This delivers $N$ complex numbers whose real parts are the scaled option prices.
6
Undo damping and recover prices: For each $u = 1, \ldots, N$: $$C_T(K_u) = \frac{e^{-\alpha k_u}}{\pi}\,\mathrm{Re}\!\left[\tilde{C}_u\right], \qquad k_u = -b + \lambda(u-1), \qquad K_u = S_0\,e^{k_u}$$
import numpy as np def carr_madan_heston(S0, r, T, v0, kappa, theta, xi, rho, alpha=1.5, eta=0.25, N=4096): # Grid parameters lam = 2 * np.pi / (N * eta) # log-strike spacing b = np.pi / eta # half-range of log-strike grid v = eta * np.arange(1, N + 1) - eta # frequency grid v_j # Heston CF at complex argument v - (alpha+1)*i u_cm = v - (1j * (alpha + 1)) phi = heston_cf(u_cm, S0, v0, kappa, theta, xi, rho, r, T) # Carr-Madan transform values denom = alpha**2 + alpha - v**2 + 1j * (2*alpha + 1) * v psi = np.exp(-r * T) * phi / denom # Simpson weights w = np.ones(N) * (2/3) w[0] = w[-1] = 1/3 w[1::2] = 4/3 # Phase factor + weighted transform values k_shift = np.arange(1, N + 1) - 1 x = np.exp(1j * v * b) * psi * w * eta # Inverse FFT → N option prices C_tilde = np.fft.ifft(x) * N # numpy ifft includes 1/N factor # Log-strike and strike grids k = -b + lam * np.arange(0, N) K = S0 * np.exp(k) # Undo damping C = (np.exp(-alpha * k) / np.pi) * np.real(C_tilde) return K, C

Parameter Selection

ParameterTypical ValueWhat It ControlsConstraints
$\alpha$ 1.5 Damping strength. Larger $\alpha$ makes the integrand decay faster but can cause numerical instability near ATM. $\alpha > 0$; $\phi_T(-(\alpha+1)i)$ must exist (inside Heston CF analyticity strip). For calls: $0 < \alpha < $ strip boundary.
$\eta$ 0.25 Frequency grid spacing. Smaller $\eta$ widens the strike range but coarsens frequency resolution. $\eta > 0$. Trade-off with $\lambda = 2\pi/(N\eta)$: finer $\eta$ means coarser $\lambda$ (wider frequency range but fewer distinct log-strikes per unit).
$N$ 4096 Grid size. Larger $N$ gives more strikes and finer log-strike spacing for fixed $\eta$. Must be a power of 2 for FFT efficiency. $N = 4096$ gives $\lambda \approx 0.0061$ — strike spacing of 0.6% of $S_0$, fine for NIFTY.
$\lambda$ $\approx 0.0061$ Log-strike spacing. Derived from $\eta$ and $N$; not a free parameter. $\lambda = 2\pi/(N\eta)$. Decreasing $\eta$ or increasing $N$ both decrease $\lambda$ (finer strike grid).
$b$ $\pi/\eta \approx 12.57$ Half-range of log-strike grid. Strike range is $[S_0 e^{-b}, S_0 e^b]$. $b = \pi/\eta$. With $\eta = 0.25$: $b \approx 12.57$, so strikes span $[S_0 e^{-12.57}, S_0 e^{12.57}]$ — from essentially 0 to astronomically large, more than enough.

In practice, a production system uses both. The FFT runs for surface pricing and calibration. The semi-closed form is kept for pricing individual options at arbitrary strikes — for example, when a client requests a quote at a non-standard strike, or when you need machine-precision accuracy for a large notional trade.

Worked Example 1 · Carr-Madan Inner Integral — Step by Step Core derivation

Verify the inner integral calculation from the derivation: show that $\int_{-\infty}^{s} e^{(iv+\alpha)k}(e^s - e^k)\,dk = \frac{e^{(iv+\alpha+1)s}}{(iv+\alpha)(iv+\alpha+1)}$.

Let $\beta = iv + \alpha$ with $\mathrm{Re}(\beta) = \alpha > 0$ to ensure convergence at $-\infty$.

Split into two integrals
$$I = \int_{-\infty}^{s} e^{\beta k} e^s\,dk - \int_{-\infty}^{s} e^{\beta k} e^k\,dk = e^s\int_{-\infty}^{s} e^{\beta k}\,dk - \int_{-\infty}^{s} e^{(\beta+1)k}\,dk$$
Evaluate each term
$$e^s\int_{-\infty}^{s} e^{\beta k}\,dk = e^s \cdot \left[\frac{e^{\beta k}}{\beta}\right]_{-\infty}^{s} = e^s \cdot \frac{e^{\beta s}}{\beta} = \frac{e^{(\beta+1)s}}{\beta}$$
$$\int_{-\infty}^{s} e^{(\beta+1)k}\,dk = \left[\frac{e^{(\beta+1)k}}{\beta+1}\right]_{-\infty}^{s} = \frac{e^{(\beta+1)s}}{\beta+1}$$

(Both lower limits vanish since $\mathrm{Re}(\beta) = \alpha > 0$ means $\mathrm{Re}(\beta+1) > 0$, so $e^{\beta k} \to 0$ as $k \to -\infty$.)

Subtract and factor
$$I = \frac{e^{(\beta+1)s}}{\beta} - \frac{e^{(\beta+1)s}}{\beta+1} = e^{(\beta+1)s}\left(\frac{1}{\beta} - \frac{1}{\beta+1}\right) = e^{(\beta+1)s}\cdot\frac{1}{\beta(\beta+1)}$$
Substitute $\beta = iv + \alpha$
$$I = \frac{e^{(iv+\alpha+1)s}}{(iv+\alpha)(iv+\alpha+1)}$$
Verified. The inner integral evaluates to $e^{(iv+\alpha+1)s}\,/\,[(iv+\alpha)(iv+\alpha+1)]$, which is why the density integral that follows yields $\phi_T(v-(\alpha+1)i)$ — the Heston CF evaluated at a complex-shifted argument. The convergence of both integrals at $k \to -\infty$ depends critically on $\alpha > 0$: this is exactly what the damping parameter buys us.

Worked Example 4 · Semi-Closed vs FFT — Accuracy and Speed Head-to-Head Method comparison

Price one option — $S_0 = 100$, $K = 110$, $T = 0.5$, $r = 3\%$, Heston parameters from Ch5 WE4 — using both methods. Quantify every source of difference.

Semi-closed result (reference)

From Ch5, Worked Example 4: $C = 4.27$ with error $< 10^{-15}$ (machine precision). This is the ground truth.

Carr-Madan FFT result

Running the FFT with $\alpha = 1.5$, $\eta = 0.25$, $N = 4096$: $C_\text{FFT} = 4.268$. The error is approximately $0.002$ — two basis points.

Sources of FFT error

(1) Frequency truncation: we truncate at $v_\text{max} = \eta N = 0.25 \times 4096 = 1024$. The Heston CF decays exponentially, so $|\psi_T(v)| \approx 0$ beyond $v \approx 50$. Error from truncation: negligible.

(2) Simpson discretisation: on the uniform frequency grid with spacing $\eta = 0.25$, the quadrature error is $O(\eta^4) = O(0.0039)$. After the $1/\pi$ prefactor and the specific integrand values, this translates to the $\sim 0.002$ error observed.

Reducing $\eta$ from $0.25$ to $0.1$ would cut the error to $O(0.0001)$ — but this forces $\lambda = 2\pi/(N\eta)$ to increase, coarsening the strike grid. For fixed $N$, there is a genuine trade-off: finer frequency resolution means coarser strike spacing. To get both fine frequency and fine strikes, you increase $N$ — but $N$ must remain a power of 2.

Why must the call price $C_T(k)$ be multiplied by $e^{\alpha k}$ (with $\alpha > 0$) before taking the Fourier transform in log-strike? What goes wrong without this damping?

A
Without damping, the Fourier transform exists but oscillates too rapidly for numerical computation.
B
Without damping, $C_T(k) \to S_0$ as $k \to -\infty$ (deep ITM), so $C_T(k)$ is not integrable on $\mathbb{R}$ and its Fourier transform does not exist in the classical sense.
C
Without damping, the characteristic function $\phi_T$ would need to be evaluated on the real axis instead of a complex-shifted argument, which causes numerical instability.
D
The damping is purely for numerical convenience — the Fourier transform exists without it, but converges more slowly.
Answer: B.

A deep ITM call with very low strike $K = e^k$ has value close to $S_0 - Ke^{-rT} \approx S_0$ — the call is almost certainly exercised. So $C_T(k) \to S_0 > 0$ as $k \to -\infty$. A function that does not decay to zero as its argument goes to $-\infty$ is not integrable on $\mathbb{R}$, so $\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{ivk} C_T(k)\,dk$ diverges. Multiplying by $e^{\alpha k}$ with $\alpha > 0$ forces $e^{\alpha k} C_T(k) \to 0$ as $k \to -\infty$ (the exponential decay dominates the constant $S_0$), restoring integrability. As $k \to +\infty$, the OTM call already decays to zero, so no additional damping is needed on the right. Option C describes a real phenomenon (the CF is evaluated at a complex argument) but this is a consequence of the damping, not a reason for it. Option D is simply wrong — the Fourier transform does not exist without damping.
finance / option-pricing / ch06 / q02 ★★ Computational

You run Carr-Madan with $\alpha = 1.5$, $\eta = 0.25$, $N = 4096$. A colleague says the log-strike spacing is $\lambda = 2\pi/(N\eta)$. You want to halve $\lambda$ (finer strike grid) while keeping the same strike range. What must you change, and what is the trade-off?

A
Double $\eta$ to 0.5. This halves $\lambda$ but also halves the maximum frequency $v_\text{max} = \eta N$, reducing frequency resolution.
B
Double $N$ to 8192 (keeping $\eta$ fixed). This halves $\lambda = 2\pi/(N\eta)$, doubles $v_\text{max}$, keeps the strike range the same ($b = \pi/\eta$ is unchanged), and costs an extra factor of $\sim 2\log 2$ in FFT time.
C
Halve $\eta$ to 0.125. This halves $\lambda$ but doubles the strike range, so you get finer spacing over a wider range — no trade-off.
D
Change $\alpha$ to 3.0. The damping parameter controls the effective log-strike spacing.
Answer: B.

The duality relation $\lambda = 2\pi/(N\eta)$ shows that $\lambda$ depends on both $N$ and $\eta$. To halve $\lambda$, either double $N$ or halve $\eta$. But the strike range is controlled by $b = \pi/\eta$: halving $\eta$ doubles $b$ and hence the range — you get finer strikes over a wider range, which sounds good but means you are computing prices at astronomically high and low strikes that are completely irrelevant for trading. Option A doubles $\eta$, which actually coarsens $\lambda$ (makes it larger). The correct approach to halve $\lambda$ while keeping the same range is to double $N$ (Option B). The cost is one extra FFT size doubling — $O(2N\log(2N))$ instead of $O(N\log N)$, roughly a factor of $2\log 2 / \log N \approx 1.2\times$ in practice. Option D is wrong — $\alpha$ is the damping parameter, unrelated to the grid spacing.

In Chapter 5 we proved that Gauss-Legendre is far superior to Simpson's rule for Heston integrals — GL achieves spectral convergence $O(e^{-cN})$ while Simpson's gives only $O(h^4)$. So why does the Carr-Madan FFT use Simpson weights instead of Gauss-Legendre nodes?

A
The Carr-Madan integrand is not smooth, so GL loses its spectral convergence advantage — Simpson's rule is just as good.
B
GL nodes are too expensive to compute for large $N$ like 4096.
C
The FFT algorithm requires a uniform frequency grid — the DFT kernel $e^{-i2\pi(j-1)(u-1)/N}$ only reduces to an FFT when $v_j$ are equally spaced. GL nodes are non-uniform, so using them would destroy the DFT structure and eliminate the $O(N\log N)$ speedup that motivates the entire FFT approach.
D
Simpson weights are more accurate than GL for oscillatory integrands, which the Carr-Madan integrand is.
Answer: C.

This question tests whether you understand why the FFT works — not just that it does. The FFT is an algorithm that exploits the structure of the DFT sum: $\sum_{j=1}^N x_j e^{-i2\pi(j-1)(u-1)/N}$. This sum has a specific form that allows $O(N\log N)$ computation via the Cooley-Tukey butterfly algorithm. The DFT structure requires the "frequencies" $v_j$ to be uniformly spaced: $v_j = \eta(j-1)$. If you replaced these with GL nodes, you would have a non-uniform DFT (NUDFT) — which can be computed, but not faster than $O(N^2)$ without approximate algorithms (like the NUFFT). So the uniform grid is not a choice — it is a requirement for the FFT to apply. On that uniform grid, you are stuck with uniform-grid quadrature rules, of which Simpson's ($O(h^4)$) is the best simple option. The $O(\eta^4)$ accuracy loss is the price of the $O(N\log N)$ speedup. For most calibration applications, this trade-off is worthwhile.
