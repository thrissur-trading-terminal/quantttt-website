Market Making & Fill Probability

A market maker posts limit orders and waits. The deeper she posts, the more she earns per trade — but the less often she trades. This chapter derives the exact mathematics of that trade-off, and the optimal depth that maximises expected profit.

3.1 The Market Maker's Problem

A market maker (MM) profits by intermediating between buyers and sellers. She posts a sell limit order on the ask side at $S_t + \delta^+$ and a buy limit order on the bid side at $S_t - \delta^-$, where $S_t$ is the current midprice and $\delta^\pm > 0$ are her chosen depths away from the mid.

Her profit per completed round trip is $\delta^+ + \delta^-$ — she buys at the bid and sells at the ask, capturing the spread she creates. But she only captures this profit if her orders actually get filled — and fill probability falls sharply as she posts deeper into the book.

The MM's core optimisation: choose depths $\delta^+$ (ask side) and $\delta^-$ (bid side) to maximise expected profit per trade, balancing a higher margin per trade (deeper posting) against a lower probability of being filled (also deeper posting). These two effects work in opposite directions — finding the optimum requires a precise model of fill probability as a function of depth.
Cartea, Jaimungal & Penalva (2015), Chapter 2, Section 2.1.4 — Market Making using Limit Orders, pp. 28–30. The static version of the Ho & Stoll (1981) model, simplified to isolate the core depth-vs-probability trade-off.

3.2 Fill Probability — The Exponential Model

If we model the distribution of resting limit orders in the LOB as exponential with parameter $\kappa^\pm$, the probability that a buy limit order posted at depth $\delta^-$ below the mid gets filled — conditional on a market sell order arriving — is:

$$P(\delta) = e^{-\kappa \delta}$$
Cartea et al. (2015), Ch. 2, §2.1.4, p. 29 — used as $p_- P_-(\delta^-) = p_- e^{-\kappa^- \delta^-}$

Where:

  • $\delta$ = depth of the limit order away from the midprice (in price units, e.g. Rs.)
  • $\kappa > 0$ = market liquidity parameter. Higher $\kappa$ means a thinner, faster book — fill probability drops more steeply with depth
  • $P(\delta) \in (0, 1]$ — at depth zero ($\delta = 0$) probability is 1; as $\delta \to \infty$ probability $\to 0$
  • $p_\pm$ = probability that a market order arrives at all (arrival rate, separate from fill probability)
Think of it this way: if the book is dense with resting orders, an incoming market order is likely to stop long before reaching your deep limit order. Your fill probability decays exponentially as you post further away. The parameter $\kappa$ is the speed of that decay — in a thin book (few resting orders between you and the touch), $\kappa$ is small and your deep orders still have a reasonable chance. In a dense book, $\kappa$ is large and posting deep is nearly hopeless.

3.3 The MM's Optimisation Problem — Equation (2.3)

The MM's total expected profit per trade, considering both the bid and ask sides independently, is:

$$\max_{\delta^+,\,\delta^-} \mathbb{E}\left[\Pi(\delta^+, \delta^-)\right] = \max_{\delta^+,\,\delta^-} \left\{ p^+ e^{-\kappa^+ \delta^+} \delta^+ + p^- e^{-\kappa^- \delta^-} \delta^- \right\}$$
Cartea et al. (2015), Eq. (2.3), p. 29

Where:

  • $p^+$ = probability a buy market order arrives (ask side fills)
  • $p^-$ = probability a sell market order arrives (bid side fills)
  • $\delta^+$ = depth of the sell limit order above midprice
  • $\delta^-$ = depth of the buy limit order below midprice
  • $e^{-\kappa^\pm \delta^\pm}$ = fill probability given a market order arrival
  • $p^\pm e^{-\kappa^\pm \delta^\pm} \cdot \delta^\pm$ = expected profit contribution from each side

Because the bid and ask sides are independent in this formulation, we can maximise each term separately. Taking the derivative with respect to $\delta^\pm$ and setting it to zero:

$$\frac{d}{d\delta}\left[ \delta \cdot e^{-\kappa\delta} \right] = e^{-\kappa\delta} - \kappa\delta\, e^{-\kappa\delta} = e^{-\kappa\delta}(1 - \kappa\delta) = 0$$

Since $e^{-\kappa\delta} > 0$ for all finite $\delta$, the solution is simply:

$$\delta^{*,\pm} = \frac{1}{\kappa^\pm}$$
Cartea et al. (2015), Ch. 2, §2.1.4, p. 29 — "the optimal depth is equal to the mean depth in the LOB"
The optimal depth equals the reciprocal of the liquidity parameter. This is elegant: $\kappa$ is the decay rate of fill probability — it is also the inverse of the mean depth in the LOB (under the exponential model). So the MM should post at the average depth of the book. Too shallow: fill probability is high but margin is low. Too deep: margin is high but fill probability collapses. The mean depth is the exact tipping point.

3.4 Fill Probability at the Optimal Depth

Substituting $\delta^* = 1/\kappa$ back into the fill probability formula:

$$P(\delta^*) = e^{-\kappa \cdot (1/\kappa)} = e^{-1} \approx 0.368$$

This is a universal constant — regardless of the market, regardless of $\kappa$, a market maker posting at the optimal depth always faces a fill probability of exactly $e^{-1} \approx 36.8\%$. She expects to be filled on roughly 1 in 3 of her limit orders that face an incoming market order.

This $e^{-1}$ result holds only under the exponential fill probability model. Real books are not perfectly exponential — depth clusters at round numbers (as we saw in Chapter 1 with the 12,415-lot cluster at Rs.24,450), large iceberg orders distort the shape, and fill probability at any given moment depends on queue position as well as depth. The model gives an analytically tractable benchmark; real-desk implementations use empirical fill probability curves estimated from historical data.

WORKED EXAMPLE — OPTIMAL DEPTH ON NIFTY FUTURES NSE:NIFTY26MAYFUT · 29 Apr 2026

Suppose a market maker estimates $\kappa = 0.20$ for NIFTY26MAYFUT in the current session (a typical mid-session value). She has equal MO arrival probabilities: $p^+ = p^- = 0.5$.

Step 1 — Optimal posting depth

$$\delta^{*,\pm} = \frac{1}{\kappa} = \frac{1}{0.20} = \text{Rs.}5.00 = 100 \text{ ticks}$$

From the midprice of Rs.24,443.85, she posts:

Buy LO at $24{,}443.85 - 5.00 = \text{Rs.}24{,}438.85$ (bid side, Level ~10 in our book)
Sell LO at $24{,}443.85 + 5.00 = \text{Rs.}24{,}448.85$ (ask side, Level ~7–8 in our book)

Step 2 — Fill probability at optimal depth

$$P(\delta^*) = e^{-0.20 \times 5.00} = e^{-1} \approx 0.368 = 36.8\%$$

Step 3 — Expected profit per filled trade (one side)

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{profit per side}] = p^\pm \cdot e^{-\kappa \delta^*} \cdot \delta^* = 0.5 \times 0.368 \times 5.00 = \text{Rs.}0.92$$

Step 4 — Total expected profit (both sides)

$$\mathbb{E}[\Pi] = \text{Rs.}0.92 + \text{Rs.}0.92 = \text{Rs.}1.84 \text{ per round trip}$$

At lot size 25, that is Rs.46 per completed round trip per lot. For a desk running 100 lots per side, that is Rs.4,600 per round trip — before considering inventory risk and adverse selection, which we cover in Chapter 4.

Optimal depth = Rs.5.00 (100 ticks from mid) · Fill prob = 36.8% · Expected profit = Rs.1.84/round trip
Look at the live order book from Chapter 1: at Rs.24,438.85 on the bid side, we are near Level 15–16 of the book. The 455-lot cluster at Rs.24,438.90 (Level 15) and 260-lot at Rs.24,438.80 (Level 16) confirm this is a populated region — consistent with other MMs posting at similar optimal depths.

3.6 The Profit–Probability Trade-off

It is worth spending a moment to see exactly why posting at $\delta^* = 1/\kappa$ is optimal and not, say, half or double that depth.

If she posts too shallow ($\delta \ll 1/\kappa$)
Fill probability is high (close to 1), but margin per trade $\delta$ is tiny. Expected profit $= \delta \cdot e^{-\kappa\delta} \approx \delta \cdot 1 = \delta$ — grows only linearly. She trades frequently but earns almost nothing per trade. In a real book, posting very close to the touch also exposes her to adverse selection — being picked off by informed traders (Chapter 4).
If she posts too deep ($\delta \gg 1/\kappa$)
Margin $\delta$ is large, but fill probability $e^{-\kappa\delta} \to 0$ exponentially. Expected profit $= \delta \cdot e^{-\kappa\delta} \to 0$ as the exponential decay dominates. She almost never trades and earns nothing. Her capital is tied up in deep orders that rarely execute.

The function $f(\delta) = \delta \cdot e^{-\kappa\delta}$ has a unique maximum at $\delta = 1/\kappa$ — as shown in the interactive chart above. This is the exact balance point. The maximum value is $f(1/\kappa) = (1/\kappa) \cdot e^{-1}$, confirming that expected profit at the optimum is $\delta^* / e$.

In NSE NIFTY Options, market makers face a fundamentally different $\kappa$ environment than Futures. ATM options have thin, fragmented books with large spreads — $\kappa$ is small, optimal depth is large (Rs.10–50 from mid), and expected profit per fill is high. But fill probability is lower and adverse selection risk is severe because options attract informed directional flow. This is why NSE options market making desks are smaller in number but much more selective in their quoting than the Futures desk.
Practice Problems 4 questions
A market maker estimates $\kappa = 0.50$ for a NIFTY option. Using the exponential fill probability model, what is the optimal posting depth $\delta^*$ and the fill probability at that depth?
A $\delta^* = 0.50$ Rs., fill probability $= e^{-0.25} \approx 77.9\%$
B $\delta^* = 2.00$ Rs., fill probability $= e^{-1} \approx 36.8\%$
C $\delta^* = 2.00$ Rs., fill probability $= e^{-0.50} \approx 60.7\%$
D $\delta^* = 0.50$ Rs., fill probability $= e^{-1} \approx 36.8\%$
Explanation: Optimal depth: $\delta^* = 1/\kappa = 1/0.50 = 2.00$ Rs. Fill probability at optimal depth: $P(\delta^*) = e^{-\kappa \cdot \delta^*} = e^{-0.50 \times 2.00} = e^{-1} \approx 36.8\%$. This is a universal result — fill probability at the optimal depth is always $e^{-1} \approx 36.8\%$ regardless of $\kappa$. Option C uses $\delta^*$ correctly but computes fill probability at a different depth ($e^{-0.5}$ instead of $e^{-1}$). Option D confuses $\kappa$ with $\delta^*$.
The liquidity parameter $\kappa$ suddenly doubles from 0.20 to 0.40 (book becomes denser). Holding everything else fixed, what happens to the MM's optimal depth and expected profit per trade?
A Optimal depth doubles to Rs.10; expected profit doubles
B Optimal depth halves to Rs.2.50; expected profit halves
C Optimal depth halves to Rs.2.50; fill probability drops below 36.8%
D Optimal depth is unchanged; only fill probability changes
Explanation: $\delta^* = 1/\kappa$. If $\kappa$ doubles to 0.40, then $\delta^* = 1/0.40 = 2.50$ Rs. — exactly half the original Rs.5.00. Expected profit at the optimum is $\delta^*/e = 2.50/e \approx \text{Rs.}0.92$ — exactly half the original Rs.1.84. The fill probability remains $e^{-1} \approx 36.8\%$ always at the optimum. Option C is wrong because fill probability at the new optimum is still $e^{-1}$. A denser book forces the MM to post closer to the mid (lower $\delta^*$) and earns less per trade — the "competition effect" Cartea describes in Section 2.1.2.
A MM posts at depth Rs.3.00 with $\kappa = 0.20$. Compare her expected profit per MO arrival to the optimal depth of Rs.5.00. Is she above or below the optimum, and by how much?
A She is above the optimum. At Rs.3.00: expected profit $\approx$ Rs.1.65, vs Rs.1.84 at Rs.5.00
B She is below the optimum. At Rs.3.00: $3.00 \times e^{-0.6} \approx \text{Rs.}1.65$, vs $5.00 \times e^{-1} \approx \text{Rs.}1.84$ at the optimal depth
C She is above the optimum. At Rs.3.00 she fills more often so total profit is higher
D She is at the optimum because Rs.3.00 is the tick-rounded version of Rs.2.50
Explanation: At Rs.3.00 with $\kappa = 0.20$: $f(3) = 3.00 \times e^{-0.20 \times 3.00} = 3.00 \times e^{-0.6} = 3.00 \times 0.549 = \text{Rs.}1.65$. At the optimum Rs.5.00: $f(5) = 5.00 \times e^{-1} = 5.00 \times 0.368 = \text{Rs.}1.84$. She is posting shallower than optimal — her fill rate is higher (54.9% vs 36.8%), but the margin per fill is too low. She earns Rs.1.65 instead of the maximum Rs.1.84 — leaving Rs.0.19 per MO arrival on the table. This is the "too shallow" case from Section 3.6: frequent fills but below-optimal margin.
Looking at the live NIFTY26MAYFUT order book from Chapter 1, bid Level 8 shows 7,475 lots at Rs.24,440.00 across 22 orders. What does this tell a competing market maker who wants to post a buy limit order near that price?
A She should post at exactly Rs.24,440.00 to join the large queue and maximise fill probability
B She should post deeper (below Rs.24,440.00) to avoid competing with the wall
C She should post one tick above the wall at Rs.24,440.05 — getting price-time priority ahead of the 7,475-lot queue, improving her fill probability significantly without sacrificing much margin
D Wall levels are irrelevant to the MM's depth choice in the exponential model
Explanation: Price-time priority means orders at a better price are filled first. A 7,475-lot queue at Rs.24,440.00 represents enormous queue depth — a competing MM joining the back of that queue has very poor expected queue position. By posting at Rs.24,440.05 (one tick better), she jumps the entire queue: any sell MO that reaches Rs.24,440.05 fills her order first, before touching the wall at Rs.24,440.00. The margin sacrifice is only Rs.0.05 (one tick = Rs.0.05 on NIFTY Futures), but the fill probability improvement is dramatic. This is a practical application of the exponential model: large resting orders at a level effectively push the local $\kappa$ lower just above that level (sparse between touch and the wall), making shallow posting above walls attractive. Option A is wrong because joining the back of a 7,475-lot queue means she is last in the execution queue. Option B wastes margin unnecessarily.
