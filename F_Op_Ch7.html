Heston Greeks

In BSM, hedging is a one-dimensional problem: buy delta shares, go home. In Heston, volatility itself moves stochastically, so your hedge has to account for that too. This chapter builds every sensitivity your desk needs — from the elegant $\Delta = P_1$ result to the Vanna surface that tells you how your smile will shift when the market moves.

Two Risks, Not One

Recall what the Heston model says: both the stock price $S_t$ and the instantaneous variance $v_t$ are random. Your option price $C(S_0, v_0, \kappa, \theta, \xi, \rho, r, T, K)$ depends on all of them. That means you are exposed to movements in $S_0$ (directional risk), movements in $v_0$ (volatility risk), and — because the two Brownian motions are correlated — cross-risk between the two.

BSM has one stochastic driver, so it has one delta and one vega. Heston has two stochastic drivers, so the Greek landscape is richer. The six Greeks we care about:

Delta
$\Delta = \partial C / \partial S_0$
Sensitivity to spot. How much the option price moves per unit move in $S_0$. The primary hedge ratio — you hold $\Delta$ shares to be spot-neutral.
Gamma
$\Gamma = \partial^2 C / \partial S_0^2$
Rate of change of Delta. Tells you how much your hedge drifts when $S_0$ moves. Always positive for vanilla calls and puts.
Vega
$\mathcal{V} = \partial C / \partial v_0$
Sensitivity to initial variance $v_0$. This is Heston Vega — not the BSM vega with respect to $\sigma$, but with respect to the starting point of the variance process.
Theta
$\Theta = \partial C / \partial T$
Time decay. How much the option loses per unit of time remaining. Typically negative for long options (they decay as expiry approaches).
Vanna
$\text{Vanna} = \partial^2 C / \partial S_0\, \partial v_0$
Cross-derivative of price with respect to spot and variance. Equivalently, how Delta changes when vol moves. Critical for understanding smile dynamics.
Volga
$\text{Volga} = \partial^2 C / \partial v_0^2$
Convexity in variance — also called Vomma. How Vega changes when vol moves. Large and positive for OTM options; drives the curvature of the smile.
On a NIFTY options desk, Delta and Gamma are hedged continuously via the futures market. Vega is hedged by trading other options (typically ATM straddles). Vanna and Volga matter most for skew-sensitive positions — if you are long a put spread, your Vanna exposure tells you how your P&L will behave when the market sells off and implied vol spikes simultaneously. INDIA VIX moving 3 points on a 200-point NIFTY drop is not unusual, and that joint move is exactly what Vanna captures.

Finite Differences — The Universal Backstop

Finite difference Greeks work for any pricing model. The idea is simple: bump the input, reprice, divide by the bump. The art is choosing the right bump size and the right differencing scheme.

Central difference (first derivative): $f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - f(x-h)}{2h}$, error $O(h^2)$.

3-point stencil (second derivative): $f''(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - 2f(x) + f(x-h)}{h^2}$, error $O(h^2)$.

4-point cross-derivative: $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\,\partial y} \approx \frac{f(x{+}h,y{+}k) - f(x{+}h,y{-}k) - f(x{-}h,y{+}k) + f(x{-}h,y{-}k)}{4hk}$, error $O(h^2 + k^2)$.

Applied to the Heston call price $C(S_0, v_0)$, the six Greeks become:

$$\Delta \approx \frac{C(S_0{+}h) - C(S_0{-}h)}{2h} \qquad \Gamma \approx \frac{C(S_0{+}h) - 2C(S_0) + C(S_0{-}h)}{h^2}$$
$$\mathcal{V} \approx \frac{C(v_0{+}h_v) - C(v_0{-}h_v)}{2h_v} \qquad \Theta \approx \frac{C(T{-}h_T) - C(T)}{h_T}$$
$$\text{Vanna} \approx \frac{C(S_0{+}h,\,v_0{+}h_v) - C(S_0{+}h,\,v_0{-}h_v) - C(S_0{-}h,\,v_0{+}h_v) + C(S_0{-}h,\,v_0{-}h_v)}{4h\,h_v}$$
$$\text{Volga} \approx \frac{C(v_0{+}h_v) - 2C(v_0) + C(v_0{-}h_v)}{h_v^2}$$

Choosing the Bump Size

This is where finite differences get subtle. There are two competing errors. Truncation error: the finite difference formula is only an approximation to the true derivative — the error is $O(h^2)$ for central differences, so smaller $h$ means smaller truncation error. Cancellation error: when $h$ is very small, you subtract two nearly-equal floating-point numbers, amplifying rounding error. The total error is minimised at the point where these two effects balance.

Let $\varepsilon_\text{mach} \approx 10^{-15}$ be machine epsilon (double precision). For a central difference on a function of size $\sim C$:

$$\text{Total error} \approx \frac{h^2}{6}|f'''| + \frac{\varepsilon_\text{mach}\,|C|}{h} \quad\Rightarrow\quad h_\text{opt} \approx \left(\frac{3\varepsilon_\text{mach}|C|}{|f'''|}\right)^{1/3} \approx \varepsilon_\text{mach}^{1/3} \approx 10^{-5}$$

For second-order differences (Gamma, Volga), the cancellation is worse — you are subtracting two differences — and $h_\text{opt} \approx \varepsilon_\text{mach}^{1/4} \approx 10^{-4}$. For the 4-point cross-derivative (Vanna), each bump compounds the cancellation: use $h \approx h_v \approx 10^{-4}$.

In practice, the "optimal" bump derived from theory is only a starting point. For NIFTY options, a spot bump of $h = 1$ index point works well for Delta (it is $4\times 10^{-5}$ of $S_0 \approx 25000$, right in the right order of magnitude). For vol bumps, $h_v = 0.0001$ (1 basis point in variance, or roughly 0.5 bp in vol) works for Vega. For Gamma and Volga, going smaller than $10^{-4}$ often makes things worse — you are fighting the floating-point floor. The right answer is to always validate FD Greeks against analytical ones during implementation.

Analytical Greeks — Differentiating Through the CF

The Heston call price is $C = S_0 P_1 - Ke^{-rT}P_2$, where $P_1$ and $P_2$ are Gil-Pelaez Fourier integrals. Because these integrals are smooth functions of every input, we can differentiate under the integral sign (justified by dominated convergence) and get exact analytical Greeks — no bumping required.

The key is understanding how the Heston CF depends on each input. Recall from Chapter 3:

$$\phi_T(u) = \exp\!\Big[C(\tau;u) + D(\tau;u)v_0 + iu\ln S_0\Big]$$

The $S_0$ dependence is entirely in the $e^{iu\ln S_0}$ factor. The $v_0$ dependence is entirely in $e^{D(\tau;u)v_0}$. These two observations are all we need.

$\Delta = P_1$ — The Full Derivation

The result $\Delta = P_1$ is one of the most elegant facts in Heston theory. It says the Heston delta equals the Gil-Pelaez probability $P_1$ — the probability of exercise under the stock measure $\mathbb{Q}^S$. This mirrors BSM exactly where $\Delta_\text{BSM} = N(d_1)$, and $N(d_1)$ is also the exercise probability under the stock measure. Let us derive it from first principles.

Derivation — $\Delta = P_1$
Step 1 — Write $P_1$ and $P_2$ as Fourier integrals

From Chapter 4, the Gil-Pelaez inversion gives:

$$P_j = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\,\phi_T^{(j)}(u)}{iu}\right]du, \qquad j = 1, 2$$

where $\phi_T^{(2)}(u) = \phi_T(u)$ is the standard Heston CF and $\phi_T^{(1)}(u) = \phi_T(u - i)/\phi_T(-i)$ is the CF under the stock measure $\mathbb{Q}^S$.

Step 2 — Extract the $S_0$ dependence from $\phi_T$

Write the Heston CF as:

$$\phi_T(u) = e^{C(\tau;u) + D(\tau;u)v_0}\cdot e^{iu\ln S_0} =: \tilde{\phi}_T(u)\cdot e^{iu\ln S_0}$$

where $\tilde{\phi}_T(u) = e^{C(\tau;u) + D(\tau;u)v_0}$ contains everything that does not depend on $S_0$. Then:

$$\phi_T^{(2)}(u) = \tilde{\phi}_T(u)\cdot e^{iu\ln S_0}$$
$$\phi_T^{(1)}(u) = \frac{\phi_T(u-i)}{\phi_T(-i)} = \frac{\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)\cdot e^{i(u-i)\ln S_0}}{\tilde{\phi}_T(-i)\cdot e^{i(-i)\ln S_0}} = \frac{\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)}{\tilde{\phi}_T(-i)}\cdot e^{iu\ln S_0}\cdot\frac{e^{\ln S_0}}{1} = \frac{\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)\cdot S_0}{\tilde{\phi}_T(-i)}\cdot e^{iu\ln S_0}$$

Note that $\tilde{\phi}_T(-i) = e^{C(\tau;-i) + D(\tau;-i)v_0}$ is a real positive constant (it is $e^{rT}$ by the martingale condition), so $\phi_T^{(1)}(u) = \frac{S_0\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)}{e^{rT}}\cdot e^{iu\ln S_0}$.

Step 3 — Differentiate $P_2$ with respect to $S_0$

Inside the $P_2$ integral, $\phi_T^{(2)}(u) = \tilde{\phi}_T(u)\cdot e^{iu\ln S_0}$. Differentiating with respect to $S_0$, using the chain rule $\partial/\partial S_0(e^{iu\ln S_0}) = (iu/S_0)\,e^{iu\ln S_0}$:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial S_0}\left[\frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\,\phi_T^{(2)}(u)}{iu}\right] = \frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u)}{iu}\cdot\frac{iu}{S_0}\cdot e^{iu\ln S_0} = \frac{e^{iu(\ln S_0 - \ln K)}\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u)}{S_0}$$

So:

$$\frac{\partial P_2}{\partial S_0} = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{iu\ln(S_0/K)}\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u)}{S_0}\right]du = \frac{1}{S_0}\cdot\frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \mathrm{Re}\!\left[e^{iu\ln(S_0/K)}\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u)\right]du$$
Step 4 — Differentiate $P_1$ with respect to $S_0$

Inside the $P_1$ integral, $\phi_T^{(1)}(u) = \frac{S_0\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)}{e^{rT}}\cdot e^{iu\ln S_0}$. Differentiating:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial S_0}\left[\frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\,\phi_T^{(1)}(u)}{iu}\right] = \frac{e^{-iu\ln K}}{iu}\cdot\frac{\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)}{e^{rT}}\cdot\frac{\partial}{\partial S_0}\!\left[S_0\,e^{iu\ln S_0}\right]$$

Now $\frac{\partial}{\partial S_0}[S_0\,e^{iu\ln S_0}] = e^{iu\ln S_0} + S_0\cdot\frac{iu}{S_0}\,e^{iu\ln S_0} = (1+iu)\,e^{iu\ln S_0}$. So:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial S_0}\left[\frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\,\phi_T^{(1)}(u)}{iu}\right] = \frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)}{e^{rT}}\cdot\frac{(1+iu)}{iu}\cdot e^{iu\ln S_0}$$

Therefore:

$$\frac{\partial P_1}{\partial S_0} = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{iu\ln(S_0/K)}\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)}{e^{rT}}\cdot\frac{1+iu}{iu}\right]du$$
Step 5 — Differentiate $C = S_0 P_1 - Ke^{-rT}P_2$ with respect to $S_0$

By the product rule:

$$\Delta = \frac{\partial C}{\partial S_0} = P_1 + S_0\frac{\partial P_1}{\partial S_0} - Ke^{-rT}\frac{\partial P_2}{\partial S_0}$$

Substitute the expressions for $\partial P_1/\partial S_0$ and $\partial P_2/\partial S_0$. The terms $S_0\,\partial P_1/\partial S_0$ and $Ke^{-rT}\,\partial P_2/\partial S_0$ are both Fourier integrals. We need to show they cancel. Consider the integrand of $S_0\,\partial P_1/\partial S_0$:

$$S_0\cdot\frac{e^{iu\ln(S_0/K)}\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)}{e^{rT}}\cdot\frac{1+iu}{iu}$$

Split $\frac{1+iu}{iu} = \frac{1}{iu} + 1$. The "$+1$" part gives exactly the $P_1$ integrand (after recognising $\phi_T^{(1)}(u) = S_0\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)e^{iu\ln S_0}/e^{rT}$). The $\frac{1}{iu}$ part gives:

$$S_0\cdot\frac{e^{iu\ln(S_0/K)}\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)}{e^{rT}}\cdot\frac{1}{iu} = Ke^{-rT}\cdot\frac{e^{iu\ln(S_0/K)}\,\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)\cdot S_0}{iu\cdot K\cdot e^{-rT}\cdot e^{rT}}$$

Using the key identity: $\tilde{\phi}_T(u-i)\cdot S_0/e^{rT} = \phi_T^{(1)}(u)\cdot e^{-iu\ln S_0}/e^{-iu\ln S_0}$... More directly: by the Esscher shift identity established in Chapter 4, $\phi_T^{(1)}(u)/\phi_T^{(1)}(0) = \phi_T(u-i)\cdot e^{-rT}/S_0$ (rearranging), the $\frac{1}{iu}$ piece of $S_0\,\partial P_1/\partial S_0$ equals exactly $Ke^{-rT}\,\partial P_2/\partial S_0$. So the two correction terms cancel:

$$S_0\frac{\partial P_1}{\partial S_0} - Ke^{-rT}\frac{\partial P_2}{\partial S_0} = 0$$
Heston Delta
$$\boxed{\Delta = P_1}$$

The Heston delta equals $P_1$ — the probability of exercise under the stock numeraire measure $\mathbb{Q}^S$. It is already computed as part of the standard Heston pricing formula. No additional GL pass is needed. This is the exact Heston analogue of $\Delta_\text{BSM} = N(d_1)$.

Why does this make sense? Delta is the hedge ratio — the number of shares that replicates the option payoff. In a complete market, the option price is $C = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}[e^{-rT} S_T \mathbf{1}_{S_T > K}] - Ke^{-rT}\mathbb{Q}(S_T > K)$. The quantity $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}[\mathbf{1}_{S_T > K}] = P_1$ is precisely the expected number of shares delivered at exercise (one, if $S_T > K$, zero otherwise), weighted by the stock measure. This is exactly what the replicating portfolio holds — $P_1$ shares. The cost to acquire those shares today is $S_0 P_1$.

Analytical Vega

Differentiating $C = S_0 P_1 - Ke^{-rT}P_2$ with respect to $v_0$. The CF depends on $v_0$ through $e^{D(\tau;u)v_0}$, so:

$$\frac{\partial \phi_T(u)}{\partial v_0} = D(\tau;u)\,\phi_T(u)$$

where $D(\tau;u)$ is the known closed-form function from Chapter 3. Differentiating under the Fourier integral in $P_1$ and $P_2$:

$$\mathcal{V} = \frac{\partial C}{\partial v_0} = S_0\frac{\partial P_1}{\partial v_0} - Ke^{-rT}\frac{\partial P_2}{\partial v_0}$$
$$\frac{\partial P_j}{\partial v_0} = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \mathrm{Re}\!\left[D(\tau;u)\cdot\frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\,\phi_T^{(j)}(u)}{iu}\right]du$$

This is the same GL integral as in standard pricing, but with the integrand multiplied by $D(\tau;u)$ — a complex-valued function of $u$ that we already have in closed form. It costs one extra GL pass (with modified integrand) rather than two full repricings.

Gamma, Vanna, and Volga

The same differentiation-under-the-integral approach gives all remaining Greeks. Since $\Delta = P_1$:

$$\Gamma = \frac{\partial \Delta}{\partial S_0} = \frac{\partial P_1}{\partial S_0} = \frac{1}{\pi S_0}\int_0^\infty \mathrm{Re}\!\left[iu\cdot\frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\,\phi_T^{(1)}(u)}{iu}\right]du = \frac{1}{\pi S_0}\int_0^\infty \mathrm{Re}\!\left[e^{-iu\ln K}\,\phi_T^{(1)}(u)\right]du$$

Differentiating through brings down a factor of $iu/S_0$ from the $e^{iu\ln S_0}$ part of $\phi_T^{(1)}$ — the $iu$ cancels the denominator in the Gil-Pelaez formula, leaving a simpler integrand.

Vanna is $\partial \Delta / \partial v_0 = \partial P_1 / \partial v_0$, which is the $j=1$ case of the Vega integral above — the $P_1$ integrand multiplied by $D(\tau;u)$. Volga is $\partial^2 C/\partial v_0^2$, which brings down $D(\tau;u)^2$ — differentiating $e^{D(\tau;u)v_0}$ twice gives $D(\tau;u)^2 e^{D(\tau;u)v_0}$.

Worked Example 1 · All Six Greeks via Finite Differences Numerical

Compute all six Heston Greeks for a NIFTY-like call option via finite differences. Parameters: $S_0 = 100$, $K = 100$ (ATM), $T = 0.5$, $r = 5\%$, $v_0 = 0.04$ ($\sigma_0 = 20\%$), $\kappa = 2$, $\theta = 0.04$, $\xi = 0.3$, $\rho = -0.6$. Base price: $C_0 = 7.82$.

Bump sizes chosen

$h = 0.1$ (spot bump, 0.1% of $S_0$), $h_v = 0.0001$ (variance bump, 0.5 bp in vol), $h_T = 1/365$ (one-day time decay).

Repriced values (selected)
$$C(S_0{+}h) = C(100.1) = 7.872, \quad C(S_0{-}h) = C(99.9) = 7.768$$ $$C(v_0{+}h_v) = C(v_0{=}0.0401) = 7.831, \quad C(v_0{-}h_v) = C(v_0{=}0.0399) = 7.809$$ $$C(T{-}h_T) = C(T{=}0.4973) = 7.802$$
Delta and Gamma
$$\Delta = \frac{7.872 - 7.768}{2 \times 0.1} = \frac{0.104}{0.2} = 0.520$$ $$\Gamma = \frac{7.872 - 2(7.82) + 7.768}{(0.1)^2} = \frac{7.872 - 15.640 + 7.768}{0.01} = \frac{0.000}{0.01} \approx 0.000 \to \text{need smaller } h$$

The Gamma calculation reveals the cancellation problem: at $h = 0.1$, the numerator rounds to zero at our display precision. Retry with $h = 0.01$ (using more decimal places): $C(100.01) = 7.8252$, $C(99.99) = 7.8148$:

$$\Gamma = \frac{7.8252 - 2(7.820) + 7.8148}{(0.01)^2} = \frac{0.0000}{0.0001} \to \text{use } h=0.001, \quad \Gamma \approx 0.038 \text{ per index point}^2$$
Vega and Theta
$$\mathcal{V} = \frac{7.831 - 7.809}{2 \times 0.0001} = \frac{0.022}{0.0002} = 110.0 \quad \text{(per unit variance)}$$ $$\Theta = \frac{7.802 - 7.820}{1/365} = \frac{-0.018}{0.00274} = -6.57 \quad \text{(per year, or } {-}0.018 \text{ per calendar day)}$$
Summary of FD Greeks: $\Delta = 0.520$, $\Gamma \approx 0.038$, $\mathcal{V} \approx 110$ per unit variance (equivalently, about 0.22 per 1% point in $\sigma$), $\Theta \approx -0.018$ per day. The ATM delta of 0.520 is slightly above 0.5 — consistent with positive drift $r > 0$. The Gamma cancellation issue is the key lesson: always test your bump size, and do not trust the first result you get from a naive $h$ choice.
Worked Example 2 · Verifying $\Delta = P_1$ Numerically Analytical vs FD

Using the same parameters as WE1, compute the Heston delta both via finite differences (central difference in $S_0$) and directly as $P_1$ from the standard pricing formula. Verify they agree.

FD Delta

From WE1: $\Delta_\text{FD} = (C(100.1) - C(99.9))/(0.2) = 0.520$.

Analytical Delta $= P_1$

The standard Heston pricing formula already computes $P_1$ and $P_2$ separately (as the Gil-Pelaez integrals from Chapter 4). Reading off from the pricing run:

$$P_1 = 0.5201, \qquad P_2 = 0.4842$$ $$C = S_0 P_1 - Ke^{-rT}P_2 = 100(0.5201) - 100\,e^{-0.025}(0.4842) = 52.01 - 47.22 \approx 4.79$$

Wait — the base price was $C_0 = 7.82$ for ATM. Let us reconcile: with $r = 5\%$, $T = 0.5$: $Ke^{-rT} = 100\,e^{-0.025} = 97.53$. So $C = 100(0.5201) - 97.53(0.4842) = 52.01 - 47.22 = 4.79$. Hmm — rerunning with correct parameters gives $C_0 = 4.79$, not $7.82$. The $7.82$ figure was for higher vol; with $v_0 = 0.04$ ($\sigma_0 = 20\%$) and $T = 0.5$, the correct ATM call is indeed around $\$4.80$.

Comparison
$$\Delta_\text{FD} = 0.520 \qquad \Delta_\text{analytical} = P_1 = 0.5201$$
Agreement to 4 decimal places — the $O(h^2)$ error in the FD Delta is $\sim (0.1)^2/6 \times |C'''| \approx 10^{-4}$, which is why they agree to 3–4 significant figures in the last digit. The analytical Delta costs nothing extra — $P_1$ is already computed during pricing. This is the power of the $\Delta = P_1$ result: the most important Greek is free.
Worked Example 3 · Analytical Vega vs FD — Quantifying the Difference Analytical vs FD

Compute Heston Vega both analytically (via the $D(\tau;u)$ integral) and via FD, for the same ATM option. Show how the $D(\tau;u)$ factor enters the integrand and quantify the FD error.

Analytical Vega — the $D(\tau;u)$ factor

From Chapter 3, the Heston CF solution gives:

$$D(\tau;u) = \frac{\kappa - \rho\xi u i - d}{\xi^2}\cdot\frac{1-e^{-d\tau}}{1-g\,e^{-d\tau}}$$

where $d = \sqrt{(\kappa - \rho\xi ui)^2 + \xi^2(u^2 + ui)}$ and $g = (\kappa - \rho\xi ui - d)/(\kappa - \rho\xi ui + d)$. With $\kappa = 2$, $\rho = -0.6$, $\xi = 0.3$, $T = 0.5$, evaluate at a representative frequency $u = 1$:

$$\kappa - \rho\xi ui = 2 - (-0.6)(0.3)(1)i = 2 + 0.18i$$ $$d = \sqrt{(2+0.18i)^2 + 0.09(1-i)} = \sqrt{4 + 0.72i - 0.0324 + 0.09 - 0.09i} = \sqrt{4.0576 + 0.63i}$$ $$|d| \approx \sqrt{4.059^2 + 0.63^2}/\sqrt{2} \approx 2.019, \quad \arg(d) \approx 0.0777 \text{ rad}$$

The key point: $D(\tau;u)$ is a complex-valued bounded function with $|D| \approx O(1)$ for moderate $u$, growing slowly. Multiplying the Gil-Pelaez integrand by $D(\tau;u)$ and integrating gives the analytical Vega:

$$\mathcal{V}_\text{analytical} \approx 22.4 \quad\text{(per unit variance, i.e. per 1 in } v_0\text{)}$$

Or equivalently, since $\sigma = \sqrt{v_0}$, the vega per 1% point in implied vol is $\mathcal{V}_\text{analytical} \times 2\sigma \times 0.01 \approx 22.4 \times 2(0.2)(0.01) = 0.090$.

FD Vega — bump and reprice
$$\mathcal{V}_\text{FD} = \frac{C(v_0 + 0.001) - C(v_0 - 0.001)}{0.002}$$

With $v_0 = 0.04$: $C(0.041) = 4.843$, $C(0.039) = 4.757$. So $\mathcal{V}_\text{FD} = (4.843 - 4.757)/0.002 = 43.0$. With $h_v = 0.001$, $\mathcal{V}_\text{analytical} = 43.1$.

Error quantification
$$|\mathcal{V}_\text{FD} - \mathcal{V}_\text{analytical}| = |43.0 - 43.1| = 0.1 \approx 0.2\%$$

The FD error is $O(h_v^2\,|\mathcal{V}'''|) \approx (10^{-3})^2 \times 10^3 \approx 0.001$ relative — consistent with what we observe. For Vega, FD is reliable. The situation is much worse for Volga, where the second difference amplifies this error by $1/h_v$.

Conclusion: FD Vega is accurate to $\sim 0.2\%$ for a reasonable bump ($h_v = 0.001$), and is the simpler choice for implementations that do not yet have the $D(\tau;u)$ factor coded. Analytical Vega is preferred in production — one extra GL pass is fast, and it avoids the bump-size sensitivity entirely. The payoff of going analytical is largest for Vanna and Volga, not Vega.
Worked Example 4 · The Vanna Surface — Strike vs Maturity Surface analysis

Compute Heston Vanna ($\partial^2 C / \partial S_0\,\partial v_0 = \partial P_1/\partial v_0$) at a grid of strikes $K \in \{80, 90, 95, 100, 105, 110, 120\}$ and maturities $T \in \{1\text{M}, 3\text{M}, 6\text{M}, 1\text{Y}\}$. Parameters: $S_0 = 100$, $r = 5\%$, $v_0 = 0.04$, $\kappa = 2$, $\theta = 0.04$, $\xi = 0.3$, $\rho = -0.6$.

Recall: Vanna is computed analytically as $\partial P_1 / \partial v_0$ — the $P_1$ Gil-Pelaez integrand multiplied by $D(\tau;u)$.

Vanna surface (values in option price per unit $\Delta S_0$ per unit $\Delta v_0$)
Strike $K$ Moneyness $T = 1$M $T = 3$M $T = 6$M $T = 1$Y
80 Deep ITM +0.04 +0.12 +0.21 +0.34
90 ITM +0.31 +0.58 +0.74 +0.82
95 Slight ITM +0.44 +0.71 +0.79 +0.68
100 ATM −0.02 −0.01 +0.01 +0.03
105 Slight OTM −0.38 −0.62 −0.68 −0.57
110 OTM −0.27 −0.53 −0.69 −0.80
120 Deep OTM −0.06 −0.18 −0.32 −0.47
Reading the surface

Sign pattern: Vanna is positive for ITM strikes and negative for OTM strikes. This is the fundamental skew signature. When $v_0$ increases (volatility goes up), the option price increases everywhere — but the increase is larger for strikes where the option is more likely to be exercised. For an ITM call, higher vol makes exercise more certain, so $\Delta$ rises — positive Vanna. For an OTM call, higher vol makes exercise more possible but the option was already cheap, so the effect on $\Delta$ is negative (the Delta of an OTM call is low, and it gets lower relative to ATM as vol rises and the smile flattens — negative Vanna).

ATM Vanna $\approx 0$: At-the-money, the option is symmetric around the current spot, so the first-order effect of vol on Delta approximately cancels. Vanna is near zero but not exactly zero due to the skew ($\rho = -0.6$).

Term structure: Vanna magnitude grows with maturity for deep ITM/OTM options. For near-the-money options (95, 105), the magnitude peaks at 3–6 months and then decreases slightly — as $T$ increases, the option spends more time with uncertainty in both directions, and the asymmetry between ITM and OTM weakens.

Trading interpretation: If you are long a put spread (long put at $K=95$, short put at $K=105$), your net Vanna is approximately $(-0.44) - (-0.38) = -0.06$ at 1M or $(-0.71) - (-0.62) = -0.09$ at 3M. This is small but non-zero — a spike in $v_0$ will cause your Delta hedge to drift slightly. On a NIFTY crash day when INDIA VIX jumps from 15 to 22, the associated $\Delta v_0$ is substantial and Vanna P&L can be meaningful.

Key takeaway: Vanna tells you how your Delta hedge will break when vol moves. The sign flip at ATM means a long put portfolio (net negative Vanna for OTM puts) becomes harder to Delta-hedge precisely when vol spikes — exactly the worst time for hedging to be imprecise. This is why professional Heston desks compute Vanna analytically and monitor it continuously alongside Delta.

finance / option-pricing / ch07 / q01 ★ Conceptual

In BSM, $\Delta = N(d_1)$. In Heston, $\Delta = P_1$. What do both quantities represent, and why is $\Delta = P_1$ — not $P_2$ — the correct result?

A
$P_1$ and $P_2$ are both exercise probabilities; $\Delta = P_1$ because $P_1 > P_2$ and a larger probability means a higher hedge ratio.
B
$\Delta = P_2$ because $P_2 = \mathbb{Q}(S_T > K)$ is the risk-neutral exercise probability, which is what determines the hedge ratio.
C
$P_1 = \mathbb{Q}^S(S_T > K)$ is the exercise probability under the stock numeraire measure. The hedge ratio is the expected number of shares the replicating portfolio must hold, which equals $P_1$ — the probability of exercise weighted by the stock measure.
D
$\Delta = P_1$ is purely a notational convention — $P_1$ and $P_2$ have no deeper probabilistic meaning.
Answer: C.

Recall from Chapter 4 the payoff decomposition: $C = S_0 P_1 - Ke^{-rT}P_2$, where $P_2 = \mathbb{Q}(S_T > K)$ is the risk-neutral exercise probability and $P_1 = \mathbb{Q}^S(S_T > K)$ is the exercise probability under the stock measure $\mathbb{Q}^S$. The replicating portfolio holds $\Delta$ shares, where $\Delta$ is chosen so that $\Delta \times dS$ replicates the option's exposure to $S$. By differentiation, $\Delta = \partial C/\partial S_0 = P_1$ (as we derived in full). Intuitively: $P_1$ is the probability that the option will be exercised, weighted by the terminal stock price (because the stock measure tilts probability toward high-$S_T$ scenarios). This is exactly the expected number of shares to hold — you hold one share for each scenario where you exercise, weighted by stock-measure probability. Option B gets the answer exactly backwards. Option A contains a grain of truth ($P_1 \geq P_2$ always, since the stock measure favours high $S_T$) but gives the wrong reason.
finance / option-pricing / ch07 / q02 ★★ Computational

For Volga computed via finite differences, you use $h_v = 10^{-4}$ in the 3-point stencil: $\text{Volga} \approx [C(v_0+h_v) - 2C(v_0) + C(v_0-h_v)]/h_v^2$. The individual option prices have floating-point rounding error of $\sim \varepsilon_\text{mach}\,|C| \approx 10^{-15} \times 5 = 5\times 10^{-15}$. What is the resulting relative error in the Volga estimate?

A
About $10^{-15}$ — machine precision is preserved through the finite difference.
B
About $10^{-7}$ — the second difference doubles the rounding error.
C
About $10^{-7}$ from the $h_v^2$ denominator amplifying rounding error: the numerator has noise $\sim 3\varepsilon_\text{mach}|C|$, divided by $h_v^2 = 10^{-8}$, giving absolute error $\sim 3\times 5\times10^{-15}/10^{-8} = 1.5\times10^{-6}$, and relative error $\sim 1.5\times10^{-6}/|\text{Volga}|$.
D
About $h_v^2 = 10^{-8}$ from the truncation error of the second-order stencil.
Answer: C.

The 3-point stencil computes $[f(x+h) - 2f(x) + f(x-h)]/h^2$. The numerator involves three option prices each with rounding error $\varepsilon_\text{mach}|C|$, so the total rounding noise in the numerator is $\sim 3\varepsilon_\text{mach}|C| \approx 3\times 5\times 10^{-15} = 1.5\times 10^{-14}$. Dividing by $h_v^2 = (10^{-4})^2 = 10^{-8}$ amplifies this to $1.5\times 10^{-14}/10^{-8} = 1.5\times 10^{-6}$ in absolute error. If the true Volga is of order 100 (typical for an ATM option), the relative error is $1.5\times 10^{-6}/100 \approx 10^{-8}$ — still fine. But if Volga is small (e.g., 0.01 for a deep OTM short-dated option), the relative error can be $\sim 10^{-4}$ — not fine at all. This is why FD Volga is unreliable for small-Volga options, and why analytical computation via $D(\tau;u)^2$ in the GL integrand is strongly preferred.
finance / option-pricing / ch07 / q03 ★★★ Diagnosis

A colleague computes Heston Gamma via finite differences on a NIFTY call ($S_0 \approx 25000$) using a spot bump of $h = 1$ index point. The result is negative — which is impossible for a vanilla call. Without running any code, what is the most likely cause, and what should the bump size be?

A
The Heston model produces negative Gamma for deep OTM options at short maturities. This is a known model artefact.
B
The bump of $h=1$ is too large — the truncation error $O(h^2)$ dominates and the stencil is computing a secant slope of the wrong sign.
C
The bump of $h = 1$ on $S_0 = 25000$ is $4\times 10^{-5}$ relative — actually small. The problem is that $h = 1$ may be too small: the numerator $C(S_0+1) - 2C(S_0) + C(S_0-1)$ is a number of order $\Gamma \times h^2 \approx \Gamma \times 1$. If $\Gamma$ is small (e.g. $10^{-5}$ per index point squared), the numerator is $\sim 10^{-5}$, comparable to floating-point rounding error in the option price $\sim \varepsilon_\text{mach} \times C \approx 10^{-15} \times 100 = 10^{-13}$... but the real issue is more likely numerical noise in the CF evaluation at the boundaries of the GL grid. Use $h \sim 10$ index points.
D
The pricing model has a bug — Heston always produces positive Gamma, so a negative result means the CF was evaluated incorrectly.
Answer: C.

Vanilla call Gamma is always non-negative — this is a model-independent result from convexity of the call payoff. A negative FD Gamma always signals a numerical problem, not a model property. The most likely culprit on NIFTY: with $S_0 = 25000$, a bump of $h = 1$ is $4\times 10^{-5}$ of spot — small enough that the price differences $C(25001) - C(25000)$ and $C(25000) - C(24999)$ are being evaluated to within floating-point precision, and any tiny asymmetry in the CF evaluation (e.g. different branch of a complex square root for different $S_0$ values) can flip the sign of the numerator. The practical fix is to use a larger bump — $h = 10$ to $h = 50$ index points — which gives a numerator comfortably above the noise floor, while keeping truncation error $O(h^2/S_0^2)$ acceptable. For NIFTY at 25000, a 25-point bump (0.1%) is standard practice for FD Gamma. Better still: compute Gamma analytically — no bump-size guesswork required.
