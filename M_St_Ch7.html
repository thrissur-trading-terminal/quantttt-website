CTMC I — Birth & Death Processes

How do you generalise a Markov chain to continuous time? Keep the Markov property, make the state space discrete, and let the chain wait in each state for an exponential holding time before jumping. This chapter builds CTMCs rigorously — from the embedded DTMC through the Delta-DTMC limit to the generator matrix $Q$ — then applies the machinery to Birth-Death chains.

From DTMC to CTMC: The Big Picture

A DTMC jumps at every integer time step. A CTMC generalises this: the chain waits in its current state for a random amount of time before jumping. The Markov property is preserved — the next state depends only on the current state, not the history.

A Continuous-Time Markov Chain (CTMC) is a stochastic process $\{X_t\}_{t \ge 0}$ that:
  • Evolves on a discrete state space $\mathcal{S} = \{0,1,2,\ldots,m\}$ (finite or countable)
  • Changes state at random time instants (not at fixed clock ticks)
  • Satisfies the Markov property: given the current state and past, the next state depends only on the current state
Its full evolution: $\{X_0, T_1, X_1, T_2, X_2, T_3, X_3, \ldots\}$ where $X_n$ is the state after the $n$-th transition and $T_n = Y_n - Y_{n-1}$ is the holding time between transitions $(n-1)$ and $n$.
The CTMC alternates between: (a) waiting in a state for a random exponential time, then (b) jumping instantaneously to the next state. The jump is infinitely fast compared to the wait. After jumping, waiting begins again.

The Holding Time Assumption

The mathematical magic of CTMCs comes from one assumption: holding times are exponential. This is not just convenient — exponential is the only continuous distribution with the memoryless property $P(T > t+s \mid T > t) = P(T > s)$, which is exactly what preserves the Markov property in continuous time.

When the current state is $i \in \mathcal{S}$, the time until the next transition satisfies: $$T_{n+1} \mid \{X_n = i\} \sim \text{Exponential}(\nu_i)$$ independent of past history and of the next state. $\nu_i > 0$ is the rate of state $i$ — the average number of transitions out of $i$ per unit time.

The chain also has an embedded DTMC: the sequence $\{X_n\}_{n \ge 0}$ of states visited (ignoring timing), with transition matrix $P = [P_{ij}]$ where $P_{ij} = P(X_{n+1}=j \mid X_n=i)$. A CTMC is fully specified by $\{\nu_i\}$ and $P$.

Transition Rates and the Generator Matrix $Q$

The transition rate from $i$ to $j$ ($i \neq j$) combines holding rate and jump probability:

$$q_{ij} := \nu_i\, P_{ij} \qquad i \neq j$$ $$\nu_i = \sum_{j \neq i} q_{ij} \qquad \text{(total rate out of state } i\text{)}$$

These form the generator matrix $Q = [q_{ij}]$ with $q_{ii} = -\nu_i$. Every row sums to zero.

$\nu_i$ = average transitions out of state $i$ per unit time. $P_{ij}$ = fraction going to $j$. So $q_{ij} = \nu_i P_{ij}$ = average transitions from $i$ to $j$ per unit time. The generator $Q$ completely characterises the CTMC — you can recover both $\nu_i$ and $P_{ij}$ from it.

Where Do Exponential Holding Times Come From? The $\Delta$-DTMC Derivation

The deepest insight: CTMCs arise as the $\Delta \to 0$ limit of DTMCs with step size $\Delta$. For each $\Delta > 0$, define the $\Delta$-DTMC:

$$P_{ij}^{(\Delta)} := \begin{cases} q_{ij}\,\Delta + o(\Delta) & i \neq j \\ 1 - \nu_i\,\Delta + o(\Delta) & i = j \end{cases}$$

where $o(\Delta)/\Delta \to 0$ as $\Delta \to 0$ (terms vanishing faster than $\Delta$). Sanity check: $\sum_j P_{ij}^{(\Delta)} = 1 + o(\Delta) \to 1$ as $\Delta \to 0$. ✓

Holding times converge to Exp$(\nu_i)$:

In the $\Delta$-DTMC, the number of self-loops $H_i^{(\Delta)}$ before leaving state $i$ satisfies: $$H_i^{(\Delta)} \sim \text{Geometric}(1-P_{ii}^{(\Delta)}) = \text{Geometric}(\nu_i\Delta + o(\Delta))$$ The continuous holding time is $T_i^{(\Delta)} = H_i^{(\Delta)} \cdot \Delta$. Its survival function: $$P(T_i^{(\Delta)} > t) = (P_{ii}^{(\Delta)})^{t/\Delta} \approx (1 - \nu_i\Delta)^{t/\Delta} \xrightarrow{\Delta\to 0} e^{-\nu_i t}$$ This is Exp$(\nu_i)$. Geometric (discrete) $\to$ Exponential (continuous). $\square$

Jump probabilities converge to $q_{ij}/\nu_i$:
$$\lim_{\Delta\to 0} P\!\left(\text{next state is } j \mid \text{leaving state } i\right) = \lim_{\Delta\to 0} \frac{P_{ij}^{(\Delta)}}{\sum_{k\neq i} P_{ik}^{(\Delta)}} = \frac{q_{ij}\Delta}{\nu_i\Delta} = \frac{q_{ij}}{\nu_i} = P_{ij}$$
This is exactly parallel to how Binomial$(\Delta) \to$ Poisson and Geometric$(\Delta) \to$ Exponential. The CTMC is a PCP generalised to have state-dependent rates and directed jumps, not just monotone counting. The $\Delta$-DTMC approach gives a concrete construction, not just an abstract definition.

Worked Example: Computing $Q$ from Verbal Description

Three-State CTMC — Full Solution

State space $\{1, 2, 3\}$. Given information:

State 1: Exp holding with mean 2 hrs. Jumps to 2 w.p. 0.7, to 3 w.p. 0.3.

State 2: Exp holding with mean 1 hr. Always jumps to 3.

State 3: Exp holding with mean 3 hrs. Always jumps to 1.

Step 1 — Holding rates = 1 / mean holding time:

$$\nu_1 = 1/2, \quad \nu_2 = 1, \quad \nu_3 = 1/3$$

Step 2 — Embedded DTMC:

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0.7 & 0.3 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Step 3 — Transition rates $q_{ij} = \nu_i P_{ij}$:

$$q_{12} = 0.5 \times 0.7 = 0.35, \quad q_{13} = 0.5 \times 0.3 = 0.15$$ $$q_{23} = 1 \times 1 = 1, \quad q_{31} = (1/3) \times 1 = 1/3$$

Generator matrix (diagonal = $-\nu_i$, rows sum to zero):

$$Q = \begin{pmatrix} -0.5 & 0.35 & 0.15 \\ 0 & -1 & 1 \\ 1/3 & 0 & -1/3 \end{pmatrix}$$

Birth-Death CTMCs

The most important special class: state space $\mathcal{S} = \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$ with transitions only to neighbouring states.

0 →λ₀→ 1 →λ₁→ 2 →λ₂→ 3 ⋯ n →λₙ→ n+1
    ←μ₁←    ←μ₂←    ←μ₃←                  ←μₙ₊₁←

When in state $n$, think of $n$ independent birth clocks Exp$(\lambda)$ and $n$ independent death clocks Exp$(\mu)$ all racing. By the minimum-of-exponentials result: holding time $\sim$ Exp$(n\lambda + n\mu) = $ Exp$(n(\lambda+\mu))$, and:

$$P(\text{birth} \mid X_t = n) = \frac{n\lambda}{n\lambda + n\mu} = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda+\mu}$$
Poisson Process
$\lambda_n = \lambda$  |  $\mu_n = 0$
Pure birth, state-independent rate. The PCP from Ch 6 is a birth-only CTMC. Holding time in any state: Exp$(\lambda)$.
Yule Process
$\lambda_n = n\lambda$  |  $\mu_n = 0$
Pure birth, linear growth. Each individual spawns at rate $\lambda$ independently. $E[X_t] = X_0 e^{\lambda t}$ — exponential growth.
M/M/$\infty$ Queue
$\lambda_n = \lambda$  |  $\mu_n = n\mu$
Arrivals at rate $\lambda$, each job exits at rate $\mu$. Steady-state: Poisson$(\lambda/\mu)$. The operating system model.
Machine Repair
$\lambda_n = (N-n)\lambda$  |  $\mu_n = n\mu$
$N$ machines: $n$ failed, $N-n$ running. Failures at rate $(N-n)\lambda$, repairs at rate $n\mu$. Classic reliability model.

Steady-State via Local Balance

For Birth-Death CTMCs, global balance simplifies to flow balance between adjacent pairs:

$$\pi_n\,\lambda_n = \pi_{n+1}\,\mu_{n+1} \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \pi_n = \pi_0 \cdot \frac{\lambda_0\lambda_1\cdots\lambda_{n-1}}{\mu_1\mu_2\cdots\mu_n}$$

Normalise $\sum_n \pi_n = 1$ to find $\pi_0$. Stationary distribution exists iff the sum converges.

CTMCs power quantitative finance across multiple domains. Credit ratings change as a CTMC with empirically estimated $Q$ matrices. Order book depth evolves as a birth-death process — limit orders are births, cancellations and market fills are deaths. The generator $Q$ is estimated from historical tick data and used to price path-dependent derivatives and compute default probabilities over arbitrary horizons.

A CTMC has holding rate $\nu_i = 4$ in state $i$. The expected holding time before the next transition is:

A
$4$ units
B
$1/4$ units
C
$e^{-4}$ units
D
$16$ units
Answer: B — $1/4$ units

Holding time $\sim$ Exp$(\nu_i)$, so $E[\text{holding time}] = 1/\nu_i = 1/4$. $\nu_i$ is a rate (transitions per unit time), not a duration. Higher rate = shorter expected stay. State with $\nu_i = 4$ ejects the chain on average every $1/4$ time unit.

A CTMC has $\nu_1 = 0.5$, $P_{12} = 0.6$, $P_{13} = 0.4$. What is the transition rate $q_{12}$, and what is the generator entry $q_{11}$?

A
$q_{12} = 0.6$, $q_{11} = -0.6$
B
$q_{12} = 0.3$, $q_{11} = -0.5$
C
$q_{12} = 0.5$, $q_{11} = -0.5$
D
$q_{12} = 0.3$, $q_{11} = -0.3$
Answer: B — $q_{12} = 0.3$, $q_{11} = -0.5$

$q_{12} = \nu_1 P_{12} = 0.5 \times 0.6 = 0.3$. Also $q_{13} = 0.5 \times 0.4 = 0.2$. The diagonal entry $q_{11} = -\nu_1 = -0.5$ (negative of total exit rate). Check: $q_{11} + q_{12} + q_{13} = -0.5 + 0.3 + 0.2 = 0$. ✓ Generator rows always sum to zero — probability flowing out equals probability flowing in at stationarity.

In the $\Delta$-DTMC approximation, the self-transition probability is $P_{ii}^{(\Delta)} = 1 - \nu_i\Delta$. As $\Delta \to 0$, the holding time $T_i^{(\Delta)} = H_i^{(\Delta)} \cdot \Delta$ converges to which distribution?

A
Geometric$(\nu_i)$
B
Poisson$(\nu_i)$
C
Exponential$(\nu_i)$
D
Uniform$(0, 1/\nu_i)$
Answer: C — Exponential$(\nu_i)$

$H_i^{(\Delta)} \sim$ Geometric$(\nu_i\Delta)$. Survival function of $T_i^{(\Delta)} = H_i^{(\Delta)}\cdot\Delta$: $$P(T_i^{(\Delta)} > t) = (1 - \nu_i\Delta)^{t/\Delta} \to e^{-\nu_i t}$$ Geometric (discrete slots) converges to Exponential (continuous time) — exactly as Binomial converges to Poisson. This is the fundamental limit that gives CTMCs their exponential holding times.
stochastic / ch07 / q04★★★ Hard

In a Birth-Death CTMC with $\lambda_n = 2$ and $\mu_n = 3n$ (constant birth rate, linear death rate), starting from state 2, what is the probability the next event is a birth?

A
$2/3$
B
$2/8 = 1/4$
C
$1/2$
D
$3/4$
Answer: B — $1/4$

From state $n=2$: birth rate $\lambda_2 = 2$, death rate $\mu_2 = 3\times2 = 6$. Total exit rate $= 8$.
$P(\text{birth}) = \lambda_2/(\lambda_2+\mu_2) = 2/8 = 1/4$.

At $n=2$ the death rate (6) dominates birth (2). As $n$ grows, deaths become increasingly likely — this system has a strong tendency to shrink, reflected in a stable steady-state distribution.
