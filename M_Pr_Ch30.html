CLT — Proof & Applications

The final chapter of the course. We prove that the Gamma distribution also converges to Normal (showing CLT holds for continuous distributions), give a complete MGF-based proof of the Lindberg-Lévy CLT for iid sequences, contrast CLT with WLLN, and solve three practical problems — dice, grading, and Poisson sums.

Gamma Distribution Converges to $N(0,1)$

$X\sim\Gamma(\lambda,\alpha)$. Show the standardised version converges to $N(0,1)$ as $\alpha\to\infty$.

$E(X)=\alpha/\lambda$, $\text{Var}(X)=\alpha/\lambda^2$. Standardise: $Y = \frac{X-\alpha/\lambda}{\sqrt{\alpha}/\lambda} = \frac{\lambda X-\alpha}{\sqrt{\alpha}}$. By the linear MGF rule with $a=\lambda/\sqrt{\alpha}$, $b=-\sqrt{\alpha}$:

$$\log M_Y(t) = -\sqrt{\alpha}\,t + \log M_X\!\left(\frac{\lambda t}{\sqrt{\alpha}}\right)$$ MGF of $\Gamma(\lambda,\alpha)$ is $(1-t/\lambda)^{-\alpha}$, so at $\lambda t/\sqrt{\alpha}$: $$\log M_X\!\left(\frac{\lambda t}{\sqrt{\alpha}}\right) = -\alpha\log\!\left(1-\frac{t}{\sqrt{\alpha}}\right)$$ Expanding $\log(1-u) = -u - u^2/2 - u^3/3 - \cdots$ with $u=t/\sqrt{\alpha}$: $$= -\alpha\left(-\frac{t}{\sqrt{\alpha}} - \frac{t^2}{2\alpha} - \frac{t^3}{3\alpha^{3/2}}-\cdots\right) = \sqrt{\alpha}\,t + \frac{t^2}{2} + \frac{t^3}{3\sqrt{\alpha}}+\cdots$$ Therefore: $$\log M_Y(t) = -\sqrt{\alpha}\,t + \sqrt{\alpha}\,t + \frac{t^2}{2} + O(\alpha^{-1/2}) = \frac{t^2}{2} + O(\alpha^{-1/2})$$ $$\therefore\lim_{\alpha\to\infty}M_Y(t) = e^{t^2/2} = \text{MGF of }N(0,1) \qquad\square$$

CLT is not just a Bernoulli/Poisson phenomenon — it holds for every distribution with finite mean and variance.

Lindberg-Lévy CLT: Complete Proof

Lindberg-Lévy CLT: Let $X_1,X_2,\ldots$ be iid with $E(X_i)=m$ and $\text{Var}(X_i)=s^2<\infty$. Then: $$Z_n = \frac{S_n - nm}{s\sqrt{n}}\xrightarrow{d}N(0,1)$$
Write $Z_n = \sum_{i=1}^n\frac{X_i-m}{s\sqrt{n}}$ — a sum of $n$ iid random variables, each with mean 0 and variance $1/n$.

Let $W = (X-m)/s$ (mean 0, variance 1). Then $Z_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i=1}^n W_i$, so the MGF of each term is $M_W(t/\sqrt{n})$.

Expand $M_W(t/\sqrt{n})$ using moments $\mu_1'=E(W)=0$, $\mu_2'=E(W^2)=1$: $$M_W\!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right) = 1 + \underbrace{\mu_1'\cdot\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}}_{=0} + \mu_2'\cdot\frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2}) = 1+\frac{t^2}{2n}+O(n^{-3/2})$$ Since $Z_n$ is a sum of $n$ independent terms: $M_{Z_n}(t) = \left[M_W(t/\sqrt{n})\right]^n$. $$\log M_{Z_n}(t) = n\log\!\left(1+\frac{t^2}{2n}+O(n^{-3/2})\right) = n\cdot\left(\frac{t^2}{2n}+O(n^{-3/2})\right)\to\frac{t^2}{2}$$ using $\log(1+u)\approx u$ for small $u$. Therefore $M_{Z_n}(t)\to e^{t^2/2}$ = MGF of $N(0,1)$. $\square$
Why the first-order term vanishes: Centering at the mean (subtracting $m$) makes $\mu_1'=0$, killing the $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ term. The $O(1/n)$ term multiplied by $n$ gives the surviving $t^2/2$. Higher terms die faster. This structure is universal — it works for any distribution with finite variance.

Historical note: De Moivre stated this for Bernoulli in 1733. Laplace generalised it in the early 19th century. Liapounov gave the first rigorous proof in 1901. Lindberg (1922) proved the most general version (independent but not necessarily iid).

WLLN vs CLT: What Each Theorem Says

Both study $\bar{X}_n = S_n/n$, but they ask different questions:

WLLN: $\bar{X}_n\xrightarrow{P}\mu$. Convergence in probability to a single value. Tells you where the average lands.

CLT: $P(S_n\leq x)\to F(x)$ where $F$ is Normal. Convergence in distribution. Tells you the shape of uncertainty around that landing point.

Together: $\bar{X}_n\approx N(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$ for large $n$. The mean is $\mu$ (LLN); the spread is $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ (CLT).

Identifying Distributions from CLT

$X_i$ iid with $E(X_i)=0$, $\text{Var}(X_i)=1$, and $S_n/\sqrt{n}$ has the same distribution for all $n$. What is the distribution of each $X_i$?

By CLT: $S_n/\sqrt{n}\xrightarrow{d}N(0,1)$. If $S_n/\sqrt{n}$ has the same distribution for all $n$, then it must equal $N(0,1)$ for all $n$, including $n=1$: $X_1\sim N(0,1)$. So all $X_i\sim N(0,1)$.

This is the characterisation of the Normal: the only distribution stable under normalised sums of iid copies is the Normal itself.

Three Worked Problems

Problem 1 — Fair Die: $P(\text{six appears} \geq 147\text{ in }1000\text{ tosses})$

$X\sim\text{Bin}(1000, 1/6)$. $E(X)=1000/6=500/3\approx166.7$, $\text{Var}(X)=1000\cdot\frac{1}{6}\cdot\frac{5}{6}=\frac{2500}{18}\approx138.9$.

By CLT: $Z = (X-500/3)/\sqrt{2500/18}\sim N(0,1)$ approximately.

$$z = \frac{147-500/3}{\sqrt{2500/18}} = \frac{147\times3-500}{3\times\sqrt{2500/18}} = \frac{441-500}{\sqrt{250\times5}} = \frac{-59}{\sqrt{1250}}\approx\frac{-59}{35.36}\approx-1.67$$

$P(X\geq 147) = P(Z\geq-1.67)\approx1-0.05 = \mathbf{95\%}$.

Problem 2 — Teacher Grading: $P(S_{100}\geq 110\text{ hours})$

$X_i$ = time per script: $E(X_i)=1$, $\text{Var}(X_i)=0.26$. $S_{100}=\sum_{i=1}^{100}X_i$.

$E(S_{100})=100$, $\text{Var}(S_{100})=100\times0.26=26$, $\text{SD}=\sqrt{26}\approx5.1$.

$$z = \frac{110-100}{\sqrt{26}} = \frac{10}{5.1}\approx 1.96$$

By CLT: $P(S_{100}\geq110) = P(Z\geq1.96)\approx \mathbf{0.025}$ (2.5%).

Problem 3 — Poisson Sums: $P\!\left(150\leq\sum_{i=1}^{75}X_i\leq170\right)$ where $X_i$ iid Poi$(2)$

$\sum_{i=1}^{75}X_i\sim\text{Poi}(150)$. $E=\text{Var}=150$, $\text{SD}=\sqrt{150}\approx12.25$.

$$z_1 = \frac{150-150}{\sqrt{150}}=0, \qquad z_2 = \frac{170-150}{\sqrt{150}} = \frac{20}{\sqrt{150}}\approx1.63$$

$P(150\leq X\leq170) = P(0\leq Z\leq1.63) = \Phi(1.63)-\Phi(0)\approx0.95-0.50 = \mathbf{0.45}$.

This completes the NPTEL Advanced Probability Theory sequence. The arc of 30 lectures — from sigma-fields through Borel-Cantelli to order statistics, modes of convergence, LLN, and CLT — is exactly the mathematical foundation of modern quantitative finance. Every option pricing model, every strategy backtest, every risk metric rests on this structure. The CLT in particular explains why Normal approximations pervade derivatives pricing despite returns being non-Normal: as time steps shrink and trade counts grow, the Central Limit Theorem pushes the aggregate distribution toward Gaussian.

In the Lindberg-Lévy proof, why does the first-order term $\mu_1' t/\sqrt{n}$ in the MGF expansion vanish?

A
Because $t/\sqrt{n}\to 0$ as $n\to\infty$
B
Because $\mu_1' = E(X_i-m) = 0$ — we centred by subtracting the mean
C
Because the $X_i$ are identically distributed
D
Because MGFs don't have odd-order terms
Answer: B

We work with $(X_i-m)/s$ which has mean zero. $\mu_1'=E(W)=0$ kills the linear term. This is why centering is essential — without subtracting the mean, the $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ term would survive and the MGF would not converge to $e^{t^2/2}$. The second-order term $\mu_2'\cdot t^2/(2n)=t^2/(2n)$ then multiplied by $n$ gives the surviving $t^2/2$.

$X_i$ iid Exp$(1)$ ($\mu=1$, $\sigma^2=1$). For large $n$, what is $P(\bar{X}_{100}\leq 1.1)$ approximately?

A
$\Phi(0.1)$ — use CLT directly without scaling
B
$\Phi(1)$ — since $z=(1.1-1)/(1/\sqrt{100})=0.1\times10=1$
C
Cannot apply CLT — Exponential is not Normal
D
$\Phi(10)$ — standardise by dividing by $\sigma^2=1$ not $\sigma=1$
Answer: B — $\Phi(1)\approx 0.841$

$\bar{X}_{100}\approx N(1, 1/100)$ by CLT. $z = (1.1-1)/(1/\sqrt{100}) = 0.1/(0.1) = 1$. So $P(\bar{X}_{100}\leq 1.1)\approx\Phi(1)\approx 0.841$. CLT applies to any distribution with finite mean and variance — Exponential qualifies perfectly. The original distribution being non-Normal is irrelevant; CLT guarantees the average is approximately Normal.
probability / ch30 / q03★★ Medium

What is the key mathematical difference between what WLLN and CLT prove about $\bar{X}_n$?

A
WLLN requires iid; CLT does not
B
CLT requires finite variance; WLLN does not
C
WLLN gives convergence in probability to a constant; CLT gives convergence in distribution to $N(0,1)$ after rescaling
D
They prove the same thing using different techniques
Answer: C

WLLN: $\bar{X}_n\xrightarrow{P}\mu$ (one point). CLT: $(\bar{X}_n-\mu)/(\sigma/\sqrt{n})\xrightarrow{d}N(0,1)$ (a distribution). WLLN tells you where; CLT tells you how spread out. The CLT cannot be stated without the $\sqrt{n}$ rescaling — without it, the standardised sum collapses to the point $\mu$ by WLLN.
