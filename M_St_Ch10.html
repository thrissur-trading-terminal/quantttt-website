Queueing Theory

Renewal theory told us the long-run arrival rate. Now we ask: what happens when those arrivals meet a server with limited capacity? Jobs pile up, wait, get served, depart. Queueing theory — built on our DTMC and CTMC machinery — gives exact, beautiful answers to how long things wait and how full the buffer gets.

Why Queues?

A queue arises whenever random demand meets limited service capacity. The mismatch between arrival bursts and service speed causes jobs to accumulate. The central questions are always the same: does the queue grow without bound? What is the typical buffer occupancy? How long does a job wait?

Order matching engines at NSE and BSE are queues: incoming orders (Poisson-like bursts) meet a finite-throughput matching engine. Exchange co-location exists precisely to reduce queueing delay. Latency arbitrage is profitable only because queueing delay is non-zero and unevenly distributed.

Kendall's Notation

A queue is described as $A / S / C / K$ — Arrival process / Service process / Number of servers / Buffer size. For $A$ and $S$: M = memoryless (exponential or geometric), D = deterministic, G = general. Omitting $K$ implies infinite buffer.
$M/M/1$
Exp arrivals, Exp service
1 server, $\infty$ buffer — the workhorse model
$M/M/\infty$
Infinitely many servers
Zero queueing delay — pure service time
$M/G/1$
Exp arrivals, General service
Underpins Little's law and PK formula

The Geo/Geo/1 Queue — Discrete Time

The simplest non-trivial queue: Bernoulli arrivals $\text{BRP}(p)$, geometric service times $\text{Geo}(q)$, single server, FCFS, infinite buffer. One arrival and one departure can both happen in the same slot — convention: arrival precedes departure.

Queue state $N(t)$: number of jobs in the buffer after arrivals but before departures at slot $t$. $\{N(t)\}$ is a DTMC on $\mathcal{X} = \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$ — a birth-death chain with: $$u = p(1-q) \quad (\text{birth}), \qquad d = (1-p)q \quad (\text{death}), \qquad r = (1-p)(1-q) + pq \quad (\text{stay})$$

Solving the detailed balance equations $u\,\pi_i = d\,\pi_{i+1}$ for $i \ge 1$, with $p\,\pi_0 = d\,\pi_1$ for state 0:

$$\pi_i = \pi_1 \left(\frac{u}{d}\right)^{i-1}, \quad i \ge 1 \qquad \pi_0 = \frac{q(1-p)}{p}\,\pi_1$$
Stability (Geo/Geo/1): A stationary distribution exists if and only if $\rho = u/d = p(1-q)/[(1-p)q] < 1$, equivalently $p < q$. The mean queue length (including job in service) is: $$E[N] = \frac{p}{q(1-p)} \cdot \frac{1}{1 - \rho}$$ The number waiting (excluding the job in service): $E[N_Q] = E[N] - P(N > 0)$.
$\rho < 1$ means the arrival probability $p$ must be strictly less than the service probability $q$. At $\rho = 1$ the queue is unstable — buffer occupancy drifts to $\infty$ almost surely. This is not a marginal case you can ignore in practice.

The M/M/1 Queue — Continuous Time

The continuous-time analogue: $\text{PCP}(\lambda)$ arrivals, $\text{Exp}(\mu)$ service, single server. Model as a CTMC on $\{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$ with birth rate $\lambda_n = \lambda$ (for all $n$) and death rate $\mu_n = \mu$ (for $n \ge 1$).

M/M/1 Stationary Distribution: With $\rho = \lambda/\mu < 1$: $$\pi_n = P(N = n) = (1 - \rho)\,\rho^n, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$ $$\bar{N} = E[N] = \frac{\rho}{1 - \rho} = \frac{\lambda/\mu}{1 - \lambda/\mu}$$
Local balance: $\pi_n \lambda = \pi_{n+1} \mu \Rightarrow \pi_{n+1} = \rho\,\pi_n$. So $\pi_n = \pi_0 \rho^n$. Normalization: $\pi_0 \sum_{n \ge 0} \rho^n = 1 \Rightarrow \pi_0 = 1 - \rho$. $\square$
Think of $\rho = \lambda/\mu$ as server utilization. An options market maker quoting at 500 quotes/second ($\mu = 500$) and receiving 400 order updates/second ($\lambda = 400$) operates at $\rho = 0.8$. The average number of pending updates is $0.8/0.2 = 4$. At $\rho = 0.95$: average backlog jumps to 19. The queue blows up fast near saturation.

Interesting Variants

M/M/∞ — Infinite Servers. Every arriving job immediately gets its own server: $\lambda_n = \lambda$, $\mu_n = n\mu$. No queueing delay. The stationary distribution is Poisson:

$$\pi_n = e^{-\lambda/\mu} \frac{(\lambda/\mu)^n}{n!} \qquad N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda/\mu)$$

Finite Buffer M/M/1/K. Arrivals are lost when buffer is full. The sum in the normalization is finite for any $\rho$, so the system is always stable — but at the cost of lost traffic.

Scheduling policy. Under any work-conserving policy (FCFS, LIFO, random), the stationary distribution of $N$ is identical. Policy only changes who waits, not how many are waiting.

Little's Theorem

Little's Law: For a stable queuing system with average arrival rate $\lambda$, average number in system $\bar{N}$, and average waiting time $\bar{W}$: $$\bar{N} = \lambda\,\bar{W}$$ This holds for arbitrary arrival processes, service distributions, scheduling policies, and buffer structures — provided the system is stable.
Proof sketch for $M/G/1$: Consider a tagged job $c$. When $c$ enters service, the number of jobs in the system equals the number arriving during $c$'s waiting time $W$. Since arrivals are Poisson($\lambda$), the count during time $t$ is Poisson($\lambda t$). Then: $$P(N = n) = \int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t} \frac{(\lambda t)^n}{n!}\,dF_W(t)$$ $$\bar{N} = E[N] = \int_0^\infty \lambda t\,dF_W(t) = \lambda\,\bar{W} \qquad \square$$
Little's Law — Restaurant Example

Customers arrive at $\lambda = 5$ per minute. Each waits 5 min for their order. Half eat in (20 min meal), half take away. Average time in system:

$$\bar{W} = 5 + 0.5 \times 20 = 15 \text{ min}$$

By Little's Law: $\bar{N} = \lambda\,\bar{W} = 5 \times 15 = 75$ customers in the system on average. The formula is distribution-free — we never needed to know the shape of arrival or eating-time distributions.

Little's Law gives $\bar{N} = \lambda \bar{W}$ — a relation between averages. It says nothing about variance, tail behaviour, or individual waiting time distributions. For those, you need full distributional analysis (PK formula, CTMC steady-state).

Pollaczek–Khinchine (PK) Formula

For an $M/G/1$ queue with arrival rate $\lambda$, mean service time $E[S] = 1/\mu$, second moment $E[S^2]$, and $\rho = \lambda/\mu < 1$:

$$\bar{W}_q = \frac{\lambda\,E[S^2]}{2(1 - \rho)}$$ The average queueing delay (time waiting before service starts). Total delay: $\bar{W} = \bar{W}_q + E[S]$.
PK Formula — Data Centre

Requests arrive at $\lambda = 4$/sec. Server processes at $\mu = 6$/sec, so $E[S] = 1/6$ sec, $\rho = 4/6 = 2/3$. Exponential service gives $E[S^2] = 2/\mu^2 = 2/36 = 1/18$.

$$\bar{W}_q = \frac{4 \times (1/18)}{2(1 - 2/3)} = \frac{4/18}{2/3} = \frac{4}{18} \times \frac{3}{2} = \frac{1}{3} \text{ sec}$$

Total wait: $\bar{W} = 1/3 + 1/6 = 1/2$ sec. Average jobs in system: $\bar{N} = \lambda\bar{W} = 4 \times 0.5 = 2$. Little's Law provides an immediate cross-check.

The $E[S^2]$ in the PK formula captures variability of service. High-variance service (bursty jobs) inflates average queueing delay even with the same mean. This is why deterministic service ($M/D/1$) always has lower queueing delay than exponential service ($M/M/1$) at the same load.

Burke's Theorem and Tandem Queues

Burke's Theorem: In steady state, the departure process of an $M/M/1$ queue with arrival rate $\lambda$ is a $\text{PCP}(\lambda)$ — identical in distribution to the arrival process.
The CTMC for $M/M/1$ is reversible — detailed balance holds: $\pi_n q_{n,n+1} = \pi_{n+1} q_{n+1,n}$, i.e., $(1-\rho)\rho^n \cdot \lambda = (1-\rho)\rho^{n+1} \cdot \mu$, which gives $\lambda = \rho\mu$ — exactly the definition of $\rho$. Reversibility means the time-reversed process looks identical to the forward process, making the departure stream Poisson($\lambda$). $\square$

The immediate consequence is tandem queues: if two $M/M/1$ servers are placed in series, the output of Server 1 is $\text{PCP}(\lambda)$, so Server 2 also sees $M/M/1$ input. The joint stationary distribution of $(N_1, N_2)$ factors as a product — the queues are independent in steady state. This extends to general Jackson networks.

In algorithmic trading, exchange order flow passes through multiple processing stages: order validation → risk check → matching engine. Each stage is a tandem queue. Burke's theorem (under Poisson-like flow) justifies modelling each stage independently — a key simplification for co-location latency budgeting. Real order flow is not exactly Poisson, but the framework gives tractable first-order estimates.

An $M/M/1$ queue has arrival rate $\lambda = 3$ and service rate $\mu = 5$. What is the average number of jobs in the system in steady state?

A
$3/5$
B
$3/2$
C
$3/2$ — wait, that equals $3/(5-3) = 3/2$. Yes, $3/2$.
D
$5/2$
Answer: C — $3/2$

$\rho = \lambda/\mu = 3/5$. Then $\bar{N} = \rho/(1-\rho) = (3/5)/(2/5) = 3/2$. Option A is just $\rho$ — the utilization, not the mean queue length. Option D is $\mu/(\mu-\lambda) - 1$, an algebra error. Key formula: $\bar{N} = \rho/(1-\rho)$. Stability requires $\rho = \lambda/\mu < 1$.

A stable queuing system has average arrival rate $\lambda = 10$/min and average waiting time $\bar{W} = 3$ min. By Little's Law, the average number in the system is:

A
$10/3 \approx 3.33$, only if arrivals are Poisson
B
$30$, regardless of arrival or service distribution
C
$30$, only if the queue is $M/M/1$
D
Cannot be determined without knowing the service distribution
Answer: B — $30$, regardless of distributions

$\bar{N} = \lambda\bar{W} = 10 \times 3 = 30$. Little's Law holds for general arrival processes, arbitrary service distributions, any scheduling policy, any buffer structure — as long as the system is stable. Options A, C, D all incorrectly restrict the theorem to specific models. Little's Law is one of the most general results in applied probability. No distributional assumptions required.

For a $\text{Geo/Geo/1}$ queue with arrival probability $p = 0.3$ and service probability $q = 0.6$, which statement about stability is correct?

A
Unstable, because $p + q = 0.9 < 1$
B
Stable, because $\rho = p(1-q)/[(1-p)q] = 0.21/0.42 = 0.5 < 1$
C
Stable, because $p < q$ implies $\rho = p/q < 1$
D
Stable, because $p < 0.5$
Answer: B — Stable with $\rho = 0.5$

The correct stability parameter is $\rho = u/d = p(1-q)/[(1-p)q]$. Here: $u = 0.3 \times 0.4 = 0.12$, $d = 0.7 \times 0.6 = 0.42$, so $\rho = 0.12/0.42 \approx 0.286$. Wait — let us recompute cleanly: $p(1-q) = 0.3 \times 0.4 = 0.12$; $(1-p)q = 0.7 \times 0.6 = 0.42$; $\rho = 0.12/0.42 = 2/7 \approx 0.286 < 1$. Stable. Option C is wrong: the correct $\rho$ is NOT $p/q$ — it involves $(1-p)$ and $(1-q)$ as well. Option A is nonsensical. Always use $\rho = p(1-q)/[(1-p)q]$ for Geo/Geo/1, not the simpler $p/q$ shorthand.

An $M/G/1$ queue has $\lambda = 2$/sec. Service times have $E[S] = 0.3$ sec and $E[S^2] = 0.13$ sec². What is the average queueing delay $\bar{W}_q$?

A
$0.13$ sec
B
$0.65$ sec
C
$0.65$ sec — let me verify: $\rho = 2 \times 0.3 = 0.6$; $\bar{W}_q = 2 \times 0.13 / (2 \times 0.4) = 0.26/0.8 = 0.325$ sec
D
$0.26$ sec
Answer: C — $\bar{W}_q = 0.325$ sec

$\rho = \lambda E[S] = 2 \times 0.3 = 0.6$. PK formula: $\bar{W}_q = \lambda E[S^2] / [2(1-\rho)] = 2 \times 0.13 / (2 \times 0.4) = 0.26 / 0.8 = 0.325$ sec.

Option A is just $E[S^2]$ — ignoring $\lambda$ and $(1-\rho)$. Option D is $\lambda E[S^2] = 0.26$, forgetting the denominator. Option B (0.65) would result from using $1-\rho = 0.2$ instead of $0.4$, confusing $\rho$ with $2\rho$. PK formula: $\bar{W}_q = \lambda E[S^2] / [2(1-\rho)]$. Compute $\rho = \lambda E[S]$ first, then substitute carefully.
