Option Pricing & Black-Scholes

Everything in this module was building to this. Brownian motion gives us the noise. Itô's lemma tells us how smooth functions of BM behave. Risk-neutral pricing removes the drift. The result is the Black-Scholes formula — the most celebrated equation in quantitative finance, and the reason volatility is the only thing that matters for option prices.

Geometric Brownian Motion

A stock price must stay positive. Ordinary BM can go negative, so we model the log-price as BM with drift. The resulting model for the price level $S(t)$ is Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM):

The stock price $S(t)$ follows GBM if: $$dS(t) = \mu\,S(t)\,dt + \sigma\,S(t)\,dW(t)$$ where $W(t)$ is standard Brownian motion, $\mu$ is the drift (expected return), and $\sigma > 0$ is the volatility. The solution is: $$S(t) = S(0)\,\exp\!\left[\left(\mu - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2\right)t + \sigma W(t)\right]$$

The $-\tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ correction in the exponent is not a typo — it is the Itô correction, which appears because $dW^2 = dt$ (the quadratic variation of BM). Without it, we would get the wrong mean.

GBM says percentage returns $dS/S$ are normally distributed with mean $\mu\,dt$ and variance $\sigma^2 dt$. This is equivalent to saying $\ln S(t) \sim N\!\left(\ln S(0)+(\mu-\sigma^2/2)t,\;\sigma^2 t\right)$ — log-prices are Gaussian, so prices are log-normal. The annualised vol $\sigma$ you compute from NIFTY daily returns is exactly this $\sigma$.
The mean of $S(t)$ is $S(0)e^{\mu t}$ (grows at rate $\mu$), but the median is $S(0)e^{(\mu-\sigma^2/2)t}$ (grows slower). The gap is the Itô correction. As $\sigma$ grows, the distribution becomes more right-skewed — high-vol assets have large upside outliers but the typical path underperforms the mean.

Itô's Lemma

If $S(t)$ follows an Itô process $dS = a\,dt + b\,dW$, and $f(S,t)$ is a smooth function, the classical chain rule fails because $(dW)^2 = dt \ne 0$. The correct rule is:

Itô's Lemma: For $f = f(S, t)$ with $S$ an Itô process $dS = a\,dt + b\,dW$: $$df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}\,dt + \frac{\partial f}{\partial S}\,dS + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial S^2}(dS)^2$$ Using $(dW)^2 = dt$, $(dt)^2 = 0$, $dW\,dt = 0$: $$df = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + a\frac{\partial f}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}b^2\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial S^2}\right)dt + b\frac{\partial f}{\partial S}\,dW$$
Deriving the GBM Solution via Itô's Lemma

Apply Itô's Lemma to $f(S,t) = \ln S$ with $dS = \mu S\,dt + \sigma S\,dW$. Here $a = \mu S$, $b = \sigma S$.

$$d(\ln S) = \frac{1}{S}\cdot\mu S\,dt + \frac{1}{S}\cdot\sigma S\,dW + \frac{1}{2}\left(-\frac{1}{S^2}\right)\sigma^2 S^2\,dt$$ $$= \left(\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\right)dt + \sigma\,dW$$

Integrating from $0$ to $t$: $\ln S(t) - \ln S(0) = (\mu - \sigma^2/2)t + \sigma W(t)$, giving $S(t) = S(0)\exp[(\mu-\sigma^2/2)t + \sigma W(t)]$. The $-\sigma^2/2$ term is Itô's correction — the extra curvature term from $\partial^2 f/\partial S^2 \ne 0$.

Itô's Lemma is the engine of derivatives pricing. Every time a quant writes down a PDE for an option — Heston, SABR, local vol, whatever the model — they are applying Itô's Lemma to $f(S,t)$ and collecting $dt$ and $dW$ terms. The Bates/Heston calibration used in TTT's short-vol red alert system is built on exactly this framework.

The Black-Scholes PDE

Consider a European call with price $C(S,t)$. To eliminate the random $dW$ term, construct a delta-hedged portfolio: long one call, short $\partial C/\partial S$ shares. The portfolio $\Pi = C - (\partial C/\partial S)\,S$ is instantaneously riskless, so by no-arbitrage it must earn the risk-free rate $r$:

Black-Scholes PDE: $$\frac{\partial C}{\partial t} + rS\frac{\partial C}{\partial S} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \frac{\partial^2 C}{\partial S^2} - rC = 0$$ with terminal condition $C(S,T) = \max(S-K, 0)$ for a call with strike $K$ and maturity $T$.
By Itô's Lemma, $dC = (\partial_t C + \mu S\partial_S C + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2 \partial_{SS}C)\,dt + \sigma S\partial_S C\,dW$. The delta-hedged portfolio has $d\Pi = dC - \partial_S C\,dS = (\partial_t C + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 S^2\partial_{SS}C)\,dt$ — the $\mu$ and $dW$ terms cancel. No-arbitrage forces $d\Pi = r\Pi\,dt = r(C - S\partial_S C)\,dt$. Equating gives the BS PDE. $\square$
The miracle: $\mu$ (the stock's real-world drift) disappears from the PDE. The option price does not depend on the investor's expectation of future returns — only on $\sigma$, $r$, $S$, $K$, $T$. This is risk-neutral pricing in action.

The Black-Scholes Formula

Solving the BS PDE with the call payoff boundary condition gives the closed-form Black-Scholes formula. Alternatively, under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$ (where $S$ drifts at $r$ instead of $\mu$), the call price is the discounted expected payoff:

Black-Scholes Call Price: $$C = S\,\Phi(d_1) - K e^{-rT}\Phi(d_2)$$ $$d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r + \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}, \qquad d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{T}$$ where $\Phi(\cdot)$ is the standard normal CDF. The put price follows from put-call parity: $$P = K e^{-rT}\Phi(-d_2) - S\,\Phi(-d_1)$$

Delta of the call
Shares to hold in the delta-hedge; also $\approx P(\text{call expires ITM})$ under $\mathbb{Q}$
$\Phi(d_2)$
Risk-neutral ITM prob
$P^\mathbb{Q}(S_T > K)$ — probability call finishes in the money under the risk-neutral measure
$Ke^{-rT}\Phi(d_2)$
PV of strike payment
Discounted expected strike, weighted by ITM probability
Black-Scholes — NIFTY ATM Call Example

NIFTY spot $S = 24{,}000$, strike $K = 24{,}000$ (ATM), $T = 7/365$ (weekly expiry), $\sigma = 0.14$ (14% annualised IV), $r = 0.065$ (6.5% risk-free).

$$d_1 = \frac{\ln(1) + (0.065 + 0.0098)\times 7/365}{0.14\times\sqrt{7/365}} = \frac{0 + 0.001462}{0.02768} \approx 0.0528$$ $$d_2 = 0.0528 - 0.02768 \approx 0.0251$$ $$\Phi(d_1) \approx 0.521, \quad \Phi(d_2) \approx 0.510$$ $$C \approx 24000\times 0.521 - 24000\times e^{-0.065\times7/365}\times 0.510$$ $$C \approx 12504 - 12222 \approx 282 \text{ points}$$

A rough sanity check: ATM weekly options on NIFTY at 14% IV typically trade near $0.4\%\times S \approx 96$ — our simplified flat-vol BS gives a ballpark figure. In practice IV skew, discrete dividends, and early exercise adjustments matter.

Put-Call Parity

Put-Call Parity (model-free, no-arbitrage): $$C - P = S - Ke^{-rT}$$ Holds for European options on non-dividend paying stocks for any continuous model — not just Black-Scholes.
Two portfolios: (A) long call + $Ke^{-rT}$ cash; (B) long put + long stock. Both pay $\max(S_T, K)$ at maturity $T$. No-arbitrage: same payoff $\Rightarrow$ same price today. Rearranging: $C - P = S - Ke^{-rT}$. $\square$
Put-call parity is what TTT's short-vol straddle/strangle strategies implicitly rely on. A short straddle (short ATM call + short ATM put) is economically equivalent to being short a synthetic forward. When IV is elevated and realised vol is lower, the decay (theta) on both legs profits. The parity relation ensures the Greeks of a put are recoverable directly from the call — no separate computation needed.

The Greeks

The Greeks measure how the option price $C$ responds to changes in model inputs. They are the quant's risk dashboard.

Δ
Delta
$\Delta = \partial C/\partial S = \Phi(d_1)$
Shares of stock per option for a delta-neutral hedge. Ranges 0–1 for calls, $-1$–0 for puts.
Γ
Gamma
$\Gamma = \partial^2 C/\partial S^2 = \phi(d_1)/({S\sigma\sqrt{T}})$
Rate of change of delta. Highest for ATM options near expiry. Long gamma profits from large moves.
Θ
Theta
$\Theta = \partial C/\partial t < 0$
Time decay — calls and puts lose value as expiry approaches (for long positions). Short-vol strategies profit from theta.
𝒱
Vega
$\mathcal{V} = \partial C/\partial\sigma = S\phi(d_1)\sqrt{T}$
Sensitivity to implied vol. Highest ATM. All options are long vega — sellers of vol are short vega.
ρ
Rho
$\rho = \partial C/\partial r = KTe^{-rT}\Phi(d_2)$
Sensitivity to interest rate. Minor for short-dated options; matters for long-dated structures.
Λ
Vanna / Volga
$\Lambda = \partial\Delta/\partial\sigma = \partial\mathcal{V}/\partial S$
Second-order cross Greeks. Used in vol surface arbitrage and exotic pricing — beyond flat BS.
BS Greeks assume $\sigma$ is constant. In reality, implied vol is a surface — different for each strike and maturity. Delta computed at flat $\sigma$ will misprice if the skew shifts. Sticky-strike vs sticky-delta conventions change how delta hedges are constructed in practice. The BS formula is a mapping from $(S,K,T,r)$ to $\sigma_{\text{imp}}$ — not a literal description of reality.

Risk-Neutral Pricing and the Fundamental Theorem

The deepest insight of BS is that option pricing is equivalent to a change of probability measure. Under the physical measure $\mathbb{P}$, $S$ drifts at $\mu$. Under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, $S$ drifts at $r$. The price of any derivative is its discounted expectation under $\mathbb{Q}$:

Fundamental Theorem of Asset Pricing: A market is arbitrage-free if and only if there exists a risk-neutral (equivalent martingale) measure $\mathbb{Q}$ such that discounted asset prices $e^{-rt}S(t)$ are $\mathbb{Q}$-martingales. The price of any attainable claim $H$ is: $$V(t) = e^{-r(T-t)}\,E^{\mathbb{Q}}[H \mid \mathcal{F}_t]$$

The measure change from $\mathbb{P}$ to $\mathbb{Q}$ is the Girsanov theorem: replace $\mu$ with $r$ by subtracting the market price of risk $\theta = (\mu - r)/\sigma$ from the drift. Under $\mathbb{Q}$, $\widetilde{W}(t) = W(t) + \theta t$ is standard BM, and the $dS$ equation becomes $dS = rS\,dt + \sigma S\,d\widetilde{W}$.

Risk-neutral pricing is why an option market maker doesn't need a view on NIFTY direction. They delta-hedge (zeroing out the $dW$ exposure), collect theta, and are left with a pure vol bet: long realised vol (gamma) vs. short implied vol (theta). The BS PDE is the mathematical statement that this hedge works perfectly in continuous time.

Under GBM with drift $\mu$ and volatility $\sigma$, the log-return $\ln(S(T)/S(0))$ is distributed as:

A
$N(\mu T,\;\sigma^2 T)$
B
$N\!\left((\mu - \sigma^2/2)T,\;\sigma^2 T\right)$
C
$N(\mu T,\;\sigma T)$
D
$N(0,\;\sigma^2 T)$ regardless of $\mu$
Answer: B — $N((\mu-\sigma^2/2)T,\,\sigma^2 T)$

Itô's Lemma applied to $\ln S$ gives $d(\ln S) = (\mu - \sigma^2/2)dt + \sigma\,dW$. Integrating: $\ln(S_T/S_0) \sim N((\mu-\sigma^2/2)T, \sigma^2 T)$. Option A omits the Itô correction $-\sigma^2/2$. Option C uses wrong variance (missing the square). Option D drops $\mu$. The $-\sigma^2/2$ Itô correction is the single most common error in quant interviews. Never forget it.
stochastic / ch12 / q02★ Easy

Put-call parity for European options states $C - P = S - Ke^{-rT}$. Which assumption is required?

A
GBM must hold for the underlying
B
Volatility must be constant
C
No-arbitrage and European-style exercise only
D
The risk-free rate must be constant
Answer: C — no-arbitrage and European-style exercise

Put-call parity follows from a pure replication argument (portfolio A = portfolio B at maturity ⟹ same price today). It requires no model for $S$ — no GBM, no constant vol, no constant $r$. It breaks for American-style options because early exercise creates asymmetry between the call and put replication portfolios. Put-call parity is model-free. It is one of the most robust results in all of derivatives theory.
stochastic / ch12 / q03★★ Medium

In the Black-Scholes model, the drift $\mu$ of the stock does not appear in the option price formula. The correct reason is:

A
$\mu$ cancels algebraically when $d_1$ and $d_2$ are computed
B
Options are insensitive to the stock direction by definition
C
Delta-hedging eliminates the $dW$ term, making the hedged portfolio riskless; no-arbitrage then forces the drift out of the PDE
D
$\mu$ is assumed to equal $r$ in the Black-Scholes model
Answer: C

The delta hedge $\Pi = C - \Delta S$ eliminates $dW$ (the stochastic term). The residual $d\Pi$ is purely $dt$ — deterministic — so no-arbitrage forces it to earn $r$. This produces the BS PDE, which contains $r$ but not $\mu$. Option A is wrong: $\mu$ never appeared in $d_1/d_2$ — those are derived under $\mathbb{Q}$ where $\mu$ is replaced by $r$. Option D is the risk-neutral measure consequence, not the cause. Delta hedging + no-arbitrage = $\mu$ disappears. This is the deepest conceptual insight of Black-Scholes.
stochastic / ch12 / q04★★ Medium

A short straddle on NIFTY (short ATM call + short ATM put) has a net position in which Greeks?

A
Long delta, short gamma, short vega, positive theta
B
Near-zero delta, short gamma, short vega, positive theta
C
Near-zero delta, long gamma, long vega, negative theta
D
Short delta, short gamma, long vega, positive theta
Answer: B — near-zero delta, short gamma, short vega, positive theta

Short ATM call: $\Delta \approx -0.5$, $\Gamma < 0$, $\mathcal{V} < 0$, $\Theta > 0$. Short ATM put: $\Delta \approx +0.5$ (put delta $\approx -0.5$, short reverses sign), same signs for $\Gamma$, $\mathcal{V}$, $\Theta$. Combined: delta $\approx 0$ (cancels), $\Gamma$ and $\mathcal{V}$ double-up negative, $\Theta$ double-up positive. Short straddles profit when realised vol $<$ implied vol. Short straddle = short gamma + short vega + long theta. It loses when the market moves far (gamma risk) or when IV spikes (vega risk).
stochastic / ch12 / q05★★★ Hard

Apply Itô's Lemma to $f(W) = W^2(t)$ where $W(t)$ is standard BM. The result $dW^2$ equals:

A
$2W\,dW$
B
$2W\,dW + 2W^2\,dt$
C
$2W\,dW + dt$
D
$W^2\,dt + 2W\,dW$
Answer: C — $d(W^2) = 2W\,dW + dt$

Itô's Lemma with $f(W,t) = W^2$: $\partial f/\partial t = 0$, $\partial f/\partial W = 2W$, $\partial^2 f/\partial W^2 = 2$. Then: $$d(W^2) = 0\cdot dt + 2W\,dW + \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 2\cdot (dW)^2 = 2W\,dW + dt$$ using $(dW)^2 = dt$. Integrating: $W^2(T) = 2\int_0^T W\,dW + T$, so $\int_0^T W\,dW = (W^2(T) - T)/2$. Option A omits the Itô correction $dt$. Options B and D get the correction term wrong. Itô's Lemma always adds $\frac{1}{2}f''(dW)^2 = \frac{1}{2}f''\,dt$. This is never zero for non-linear $f$.
