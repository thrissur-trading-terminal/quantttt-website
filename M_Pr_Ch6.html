Bayes' Theorem

Something has happened. You want to find the most likely cause. Bayes' theorem is the mathematical machinery for reasoning backwards from effects to causes — the engine of inference, signal detection, and modern machine learning.

A Quick Note on Mutual Independence

Before Bayes, one important clarification from the previous chapter. Two events being independent is clear enough. But for $n$ events, independence is stricter than it looks.

Events $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ are mutually independent if every subset of them satisfies the product rule: $$P(A_i \cap A_j) = P(A_i) \cdot P(A_j) \quad \forall\, i \neq j$$ $$P(A_i \cap A_j \cap A_k) = P(A_i) \cdot P(A_j) \cdot P(A_k) \quad \forall\, i < j < k$$ $$\vdots$$ $$P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(A_i)$$ All $2^n - n - 1$ conditions must hold simultaneously.
Pairwise independence does not imply mutual independence. You can construct three events where every pair is independent but the triple is not. Always check all conditions when $n \geq 3$.
📦 n Boxes Example — Independence in Action

$n$ boxes, each containing 10 balls numbered 1–10. One ball drawn at random from each box. Let $A_i$ = "ball drawn from box $i$ has value $\leq 6$." All draws are independent, $P(A_i) = \frac{6}{10} = 0.6$.

(a) P(largest number drawn ≤ 6) = all $n$ draws $\leq 6$ = $P(A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = (0.6)^n$

(b) P(largest number drawn = 6) = all draws $\leq 6$ AND at least one draw = 6 = $P(\leq 6) - P(\leq 5) = (0.6)^n - (0.5)^n$

(c) P(largest number drawn > 6) = complement of (a) = $1 - (0.6)^n$

The Law of Total Probability

Before we can state Bayes' theorem, we need one crucial lemma: the Law of Total Probability. It lets us compute $P(A)$ by breaking the sample space into manageable pieces.

Let $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ be mutually exclusive and exhaustive events — meaning they partition $\Omega$: $$A_i \cap A_j = \emptyset \text{ for } i \neq j, \quad \text{and} \quad A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_n = \Omega$$ Then for any event $A$: $$P(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(A \mid A_i)\, P(A_i)$$

Event $A$ cuts across all partitions. Its total probability is the weighted sum of its conditional probabilities within each slice.

$$P(A) = P(A \cap \Omega) = P\!\left(A \cap \bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i\right) = P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n (A \cap A_i)\right)$$ Since $A_i$'s are disjoint, so are $A \cap A_i$. By countable additivity: $$= \sum_{i=1}^n P(A \cap A_i) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(A \mid A_i)\, P(A_i) \qquad \square$$
🎲 Die Example — Law of Total Probability

Throw a fair die. Partition $\Omega = \{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$ as: $A_1 = \{1,2,3\}$, $A_2 = \{4,5\}$, $A_3 = \{6\}$. Let $A$ = even number = $\{2,4,6\}$.

$$P(A) = P(A|A_1)\cdot P(A_1) + P(A|A_2)\cdot P(A_2) + P(A|A_3)\cdot P(A_3)$$ $$= \frac{1}{3}\cdot\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{3} + 1\cdot\frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{2} \checkmark$$
🎱 Balls Example — A Beautiful Result

Bag: 10 White + 20 Red. Drawn without replacement. What is $P(\text{2nd ball is White})$?

Condition on the 1st draw:

$$P(\text{2nd W}) = P(\text{2nd W} \mid \text{1st W})\cdot P(\text{1st W}) + P(\text{2nd W} \mid \text{1st R})\cdot P(\text{1st R})$$ $$= \frac{9}{29}\cdot\frac{10}{30} + \frac{10}{29}\cdot\frac{20}{30} = \frac{90}{870} + \frac{200}{870} = \frac{290}{870} = \frac{1}{3}$$

The same as $P(\text{1st ball is White}) = \frac{10}{30} = \frac{1}{3}$. This is not a coincidence — by symmetry, each ball is equally likely to be in any position, so the colour distribution of any draw is the same as the first.

Bayes' Theorem

Now the main event. We know an outcome $B$ has occurred. We want to find the probability that it was caused by $A_i$. We're reasoning backwards — from effect to cause.

Bayes' Theorem: Let $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ be a partition of $\Omega$ with $P(A_i) > 0$. For any event $B$ with $P(B) > 0$: $$P(A_i \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A_i)\, P(A_i)}{\sum_{j=1}^n P(B \mid A_j)\, P(A_j)}$$
From the definition of conditional probability: $$P(A_i \mid B) = \frac{P(A_i \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(B \mid A_i)\, P(A_i)}{P(B)}$$ Apply the Law of Total Probability to the denominator: $$P(B) = \sum_{j=1}^n P(B \mid A_j)\, P(A_j)$$ Substituting: $\displaystyle P(A_i \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A_i)\, P(A_i)}{\sum_{j=1}^n P(B \mid A_j)\, P(A_j)} \qquad \square$
Prior probability $P(A_i)$: What we believed about cause $A_i$ before observing $B$. Our starting knowledge.

Likelihood $P(B \mid A_i)$: How probable is the observed evidence $B$, assuming $A_i$ caused it?

Posterior probability $P(A_i \mid B)$: What we now believe about cause $A_i$ after observing $B$. Our updated belief.

Bayes' theorem is the formal rule for updating beliefs: $\text{posterior} \propto \text{likelihood} \times \text{prior}$.

Worked Example — The MCQ Student

📝 Exam Problem — Guessing vs Knowing

A student answers an MCQ with 5 options. Let $p$ = probability the student knows the answer. If they know it, they answer correctly with probability 1. If they don't know (probability $1-p$), they guess randomly — correct with probability $\frac{1}{5}$.

Question: Given that the student answered correctly, what is the probability they were guessing?

Let $G$ = "guessed", $K$ = "knew", $C$ = "answered correctly". We want $P(G \mid C)$.

By Bayes' theorem:

$$P(G \mid C) = \frac{P(C \mid G)\, P(G)}{P(C \mid G)\, P(G) + P(C \mid K)\, P(K)} = \frac{\frac{1}{5}(1-p)}{\frac{1}{5}(1-p) + 1 \cdot p}$$ $$= \frac{\frac{1-p}{5}}{\frac{1-p}{5} + p} = \frac{1-p}{1-p+5p} = \boxed{\frac{1-p}{1+4p}}$$

Check: if $p = 1$ (student always knows), $P(G|C) = 0$. If $p=0$ (always guesses), $P(G|C) = 1$. Both make intuitive sense.

Worked Example — Factory Defects

🏭 Three Machines, One Defective Item

A factory has 3 machines: $M_1$ (100 items/hr, defect rate 1%), $M_2$ (200 items/hr, defect rate 5%), $M_3$ (300 items/hr, defect rate 3%). One item is found defective. Which machine most likely produced it?

Step 1 — Prior probabilities (based on production share, total = 600 items/hr):

$$P(M_1) = \frac{100}{600} = \frac{1}{6}, \quad P(M_2) = \frac{200}{600} = \frac{1}{3}, \quad P(M_3) = \frac{300}{600} = \frac{1}{2}$$

Step 2 — Likelihoods (defect rate per machine):

$$P(D \mid M_1) = 0.01, \quad P(D \mid M_2) = 0.05, \quad P(D \mid M_3) = 0.03$$

Step 3 — Total probability of a defect:

$$P(D) = 0.01 \cdot \frac{1}{6} + 0.05 \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 0.03 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{0.01}{6} + \frac{0.05}{3} + \frac{0.03}{2}$$ $$= \frac{0.01 + 0.10 + 0.09}{6} = \frac{0.20}{6} = \frac{1}{30}$$

Step 4 — Posterior probabilities via Bayes:

MachinePrior $P(M_i)$Likelihood $P(D|M_i)$Joint $P(D \cap M_i)$Posterior $P(M_i|D)$
$M_1$$1/6$$0.01$$0.01/6 \approx 0.00167$$\approx 5.0\%$
$M_2$$1/3$$0.05$$0.05/3 \approx 0.01667$$\approx 50.0\%$
$M_3$$1/2$$0.03$$0.03/2 = 0.01500$$\approx 45.0\%$
Total$1$—$\approx 0.03333 = 1/30$$100\%$

Conclusion: $M_2$ is the most likely culprit at 50%, despite being responsible for only $\frac{1}{3}$ of production — because its defect rate (5%) is the highest. This is exactly how Bayes updates prior beliefs with new evidence.

Bayes' theorem is the backbone of algorithmic trading signal filters. You observe a technical signal (say, a moving average crossover). You want to know: given this signal, what is the probability the market actually trends? You need the prior probability of a trend, the likelihood of seeing this signal given a trend, and the likelihood of seeing it given no trend. Bayes gives you the posterior. Every signal-based system is implicitly Bayesian — most traders just don't know it.

What does the Law of Total Probability require of $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$?

A
They must be independent events
B
They must all have equal probability
C
They must be mutually exclusive and exhaustive (a partition of $\Omega$)
D
They must be mutually exclusive only
Answer: C

The $A_i$'s must form a partition of $\Omega$: pairwise disjoint (mutually exclusive) AND their union equals $\Omega$ (exhaustive). Both conditions are essential. Disjoint alone isn't enough — if they don't cover all of $\Omega$, the formula breaks down. Think of slicing a pizza: every piece is separate and together they form the whole pizza.

$\dfrac{1}{3}$

$\dfrac{1}{8}$

$\dfrac{2}{5}$

$\dfrac{0.4}{3.4}$

$$P(G \mid C) = \frac{1-p}{1+4p} = \frac{1-0.6}{1+4(0.6)} = \frac{0.4}{1+2.4} = \frac{0.4}{3.4} = \frac{4}{34} = \frac{2}{17}$$ Wait — let's recompute carefully: $\frac{0.4}{3.4} = \frac{2}{17} \approx 0.118$. probability / ch06 / q03 ★★ Medium

In the factory example, $M_2$ has the highest posterior probability despite being responsible for only $\frac{1}{3}$ of production. Why?

A
Because $M_2$ has the highest prior probability
B
Because $M_2$'s defect rate (5%) is highest, so its likelihood dominates over its moderate prior
C
Because the posterior always equals the prior for the most productive machine
D
Because Bayes' theorem always assigns highest probability to the middle option
Answer: B

Bayes' theorem weights each machine by both its prior (production share) and its likelihood (defect rate). $M_3$ has the largest prior ($\frac{1}{2}$) but a moderate defect rate (3%). $M_2$ has a smaller prior ($\frac{1}{3}$) but the highest defect rate (5%). The product prior × likelihood determines the posterior. $M_2$: $\frac{1}{3} \times 0.05 = 0.01\overline{6}$. $M_3$: $\frac{1}{2} \times 0.03 = 0.015$. $M_2$ wins.
probability / ch06 / q04 ★★★ Hard

A medical test for a rare disease (prevalence 1%) is 99% sensitive (true positive rate) and 95% specific (true negative rate). You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease?

A
99% — the test is almost perfect
B
About 50%
C
About 16.7% — surprisingly low due to the rare prior
D
About 95%
Answer: C — ≈ 16.7%

Let $D$ = has disease, $+$ = tests positive. Prior: $P(D) = 0.01$. Likelihoods: $P(+|D) = 0.99$, $P(+|D^c) = 0.05$ (false positive rate = 1 − specificity).

$$P(D|+) = \frac{P(+|D)\,P(D)}{P(+|D)\,P(D) + P(+|D^c)\,P(D^c)} = \frac{0.99 \times 0.01}{0.99 \times 0.01 + 0.05 \times 0.99}$$ $$= \frac{0.0099}{0.0099 + 0.0495} = \frac{0.0099}{0.0594} \approx 16.7\%$$ This is the base rate fallacy. A highly accurate test on a rare disease still produces many false positives because most people don't have the disease. The low prior ($P(D) = 1\%$) overwhelms even a very high accuracy. This is why medical screening guidelines are so carefully designed.
