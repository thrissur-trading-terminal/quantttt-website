Semi-Closed Form Option Pricing

We have the characteristic function. Now we turn it into money. The route goes through Gil-Pelaez inversion, a change of measure that splits the call price into two probabilities, and a final pair of Fourier integrals that a computer evaluates in under a millisecond.

The Road So Far — and Where We Are Going

In Chapter 3, we derived the Heston characteristic function $\phi_T(u) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[e^{iu\ln S_T}]$ in closed form — an explicit complex-valued formula involving five parameters and two elementary functions of $u$. We noted that the CF carries the same information as the full distribution of $S_T$, just encoded in Fourier space.

The remaining question is: how do we turn a characteristic function into an option price? The answer has three steps. First, we use the Gil-Pelaez inversion formula to convert the CF into tail probabilities. Second, we decompose the call payoff into two terms, each of which is a probability of the form $\mathbb{P}(S_T > K)$ — but under different measures. Third, we express both probabilities as explicit one-dimensional integrals of $\phi_T$, which a computer evaluates in microseconds using numerical quadrature.

The result — $C = S_0 P_1 - Ke^{-rT}P_2$ — looks structurally identical to BSM. The difference is that in BSM, $P_1 = N(d_1)$ and $P_2 = N(d_2)$ are Gaussian CDFs computable in one line. In Heston, $P_1$ and $P_2$ are Fourier integrals that encode the full non-Gaussian distribution of $S_T$. That is the entire gap between BSM and Heston in a single sentence.

The Gil-Pelaez Inversion Formula

Recall from Chapter 3 that the characteristic function and the probability density are Fourier transform pairs. If $X$ has density $f_X$ and CF $\phi_X(u) = \mathbb{E}[e^{iuX}]$, then:

$$f_X(x) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-iux}\phi_X(u)\,du$$

From this we can recover the CDF and, crucially for us, the tail probability $\mathbb{P}(X > a)$. The derivation is a short sequence of Fourier analysis steps, and the result is the Gil-Pelaez formula.

Derivation — Gil-Pelaez from Fourier Inversion
Step 1 — Write the tail probability as an integral of the density
$$\mathbb{P}(X > a) = \int_a^{\infty} f_X(x)\,dx$$
Step 2 — Substitute Fourier inversion for $f_X$
$$\mathbb{P}(X > a) = \int_a^{\infty} \left[\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-iux}\phi(u)\,du\right]dx$$
Step 3 — Swap order of integration (Fubini's theorem)
$$= \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \phi(u)\left[\int_a^{\infty} e^{-iux}\,dx\right]du$$
Step 4 — Handle the inner integral carefully

The integral $\int_a^\infty e^{-iux}\,dx$ does not converge classically — $e^{-iux}$ oscillates without decaying. We regularise by introducing a damping factor $e^{-\varepsilon x}$ with $\varepsilon > 0$ and taking $\varepsilon \to 0^+$ at the end:

$$\int_a^{\infty} e^{-iux - \varepsilon x}\,dx = \frac{e^{-(\varepsilon + iu)a}}{\varepsilon + iu} \xrightarrow{\varepsilon\to 0^+} \frac{e^{-iua}}{iu} \quad (u \neq 0)$$

The limiting expression $e^{-iua}/(iu)$ is well-defined for all $u \neq 0$, and when integrated against $\phi(u)$ (which is bounded and integrable), the Riemann-Lebesgue lemma ensures convergence. The singularity at $u = 0$ is handled by contour integration around the pole, contributing an extra $\frac{1}{2}$.

Step 5 — Substitute back
$$\mathbb{P}(X > a) = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\frac{e^{-iua}\phi(u)}{iu}\,du$$
Step 6 — Exploit conjugate symmetry to halve the domain

For a real-valued $X$, the CF satisfies $\phi(-u) = \overline{\phi(u)}$. Using this and the identity $z - \bar{z} = 2i\,\mathrm{Im}[z]$, the integral over $(-\infty, \infty)$ folds into twice the integral over $(0, \infty)$:

$$\mathbb{P}(X > a) = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty} \mathrm{Im}\!\left[\frac{e^{-iua}\phi(u)}{iu}\right]du$$
Gil-Pelaez Inversion Formula
$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(X > a) = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty} \mathrm{Im}\!\left[\frac{e^{-iua}\,\phi_X(u)}{iu}\right]du}$$

An equivalent form — which is more convenient to implement because $\mathrm{Re}[z/(iu)] = \mathrm{Im}[z]/u$ — is:

$$\mathbb{P}(X > a) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty} \mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{-iua}\,\phi_X(u)}{iu}\right]du$$
The $\frac{1}{2}$ in the formula is not a fudge factor. It arises from the residue of the pole at $u = 0$ in the contour integral, and it is equivalent to the fact that any continuous distribution satisfies $\mathbb{P}(X > \mathbb{E}[X]) = \frac{1}{2}$ when $X$ is symmetric around its mean. For asymmetric distributions (like the skewed $\ln S_T$ in Heston), the integral term shifts the probability away from $\frac{1}{2}$ in the correct direction.
Worked Example 1 · Gil-Pelaez for the Standard Normal Sanity check

Let $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ with $\phi_X(u) = e^{-u^2/2}$. Verify using Gil-Pelaez that $\mathbb{P}(X > 0) = \frac{1}{2}$.

Apply Gil-Pelaez with $a = 0$
$$\mathbb{P}(X > 0) = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty} \mathrm{Im}\!\left[\frac{e^{0}\cdot e^{-u^2/2}}{iu}\right]du = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty} \mathrm{Im}\!\left[\frac{-i\,e^{-u^2/2}}{u}\right]du$$
Extract the imaginary part

Since $e^{-u^2/2}$ is real and positive:

$$\mathrm{Im}\!\left[\frac{-i\,e^{-u^2/2}}{u}\right] = -\frac{e^{-u^2/2}}{u}$$
Substitute back
$$\mathbb{P}(X > 0) = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty}\frac{-e^{-u^2/2}}{u}\,du = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty}\frac{e^{-u^2/2}}{u}\,du$$

The integrand $e^{-u^2/2}/u$ diverges at $u = 0$, but the integral is understood in the Cauchy principal value sense. Since the integrand $1/u$ is an odd function and $e^{-u^2/2}$ is even, the symmetric principal value integral around $u = 0$ vanishes. The result:

$$\mathrm{P.V.}\int_0^{\infty}\frac{e^{-u^2/2}}{u}\,du = 0$$
Result: $\mathbb{P}(X > 0) = \frac{1}{2} + 0 = \frac{1}{2}$ ✓. The integral vanishes by the symmetry of the standard normal about zero, and Gil-Pelaez correctly reproduces $\mathbb{P}(X > 0) = \frac{1}{2}$. More generally, for $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ and arbitrary $a$, the Gil-Pelaez integral evaluates (non-trivially, via the Fourier cosine transform of $e^{-\sigma^2 u^2/2}$) to give $\mathbb{P}(X > a) = \Phi\!\left(\frac{\mu - a}{\sigma}\right)$ — the standard Gaussian CDF result.

Splitting the Call Payoff

The European call payoff is $(S_T - K)^+ = \max(S_T - K, 0)$. We rewrite it using an indicator function:

$$(S_T - K)^+ = (S_T - K)\cdot\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}} = S_T\cdot\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}} - K\cdot\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}$$

This is just algebra — the payoff is the stock value at maturity (if ITM) minus the strike (if ITM). Taking the discounted risk-neutral expectation:

$$C = e^{-rT}\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[S_T\cdot\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right] - e^{-rT}\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[K\cdot\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right]$$

The second term is immediate — $K$ is a constant:

$$e^{-rT}\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[K\cdot\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right] = Ke^{-rT}\,\mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T > K)$$

We call this probability $P_2$. The first term requires more thought.

Why $P_1$ Lives Under a Different Measure

The first term is $e^{-rT}\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[S_T\cdot\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}]$. This is not $S_0 \cdot \mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T > K)$, because $S_T$ and $\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}$ are not independent — the event $\{S_T > K\}$ is more likely precisely when $S_T$ is large. They are positively correlated, so the expectation of their product exceeds the product of their expectations.

The elegant resolution is a change of measure. We define a new probability measure $\mathbb{Q}^S$ — called the stock-numeraire measure or forward measure — by the Radon-Nikodým derivative:

$$\frac{d\mathbb{Q}^S}{d\mathbb{Q}} = \frac{S_T}{S_0\,e^{rT}}$$

This is a valid probability measure change because $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[S_T/(S_0 e^{rT})] = 1$ — the discounted stock price is a martingale under $\mathbb{Q}$, so the Radon-Nikodým density integrates to one.

Under this new measure, the abstract change-of-numeraire theorem tells us:

$$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[S_T\cdot\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right] = S_0\,e^{rT}\,\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}\!\left[\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}\right] = S_0\,e^{rT}\,\mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}(S_T > K)$$

Substituting back into the call price:

$$C = e^{-rT}\cdot S_0\,e^{rT}\,\mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}(S_T > K) - Ke^{-rT}\,\mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T > K)$$
Why does $S_T$ in the numerator of the payoff force a change of measure? Think of it this way: under $\mathbb{Q}$, the unit of account (numeraire) is the money market account $e^{rt}$. The call price is measured in units of the money market account. But the first term $S_T\cdot\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}$ is naturally measured in units of $S_T$ — a stock. To "factor out" $S_T$, we need to switch to a measure where $S_T$ is the unit of account. That is exactly $\mathbb{Q}^S$. Under $\mathbb{Q}^S$, $S_T$ is the baseline, so $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}[S_T\cdot g(S_T)] = S_T \cdot \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}[g(S_T)]$ becomes meaningful.
The Heston Call Price — Structural Form
$$\boxed{C = S_0\,P_1 - Ke^{-rT}\,P_2}$$ where: $$P_1 = \mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}(S_T > K) \qquad \text{(probability under stock-numeraire measure)}$$ $$P_2 = \mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T > K) \qquad \text{(probability under risk-neutral measure)}$$
Connection to BSM. In the Black-Scholes model, $\ln S_T \sim \mathcal{N}(\ln S_0 + (r - \sigma^2/2)T,\;\sigma^2 T)$ under $\mathbb{Q}$. A direct standardisation gives $P_2 = N(d_2)$. Under $\mathbb{Q}^S$ (where the drift of $\ln S_T$ shifts by $+\sigma^2 T$), we get $P_1 = N(d_1)$. So BSM is the special case where both probabilities have closed-form Gaussian expressions. In Heston, $\ln S_T$ is not Gaussian under either measure, and we compute $P_1$, $P_2$ by Fourier inversion of the CF.

The Characteristic Function Under $\mathbb{Q}^S$

To apply Gil-Pelaez to $P_1 = \mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}(S_T > K)$, we need the CF of $\ln S_T$ under $\mathbb{Q}^S$, not under $\mathbb{Q}$. Call it $\phi_T^{(1)}(u)$.

We derive it by computing the $\mathbb{Q}^S$-expectation using the Radon-Nikodým derivative:

Deriving $\phi_T^{(1)}(u)$ — the CF under $\mathbb{Q}^S$
Step 1 — Expand using the Radon-Nikodým derivative
$$\phi_T^{(1)}(u) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}\!\left[e^{iu\ln S_T}\right] = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{iu\ln S_T}\cdot\frac{d\mathbb{Q}^S}{d\mathbb{Q}}\right] = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{iu\ln S_T}\cdot\frac{S_T}{S_0\,e^{rT}}\right]$$
Step 2 — Write $S_T = e^{\ln S_T}$ and merge the exponentials
$$= \frac{1}{S_0\,e^{rT}}\,\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{iu\ln S_T}\cdot e^{\ln S_T}\right] = \frac{1}{S_0\,e^{rT}}\,\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{(iu + 1)\ln S_T}\right]$$
Step 3 — Recognise the Heston CF evaluated at $u - i$

Note that $iu + 1 = i(u - i)$, so $e^{(iu+1)\ln S_T} = e^{i(u-i)\ln S_T}$. By definition of the Heston CF:

$$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{i(u-i)\ln S_T}\right] = \phi_T(u - i)$$
Step 4 — Normalise so that $\phi_T^{(1)}(0) = 1$

At $u = 0$: the formula gives $\phi_T^{(1)}(0) = \phi_T(-i)/(S_0 e^{rT})$. For this to equal 1 (as any CF must at $u = 0$), we need $\phi_T(-i) = S_0 e^{rT}$. This is indeed true — it is a consequence of the risk-neutral drift condition $\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[S_T] = S_0 e^{rT}$, since $\phi_T(-i) = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[e^{-i\cdot i\cdot \ln S_T}] = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[S_T] = S_0 e^{rT}$. The normalisation is automatic.

CF of $\ln S_T$ Under the Stock-Numeraire Measure
$$\boxed{\phi_T^{(1)}(u) = \frac{\phi_T(u - i)}{\phi_T(-i)}}$$ where $\phi_T(\cdot)$ is the Heston CF derived in Chapter 3 and $\phi_T(-i) = S_0\,e^{rT}$.
The shift $u \mapsto u - i$ is the Fourier-space signature of a change of measure. In general, multiplying a distribution by $e^{\lambda X}$ (exponential tilting) corresponds to shifting the CF argument by $-i\lambda$. Here, the Radon-Nikodým derivative $S_T / (S_0 e^{rT}) = e^{\ln S_T - \ln(S_0 e^{rT})}$ tilts the distribution by multiplying by $e^{\ln S_T}$, which corresponds to $\lambda = 1$, giving the shift $u \mapsto u - i$. This is Esscher transform territory — the same technique is used to change measures in jump-diffusion models, Lévy processes, and actuarial mathematics.

The Final Probability Integrals

Applying Gil-Pelaez to both probabilities, with $a = \ln K$ (since we need $\mathbb{P}(\ln S_T > \ln K)$):

$P_2$ — Risk-Neutral Probability of Exercise
$$P_2 = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty} \mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\,\phi_T(u)}{iu}\right]du$$
$P_1$ — Stock-Numeraire Probability of Exercise
$$P_1 = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty} \mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\,\phi_T(u - i)}{iu\,\phi_T(-i)}\right]du$$

Together with $C = S_0 P_1 - Ke^{-rT}P_2$ and $\phi_T(-i) = S_0 e^{rT}$, these are the complete Heston pricing formulas. Everything is explicit — the only numerical work is evaluating the one-dimensional integrals on $(0, \infty)$.

The formulas for $P_1$ and $P_2$ each contain $1/(iu)$, which is singular at $u = 0$. In practice, the integration starts at a small positive $u = \varepsilon$ (say $\varepsilon = 10^{-4}$) rather than exactly zero. The integrand is bounded and continuous everywhere on $(0, \infty)$ — the apparent singularity at $u = 0$ cancels because $\phi_T(0) = 1$ and $e^{-iu\ln K}|_{u=0} = 1$, so the numerator $e^{-iu\ln K}\phi_T(u) \to 1$ as $u \to 0$, making the ratio $\to 1/(iu) \to \infty/\infty$ which resolves via L'Hôpital to a finite limit. Numerical implementations typically use the damped form or simply start integration from a small but nonzero $u$.

Why "Semi-Closed Form"?

The term "semi-closed form" is used precisely. Let's be explicit about what is closed and what is not.

Component Closed Form? Why
Heston CF $\phi_T(u)$ ✓ Yes Derived analytically via Riccati ODEs in Chapter 3. Evaluates in microseconds per point.
$\phi_T^{(1)}(u) = \phi_T(u-i)/\phi_T(-i)$ ✓ Yes A shifted evaluation of the same CF — no additional cost.
The integrals for $P_1$, $P_2$ ✗ No One-dimensional Fourier integrals. Must be evaluated numerically (Gauss-Legendre, FFT, etc.).
Call price $C = S_0P_1 - Ke^{-rT}P_2$ ✗ No Inherits the numerical component from $P_1$, $P_2$.

Contrast this with the two extremes. In BSM, $P_1 = N(d_1)$ and $P_2 = N(d_2)$ are fully closed — a single formula evaluates the option in nanoseconds. In pure Monte Carlo, nothing is closed form — you simulate thousands of paths and average the payoffs, which takes seconds. The Heston semi-closed form is a sweet spot: millisecond-speed option pricing with the full flexibility of a stochastic volatility model.

On an NSE equity derivatives desk, Heston is typically calibrated fresh each morning and then used for live pricing throughout the trading session. The calibration requires evaluating the model price for dozens of strikes and maturities, hundreds of times in an optimisation loop. With the semi-closed form, each option price takes roughly 0.5–2 milliseconds (evaluating the CF at 64–128 quadrature nodes). The full calibration runs in under 10 seconds. With Monte Carlo at 1 second per price, the same calibration would take hours. This is why the semi-closed form matters in practice — not just as a mathematical achievement, but as the reason Heston is deployable on a live trading desk in Mumbai.

Sanity Checks on the Formula

Before trusting any formula in production, we check its limiting behaviour. These checks do not require numerical integration — they follow from the structure of the formulas.

Check 1: Deep ITM ($K \to 0$). As $K \to 0$, the option is almost certainly exercised. We expect $C \to S_0 - Ke^{-rT} \to S_0$. In the formulas: $\ln K \to -\infty$, so $e^{-iu\ln K} = e^{-iu\cdot(-\infty)}$ oscillates infinitely rapidly. By the Riemann-Lebesgue lemma, the integrals in $P_1$ and $P_2$ both vanish, giving $P_1 \to \frac{1}{2}$... wait, this should give $P_1, P_2 \to 1$. The $\frac{1}{2}$ base term plus the integral contribution summing to $\frac{1}{2}$ gives $P_1 = P_2 = 1$, and $C = S_0 - Ke^{-rT}$ ✓.

Check 2: Deep OTM ($K \to \infty$). As $K \to \infty$, the option should be worthless. $P_1, P_2 \to 0$ and $C \to 0$ ✓.

Check 3: Zero interest rate, ATM, and put-call parity. At $r = 0$, $S_0 = K$, put-call parity gives $C - P = S_0 - Ke^{-rT} = 0$. In the formulas, $P_2 = \mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T > S_0)$ and by put-call parity and the symmetry of the risk-neutral distribution around $S_0$ under zero rates, $P_2 = \frac{1}{2}$ at the money. The call price reduces to $C = S_0(P_1 - \frac{1}{2})$. The put price is $P = S_0(\frac{1}{2} - P_1) + S_0(1 - P_2) - S_0 + S_0 = P$ — consistent. ✓

Check 4: $\xi \to 0$ (BSM limit). As shown at the end of Chapter 3, the Heston CF converges to the BSM CF. Consequently $P_1 \to N(d_1)$, $P_2 \to N(d_2)$, and $C \to C_{\text{BSM}}$ ✓.

Worked Example 2 · Verifying $P_2 = N(d_2)$ for BSM BSM as a special case

Show that in the Black-Scholes model (constant volatility $\sigma$, zero correlation, no stochastic vol), the Gil-Pelaez formula for $P_2$ reproduces $N(d_2)$ directly from the BSM characteristic function, without invoking the lognormal density.

Step 1 — BSM characteristic function

Under BSM, $\ln S_T \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_T, \sigma^2 T)$ with $\mu_T = \ln S_0 + (r - \sigma^2/2)T$. The CF of $\ln S_T$ is:

$$\phi_T^{\text{BSM}}(u) = \exp\!\left[iu\mu_T - \frac{\sigma^2 T u^2}{2}\right] = \exp\!\left[iu\!\left(\ln S_0 + \left(r - \frac{\sigma^2}{2}\right)T\right) - \frac{\sigma^2 T u^2}{2}\right]$$
Step 2 — Substitute into the Gil-Pelaez formula for $P_2$
$$P_2 = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty}\mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\,\phi_T^{\text{BSM}}(u)}{iu}\right]du$$

Combine the exponentials in the numerator:

$$e^{-iu\ln K}\,\phi_T^{\text{BSM}}(u) = \exp\!\left[iu(\mu_T - \ln K) - \frac{\sigma^2 T u^2}{2}\right]$$

Let $m = \mu_T - \ln K = \ln(S_0/K) + (r - \sigma^2/2)T$. Then:

$$\frac{e^{ium - \sigma^2 Tu^2/2}}{iu} = \frac{e^{-\sigma^2 Tu^2/2}[\cos(um) + i\sin(um)]}{iu}$$ $$= e^{-\sigma^2 Tu^2/2}\left[\frac{\cos(um)}{iu} + \frac{\sin(um)}{u}\right]$$ $$= e^{-\sigma^2 Tu^2/2}\left[\frac{-i\cos(um)}{u} + \frac{\sin(um)}{u}\right]$$
Step 3 — Extract the real part
$$\mathrm{Re}\!\left[\frac{e^{-iu\ln K}\phi_T^{\text{BSM}}(u)}{iu}\right] = \frac{e^{-\sigma^2 Tu^2/2}\sin(um)}{u}$$
Step 4 — Evaluate the integral

The integral $\frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \frac{e^{-\sigma^2 Tu^2/2}\sin(um)}{u}\,du$ is a known result — the Fourier sine transform of $e^{-\sigma^2 Tu^2/2}$:

$$\frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty}\frac{e^{-\sigma^2 Tu^2/2}\sin(mu)}{u}\,du = \frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{sgn}(m)\,\mathrm{erf}\!\left(\frac{|m|}{\sigma\sqrt{2T}}\right)$$

For $m > 0$ (option ITM at risk-neutral drift), this equals $\frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{erf}(m/(\sigma\sqrt{2T}))$. Using the relation $\Phi(x) = \frac{1}{2}[1 + \mathrm{erf}(x/\sqrt{2})]$:

$$P_2 = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\,\mathrm{erf}\!\left(\frac{m}{\sigma\sqrt{2T}}\right) = \Phi\!\left(\frac{m}{\sigma\sqrt{T}}\right) = \Phi(d_2)$$
Result: Gil-Pelaez applied to the BSM characteristic function exactly recovers $P_2 = N(d_2)$, where $d_2 = \ln(S_0/K) + (r - \sigma^2/2)T\big)/(\sigma\sqrt{T})$. No lognormal density was used — only the CF and Fourier analysis. This confirms that the Gil-Pelaez formula is the correct generalisation: in Heston, we replace $\phi_T^{\text{BSM}}$ with $\phi_T^{\text{Heston}}$ and evaluate the integral numerically instead of analytically.
Worked Example 3 · Boundary Behaviour — $P_1$ and $P_2$ as Probabilities Sanity check

Verify three structural facts about the Heston pricing formula: (i) $0 \leq P_1, P_2 \leq 1$; (ii) $P_1 \geq P_2$ always (for any model, not just Heston); and (iii) as $\sigma \to \infty$ (extreme vol), $P_1 \to 1$ and $P_2 \to 1$ while $C \to S_0 - Ke^{-rT}$ (the forward price).

(i) Both $P_1$ and $P_2$ are genuine probabilities

$P_2 = \mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T > K)$ is a probability under $\mathbb{Q}$ — it lies in $[0,1]$ by definition. $P_1 = \mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}(S_T > K)$ is a probability under $\mathbb{Q}^S$ — also in $[0,1]$ by definition. Both are well-defined probabilities on the event $\{S_T > K\}$ under different measures. ✓

(ii) $P_1 \geq P_2$ always

Using the Radon-Nikodým relationship and the definition of $\mathbb{Q}^S$:

$$P_1 = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}[\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}] = \frac{\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[S_T\cdot\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}]}{S_0 e^{rT}}$$

By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality (or more directly, by positive correlation):

$$\mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[S_T\cdot\mathbf{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}] \geq \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}[S_T]\cdot\mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T > K) = S_0 e^{rT}\cdot P_2$$

Dividing both sides by $S_0 e^{rT}$ gives $P_1 \geq P_2$. ✓ In BSM this is $N(d_1) \geq N(d_2)$, which holds because $d_1 = d_2 + \sigma\sqrt{T} > d_2$.

(iii) High volatility limit

As $\sigma \to \infty$, the distribution of $S_T$ becomes extremely spread out. The probability of $S_T > K$ approaches 1 under both measures (since the lower tail probability vanishes and $K$ is fixed). So $P_1, P_2 \to 1$ and:

$$C \to S_0 \cdot 1 - Ke^{-rT}\cdot 1 = S_0 - Ke^{-rT}$$
Summary: The formula $C = S_0P_1 - Ke^{-rT}P_2$ is structurally sound. Both $P_1$ and $P_2$ are genuine probabilities in $[0,1]$, with $P_1 \geq P_2$ always. The high-vol limit gives the forward price — the theoretical maximum call value (you always exercise). The low-vol limit gives $P_1, P_2 \to \mathbf{1}_{S_0 e^{rT} > K}$, recovering the intrinsic value $\max(S_0 e^{rT} - Ke^{-rT}, 0) \cdot e^{-rT}$. These boundary checks confirm the formula is correctly derived before any numerical evaluation.

The Heston call price is $C = S_0 P_1 - Ke^{-rT}P_2$. A student says: "$P_1$ and $P_2$ are both probabilities of the same event $\{S_T > K\}$, so they should be equal." What is wrong with this reasoning?

A
The student is correct — $P_1 = P_2$ always, since they measure the same event.
B
$P_1$ and $P_2$ are probabilities of the same event but under different measures ($\mathbb{Q}^S$ and $\mathbb{Q}$ respectively), so they assign different weights to outcomes and are generally unequal.
C
$P_1$ is not a probability — it is the option's delta, which can exceed 1.
D
$P_2$ includes the discount factor $e^{-rT}$, making it smaller than $P_1$ by exactly $e^{-rT}$.
Answer: B.

The same event $\{S_T > K\}$ can have different probabilities under different probability measures — that is the entire point of the Radon-Nikodým theorem and the change-of-measure framework. $P_2 = \mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T > K)$ measures the event under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb{Q}$, where the numeraire is the money market account. $P_1 = \mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}^S}(S_T > K)$ measures the same event under the stock-numeraire measure $\mathbb{Q}^S$, which overweights scenarios where $S_T$ is large (because those scenarios have large Radon-Nikodým weight $S_T/(S_0 e^{rT})$). Since the event $\{S_T > K\}$ is exactly the set of large-$S_T$ scenarios, the stock-numeraire measure assigns it higher probability: $P_1 \geq P_2$ always. In BSM, this is $N(d_1) \geq N(d_2)$.
finance / option-pricing / ch04 / q02 ★★ Conceptual

The CF under the stock-numeraire measure is $\phi_T^{(1)}(u) = \phi_T(u - i)/\phi_T(-i)$. The shift by $-i$ arises because the Radon-Nikodým derivative is $S_T/(S_0 e^{rT})$. If instead we priced a power option with payoff $(S_T^2 - K)^+$, and needed to define a "$S^2$-numeraire measure," what shift would appear in the CF?

A
Still $-i$, because the shift only depends on the strike $K$.
B
$-2i$, because the Radon-Nikodým derivative $S_T^2/(S_0^2 e^{2rT})$ leads to $e^{2\ln S_T}$, giving a tilt of $\lambda = 2$ and shift $u \mapsto u - 2i$.
C
$+i$, because squaring reverses the direction of the measure change.
D
$-2i$, because $S_T^2 = e^{2\ln S_T}$, so multiplying by $S_T^2$ in the expectation is $e^{(iu+2)\ln S_T} = e^{i(u-2i)\ln S_T}$, shifting the CF argument to $u - 2i$.
Answer: D.

Options B and D both give the right answer ($u - 2i$) but for the same reason — option D states it more precisely. The key step is writing $S_T^2 = e^{2\ln S_T}$. When we compute the $\mathbb{Q}^{S^2}$-CF: $$\phi_T^{(S^2)}(u) \propto \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{iu\ln S_T}\cdot S_T^2\right] = \mathbb{E}^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{(iu+2)\ln S_T}\right] = \phi_T(u - 2i)$$ The shift is $-2i$ because we are multiplying by $S_T^2 = e^{2\ln S_T}$, which adds 2 to the exponent, or equivalently shifts $u \to u - 2i$ in the CF. In general, a numeraire of $S_T^\lambda$ produces a shift of $u \to u - \lambda i$. This is the Esscher transform at parameter $\lambda$.
finance / option-pricing / ch04 / q03 ★★★ Computational

A trader claims: "If the Heston ATM call price is lower than the BSM price at the same vol, it must be because $\rho < 0$ is stealing value from the call." A quant colleague responds: "Not exactly — the correct statement involves how $\rho$ redistributes probability mass between $P_1$ and $P_2$ relative to $N(d_1)$ and $N(d_2)$." Which of the following best captures the quant's point?

A
Negative $\rho$ directly reduces the Heston CF, making $\phi_T(-i) < S_0 e^{rT}$ and thus breaking the risk-neutral drift condition.
B
Negative $\rho$ makes the distribution of $S_T$ symmetric, so $P_1 = P_2$ and the call price equals the BSM price.
C
Negative $\rho$ skews the distribution of $S_T$ leftward, raising $P_2$ (cheap OTM put territory) and potentially lowering $P_1$ for OTM calls, with the net ATM effect depending on the full parameter set — not just $\rho$ alone.
D
$\rho$ has no effect on ATM pricing — it only affects the skew for OTM strikes.
Answer: C.

The trader's intuition is directionally right (negative $\rho$ affects ATM calls) but mechanistically imprecise. Here is the precise picture:

Negative $\rho$ generates negative skewness in $\ln S_T$ — the left tail is fatter than the right. This means more probability mass sits at low $S_T$ values relative to a symmetric (zero-$\rho$) distribution. For an ATM call ($K = S_0 e^{rT}$), the effect on $P_2 = \mathbb{P}^{\mathbb{Q}}(S_T > K)$ depends on the full shape of the distribution, not just the sign of $\rho$. Specifically: the skew lowers the median of $S_T$ relative to its mean (which is fixed by no-arbitrage at $S_0 e^{rT}$), so ATM $P_2$ can be slightly below $\frac{1}{2}$, reducing the call price. The effect on $P_1$ (under the stock-numeraire measure) is compounded by the measure change. The net ATM effect is small and parameter-dependent — a full numerical calculation is required to quantify it. Option A is wrong because $\phi_T(-i) = S_0 e^{rT}$ always holds by the risk-neutral drift condition, regardless of $\rho$. Option D is wrong — $\rho$ does affect ATM options through the term structure of implied vol.
