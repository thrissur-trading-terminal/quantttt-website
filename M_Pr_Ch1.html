Introduction & Sample Space

Before we price a single option or model a single stock, we need to speak the language of uncertainty. That language is probability.

Why Probability? Why Now?

You've been told that trading is about charts, gut feelings, or "reading the market." Some of that has merit. But if you want to understand why an option is priced the way it is, why Nifty behaves the way it does on expiry days, or why your stop-loss gets hit in ways that feel statistically impossible — you need probability.

Not the probability of flipping coins (we'll get there, briefly). The deep kind — the kind that tells you how to reason about the future given incomplete information. That's what we're building here, from the ground up.

Probability is not about predicting the future. It is about quantifying our uncertainty about the future. A quant who says "Nifty has a 65% chance of closing above 24,000 today" isn't predicting — they're assigning a coherent number to their uncertainty. That number can be traded on.

The Experiment and the Sample Space

Everything in probability starts with an experiment — any process whose outcome is uncertain. Flipping a coin. Rolling a die. Observing whether Nifty closes up or down today. Checking if a Reliance call option expires in the money.

The sample space $\Omega$ (capital omega) is the set of all possible outcomes of an experiment. Not the outcomes you expect — all outcomes.

The sample space $\Omega$ is the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. Each individual outcome $\omega \in \Omega$ is called a sample point or elementary event.

Examples — From Simple to Financial

Example 1 — A coin flip:

$$\Omega = \{H, T\}$$

Example 2 — A single die roll:

$$\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$$

Example 3 — Nifty closing direction tomorrow:

$$\Omega = \{\text{Up}, \text{Down}, \text{Unchanged}\}$$

Example 4 — Nifty closing price tomorrow (continuous):

$$\Omega = (0, \infty)$$

Here the sample space is uncountably infinite — Nifty could close at 23,847.35 or 24,002.10 or any positive real number. This is the kind of sample space that matters in options pricing.

When we model Nifty 50 as a continuous random variable, we are saying: the closing price tomorrow lives somewhere in $(0, \infty)$, and our job is to assign probabilities to ranges of that space. The Black-Scholes model assumes Nifty follows a specific distribution over this sample space — more on that when we get to Finance.

Events — Subsets of the Sample Space

An event is any subset of the sample space $\Omega$. We say an event $A$ occurs if the actual outcome of the experiment belongs to $A$.

An event $A$ is a subset of $\Omega$: $A \subseteq \Omega$. The event $A$ occurs if and only if the observed outcome $\omega \in A$.

Some important special events:

The certain event is $\Omega$ itself — it always occurs. The impossible event is $\emptyset$ (the empty set) — it never occurs. These seem trivial, but they anchor the entire axiom system we'll build in Chapter 2.

Set Operations on Events

Since events are sets, we can combine them using set operations. These map directly to everyday probabilistic language:

$$A \cup B \quad \text{(A or B)} \qquad A \cap B \quad \text{(A and B)} \qquad A^c \quad \text{(not A)}$$

Union $A \cup B$: the event that $A$ occurs, or $B$ occurs, or both.
Intersection $A \cap B$: the event that both $A$ and $B$ occur.
Complement $A^c$: the event that $A$ does not occur.

Students often confuse "mutually exclusive" with "independent." Mutually exclusive means $A \cap B = \emptyset$ — the two events cannot both happen. Independent means knowing $A$ occurred tells you nothing about $B$. These are very different concepts. We will treat independence carefully in Chapter 3.

Finite vs Infinite Sample Spaces

When $\Omega$ is finite or countably infinite (like the set of integers), we call it a discrete sample space. When $\Omega$ is uncountably infinite (like an interval of real numbers), we call it continuous.

This distinction matters enormously for how we assign probabilities. For discrete spaces, we can sometimes just list and count. For continuous spaces, we need measure theory — but we'll build the intuition first and bring in the heavy machinery carefully.

Think of it this way: if you can list every possible outcome (even if the list is infinitely long), it's discrete. If even between two outcomes there are infinitely many others — like between ₹24,000.00 and ₹24,000.01 for Nifty — it's continuous.

probability / ch01 / q01 ★ Easy

A trader is watching whether a Nifty 50 call option expires in the money (ITM), at the money (ATM), or out of the money (OTM) on expiry day. She decides to model this as a probability experiment. What is the correct sample space $\Omega$ for this experiment?

A
$\Omega = \{ITM, OTM\}$
B
$\Omega = \{0, 1\}$ where 1 means profit
C
$\Omega = \{ITM, ATM, OTM\}$
D
$\Omega = \mathbb{R}$ (all real numbers)
Answer: C — $\Omega = \{ITM, ATM, OTM\}$

The sample space must include all possible outcomes of the experiment. The experiment here is observing the moneyness at expiry — three distinct, exhaustive possibilities: the option finishes in the money, at the money, or out of the money.

Option A is wrong because it ignores the ATM case. Option B encodes the wrong experiment (it's asking about profit, not moneyness). Option D would be appropriate if we were modelling the Nifty closing price itself — a continuous quantity — but the experiment here is about the moneyness category.
probability / ch01 / q02 ★★ Medium

Let $\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}$ be the sample space for a die roll. Define events $A = \{2, 4, 6\}$ (even) and $B = \{4, 5, 6\}$ (greater than 3). Which of the following correctly identifies $A \cap B$?

A
$\{2, 4, 5, 6\}$
B
$\{4, 6\}$
C
$\{2, 5\}$
D
$\emptyset$
Answer: B — $\{4, 6\}$

$A \cap B$ is the event that both A and B occur — i.e., outcomes that are even AND greater than 3.

$A = \{2, 4, 6\}$ and $B = \{4, 5, 6\}$. The elements common to both sets are $4$ and $6$.
$$A \cap B = \{4, 6\}$$
Option A is $A \cup B = \{2, 4, 5, 6\}$ — the union, not the intersection. Option C has no logic supporting it. Option D would mean the events are mutually exclusive — but they clearly share outcomes 4 and 6.
