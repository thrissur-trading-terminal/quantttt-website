Markov Chains — Stationarity, Return Times, and Hidden Sequences

The first three chapters assumed data was i.i.d. — each observation drawn independently from the same distribution. But financial returns have memory. Spoken words depend on what came before. Biological sequences carry structure. This chapter develops the probabilistic machinery for sequences with Markovian dependence: stationary distributions, recurrence times, Hidden Markov Models, and branching processes — four tools that power everything from regime detection to speech recognition.

Beyond i.i.d. — When the Past Matters

Chapter 1 introduced the joint distribution $P_{XY}$ as the fundamental object of ML, and assumed samples from it were independent and identically distributed. The i.i.d. assumption is mathematically convenient — it is what lets us write the joint likelihood as a product $\prod_i P_\theta(v_i)$, which is what made MLE tractable in Chapter 3.

But the real world is not i.i.d. NIFTY's price tomorrow is not independent of its price today. The word "bank" in a sentence depends on whether "river" or "money" appeared before it. A patient's health state at time $t+1$ depends on their state at time $t$. In all these cases, the data is a sequence with dependence structure.

The simplest and most tractable model of sequential dependence is the Markov chain: a sequence where the future depends on the present, but not on anything older. Once you know the current state, the past is irrelevant. This is the Markov property — and it is both a simplification and a profoundly useful one.

A sequence of random variables $\{X_n\}_{n \geq 0}$ taking values in a state space $S$ is a Markov chain if for all $n \geq 0$ and all states $i_0, i_1, \ldots, i_{n+1} \in S$: $$P(X_{n+1} = i_{n+1} \mid X_0 = i_0, X_1 = i_1, \ldots, X_n = i_n) = P(X_{n+1} = i_{n+1} \mid X_n = i_n)$$ The probability of the next state depends only on the current state, not on how the chain arrived there. The transition matrix $P$ collects these probabilities: $P_{ij} = P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i) \geq 0$, with each row summing to 1.
NIFTY regime models are often Markov: a bull regime tomorrow depends on the regime today, not on whether the bull run started 3 days or 3 months ago. Credit rating transitions (AAA → AA → A → …) are modelled as Markov chains by rating agencies. The Markov property is not always exactly true — but it is the tractable approximation that makes inference possible.

Stationary Distributions — When Does the Chain Settle?

One of the first questions about a Markov chain is: does the distribution of the chain converge over time? If you run the chain long enough, does it "settle" into some stable distribution that it stays in forever?

A probability distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ (a row vector with non-negative entries summing to 1) is a stationary distribution of a Markov chain with transition matrix $P$ if: $$\boldsymbol{\pi} = \boldsymbol{\pi} P \qquad \text{equivalently,} \qquad \pi_j = \sum_{i} \pi_i P_{ij} \quad \text{for all } j$$ If the chain starts with distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$, it stays in distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ forever. Stationary distributions are fixed points of the distribution-update map.

The central existence and uniqueness theorem — the backbone of this chapter — says that for well-behaved chains on finite state spaces, a unique stationary distribution always exists.

Theorem (Stationary Distribution — Existence and Uniqueness). Consider an irreducible Markov chain on a finite state space $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$. Then this chain has a unique non-zero stationary distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$.

Recall: a chain is irreducible if every state can be reached from every other state — the chain doesn't get trapped in a subset of states.

Proof Sketch · Uniqueness via Kernel Dimension Linear Algebra Argument
Step 1 — Rewrite the stationarity condition

We seek row vectors $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ satisfying $\boldsymbol{\pi} P = \boldsymbol{\pi}$, i.e. $\boldsymbol{\pi}(P - I) = 0$. Equivalently, the right kernel: column vectors $w$ with $(P - I)w = 0$.

Step 2 — The all-ones vector is always in the kernel

Since each row of $P$ sums to 1, we have $P\mathbf{1}_n = \mathbf{1}_n$, so $(P - I)\mathbf{1}_n = 0$. Thus $\dim(\ker(P-I)) \geq 1$.

Step 3 — Irreducibility forces the kernel to be exactly 1-dimensional

Take any $w \in \ker(P - I)$ and let $i_0 = \arg\max_i w(i)$. Since the chain is irreducible, every state $j$ is reachable from $i_0$, so there exists a path $i_0 \to i_1 \to \cdots \to j$ with positive probability. If $w(j) < w(i_0)$ for any $j$, then $w(i_0) = (Pw)(i_0) = \sum_k P_{i_0 k} w(k) < w(i_0)$ — a contradiction. Therefore $w(j) = w(i_0)$ for all $j$: every $w \in \ker(P-I)$ is a constant vector. So $\ker(P-I) = \text{span}\{\mathbf{1}_n\}$, which is 1-dimensional.

Step 4 — Transfer to stationary distributions

The space of stationary distributions $\{\boldsymbol{\pi} : \boldsymbol{\pi} P = \boldsymbol{\pi}\} = \ker(P-I)^T$ also has dimension 1 (since $\text{rank}(P-I) = \text{rank}(P-I)^T$). Normalising to $\sum_j \pi_j = 1$ with $\pi_j \geq 0$ gives a unique solution.

Uniqueness follows from irreducibility. A chain that can reach everywhere from everywhere cannot settle into multiple different long-run distributions — it must have exactly one.

Existence is proved by a beautiful Cesàro averaging argument: define the running average $v_k = \frac{1}{k}(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{u}P + \mathbf{u}P^2 + \cdots + \mathbf{u}P^{k-1})$ starting from any distribution $\mathbf{u}$. The sequence $\{v_k\}$ is bounded (each $v_k$ is a probability vector), so by Bolzano-Weierstrass a subsequence converges; one shows that limit satisfies $\boldsymbol{\pi} = \boldsymbol{\pi} P$ and has all strictly positive entries (by irreducibility). Existence established.

The stationary distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ is the long-run fraction of time the chain spends in each state. If $\pi_j = 0.3$, the chain visits state $j$ about 30% of the time in the long run, regardless of where it started. For NIFTY regime models: the stationary distribution tells you how often the market is in bull vs. bear vs. sideways regimes on average — the equilibrium allocation of time across regimes.

Positive Recurrence and the Return Time Formula

The stationary probability $\pi_j$ has a beautiful and direct interpretation in terms of how quickly the chain returns to state $j$.

For a recurrent state $j$, define $N_j = \min\{n > 0 : X_n = j\}$ as the first return time to $j$ after leaving it. The mean return time is: $$m_j = E(N_j \mid X_0 = j)$$ State $j$ is positive recurrent if $m_j < \infty$ (the chain returns in finite expected time). State $j$ is null recurrent if $m_j = \infty$ (recurrent, but expected return time is infinite).

The connection between stationary probabilities and mean return times is one of the deepest results in Markov chain theory.

Theorem (Return Time Formula). For an irreducible, positive recurrent Markov chain: $$\pi_j = \frac{1}{m_j}$$ The stationary probability of state $j$ equals the reciprocal of the mean return time to $j$. Equivalently: states visited frequently (low $m_j$) get high stationary probability, and vice versa.
Derivation · $\pi_j = 1/m_j$ via the Strong Law of Large Numbers Renewal Theory
Setup — Decompose time into excursions

Starting from state $j$, let $T_1$ be the time of the first return, $T_2$ the time between the 1st and 2nd returns, $T_3$ between the 2nd and 3rd, and so on. By the Markov property, $T_2, T_3, \ldots$ are i.i.d. with $E(T_k) = m_j$ for $k \geq 2$. (Note $T_1$ may have a different distribution if we start in state $j$ at time 0; however $T_1 < \infty$ a.s. by recurrence.)

The long-run visit frequency

The number of visits to state $j$ in the first $\sum_{k=1}^n T_k$ steps is exactly $n$. So the long-run fraction of time in state $j$ is:

$$\pi_j = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n}{\sum_{k=1}^n T_k} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n T_k}$$
Apply the Strong Law of Large Numbers

Since $T_1 < \infty$, the term $T_1/n \to 0$. For $k \geq 2$, $T_k$ are i.i.d. with mean $m_j$, so by the SLLN:

$$\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{k=2}^n T_k \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} m_j$$
$$\pi_j = \frac{1}{m_j}$$ Positive recurrent ($m_j < \infty$): $\pi_j > 0$. The chain returns often enough to have a positive long-run fraction in $j$.
Null recurrent ($m_j = \infty$): $\pi_j = 0$. The chain does return (recurrent), but so rarely that the long-run fraction is zero.
Null recurrence: a subtle failure mode. The symmetric random walk on $\mathbb{Z}$ (with $p = q = 1/2$) is recurrent — the walk will return to 0 with probability 1. But the expected return time is infinite: $m_0 = \infty$. This means the walk returns to 0 infinitely often, but the gaps between visits grow without bound. The stationary distribution, if it existed, would have $\pi_j = 1/m_j = 0$ for all $j$. No valid probability distribution can have all zeros — so null recurrent chains have no stationary distribution. Finite state spaces avoid this: all irreducible chains on finite spaces are automatically positive recurrent.
Positive Recurrent — Finite States

Any irreducible Markov chain on a finite state space $\{1, \ldots, n\}$ is automatically positive recurrent. The proof: since $\sum_j \pi_j = 1$ and there are only $n$ states, at least one $\pi_j \geq 1/n > 0$, so $m_j \leq n < \infty$ for that state. Irreducibility then forces all states to be positive recurrent — it is a class property.

Null Recurrent — Infinite States

The symmetric random walk on $\mathbb{Z}$ shows null recurrence is possible only on infinite state spaces. A 2-state weather model (sunny/cloudy) on a finite space will always have a stationary distribution. A random walk on the integers — e.g., modelling net P&L of a zero-edge strategy — can wander forever with an infinite mean return time.

The Gambler's Ruin — A Complete Worked Example

Worked Example 1 · Gambler's Ruin on $\{0, 1, \ldots, N\}$ 🎲 Classic Result with a Finance Twist

A gambler starts with fortune $i \in \{0, 1, \ldots, N\}$. At each step, their fortune increases by 1 with probability $p$ (a win) or decreases by 1 with probability $q = 1-p$ (a loss). States 0 and $N$ are absorbing: ruin and target respectively. Let $P_i$ be the probability of reaching $N$ starting from $i$.

Step 1 — Set up the recurrence

Conditioning on the first step:

$$P_i = p \cdot P_{i+1} + q \cdot P_{i-1}, \quad i = 1, 2, \ldots, N-1$$

with boundary conditions $P_0 = 0$ and $P_N = 1$. Rearranging: $P_{i+1} - P_i = \frac{q}{p}(P_i - P_{i-1})$. Let $r = q/p$. This is a geometric recurrence in the differences.

Step 2 — Solve by telescoping

Let $d_i = P_i - P_{i-1}$. Then $d_{i+1} = r \cdot d_i$, so $d_i = r^{i-1} d_1$. Summing:

$$P_i = P_0 + \sum_{k=1}^i d_k = d_1 \sum_{k=0}^{i-1} r^k = \begin{cases} d_1 \cdot \frac{1 - r^i}{1-r} & r \neq 1 \\ i \cdot d_1 & r = 1 \end{cases}$$

Using $P_N = 1$ to find $d_1$:

$$\boxed{P_i = \begin{cases} \dfrac{1-(q/p)^i}{1-(q/p)^N} & p \neq \frac{1}{2} \\[1em] \dfrac{i}{N} & p = \frac{1}{2} \end{cases}}$$
Step 3 — The infinite table limit ($N \to \infty$)

As $N \to \infty$, the probability of ever reaching an infinite target from fortune $i$:

$$P_i \to \begin{cases} 1 - (q/p)^i & p > \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & p \leq \frac{1}{2} \end{cases}$$
The brutal result: if the game is fair ($p = 1/2$) or unfavourable ($p < 1/2$), a gambler playing against an infinitely rich opponent is certain to be ruined eventually. Only a strictly favourable edge ($p > 1/2$) gives a chance of survival, and that chance is $1 - (q/p)^i$ — decreasing in $q/p$ and increasing in starting fortune $i$.

Finance interpretation: a systematic trading strategy with zero edge that starts with capital $i$ will eventually be wiped out with probability 1, no matter how large $i$ is, when competing with a market of unlimited depth ($N = \infty$). The only protection is a genuine edge ($p > 1/2$).

Mean Time in Transient States — The Fundamental Matrix

Before states are absorbed (as in the gambler's ruin), the chain visits transient states a random number of times. How many times, in expectation? The answer is elegant.

Let $T = \{1, 2, \ldots, t\}$ be the set of transient states with sub-stochastic transition matrix $P_T$. Define $s_{ij}$ as the expected number of times the chain is in state $j$, given it started in state $i$. Stacking these into a matrix $S$: $$S_{ij} = \delta_{ij} + \sum_{k \in T} P_{ik} \cdot S_{kj} \implies S = I + P_T S \implies S = (I - P_T)^{-1}$$ This matrix $S = (I - P_T)^{-1}$ is called the fundamental matrix of the Markov chain. It exists because $P_T$ is sub-stochastic (spectral radius $< 1$).

The fundamental matrix also gives the probability of ever visiting a transient state $j$ starting from transient state $i$:

$$f_{ij} = \frac{S_{ij} - \delta_{ij}}{S_{jj}}$$
The matrix inversion $(I - P_T)^{-1}$ is the Markov chain analogue of the geometric series $\sum_{k=0}^\infty r^k = \frac{1}{1-r}$ for $|r| < 1$. Indeed: $S = \sum_{k=0}^\infty P_T^k$, where $(P_T^k)_{ij}$ is the probability of being in state $j$ at step $k$ given start in $i$. Summing over all $k$ gives the expected total time in $j$. The matrix inversion is the closed-form shortcut.

Hidden Markov Models — When the State Is Invisible

A Markov chain $\{X_n\}$ models a hidden process — an underlying sequence of states. But in many real applications, we cannot directly observe the states. Instead, we observe a signal $S_n$ emitted by the chain at each step, with an emission probability that depends only on the current state. This is a Hidden Markov Model (HMM).

A Hidden Markov Model consists of:
  • A hidden Markov chain $\{X_n\}$ with transition matrix $P = (P_{ij})$ and initial state distribution $(\pi_i)$
  • A finite set of observable signals $S$
  • Emission probabilities $P(s \mid j)$ — the probability of signal $s$ when the chain is in state $j$, satisfying $\sum_{s \in S} P(s \mid j) = 1$
We observe the signal sequence $s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n$. The underlying state sequence $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ is unobserved.
HMMs are the backbone of regime detection in finance. The hidden states are market regimes — bull, bear, high-volatility, low-volatility. The observed signals are daily returns, VIX levels, or order flow imbalance. We don't directly see "the market is in a bear regime" — we infer it from the signals. Equity research desks at major Indian brokerages use variants of HMMs to detect regime changes on NIFTY 50 before they become obvious to the market.

Two fundamental inference tasks arise in HMMs: filtering (what is the current hidden state, given signals so far?) and decoding (what was the most likely sequence of hidden states?). These are solved by the Forward Algorithm and the Viterbi Algorithm respectively.

The Forward Algorithm

Define the forward variable: $$F_n(j) = P(S^n = s_n,\; X_n = j)$$ where $S^n = (S_1, \ldots, S_n)$ is the vector of first $n$ signals and $s_n = (s_1, \ldots, s_n)$ is the observed sequence. This is the joint probability that the chain is in state $j$ at step $n$ AND the first $n$ signals match what we saw.

Once we have $F_n(j)$ for all states $j$, the filtering distribution follows immediately: $$P(X_n = j \mid S^n = s_n) = \frac{F_n(j)}{\sum_i F_n(i)}$$

Derivation · The Forward Recursion Dynamic Programming
Expand the joint probability
$$F_n(j) = P(S^n = s_n,\; X_n = j) = \sum_i P(S^{n-1} = s_{n-1},\; X_{n-1} = i,\; X_n = j,\; S_n = s_n)$$
Factor using the Markov and emission properties
\begin{align} &= \sum_i P(S^{n-1} = s_{n-1},\; X_{n-1} = i) \cdot P(X_n = j \mid X_{n-1} = i) \cdot P(S_n = s_n \mid X_n = j)\\ &= \sum_i F_{n-1}(i) \cdot P_{ij} \cdot P(s_n \mid j) \end{align}
$$\boxed{F_n(j) = P(s_n \mid j) \sum_i P_{ij}\, F_{n-1}(i)}$$ Initialisation: $F_1(j) = \pi_j \cdot P(s_1 \mid j)$. This recursion computes $F_n(j)$ for all $j$ in $O(|S|^2 \cdot n)$ time — vastly more efficient than summing over all $|S|^n$ possible state sequences.
Worked Example 2 · HMM Forward Algorithm 🏭 Production Quality Control

A factory production line has two hidden states: Good (1) and Poor (2). Items produced are either Acceptable ($a$) or Unacceptable ($u$).

Transition matrix: $P = \begin{pmatrix} 0.9 & 0.1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ (once in Poor, it stays there).

Emission probabilities: $P(a \mid 1) = 0.99$, $P(u \mid 1) = 0.01$; $P(a \mid 2) = 0.96$, $P(u \mid 2) = 0.04$.

Initial distribution: $P(X_1 = 1) = 0.8$, $P(X_1 = 2) = 0.2$.

Observed sequence: $(s_1, s_2, s_3) = (a, u, a)$. Find $P(X_3 = 1 \mid S^3 = (a,u,a))$.

Step 1 — Initialise $F_1$
$$F_1(1) = 0.8 \times 0.99 = 0.792, \quad F_1(2) = 0.2 \times 0.96 = 0.192$$
Step 2 — Compute $F_2$ (signal $s_2 = u$)
\begin{align} F_2(1) &= P(u \mid 1)\left[P_{11} F_1(1) + P_{21} F_1(2)\right] = 0.01\,(0.9 \times 0.792 + 0 \times 0.192) = 0.01 \times 0.7128 = 0.007128\\ F_2(2) &= P(u \mid 2)\left[P_{12} F_1(1) + P_{22} F_1(2)\right] = 0.04\,(0.1 \times 0.792 + 1 \times 0.192) = 0.04 \times 0.2712 = 0.010848 \end{align}
Step 3 — Compute $F_3$ (signal $s_3 = a$)
\begin{align} F_3(1) &= P(a \mid 1)\left[P_{11} F_2(1) + P_{21} F_2(2)\right] = 0.99\,(0.9 \times 0.007128 + 0) = 0.99 \times 0.006415 \approx 0.006351\\ F_3(2) &= P(a \mid 2)\left[P_{12} F_2(1) + P_{22} F_2(2)\right] = 0.96\,(0.1 \times 0.007128 + 1 \times 0.010848) = 0.96 \times 0.011561 \approx 0.011098 \end{align}
Step 4 — Compute the posterior
$$P(X_3 = 1 \mid S^3 = (a,u,a)) = \frac{F_3(1)}{F_3(1) + F_3(2)} = \frac{0.006351}{0.006351 + 0.011098} = \frac{0.006351}{0.017449} \approx 0.364$$
Interpretation: after observing an unacceptable item on day 2, the probability that the line is in the Good state on day 3 drops dramatically — from the prior of 80% to about 36%. The single bad item is strong evidence of a regime shift toward Poor. Notice that state 2 is absorbing ($P_{21}=0$): once the process deteriorates, it never self-recovers — so even after a good item on day 3, the posterior on Good barely recovers. In the analogous financial setting: a single sharp VIX spike dramatically raises the posterior probability of being in a high-volatility regime that may persist.

The Viterbi Algorithm — Decoding the Hidden Sequence

Filtering gives the posterior on the current state. Sometimes we want the most probable entire sequence of states. Define: $$V_k(j) = \max_{i_1, \ldots, i_{k-1}} P(X_1 = i_1, \ldots, X_{k-1} = i_{k-1}, X_k = j,\; S^k = s_k)$$

This is the probability of the most likely path that ends in state $j$ at step $k$ and produced signals $s_1, \ldots, s_k$. By the same factorisation argument as the Forward Algorithm, we get a recursion:

Viterbi Recursion
Initialise: $V_1(j) = \pi_j \cdot P(s_1 \mid j)$ for all states $j$
Recursion for $k = 2, 3, \ldots, n$:
$$V_k(j) = P(s_k \mid j) \cdot \max_i\!\left[ P_{ij} \cdot V_{k-1}(i) \right]$$
Track: $\psi_k(j) = \arg\max_i\!\left[ P_{ij} \cdot V_{k-1}(i) \right]$ — the best predecessor of $j$ at step $k$
Decode: $\hat{i}_n = \arg\max_j V_n(j)$, then backtrack via $\hat{i}_{k-1} = \psi_k(\hat{i}_k)$
Output: the most probable hidden state sequence $\hat{i}_1, \hat{i}_2, \ldots, \hat{i}_n$
Viterbi is dynamic programming: the key insight is that the best path ending at state $j$ at step $k$ must have arrived there via the single best predecessor at step $k-1$ — so we only need to track the maximum, not sum over all predecessors. This reduces the search from exponential (all $|S|^n$ paths) to polynomial ($O(|S|^2 \cdot n)$) time.

Branching Processes — When Populations Die

A branching process models a population of individuals that reproduce and die. Each individual independently produces $j$ offspring with probability $P_j$ (the offspring distribution). The process $\{X_n\}_{n \geq 0}$ tracks the population size across generations.

In a branching process with offspring distribution $\{P_j : j \geq 0\}$: $$\mu = \sum_{j=0}^\infty j P_j \quad (\text{mean offspring per individual}), \qquad \sigma^2 = \sum_{j=0}^\infty (j - \mu)^2 P_j \quad (\text{variance})$$ Starting from $X_0 = 1$: $$E(X_n) = \mu^n, \qquad \text{Var}(X_n) = \begin{cases} \sigma^2 \mu^{n-1} \dfrac{1 - \mu^n}{1 - \mu} & \mu \neq 1 \\ n\sigma^2 & \mu = 1 \end{cases}$$
Derivation · $E(X_n) = \mu^n$ Tower Property
Key identity

Given $X_{n-1}$ individuals at generation $n-1$, generation $n$ is the sum of their independent offspring counts: $X_n = \sum_{i=1}^{X_{n-1}} Z_i$, where $Z_i \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \{P_j\}$ with $E(Z_i) = \mu$.

Apply the tower property
$$E(X_n) = E\!\left[E(X_n \mid X_{n-1})\right] = E\!\left[E\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^{X_{n-1}} Z_i \;\Big|\; X_{n-1}\right)\right] = E\!\left[\mu X_{n-1}\right] = \mu \cdot E(X_{n-1})$$
Recurse from $X_0 = 1$
$$E(X_1) = \mu, \quad E(X_2) = \mu^2, \quad \ldots \quad E(X_n) = \mu^n$$
$\mu < 1$: population expected to decay geometrically to 0. $\mu = 1$: population size is a martingale — expected to stay constant. $\mu > 1$: population expected to grow exponentially.

The Extinction Probability

Knowing $E(X_n)$ isn't enough — a population can have positive expected size but still go extinct with probability 1. The extinction probability $\Pi_0 = \lim_{n \to \infty} P(X_n = 0 \mid X_0 = 1)$ is the probability the population eventually dies out.

Theorem (Extinction Probability). $\Pi_0$ is the smallest positive solution to the fixed-point equation: $$\Pi_0 = \sum_{j=0}^\infty P_j \cdot \Pi_0^j = g(\Pi_0)$$ where $g(s) = \sum_{j=0}^\infty P_j s^j$ is the probability generating function of the offspring distribution. Moreover: $$\Pi_0 = 1 \;\iff\; \mu \leq 1, \qquad \Pi_0 < 1 \;\iff\; \mu > 1$$

The fixed-point equation has a beautiful derivation: if the ancestor has $j$ offspring, the population dies out iff all $j$ of the offspring's sub-populations die out independently. Each sub-population dies out with probability $\Pi_0$, so the probability of extinction given $j$ offspring is $\Pi_0^j$. Averaging over the offspring distribution:

$$\Pi_0 = \sum_{j=0}^\infty P_j \cdot \Pi_0^j$$
Worked Example 3 · Finding the Extinction Probability 🌱 Subcritical vs Supercritical

Case A (subcritical): $P_0 = \frac{1}{2}$, $P_1 = \frac{1}{8}$, $P_2 = \frac{3}{8}$.

Mean offspring: $\mu = 0 \cdot \frac{1}{2} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{8} + 2 \cdot \frac{3}{8} = \frac{7}{8} < 1$. By the theorem, $\Pi_0 = 1$ — certain extinction.

Case B (supercritical) — solving the fixed-point equation

$P_0 = \frac{3}{8}$, $P_1 = \frac{1}{8}$, $P_2 = \frac{1}{2}$. Mean: $\mu = 0 \cdot \frac{3}{8} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{8} + 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{9}{8} > 1$.

The fixed-point equation $\Pi_0 = g(\Pi_0)$ becomes:

$$\Pi_0 = \frac{3}{8} + \frac{1}{8}\Pi_0 + \frac{1}{2}\Pi_0^2$$

Multiply through by 8: $8\Pi_0 = 3 + \Pi_0 + 4\Pi_0^2$, giving $4\Pi_0^2 - 7\Pi_0 + 3 = 0$.

$$\Pi_0 = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{49 - 48}}{8} = \frac{7 \pm 1}{8} \implies \Pi_0 = 1 \text{ or } \Pi_0 = \frac{3}{4}$$
Take the smallest positive solution: $\Pi_0 = \frac{3}{4}$. Even though the population is supercritical (expected to grow), there is still a 75% chance of eventual extinction. $\Pi_0 = 1$ is also a solution but not the answer — the theorem specifies the smallest positive solution. Only in the subcritical ($\mu \leq 1$) case does the smallest positive solution coincide with 1.
Branching processes are the prototype for analysing any propagating structure: viral transmission (each infected person infects $j$ others with probability $P_j$), nuclear fission (each neutron creates $j$ new neutrons), startup idea propagation, word-of-mouth marketing. The critical threshold $\mu = 1$ separates extinction from persistence — and the extinction probability is never 0, even when $\mu > 1$. A pandemic, a nuclear reaction, or a market bubble can fizzle out even when the average reproduction number exceeds 1.

The colleague is right — $\boldsymbol{\pi} = \boldsymbol{\pi}P$ means: if you start with distribution $\boldsymbol{\pi}$, you stay in $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ forever. Convergence from arbitrary starts requires additional conditions (aperiodicity).
D
Both are wrong — $\boldsymbol{\pi} = \boldsymbol{\pi}P$ is just a computational definition with no probabilistic meaning.
Answer: C.

The stationarity equation $\boldsymbol{\pi} = \boldsymbol{\pi}P$ precisely means: if the chain's distribution at time $n$ is $\boldsymbol{\pi}$, then its distribution at time $n+1$ is also $\boldsymbol{\pi}$. It is a fixed-point condition — it says nothing about whether you converge to $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ from an arbitrary starting state.

Convergence from any start (the "mixing" property) requires the chain to also be aperiodic in addition to irreducible and positive recurrent. A periodic irreducible chain — e.g., one that alternates between two states — has a unique stationary distribution but doesn't converge to it from an arbitrary start: it oscillates. The stationarity equation is necessary but not sufficient for convergence. This distinction matters in MCMC, where the goal is to sample from $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ by running the chain long enough.

A two-state Markov chain (states 1 and 2) has transition matrix $P = \begin{pmatrix} 2/3 & 1/3 \\ 1/2 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix}$. What is the stationary distribution $(\pi_1, \pi_2)$?

A
$(\pi_1, \pi_2) = (1/2,\; 1/2)$ — all two-state chains are symmetric.
B
$(\pi_1, \pi_2) = (3/5,\; 2/5)$ — computed from $\pi_1 + \pi_2 = 1$ and $\pi_1 = \pi_1 \cdot \frac{2}{3} + \pi_2 \cdot \frac{1}{2}$.
C
$(\pi_1, \pi_2) = (2/3,\; 1/3)$ — the first row of $P$ is the stationary distribution.
D
$(\pi_1, \pi_2) = (1/3,\; 2/3)$ — computed by inverting the off-diagonal entries of $P$.
Answer: B.

Solve $\boldsymbol{\pi} = \boldsymbol{\pi}P$ with $\pi_1 + \pi_2 = 1$. The equation $\pi_1 = \pi_1 \cdot \frac{2}{3} + \pi_2 \cdot \frac{1}{2}$ gives $\pi_1 - \frac{2}{3}\pi_1 = \frac{1}{2}\pi_2$, so $\frac{1}{3}\pi_1 = \frac{1}{2}\pi_2$, meaning $\pi_1 = \frac{3}{2}\pi_2$. With $\pi_1 + \pi_2 = 1$: $\frac{3}{2}\pi_2 + \pi_2 = 1 \Rightarrow \pi_2 = \frac{2}{5}$, $\pi_1 = \frac{3}{5}$.

The mean return time to state 1 is $m_1 = 1/\pi_1 = 5/3$ steps, and to state 2 is $m_2 = 1/\pi_2 = 5/2$ steps. State 1 is visited more frequently (3/5 of the time) because it is "stickier" — once in state 1, there is a 2/3 probability of staying.

In the HMM Forward Algorithm, the quantity $F_n(j)$ computes which of the following?

A
$P(X_n = j)$ — the marginal probability of being in state $j$ at time $n$, ignoring observations.
B
$P(X_n = j \mid S^n = s_n)$ — the posterior probability of state $j$ given all observations so far.
C
$P(S^n = s_n,\; X_n = j)$ — the joint probability of the observation sequence AND being in state $j$ at time $n$.
D
$P(S_n = s_n \mid X_n = j)$ — the emission probability of the $n$-th signal given the current state.
Answer: C.

By definition, $F_n(j) = P(S^n = s_n,\; X_n = j)$ — the joint probability that the hidden chain is in state $j$ at step $n$ AND the first $n$ signals match the observed sequence. It is a joint, not a conditional, probability.

Option B (the posterior) is derived from $F_n(j)$ by normalising: $P(X_n = j \mid S^n = s_n) = F_n(j) / \sum_i F_n(i)$. Option D is just the emission probability $P(s_n \mid j)$ — one factor inside the recursion, not the full forward variable. The reason we compute the joint (C) rather than the posterior (B) directly is that the joint satisfies a clean recursion — and we can always normalise at the end.

A branching process has offspring distribution $P_0 = 1/4$, $P_1 = 1/2$, $P_2 = 1/4$. The mean offspring is $\mu = 0 \cdot \frac{1}{4} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2} + 2 \cdot \frac{1}{4} = 1$. What is the extinction probability $\Pi_0$?

A
$\Pi_0 = 1/2$ — since $P_0 = 1/4$ and survival requires at least one offspring.
B
$\Pi_0 = 3/4$ — the smallest positive root of the generating function equation.
C
$\Pi_0 = 1$ — since $\mu = 1$ (critical case), extinction is certain.
D
$\Pi_0 = 1/4$ — equals $P_0$ because extinction requires the first individual to have zero offspring.
Answer: C.

The theorem states: $\Pi_0 = 1$ if and only if $\mu \leq 1$. Here $\mu = 1$ (the critical case), so $\Pi_0 = 1$ — extinction is certain even though the population is expected to persist ($E(X_n) = \mu^n = 1^n = 1$ for all $n$).

This is a profound and counter-intuitive result. A critical branching process ($\mu = 1$) has constant expected population size — it is a martingale. Yet it goes extinct with probability 1. The resolution: while the expected size stays at 1, the variance grows as $\text{Var}(X_n) = n\sigma^2 \to \infty$. The distribution of $X_n$ spreads out — with high probability it hits 0 (absorbing), and a small probability of being very large. In the long run, the 0 absorbs everything. Critical systems look stable in expectation but are doomed almost surely.
