Continuous Distributions

When a random variable can take any value on an interval, probability mass becomes probability density. This chapter builds the continuous framework from scratch, works through five concrete examples, then develops the Exponential distribution in full — memoryless property, minimum of exponentials, and convolution leading to the Gamma.

Why Continuous Needs a Different Framework

For a discrete RV, we assigned a positive probability $p_i > 0$ to each outcome $x_i$. This works because the set of outcomes is at most countable — we can list them, and the sum $\sum p_i = 1$ is well-defined.

But if $X$ can take any value on an interval, the outcomes are uncountably many. There is no positive number $\varepsilon > 0$ small enough that $\varepsilon$ summed over uncountably many points stays $\leq 1$. So we cannot assign positive probability to individual points.

Instead of assigning mass to points, we assign density to regions. Probability becomes area under a curve, not a sum of spikes. The tool that connects density to probability is integration.

The PDF and CDF for Continuous RVs

A random variable $X$ is continuous if there exists a function $f(x) \geq 0$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$ such that: $$F(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t)\, dt$$ The function $f(x)$ is called the Probability Density Function (PDF).

From this definition, several facts follow immediately:

(i) $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx = 1$    (total probability is 1)

(ii) $P(a < X \leq b) = F(b) - F(a) = \displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$

(iii) $f(x) = F'(x)$    (PDF is the derivative of CDF, wherever it exists)

(iv) $P(X = x) = 0$ for every individual point $x$    (single points have zero width, hence zero area)
PDF $\neq$ PMF. The PMF $p_i$ is a probability and must satisfy $0 \leq p_i \leq 1$. The PDF $f(x)$ is a density — it can exceed 1. For example, Uniform$[0, \frac{1}{2}]$ has $f(x) = 2$ on $[0, \frac{1}{2}]$. The density is 2, but the total area is still $2 \times \frac{1}{2} = 1$. It is not a probability at a point — it is probability per unit length.
What does $f(x)$ actually mean? By Taylor's theorem, for small $\Delta x$: $$F(x + \Delta x) \approx F(x) + \Delta x \cdot F'(x) = F(x) + \Delta x \cdot f(x)$$ $$\therefore\; P(x \leq X \leq x + \Delta x) \approx f(x)\,\Delta x$$ The PDF at $x$ tells you: the probability of landing in a tiny interval of length $\Delta x$ around $x$ is approximately $f(x) \cdot \Delta x$. Density $\times$ length $=$ probability.

Convolution — PDF of a Sum

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent continuous RVs, the PDF of $Z = X + Y$ is their convolution:

$$f_Z(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x)\cdot f_Y(z-x)\,dx$$

For positive RVs, the limits become $\int_0^z$. This is the continuous analogue of the sum-over-cases we used for Binomial and Poisson.

Worked Examples — Verifying PDFs and Finding Constants

Q1 — $f(x) = 6x(1-x)$ on $(0,1)$

First verify: $\int_0^1 x(1-x)\,dx = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{6}$. So the normalising constant is $6$, giving $f(x) = 6x(1-x)$.

Find $P(X \leq \frac{1}{2})$:

$$F\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right) = 6\int_0^{1/2} x(1-x)\,dx = 6\left[\frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^{1/2} = 6\left(\frac{1}{8} - \frac{1}{24}\right) = 6 \cdot \frac{1}{12} = \frac{1}{2}$$

This is expected: $f(x) = 6x(1-x)$ is symmetric around $x = \frac{1}{2}$ on $(0,1)$, so exactly half the probability lies below $\frac{1}{2}$.

Q2 — $f(x) = C\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{6}x\right)$ on $[0,6]$

Substitute $y = \frac{\pi}{6}x$, so $dx = \frac{6}{\pi}dy$ and the limits become $[0, \pi]$:

$$\int_0^6 C\sin\!\left(\tfrac{\pi}{6}x\right)dx = C \cdot \frac{6}{\pi}\int_0^\pi \sin y\,dy = C \cdot \frac{6}{\pi}\cdot 2 = \frac{12C}{\pi}$$

Setting equal to 1: $C = \dfrac{\pi}{12}$.

Q3 — Piecewise linear PDF, find $C$

$f(x) = C(x+1)$ on $[-1,0]$, $C$ on $[0,1]$, $C(2-x)$ on $[1,2]$, zero elsewhere.

$$C\int_{-1}^0(x+1)\,dx + C\int_0^1 dx + C\int_1^2(2-x)\,dx = C\cdot\frac{1}{2} + C\cdot 1 + C\cdot\frac{1}{2} = 2C$$

Set $2C = 1$: $C = \dfrac{1}{2}$.

Q4 — Mixed Distribution

$P(X=1) = \frac{1}{6}$, $P(X=2) = \frac{1}{4}$, $P(X=3) = \frac{1}{3}$, and $X$ is uniform on $(3,5]$ with constant density $C$.

Total probability = 1:

$$\frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{3} + \int_3^5 C\,dx = \frac{9}{12} + 2C = 1 \implies C = \frac{1}{8}$$

This is a mixed distribution: probability mass at discrete points plus a continuous density on an interval. The CDF has jumps at $x=1,2,3$ and a linear ramp on $(3,5]$.

Q5 — $f(x) = e^{-x}$ on $(0,\infty)$
$$\int_0^\infty e^{-x}\,dx = \left[-e^{-x}\right]_0^\infty = 0 - (-1) = 1 \checkmark$$

This is a valid PDF on an infinite interval — our first example where $X$ is unbounded above.

The Exponential Distribution

$X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, $\lambda > 0$. PDF and CDF: $$f(x) = \begin{cases}\lambda e^{-\lambda x} & x > 0 \\ 0 & x \leq 0\end{cases} \qquad F(x) = \begin{cases}1 - e^{-\lambda x} & x > 0 \\ 0 & x \leq 0\end{cases}$$ $X$ models waiting times: time until the first event in a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$.
In options trading: time until the next large move ($> 2\sigma$) on the Nifty is approximately exponential. In order flow: inter-arrival times between large institutional orders follow an exponential distribution. The memoryless property means the time you've already waited gives zero information about when the next event will occur.

Property 1 — Memoryless

$$P(X \leq a+t \mid X > a) = P(X \leq t)$$ Given that $X$ has already exceeded $a$, the distribution of the remaining excess is identical to starting fresh.
$$P(X \leq a+t \mid X > a) = \frac{P(a < X \leq a+t)}{P(X > a)} = \frac{F(a+t) - F(a)}{1 - F(a)}$$ Substitute $F(x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}$: $$= \frac{(1-e^{-\lambda(a+t)}) - (1-e^{-\lambda a})}{e^{-\lambda a}} = \frac{e^{-\lambda a} - e^{-\lambda(a+t)}}{e^{-\lambda a}} = \frac{e^{-\lambda a}(1 - e^{-\lambda t})}{e^{-\lambda a}} = 1 - e^{-\lambda t} = P(X \leq t) \quad\square$$
The Exponential is the continuous analogue of the Geometric distribution. Both are memoryless — the only distributions with this property in their respective domains (continuous and discrete). A Geometric RV counts discrete trials until the first success; an Exponential RV measures continuous time until the first event.

Property 2 — Minimum of Independent Exponentials

If $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ are independent with $X_i \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda_i)$, then: $$\min(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n) \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \cdots + \lambda_n)$$
Let $Z = \min(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$. The event $\{Z > z\}$ = "all $X_i > z$": $$P(Z > z) = P(X_1 > z, \ldots, X_n > z) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(X_i > z) = \prod_{i=1}^n e^{-\lambda_i z} = e^{-(\lambda_1+\cdots+\lambda_n)z}$$ So $F_Z(z) = 1 - e^{-(\sum \lambda_i)z}$, and differentiating: $f_Z(z) = \left(\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i\right)e^{-(\sum \lambda_i)z}$, which is the Exp$(\sum \lambda_i)$ PDF. $\square$
This is the competing risks model. Suppose $n$ independent "events" can occur — each with its own Exponential rate. The time until the first event is Exp with rate equal to the sum. Applied to options: if there are multiple catalysts that could trigger a market move (RBI policy, earnings, global risk-off), each arriving at its own rate, the time until the first catalyst is Exp(total rate). This is why implied volatility aggregates across events.

Property 3 — Sum of Exponentials and the Gamma

If $X_1, X_2 \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$ independent, what is the PDF of $X_1 + X_2$?

Using the convolution formula with $f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ for $x > 0$: $$f_{X_1+X_2}(z) = \int_0^z \lambda e^{-\lambda x} \cdot \lambda e^{-\lambda(z-x)}\,dx = \lambda^2 \int_0^z e^{-\lambda z}\,dx = \lambda^2 z e^{-\lambda z}$$ This is the PDF of a Gamma(2, $\lambda$) distribution. More generally: $$X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda), \quad\text{with PDF } \frac{\lambda^n x^{n-1} e^{-\lambda x}}{(n-1)!}$$ Check $n=2$: $\frac{\lambda^2 x^1 e^{-\lambda x}}{1!} = \lambda^2 x e^{-\lambda x}$ — matches. $\square$
The sum of $n$ independent Exp($\lambda$) waiting times is the total time until the $n$th event. This is the Gamma distribution — it models aggregate waiting time in a Poisson process. The Exponential is the special case $n=1$.

$f(x) = 3x^2$ for $x \in [0,1]$, zero elsewhere. What is $P(X \leq 0.5)$?

A
$0.5$
B
$0.125$
C
$0.75$
D
$0.25$
Answer: B — $0.125$

$P(X \leq 0.5) = \int_0^{0.5} 3x^2\,dx = [x^3]_0^{0.5} = (0.5)^3 = 0.125$. The CDF here is $F(x) = x^3$, a power-law that concentrates most probability near 1, not near 0. Only 12.5% of the probability lies below the midpoint.

Can a PDF $f(x)$ take the value $f(x) = 5$ at some point $x$?

A
No — probabilities can never exceed 1
B
Yes — $f(x)$ is a density, not a probability; it only needs $f(x) \geq 0$ and $\int f = 1$
C
Only if the distribution is uniform
D
Only on infinite support intervals
Answer: B

$f(x)$ is probability per unit length, not probability itself. The constraint is $f(x) \geq 0$ everywhere and $\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\,dx = 1$. There is no upper bound on $f(x)$ at any individual point. Example: Uniform$[0, \frac{1}{5}]$ has $f(x) = 5$ throughout — perfectly valid, since the total area is $5 \times \frac{1}{5} = 1$.
probability / ch10 / q03★★ Medium

$X \sim \text{Exp}(2)$. You observe that $X > 3$. What is $P(X \leq 3.5 \mid X > 3)$?

A
$F(3.5) - F(3) = e^{-6} - e^{-7}$
B
$F(3.5) = 1 - e^{-7}$
C
$P(X \leq 0.5) = 1 - e^{-1}$
D
$0$ — $X > 3$ means $X$ can no longer be $\leq 3.5$
Answer: C — $1 - e^{-1}$

By the memoryless property: $P(X \leq a+t \mid X > a) = P(X \leq t)$. Here $a=3$, $t=0.5$: $$P(X \leq 3.5 \mid X > 3) = P(X \leq 0.5) = 1 - e^{-2 \times 0.5} = 1 - e^{-1} \approx 0.632$$ The 3 units already waited are forgotten. We start fresh with Exp(2) from zero.

Three independent order streams arrive at rates $\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = 2$, $\lambda_3 = 3$ orders/sec (each Exponential). What is the distribution of the time until the first order arrives from any stream?

A
Exp(1) — use the slowest stream
B
Exp(6) — minimum of independent exponentials
C
Exp(2) — use the average rate
D
Not exponential — minimum of different distributions is complex
Answer: B — Exp(6)

Property 2: $\min(X_1, X_2, X_3) \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \lambda_3) = \text{Exp}(1+2+3) = \text{Exp}(6)$. The combined rate is 6 orders/sec. The first order arrives 6 times faster than a single stream with rate 1. This is why aggregating liquidity from multiple venues (NSE + BSE + SGX) dramatically reduces average waiting time for execution.

$X_1, X_2, X_3$ are independent Exp($\lambda$). The PDF of $X_1 + X_2 + X_3$ is $\frac{\lambda^3 x^2 e^{-\lambda x}}{2}$. What is the name of this distribution, and what is its relationship to the Exponential?

A
Normal distribution — by the Central Limit Theorem
B
Poisson distribution — since exponentials model Poisson inter-arrivals
C
Gamma(3, $\lambda$) — sum of $n$ independent Exp($\lambda$) gives Gamma($n$, $\lambda$)
D
Another Exponential with rate $3\lambda$
Answer: C — Gamma(3, $\lambda$)

The sum of $n$ independent Exp($\lambda$) RVs follows Gamma($n$, $\lambda$) with PDF $\frac{\lambda^n x^{n-1} e^{-\lambda x}}{(n-1)!}$. For $n=3$: $\frac{\lambda^3 x^2 e^{-\lambda x}}{2!} = \frac{\lambda^3 x^2 e^{-\lambda x}}{2}$ — exactly what we have. Gamma(3, $\lambda$) is the waiting time until the 3rd event in a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$. Option D is wrong: the minimum of exponentials gives another exponential, but the sum does not.
