Distributions, Expectation & Limit Theorems

Continuous random variables, four key distributions, what "expectation" actually means, joint distributions, covariance, moment generating functions, the Law of Total Expectation, and why the Central Limit Theorem is the most important theorem in applied probability.

Continuous Random Variables

In Chapter 1 we had discrete RVs — finitely or countably many outcomes. Now we go continuous. A random variable $X$ is continuous if there exists a non-negative function $f(x)$ — the probability density function (PDF) — such that:

$$\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\,dx = 1 \qquad \text{and} \qquad P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$$
For a continuous RV, $P(X = a) = 0$ for any specific value $a$ — you can't hit an exact real number. But for a tiny window of width $\varepsilon$ around $a$: $$P\!\left(a - \tfrac{\varepsilon}{2} \le X \le a + \tfrac{\varepsilon}{2}\right) \approx \varepsilon \cdot f(a)$$ So $f(a)$ measures density of probability near $a$, not probability itself. Think of it like a crowd: density is people per square metre, not the number at an exact point.

The CDF and PDF are related by:

$$F(a) = P(X \le a) = \int_{-\infty}^a f(x)\,dx \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad \frac{d}{da}F(a) = f(a)$$

The Four Key Continuous Distributions

1. Uniform — $X \sim U[\alpha, \beta]$

Equal probability density everywhere in $[\alpha, \beta]$. The "I have no idea" distribution.

$$f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\beta - \alpha}, \quad \alpha < x < \beta \qquad F_X(a) = \frac{a - \alpha}{\beta - \alpha}, \quad \alpha \le a \le \beta$$

2. Exponential — $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$

Models waiting times between events. The continuous analogue of the Geometric — and just as memoryless.

$$f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x > 0 \qquad F_X(a) = 1 - e^{-\lambda a}, \quad a > 0$$

3. Gamma — $X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \lambda)$

The sum of $\alpha$ independent Exp$(\lambda)$ random variables. Models the time until the $\alpha$-th event in a Poisson process.

$$f_X(x) = \frac{\lambda e^{-\lambda x}(\lambda x)^{\alpha-1}}{\Gamma(\alpha)}, \quad x > 0 \qquad \Gamma(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty e^{-x} x^{\alpha-1}\,dx$$

When $\alpha$ is a positive integer: $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$

4. Normal — $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$

The bell curve. The central limit theorem will tell us why it's everywhere.

$$f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \quad -\infty < x < \infty$$
Daily returns on Nifty 50 are often modelled as $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ as a first approximation. The real distribution has fat tails — more extreme moves than normal predicts — which is why Black-Scholes underprices deep OTM options. You'll see this precisely in Chapter 12. For now, think of normal as the "default" continuous distribution that appears whenever many small independent effects add up.

Expectation: The Long-Run Average

The expectation $E(X)$ is the probability-weighted average of all values $X$ can take:

$$E(X) = \begin{cases} \displaystyle\sum_{x} x\, p(x) & \text{discrete} \\[1em] \displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty x\, f(x)\,dx & \text{continuous} \end{cases}$$

For a function $g(X)$ of a random variable — the Law of the Unconscious Statistician (LOTUS):

$$E(g(X)) = \sum_x g(x)\,p(x) \quad \text{or} \quad \int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x)\,f(x)\,dx$$

Key linearity property: $E(aX + b) = aE(X) + b$ for any constants $a, b$.

Variance: How Spread Out Is $X$?

$$\text{Var}(X) = E\!\left[(X - E(X))^2\right] = E(X^2) - (E(X))^2$$
Shortcut formula derivation:
$\text{Var}(X) = E[(X - \mu)^2] = E[X^2 - 2\mu X + \mu^2] = E(X^2) - 2\mu^2 + \mu^2 = E(X^2) - \mu^2$   $\square$

Where $\mu = E(X)$. The shortcut $E(X^2) - (E(X))^2$ is almost always easier to compute.

Notice that for Poisson$(\lambda)$, the mean equals the variance — both are $\lambda$. This is a signature property of the Poisson distribution. If you're looking at count data and the sample mean roughly equals the sample variance, Poisson is a natural first model.

Joint Distributions

When we have two random variables $X$ and $Y$, we need their joint distribution to understand how they interact — not just their individual (marginal) distributions.

For jointly continuous $(X, Y)$ with joint PDF $f_{XY}(x,y)$:

$$\int_{-\infty}^\infty\!\!\int_{-\infty}^\infty f_{XY}(x,y)\,dx\,dy = 1 \qquad P(X \in A, Y \in B) = \int_B\!\!\int_A f_{XY}(x,y)\,dx\,dy$$

The marginal PDFs are obtained by integrating out the other variable:

$$f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f_{XY}(x,y)\,dy \qquad f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f_{XY}(x,y)\,dx$$

$X$ and $Y$ are independent if and only if their joint PDF factorises:

$$f_{XY}(x,y) = f_X(x)\cdot f_Y(y)$$

Linearity of expectation holds regardless of dependence:

$$E(X + Y) = E(X) + E(Y) \qquad \text{always true, even if } X,Y \text{ are dependent}$$

Covariance: How Do $X$ and $Y$ Move Together?

$$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - E(X))(Y - E(Y))] = E(XY) - E(X)E(Y)$$

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent then $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$. But the converse is false:

Zero covariance does not imply independence. Let $X \sim U[-1,1]$ and $Y = X^2$. Then $E(X) = 0$ and $E(XY) = E(X^3) = \int_{-1}^1 x^3\,dx = 0$, so $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$. Yet clearly $Y$ is completely determined by $X$ — they're maximally dependent! Covariance only captures linear dependence.

Key variance formula for sums:

$$\text{Var}\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) + 2\sum_{i < j} \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$$
This formula is the foundation of portfolio variance. If you hold $n$ assets with returns $X_1, \ldots, X_n$, the variance of portfolio return is not just the sum of individual variances — the cross-covariance terms matter enormously. When all assets are perfectly correlated, portfolio variance doesn't reduce at all. This is why diversification works only when assets are uncorrelated (or negatively correlated).

Moment Generating Functions (MGFs)

The MGF of $X$ is $\phi_X(t) = E(e^{tX})$. It "generates" all moments:

$$\left.\frac{d^n}{dt^n}\phi_X(t)\right|_{t=0} = E(X^n) \qquad \text{(the } n\text{-th raw moment)}$$

The killer property: if $X$ and $Y$ are independent,

$$\phi_{X+Y}(t) = \phi_X(t)\cdot\phi_Y(t)$$
🎲 Worked Example — Sum of Binomials

Let $X_1 \sim \text{Binomial}(n_1, p)$ and $X_2 \sim \text{Binomial}(n_2, p)$ be independent. What is the distribution of $X_1 + X_2$?

The MGF of Binomial$(n, p)$ is $\phi(t) = (pe^t + (1-p))^n$. So:

$$\phi_{X_1+X_2}(t) = (pe^t + (1-p))^{n_1} \cdot (pe^t + (1-p))^{n_2} = (pe^t + (1-p))^{n_1+n_2}$$

This is the MGF of Binomial$(n_1 + n_2, p)$. So the sum of independent Binomials with the same $p$ is also Binomial.

☁️ Worked Example — Sum of Poissons

If $X_1 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1)$ and $X_2 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_2)$ are independent, then $X_1 + X_2 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)$.

Proof by direct convolution:

$$P(X_1+X_2 = n) = \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{e^{-\lambda_1}\lambda_1^k}{k!}\cdot\frac{e^{-\lambda_2}\lambda_2^{n-k}}{(n-k)!} = \frac{e^{-(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)}}{n!}\sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}\lambda_1^k\lambda_2^{n-k} = \frac{e^{-(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)}(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)^n}{n!}$$

And a beautiful bonus: given $X_1 + X_2 = n$, the conditional distribution of $X_1$ is Binomial$(n, \frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda_1+\lambda_2})$. The Poisson rates determine the "share" of total events.

Conditional Expectation

The conditional expectation $E(X \mid Y = y)$ is the expected value of $X$ given that $Y$ took the value $y$. For continuous random variables:

$$f_{X|Y}(x \mid y) = \frac{f_{XY}(x,y)}{f_Y(y)} \qquad E(X \mid Y=y) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x\, f_{X|Y}(x \mid y)\,dx$$

Now here's the subtle and powerful idea: as $y$ varies, $E(X \mid Y=y)$ changes. So $E(X \mid Y)$ is itself a random variable — a function of $Y$.

Law of Total Expectation (Tower Property): $$E(E(X \mid Y)) = E(X)$$ Law of Total Variance: $$\text{Var}(X) = E(\text{Var}(X \mid Y)) + \text{Var}(E(X \mid Y))$$
Proof of Law of Total Expectation (continuous case):
$$E(E(X|Y)) = \int_y E(X|Y=y)\,f_Y(y)\,dy = \int_y \int_x x\,f_{X|Y}(x|y)\,f_Y(y)\,dx\,dy$$ $$= \int_x x \underbrace{\int_y f_{XY}(x,y)\,dy}_{= f_X(x)}\,dx = \int_x x\,f_X(x)\,dx = E(X) \quad \square$$
🎯 Worked Example — Mean of Geometric via Conditioning

Let $N \sim \text{Geometric}(p)$ = number of trials to get first success. Derive $E(N)$ using conditioning.

Define $Y = 1$ if the first trial succeeds, $Y = 0$ otherwise.

$E(N|Y=1) = 1$    (already done in one trial)

$E(N|Y=0) = 1 + E(N)$    (one failed trial, then start fresh)

$$E(N) = E(N|Y=1)\cdot P(Y=1) + E(N|Y=0)\cdot P(Y=0) = 1\cdot p + (1+E(N))\cdot(1-p)$$ $$E(N) = p + (1-p) + (1-p)E(N) \implies pE(N) = 1 \implies E(N) = \frac{1}{p} \quad \square$$

No calculus. Pure conditioning. This technique — conditioning on the first step — is the workhorse of stochastic processes.

📦 Worked Example — Compound Random Variable

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be iid with mean $\mu$. Let $N$ (independent of the $X_i$'s) be a random count. Define $S = \sum_{i=1}^N X_i$.

Find $E(S)$.

Condition on $N$: $E(S|N=n) = E\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = n\mu$, so $E(S|N) = N\mu$.

$$E(S) = E(E(S|N)) = E(N\mu) = \mu\, E(N)$$

Elegant: the expected total is just (expected count) × (expected size per item). This compound structure appears everywhere — total insurance claims, total trade PnL over random number of trades.

Order Statistics

Given $n$ iid continuous RVs $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ with CDF $F$ and PDF $f$, sort them: $X_{(1)} \le X_{(2)} \le \cdots \le X_{(n)}$. These are the order statistics.

The PDF of the $i$-th order statistic $X_{(i)}$ is:

$$f_{X_{(i)}}(x) = \frac{n!}{(i-1)!(n-i)!} [F(x)]^{i-1}[1-F(x)]^{n-i} f(x)$$
The formula makes intuitive sense: to be the $i$-th smallest, exactly $i-1$ of the $n$ values must fall below $x$ (probability $[F(x)]^{i-1}$), exactly $n-i$ must fall above $x$ (probability $[1-F(x)]^{n-i}$), and one value must be exactly at $x$ (density $f(x)$). The multinomial coefficient $\frac{n!}{(i-1)!(n-i)!}$ counts the arrangements.
In limit order books, the best bid is the maximum of all buy orders — the $n$-th order statistic of bid prices. The best ask is the minimum of all sell orders — the 1st order statistic. The bid-ask spread is $X_{(n)}^{\text{sell}} - X_{(1)}^{\text{buy}}$. Understanding order statistics tells you how spreads behave as market depth $n$ changes.

The Strong Law of Large Numbers (SLLN)

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be iid with $E(X_i) = \mu$. Then:

$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n}{n} \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} \mu \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

"Almost surely" (a.s.) means: with probability 1, the sample average converges to the true mean. This is the mathematical justification for the frequentist interpretation of probability — the relative frequency of an event converges to its probability.

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

This is the most important theorem in applied probability. Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be iid with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. Then:

$$\frac{X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{D} N(0, 1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$
The CLT says: regardless of the distribution of each $X_i$ (as long as variance is finite), their standardised sum converges to a standard normal. This is why the normal distribution appears everywhere — whenever you're averaging many small independent effects, the result is approximately normal. Stock returns (sum of many micro-trades), measurement errors, noise in models — all approximately normal by CLT.
Heuristic MGF Proof (assume $\mu=0, \sigma^2=1$ for simplicity):

The MGF of $S_n / \sqrt{n}$ is $\phi_{S_n/\sqrt{n}}(t) = [\phi_{X_1}(t/\sqrt{n})]^n$.

For large $n$, Taylor expand: $e^{(t/\sqrt{n})x} \approx 1 + \frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}x + \frac{t^2}{2n}x^2$, so $\phi_{X_1}(t/\sqrt{n}) \approx 1 + \frac{t^2}{2n}$.

Therefore: $\phi_{S_n/\sqrt{n}}(t) \approx \left(1 + \frac{t^2}{2n}\right)^n \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} e^{t^2/2}$

And $e^{t^2/2}$ is exactly the MGF of $N(0,1)$.   $\square$
📊 Worked Example — CLT in Action

$X \sim \text{Binomial}(40, 1/2)$. Compute $P(X = 20)$ using CLT.

$\mu = np = 20$, $\sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)} = \sqrt{10}$. With continuity correction:

$$P(X=20) \approx P\!\left(\frac{19.5 - 20}{\sqrt{10}} < Z < \frac{20.5 - 20}{\sqrt{10}}\right) = P(-\alpha < Z < \alpha)$$

where $\alpha = 0.5/\sqrt{10} \approx 0.158$. Using normal tables: $\approx 2\Phi(0.158) - 1 \approx 0.1272$.

Exact answer: $\binom{40}{20}(1/2)^{40} = 0.1254$. CLT gives $0.1272$ — about 1.4% error with $n=40$. Not bad.

When backtesting a strategy over $n$ trades, the total PnL is approximately $N(n\mu, n\sigma^2)$ by CLT — even if individual trade PnL is not normally distributed. This is why Sharpe ratios (which assume normality) are more reliable over longer backtests. With $n=252$ trading days, the CLT approximation is excellent for most return distributions.

A trade execution time $X$ follows $\text{Exp}(\lambda = 2)$ (in seconds). What is the probability that execution takes more than 1 second?

A
$1 - e^{-1} \approx 0.632$
B
$e^{-2} \approx 0.135$
C
$e^{-1} \approx 0.368$
D
$2e^{-2} \approx 0.271$
Answer: B — $e^{-2} \approx 0.135$

For $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$, the survival function is: $$P(X > t) = 1 - F(t) = 1 - (1 - e^{-\lambda t}) = e^{-\lambda t}$$ With $\lambda = 2$ and $t = 1$: $P(X > 1) = e^{-2} \approx 0.135$.

Option A is $P(X \le 1)$ — the CDF, not the survival. Option C confuses $\lambda = 1$. The exponential survival function $e^{-\lambda t}$ is one of the most useful formulas in stochastic processes — memorise it.

Two trading strategies have daily PnL $X$ and $Y$ with $\text{Var}(X) = 4$, $\text{Var}(Y) = 9$, and $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = -3$. What is $\text{Var}(X + Y)$?

A
$13$
B
$7$
C
$7$ ... wait — $4 + 9 + 2(-3) = 7$
D
$1$
Answer: C — $7$

$$\text{Var}(X+Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2\,\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 4 + 9 + 2(-3) = 7$$ Option A ($= 13$) ignores the covariance term — a common error. The negative covariance reduces total variance, which is exactly the benefit of combining negatively correlated strategies. Negative covariance = natural hedge. This is diversification working mathematically.
stochastic / ch02 / q03 ★★ Medium

$X \sim \text{Poisson}(3)$ and $Y \sim \text{Poisson}(5)$ are independent. A trader sees $X + Y = 10$ total events. What is the conditional expected number of events from source $X$?

A
$3$
B
$\dfrac{10 \times 3}{3+5} = 3.75$
C
$5$
D
$10/2 = 5$
Answer: B — $3.75$

From the worked example: given $X_1+X_2 = n$, we have $X_1 | (X_1+X_2=n) \sim \text{Binomial}(n, \frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda_1+\lambda_2})$.

Here $n=10$, $\lambda_1=3$, $\lambda_2=5$: $$E(X \mid X+Y=10) = 10 \times \frac{3}{3+5} = 10 \times \frac{3}{8} = 3.75$$ The rates $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ act as "weights" — source $X$ contributes its proportional share $\lambda_1/(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)$ of total events.
stochastic / ch02 / q04 ★★ Medium

Which statement about the Law of Total Expectation is correct?

A
$E(X|Y) = E(X)$ always
B
$E(X|Y)$ is a number, not a random variable
C
$E(E(X|Y)) = E(X)$, and $E(X|Y)$ is itself a random variable (a function of $Y$)
D
$E(X|Y) = E(Y|X)$ always
Answer: C

$E(X|Y=y)$ is a number for each fixed $y$. But as $y$ varies, it defines a function — so $E(X|Y)$ as a function of the random variable $Y$ is itself random. Taking its expectation gives back $E(X)$: $$E(E(X|Y)) = E(X)$$ Option A says conditioning never changes expectations — false in general (true only when $X,Y$ are independent). Option B is the common confusion: $E(X|Y=y)$ is a number but $E(X|Y)$ is a RV. The tower property $E(E(X|Y)) = E(X)$ is the single most-used tool in computing expectations in stochastic processes.
stochastic / ch02 / q05 ★★★ Hard

A quant strategy has daily PnL $X_i$ with mean $\mu =$ ₹500 and standard deviation $\sigma =$ ₹2000. Over 252 trading days, by the CLT, the total annual PnL is approximately normal with what parameters?

A
$N(500,\ 2000^2)$
B
$N(126000,\ 2000^2)$
C
$N(126000,\ 252 \times 2000^2)$
D
$N(126000,\ \sqrt{252} \times 2000)$
Answer: C — $N(126000,\ 252 \times 2000^2)$

Total PnL $S = \sum_{i=1}^{252} X_i$.

$E(S) = 252 \times 500 = 126{,}000$ (linearity of expectation)
$\text{Var}(S) = 252 \times \text{Var}(X_i) = 252 \times 2000^2$ (independence, so variances add)

By CLT, $S \approx N(126000,\ 252 \times 4{,}000{,}000)$.

Option D confuses variance and standard deviation. The standard deviation of $S$ is $\sqrt{252} \times 2000 \approx 31{,}749$ — but the variance is $252 \times 2000^2$. Variance scales with $n$; standard deviation scales with $\sqrt{n}$ — the square-root-of-time rule familiar from options pricing.
