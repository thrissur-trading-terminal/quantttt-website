Central Limit Theorem — Introduction

The CLT is the deepest result in probability: standardised sums of iid random variables converge in distribution to $N(0,1)$, regardless of the original distribution. This chapter motivates the theorem via MGF limits, derives two special cases (Poisson and Binomial both converge to Normal), and states the general CLT.

From LLN to CLT: What More Is There?

The LLN says $\bar{X}_n\xrightarrow{P}\mu$ — the average converges to a point. But it says nothing about the shape of the distribution of $\bar{X}_n$ or $S_n = \sum X_i$. The CLT answers: what distribution does $S_n$ approach as $n\to\infty$?

Asymptotic distribution: $S_n$ has asymptotic distribution $F$ if $P(S_n\leq x)\to F(x)$ as $n\to\infty$ at all continuity points of $F$, i.e. $S_n\xrightarrow{d}X$ where $X$ has CDF $F$.

Strategy: By the MGF uniqueness theorem, it suffices to show $M_{S_n}(t)\to M_X(t)$ for all $t$ in a neighbourhood of 0.
Warning: MGF limits can fail

$X_n = n$ with probability 1: $M_{X_n}(t) = e^{nt}$. As $n\to\infty$: $e^{nt}\to\infty$ for $t>0$ and $\to 0$ for $t<0$. The limit is not a valid MGF. This happens when probability escapes to infinity — exactly the pathological case from Ch24.

Example 1: Bin$(n,p)$ with $np=\lambda$ $\to$ Poisson (MGF method)

$X_n\sim\text{Bin}(n,\lambda/n)$. Show $X_n\xrightarrow{d}\text{Poi}(\lambda)$.

$M_{X_n}(t) = \left(q+pe^t\right)^n = \left(1-\frac{\lambda}{n}+\frac{\lambda}{n}e^t\right)^n = \left(1-\frac{\lambda(1-e^t)}{n}\right)^n$

Using $\lim_{n\to\infty}(1+x/n)^n = e^x$:

$$\lim_{n\to\infty}M_{X_n}(t) = e^{\lambda(e^t-1)} = \text{MGF of Poi}(\lambda) \qquad \checkmark$$

Example 2: Poi$(n)$ Standardised $\to$ $N(0,1)$

$X_n\sim\text{Poi}(n)$. Show $Y_n = (X_n-n)/\sqrt{n}\xrightarrow{d}N(0,1)$.

$E(X_n)=\text{Var}(X_n)=n$, so $Y_n$ is standardised. Using $M_{aX+b}(t) = e^{bt}M_X(at)$:

$$M_{Y_n}(t) = e^{-\sqrt{n}\,t}\cdot M_{X_n}\!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right) = e^{-\sqrt{n}\,t}\cdot e^{n(e^{t/\sqrt{n}}-1)}$$

Expand $e^{t/\sqrt{n}} = 1 + \frac{t}{\sqrt{n}} + \frac{t^2}{2n} + \frac{t^3}{6n^{3/2}} + \cdots$, so $e^{t/\sqrt{n}}-1 = \frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}+\frac{t^2}{2n}+O(n^{-3/2})$:

$$M_{Y_n}(t) = e^{-\sqrt{n}\,t}\cdot\exp\!\left(n\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}+\frac{t^2}{2n}+O(n^{-3/2})\right)\right) = e^{-\sqrt{n}\,t}\cdot e^{\sqrt{n}\,t+t^2/2+O(n^{-1/2})}$$ $$= e^{t^2/2+O(n^{-1/2})} \to e^{t^2/2} \quad\text{as }n\to\infty$$

This is the MGF of $N(0,1)$. Therefore $Y_n\xrightarrow{d}N(0,1)$. ✓

Note: Poi$(n)$ takes values in $\{0,1,2,\ldots\}$, but after standardising by $\sqrt{n}$, the support $\{-\sqrt{n}, -\sqrt{n}+1/\sqrt{n}, \ldots\}$ fills $(-\infty,\infty)$ as $n\to\infty$, making the Normal approximation sensible.

Example 3: Bin$(n,p)$ Standardised $\to$ $N(0,1)$

$X_n\sim\text{Bin}(n,p)$. Show $Y_n = (X_n-np)/\sqrt{npq}\xrightarrow{d}N(0,1)$.

$E(Y_n)=0$, $\text{Var}(Y_n)=1$. Using $M_{aX+b}(t) = e^{bt}M_X(at)$ with $b=-np/\sqrt{npq}$ and $a=1/\sqrt{npq}$:

$$M_{Y_n}(t) = e^{-\sqrt{np/q}\,t}\cdot\left(q+pe^{t/\sqrt{npq}}\right)^n = \left(qe^{-pt/\sqrt{npq}} + pe^{qt/\sqrt{npq}}\right)^n$$

Let $u = t/\sqrt{npq}$. Expand each exponential to order $u^2 = t^2/(npq)$:

$$qe^{-pu} = q\!\left(1-pu+\frac{p^2u^2}{2}+O(u^3)\right), \quad pe^{qu} = p\!\left(1+qu+\frac{q^2u^2}{2}+O(u^3)\right)$$ Adding (and using $p+q=1$, $pq\cdot u^2 = t^2/n$): $$M_{Y_n}(t) = \left(1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})\right)^n$$ Taking logs: $\log M_{Y_n}(t) = n\log\!\left(1+\frac{t^2}{2n}+\cdots\right)\approx n\cdot\frac{t^2}{2n} = \frac{t^2}{2}$. $$\lim_{n\to\infty}M_{Y_n}(t) = e^{t^2/2} = \text{MGF of }N(0,1) \qquad \checkmark$$

The General CLT

General CLT (Lindeberg-Lévy): If $X_1, X_2,\ldots$ are independent (not necessarily iid) with $E(X_i)=\mu_i$ and $\text{Var}(X_i)=\sigma_i^2$, then under suitable conditions (Lindeberg condition): $$S_n = X_1+\cdots+X_n \;\xrightarrow{d}\; N\!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n\mu_i,\;\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_i^2\right)$$ iid case (to be proved in Ch30): If $X_1, X_2,\ldots$ iid with $E(X_i)=\mu$ and $\text{Var}(X_i)=\sigma^2<\infty$: $$\frac{S_n - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\xrightarrow{d}N(0,1)$$ This is independent of the original distribution of $X_i$ — Normal, Exponential, Bernoulli, or any distribution with finite variance.
The CLT is the theorem behind every confidence interval, every $t$-test, every $p$-value in quantitative finance. On NSE: the daily PnL of a strategy with iid trades (each with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$) has the distribution of its monthly total approximately $N(20\mu, 20\sigma^2)$ — regardless of whether individual trades are Normal, skewed, or bimodal. This is why Gaussian assumptions are ubiquitous in risk models even when individual returns are clearly non-Normal: the CLT justifies them at the portfolio/aggregate level.

probability / ch29 / q01★ Easy

$X_1,\ldots,X_n$ iid with $E(X_i)=2$ and $\text{Var}(X_i)=4$. By the CLT, what is the approximate distribution of $\bar{X}_n = S_n/n$ for large $n$?

A
$N(2, 4)$ — same as each $X_i$
B
$N(2, 4/n)$ — mean 2, variance $\sigma^2/n$
C
$N(0, 1)$ — CLT always gives standard Normal
D
$N(2n, 4n)$ — for $S_n$, not $\bar{X}_n$
Answer: B — $N(2, 4/n)$

$E(\bar{X}_n)=\mu=2$, $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n)=\sigma^2/n=4/n$. By CLT: $\bar{X}_n\approx N(2, 4/n)$. Option C is the standardised version: $(\bar{X}_n-2)/(2/\sqrt{n})\approx N(0,1)$. Option D gives the distribution of $S_n = n\bar{X}_n$, not $\bar{X}_n$.
probability / ch29 / q02★★ Medium

$X_n\sim\text{Bin}(n, 1/n)$. As $n\to\infty$, $X_n\xrightarrow{d}$ ?

A
$N(0,1)$ — by CLT, Binomial always gives Normal
B
$\text{Poi}(1)$ — since $np=n\cdot(1/n)=1=\lambda$ stays constant
C
$\text{Bin}(n, 1/n)$ — the distribution doesn't change
D
Degenerate at 1 — converges to its mean
Answer: B — Poi(1)

$np = n\cdot(1/n) = 1 = \lambda$. This is the Poisson limit theorem: Bin$(n, \lambda/n)\xrightarrow{d}$Poi$(\lambda)$. The CLT applies when $np$ grows with $n$ (so we can standardise). Here $np=1$ is fixed, so the Poisson limit applies instead.
probability / ch29 / q03★★ Medium

Why do we standardise to $Y_n = (X_n - \mu_n)/\sigma_n$ before taking the MGF limit?

A
To make $E(Y_n) = \sigma_n$
B
To get a sequence with mean 0 and variance 1 that has a non-degenerate limit distribution
C
Because the CLT only applies to standard Normal random variables
D
To satisfy the assumption $np = \lambda$
Answer: B

Without standardisation, $S_n$ diverges in mean and variance, so $M_{S_n}(t)$ has no useful limit. Standardising by subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation gives $E(Y_n)=0$, $\text{Var}(Y_n)=1$, and a non-trivial limiting distribution. The limit turns out to be $N(0,1)$ regardless of the original distribution — that's the miracle of the CLT.

In the Poi$(n)$ MGF calculation: $M_{Y_n}(t) = e^{-\sqrt{n}\,t}\cdot e^{n(e^{t/\sqrt{n}}-1)}$. After expanding $e^{t/\sqrt{n}}$, which terms survive as $n\to\infty$?

A
Only the $t/\sqrt{n}$ term — all others vanish
B
The $t/\sqrt{n}$ and $t^2/(2n)$ terms survive; higher terms vanish, leaving $e^{t^2/2}$
C
All terms survive equally
D
The $t^3/(6n^{3/2})$ term also survives
Answer: B

$n(e^{t/\sqrt{n}}-1) = n(t/\sqrt{n} + t^2/(2n) + t^3/(6n^{3/2})+\cdots) = \sqrt{n}\,t + t^2/2 + O(n^{-1/2})$. Multiplied by $e^{-\sqrt{n}\,t}$: the $\sqrt{n}\,t$ cancels, leaving $e^{t^2/2 + O(n^{-1/2})}\to e^{t^2/2}$. The $t/\sqrt{n}$ term produces $\sqrt{n}\,t$ which cancels with the centering $e^{-\sqrt{n}\,t}$. The $t^2/(2n)$ produces $t^2/2$ which gives the Normal. Higher terms $\to 0$.

A Nifty options strategy has trades with $E(\text{PnL})=\mu=500$ and $\text{SD}(\text{PnL})=\sigma=2000$ per trade. After 100 iid trades, the total PnL $S_{100}$ is approximately:

A
$N(500, 2000^2)$ — same as each trade
B
$N(500, 200^2)$ — standard error $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$
C
$N(50000, 20000^2)$ — total: mean $n\mu$, SD $\sigma\sqrt{n}$
D
$N(0, 1)$ — CLT always gives standard Normal
Answer: C — $N(50000, 20000^2)$

$E(S_{100})=100\times 500=50000$ and $\text{SD}(S_{100})=\sigma\sqrt{n}=2000\sqrt{100}=20000$. The CLT says $S_n\approx N(n\mu, n\sigma^2)$. Option B gives $\bar{X}_n$'s distribution, not $S_n$'s. Option D is the standardised version $(S_n-n\mu)/(\sigma\sqrt{n})$.
