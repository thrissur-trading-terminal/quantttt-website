LLN — Converse, Strong LLN & the Theorem Catalogue

This chapter completes the N&S condition proof, establishes the Continuous Mapping Theorem (with an elegant geometric mean application), defines the Strong LLN, and presents the complete catalogue of seven LLN variants from Bernoulli to Kolmogorov.

Continuous Mapping Theorem

If $X_n\xrightarrow{P}C$ (constant) and $h$ is continuous at $C$, then $h(X_n)\xrightarrow{P}h(C)$.
Given $\varepsilon,\eta>0$. Since $h$ is continuous at $C$: $\exists\delta>0$ s.t. $|x-C|<\delta\Rightarrow|h(x)-h(C)|<\varepsilon$. Therefore $\{|X_n-C|<\delta\}\subseteq\{|h(X_n)-h(C)|<\varepsilon\}$, so: $$P(|h(X_n)-h(C)|<\varepsilon)\geq P(|X_n-C|<\delta)$$ Since $X_n\xrightarrow{P}C$: $\exists N$ s.t. $\forall n\geq N$: $P(|X_n-C|<\delta)>1-\eta$. Therefore $P(|h(X_n)-h(C)|<\varepsilon)>1-\eta$. $\square$
Application: Geometric Mean of $U(0,1)$ Variables

$X_1,\ldots,X_n$ iid $U(0,1)$. Show that the geometric mean $G_n = (X_1\cdots X_n)^{1/n}\xrightarrow{P}1/e$.

Step 1: Let $Y_i = -\log X_i$. For $y>0$: $P(Y_i\leq y) = P(X_i\geq e^{-y}) = 1-e^{-y}$. So $Y_i\sim\text{Exp}(1)$ with $E(Y_i)=1$.

Step 2: By WLLN (iid, finite mean): $\bar{Y}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i\xrightarrow{P}1$.

Step 3: Note $\bar{Y}_n = -\frac{1}{n}\sum\log X_i = -\log G_n$.

Step 4: Apply $h(t)=e^{-t}$ (continuous): $G_n = e^{-\bar{Y}_n}\xrightarrow{P}e^{-1} = 1/e$.

So $G_n\xrightarrow{P}C$ where $C=1/e$.

Completing the N&S Condition: The Converse

Prove: WLLN for $\{X_n\}$ $\Rightarrow$ $E\!\left(\frac{Y_n^2}{1+Y_n^2}\right)\to 0$ where $Y_n=(S_n-E(S_n))/n$.

Split the expectation over $A=\{|Y_n|>\varepsilon\}$ and $A^c=\{|Y_n|\leq\varepsilon\}$: $$E\!\left(\frac{Y_n^2}{1+Y_n^2}\right) = \int_A\frac{y^2}{1+y^2}f_n(y)\,dy + \int_{A^c}\frac{y^2}{1+y^2}f_n(y)\,dy$$ On $A$: $\frac{y^2}{1+y^2}\leq 1$, so the first integral $\leq P(A) = P(|Y_n|>\varepsilon)$. On $A^c$: $\frac{y^2}{1+y^2}\leq y^2\leq\varepsilon^2$, so the second integral $\leq\varepsilon^2 P(A^c)\leq\varepsilon^2$. Therefore: $$E\!\left(\frac{Y_n^2}{1+Y_n^2}\right)\leq P(|Y_n|>\varepsilon) + \varepsilon^2$$ Since WLLN holds: $Y_n\xrightarrow{P}0$, so $P(|Y_n|>\varepsilon)\to 0$ as $n\to\infty$. For any $\delta>0$: choose $\varepsilon^2<\delta/2$, then $N$ large enough that $P(|Y_n|>\varepsilon)<\delta/2$. For all $n\geq N$: $$E\!\left(\frac{Y_n^2}{1+Y_n^2}\right)\leq\frac{\delta}{2}+\frac{\delta}{2}=\delta \qquad\square$$

Strong Law of Large Numbers (SLLN)

$\{X_n\}$ obeys the Strong Law of Large Numbers with norming constants $B_n\to\infty$ if there exist centering constants $A_n$ such that: $$\frac{S_n - A_n}{B_n}\xrightarrow{a.s.}0$$ i.e. $P\!\left(\left\{\omega: \frac{S_n(\omega)-A_n}{B_n}\to 0\right\}\right) = 1$.

Standard form: $A_n = \sum_{i=1}^n\mu_i$, $B_n = n$, giving $\bar{X}_n - \bar{\mu}_n\xrightarrow{a.s.}0$.
WLLN: the probability of being far from the mean goes to zero. SLLN: almost every sample path of $\bar{X}_n$ actually converges to $\mu$. The SLLN is much stronger — it rules out the possibility of infinitely many future deviations beyond $\varepsilon$.

The Seven LLN Theorems

1. Bounded iid SLLN: $X_i$ iid with $P(|X_i|\leq k)=1$ for some $k>0$: $\bar{X}_n\xrightarrow{a.s.}\mu$.

2. Independent Convergence Criterion: $X_n\xrightarrow{a.s.}0\iff\sum_{n=1}^\infty P(|X_n|>\varepsilon)<\infty$ for every $\varepsilon>0$.

3. Kolmogorov's Maximal Inequality: If $X_1,\ldots,X_n$ independent with $E(X_i)=0$ and $\text{Var}(X_i)=\sigma_i^2$: $$P\!\left(\max_{1\leq k\leq n}|S_k|>\varepsilon\right)\leq\frac{1}{\varepsilon^2}\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_i^2$$ This is the SLLN analogue of Chebyshev — controls the maximum partial sum.

4. Variance-Sum Criterion: If $\{X_n\}$ independent and $\sum_{k=1}^\infty\frac{\text{Var}(X_k)}{B_k^2}<\infty$ with $B_n\to\infty$: SLLN holds.

5. Borel's SLLN: $X_i$ iid Bernoulli$(p)$: $S_n/n\xrightarrow{a.s.}p$.

6. Poisson's WLLN: Non-iid Bernoulli trials with $P(X_i=1)=p_i$: $S_n/n\xrightarrow{P}\frac{1}{n}\sum p_i$.

7. Kolmogorov's SLLN: $X_i$ iid: $\bar{X}_n\xrightarrow{a.s.}\mu\iff E|X_i|<\infty$.

Result 7 is the definitive theorem. It says the SLLN holds for iid sequences if and only if the mean is finite — no stronger moment condition is needed.
Kolmogorov's SLLN (Result 7) is the theorem that makes the Cauchy case precise: since $E|X_i|=\infty$ for the Cauchy distribution, $\bar{X}_n\not\xrightarrow{a.s.}$ anything. It also shows that the WLLN variance condition from Ch27 is sufficient but not necessary for iid sequences — finite mean alone is enough for the stronger almost sure convergence.
Borel's SLLN (Result 5) is the mathematical justification for frequentist probability: toss a fair coin forever and the proportion of heads converges to $1/2$ with probability 1 — not just in probability, but along almost every sample path. In NSE: run an edge strategy long enough and your average trade PnL almost surely converges to the true edge per trade. Kolmogorov's SLLN tells you exactly when this fails: if your PnL distribution has infinite mean (e.g. a power-law distributed return), the average will never settle.

$X_n\xrightarrow{P}4$ and $h(x)=\sqrt{x}$. What does $\sqrt{X_n}$ converge to in probability?

A
$4$ — convergence is preserved without change
B
$2$ — by the Continuous Mapping Theorem
C
$\sqrt{n}$ — depends on $n$
D
Doesn't converge — square root is not linear
Answer: B — $2$

$X_i$ iid $U(0,1)$. The geometric mean $G_n = (X_1\cdots X_n)^{1/n}$ converges in probability to:

A
$0.5$ — the mean of $U(0,1)$
B
$1/e\approx 0.368$ — via $-\log X_i\sim\text{Exp}(1)$ and WLLN
C
$0$ — product of many uniforms goes to zero
D
$e\approx 2.718$

This is a beautiful application: the geometric mean is harder to handle than the arithmetic mean, but a log transformation reduces it to the WLLN.

What is the key difference between WLLN and SLLN?

A
WLLN requires iid; SLLN does not
B
WLLN requires finite variance; SLLN only requires finite mean
C
WLLN is convergence in probability; SLLN is almost sure convergence
D
SLLN applies only to Bernoulli trials
Answer: C

WLLN: $\bar{X}_n\xrightarrow{P}\mu$ (convergence in probability). SLLN: $\bar{X}_n\xrightarrow{a.s.}\mu$ (almost sure convergence, stronger). The SLLN is not just that occasional deviations become unlikely — it says that almost every sample path of $\bar{X}_n$ actually settles down to $\mu$.

Kolmogorov's SLLN states: $X_i$ iid, $\bar{X}_n\xrightarrow{a.s.}\mu\iff E|X_i|<\infty$. What does this say about iid Cauchy variables?

A
$\bar{X}_n\xrightarrow{a.s.}0$ since $E(X_i)=0$ for Cauchy
B
The SLLN holds but with a different limit
C
The SLLN fails: $E|X_i|=\infty$ for Cauchy, so $\bar{X}_n$ has no a.s. limit
D
SLLN requires only finite variance, not finite mean
Answer: C

Cauchy has no finite mean: $E|X_i|=\infty$. By Kolmogorov's iff: $\bar{X}_n$ does not converge almost surely to any limit. In fact $\bar{X}_n\sim\text{Cauchy}(1,0)$ for every $n$ — the average of $n$ iid Cauchy variables has the same distribution as each individual one. Averaging never concentrates the distribution.
probability / ch28 / q05★★★ Hard

In the converse proof ($\text{WLLN}\Rightarrow E(Y_n^2/(1+Y_n^2))\to 0$), why is the bound $E(Y_n^2/(1+Y_n^2))\leq P(|Y_n|>\varepsilon)+\varepsilon^2$ valid?

A
Because $Y_n^2/(1+Y_n^2)$ is always $\leq Y_n^2$
B
On $A$: use $y^2/(1+y^2)\leq 1$; on $A^c$: use $y^2/(1+y^2)\leq y^2\leq\varepsilon^2$; the two integrals bound the total expectation
C
By Markov's inequality applied to $Y_n^2$
D
Because $E(Y_n^2)\leq 1$ for all $n$
Answer: B

Integral over $A=\{|Y_n|>\varepsilon\}$: $y^2/(1+y^2)\leq 1$, so this part $\leq\int_A f_n\,dy = P(A)$. Integral over $A^c=\{|Y_n|\leq\varepsilon\}$: $y^2/(1+y^2)\leq y^2\leq\varepsilon^2$, so this part $\leq\varepsilon^2\int_{A^c}f_n\,dy\leq\varepsilon^2$. Adding the two bounds gives $E(Y_n^2/(1+Y_n^2))\leq P(|Y_n|>\varepsilon)+\varepsilon^2$. The WLLN sends $P(|Y_n|>\varepsilon)\to 0$, and $\varepsilon^2$ can be made arbitrarily small.
