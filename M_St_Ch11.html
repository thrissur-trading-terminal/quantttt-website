Brownian Motion

Every stochastic process we've built so far — BRP, PCP, DTMC, CTMC — had discrete state spaces or discrete time. Now we pass to the continuous limit. Brownian motion is the canonical continuous-time, continuous-state process: the universal scaling limit of random walks, the backbone of mathematical finance, and the engine that drives the Black-Scholes world.

From Random Walk to Brownian Motion

Start with a symmetric simple random walk: at each step, move $+1$ or $-1$ with equal probability. Now speed it up — shrink both the time step $\Delta t$ and the spatial step $\Delta x$ simultaneously. The key question is: at what rate must $\Delta x \to 0$ relative to $\Delta t$ to get a non-degenerate limit?

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be iid with $P(X_i = \pm 1) = 1/2$, so $E[X_i] = 0$, $\text{Var}(X_i) = 1$. Position at time $t$:

$$X(t) = \Delta x \sum_{i=1}^{\lfloor t/\Delta t \rfloor} X_i \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad E[X(t)] = 0, \quad \text{Var}(X(t)) = (\Delta x)^2 \cdot \frac{t}{\Delta t}$$

If $\Delta x = \Delta t$: variance $\to 0$ as $\Delta t \to 0$ — degenerate. The right scaling is $\Delta x = \sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}$. Then:

$$\text{Var}(X(t)) = \sigma^2 \Delta t \cdot \frac{t}{\Delta t} = \sigma^2 t \quad \text{for all } \Delta t$$

As $\Delta t \to 0$, the CLT kicks in: $X(t)$ converges in distribution to $N(0, \sigma^2 t)$. The limiting process is Brownian motion.

The $\sqrt{\Delta t}$ scaling is the entire reason Brownian motion paths are nowhere differentiable. The increments over interval $\Delta t$ have magnitude $\sim \sqrt{\Delta t}$, so the "velocity" $\Delta x / \Delta t \sim 1/\sqrt{\Delta t} \to \infty$. No derivative exists — the path is continuous but wildly rough.

Definition of Brownian Motion

A stochastic process $\{X(t) : t \ge 0\}$ is a Brownian motion (Wiener process) with parameter $\sigma^2$ if:
  • $X(0) = 0$
  • $\{X(t)\}$ has stationary and independent increments
  • For every $t > 0$: $X(t) \sim N(0,\, \sigma^2 t)$
  • Sample paths $t \mapsto X(t)$ are continuous a.s.
When $\sigma = 1$: standard Brownian motion. Any BM converts via $X(t)/\sigma$.
Increments
$X(t+s)-X(t) \sim N(0,\sigma^2 s)$
Depends only on gap $s$, not on $t$
Independence
Non-overlapping increments
$X(t_2)-X(t_1) \perp X(t_4)-X(t_3)$ for $t_1 \le t_2 \le t_3 \le t_4$
Covariance
$\text{Cov}(X(s),X(t)) = \min(s,t)$
Standard BM ($\sigma=1$)
The log-price process of a financial asset — the foundation of Black-Scholes — is modelled as Brownian motion with drift: $\ln S(t) = \mu t + \sigma W(t)$, where $W(t)$ is standard BM. The $\sigma$ here is exactly the annualised volatility you estimate from realised variance of NIFTY returns. Every option you price assumes Brownian motion lives inside the stock price.

Joint Distribution and Finite-Dimensional Densities

The joint density of $X(t_1), X(t_2), \ldots, X(t_n)$ at $0 < t_1 < t_2 < \cdots < t_n$ factors via independent increments. Writing $X(t_k) - X(t_{k-1}) \sim N(0, t_k - t_{k-1})$ (standard BM), the increments are independent, so:

$$f(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = \frac{\exp\!\left[-\dfrac{1}{2}\left(\dfrac{x_1^2}{t_1} + \dfrac{(x_2-x_1)^2}{t_2-t_1} + \cdots + \dfrac{(x_n - x_{n-1})^2}{t_n - t_{n-1}}\right)\right]}{(2\pi)^{n/2}\,\bigl[t_1(t_2-t_1)\cdots(t_n-t_{n-1})\bigr]^{1/2}}$$

Brownian motion is entirely determined by its mean (zero) and its covariance function $\text{Cov}(X(s),X(t)) = \min(s,t)$ — it is a Gaussian process.

Conditional Distribution — Brownian Bridge Preview

Given $X(t) = B$ (a fixed endpoint at time $t$), the conditional distribution of $X(s)$ for $s < t$ is normal:

$$E[X(s) \mid X(t) = B] = \frac{s}{t}\,B \qquad \text{Var}(X(s) \mid X(t) = B) = \frac{s(t-s)}{t}$$
Write $f_{s|t}(x|B) \propto f_s(x)\,f_{t-s}(B-x)$. Expanding the exponents and completing the square in $x$ gives a Gaussian with mean $sB/t$ and variance $s(t-s)/t$. $\square$
Conditioning on the endpoint pins the path. The conditional mean $sB/t$ is the straight line from 0 to $B$ — BM is "pulled" toward the endpoint linearly in time. The variance $s(t-s)/t$ peaks at $s = t/2$ and vanishes at $s = 0$ and $s = t$, as it must.

Hitting Times

Let $T_a = \inf\{t \ge 0 : X(t) = a\}$ be the first time BM hits level $a > 0$. By the reflection principle: once BM hits $a$, the subsequent path is equally likely to be above or below $a$ by time $t$.

$$P(T_a \le t) = 2\,P(X(t) \ge a) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{a/\sqrt{t}}^{\infty} e^{-y^2/2}\,dy = 2\left[1 - \Phi\!\left(\frac{a}{\sqrt{t}}\right)\right]$$ By symmetry, $P(T_a \le t) = P(T_{|a|} \le t)$ for any $a \ne 0$.
$E[T_a] = \infty$ for all $a \ne 0$ — Brownian motion hits every level with probability 1, but the expected hitting time is infinite. This is a warning for financial models: barrier-crossing is certain, but its timing is fat-tailed.
Barrier options on NIFTY — knock-in, knock-out — have payoffs triggered by $T_a \le T$ (maturity). The distribution of $T_a$ derived above is the foundation for closed-form barrier option pricing. The reflection principle turns a first-passage problem into a tail probability of a Normal.

White Noise and Stochastic Integration

The "derivative" of BM — which does not exist pathwise — is called white noise: $\{dX(t)/dt\}$, a zero-mean, uncorrelated process with infinite variance. Instead, we work with stochastic integrals.

For a smooth function $f: [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$, the stochastic integral is defined as a Riemann–Stieltjes limit:

$$\int_a^b f(t)\,dX(t) = \lim_{\max(t_i - t_{i-1}) \to 0} \sum_{i=1}^n f(t_{i-1})\,[X(t_i) - X(t_{i-1})]$$
$$E\!\left[\int_a^b f(t)\,dX(t)\right] = 0 \qquad \text{Var}\!\left[\int_a^b f(t)\,dX(t)\right] = \int_a^b f^2(t)\,dt$$ Integration by parts: $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(t)\,dX(t) = f(b)X(b) - f(a)X(a) - \int_a^b X(t)\,f'(t)\,dt$
The variance formula $\int_a^b f^2(t)\,dt$ is the Itô isometry in disguise — it says the stochastic integral is an $L^2$ isometry from functions on $[a,b]$ into random variables. The "energy" of $f$ (its $L^2$ norm squared) equals the variance of the corresponding stochastic integral.

The Langevin Equation

A classic application: a particle of unit mass in a liquid, subject to viscous drag proportional to velocity, plus a random white-noise forcing:

$$V'(t) = -\beta\,V(t) + \alpha\,X'(t)$$

where $X(t)$ is standard BM and $\beta > 0$ is the drag coefficient. Multiplying by the integrating factor $e^{\beta t}$:

$$V(t) = V(0)\,e^{-\beta t} + \alpha \int_0^t e^{-\beta(t-s)}\,dX(s)$$

The first term is exponential decay of the initial velocity. The second is a stochastic convolution — the accumulated effect of all past noise, weighted by how long ago it occurred. $V(t)$ is a Gaussian process with mean $V(0)e^{-\beta t} \to 0$ and a variance that converges to $\alpha^2/(2\beta)$ in steady state.

The Langevin equation is the stochastic differential equation (SDE) ancestor of the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process — the model used for mean-reverting spreads in pairs trading. Replace $V(t)$ with a spread, $\beta$ with mean-reversion speed, and you have the TTT short-vol strategy's theoretical underpinning for IV mean reversion.

Gaussian Processes

A stochastic process $\{X(t), t \ge 0\}$ is a Gaussian process if $(X(t_1), \ldots, X(t_n))$ has a multivariate normal distribution for every finite collection of times $t_1, \ldots, t_n$. A Gaussian process is completely determined by its mean function $m(t) = E[X(t)]$ and covariance function $C(s,t) = \text{Cov}(X(s), X(t))$.

Standard BM is Gaussian with $m(t) = 0$ and $C(s,t) = \min(s,t)$. Three important derived processes:

Brownian Bridge
$Z(t) = X(t) - t\,X(1)$
$\text{Cov}(Z(s),Z(t)) = s(1-t)$, $s \le t$, pinned at 0
Integrated BM
$Y(t) = \int_0^t X(s)\,ds$
$\text{Cov}(Y(s),Y(t)) = s^2(t/2 - s/6)$, $s \le t$
BM with Drift
$\mu t + \sigma X(t)$
$m(t) = \mu t$, $C(s,t)=\sigma^2\min(s,t)$

Brownian Bridge

Condition standard BM on $X(1) = 0$: the result is the Brownian bridge $\{X(t)\mid X(1)=0,\; 0 \le t \le 1\}$. It starts and ends at 0, with Gaussian behaviour in between.

The Brownian bridge has $E[X(t)\mid X(1)=0] = 0$ and covariance: $$\text{Cov}(X(s), X(t) \mid X(1) = 0) = s(1-t), \quad s \le t \le 1$$ Equivalently, $Z(t) = X(t) - t\,X(1)$ is a Brownian bridge for $t \in [0,1]$.
$\text{Cov}(Z(s), Z(t)) = \text{Cov}(X(s) - sX(1),\; X(t) - tX(1))$ $= \text{Cov}(X(s),X(t)) - t\,\text{Cov}(X(s),X(1)) - s\,\text{Cov}(X(1),X(t)) + st\,\text{Var}(X(1))$ $= s - ts - st + st = s(1-t)$ $\square$
The Brownian bridge appears in the Kolmogorov–Smirnov test — a non-parametric test used in strategy backtesting to check whether a return distribution matches a hypothesised one. The KS test statistic converges in distribution to the supremum of a Brownian bridge.

Stationary Processes

A process $\{X(t)\}$ is (strictly) stationary if the joint distribution of $(X(t_1+s), \ldots, X(t_n+s))$ is the same as $(X(t_1), \ldots, X(t_n))$ for any shift $s > 0$. BM itself is not stationary (variance grows). But several derived processes are.

The Random Telegraph Signal is a canonical example. Let $N(t)$ be a Poisson process with rate $\lambda$, and $X_0$ uniform on $\{-1, +1\}$:

$$X(t) = X_0\,(-1)^{N(t)}$$
The Random Telegraph Signal is stationary with: $$E[X(t)] = 0 \qquad \text{Cov}(X(t), X(t+s)) = e^{-2\lambda s}$$ Its displacement $D(t) = \int_0^t X(s)\,ds$ satisfies $E[D(t)] = 0$ and: $$\text{Var}(D(t)) = \frac{1}{\lambda}\left(t - \frac{1 - e^{-2\lambda t}}{2\lambda}\right)$$
$\text{Cov}(X(t), X(t+s)) = E[X_0^2\,(-1)^{N(t)+N(t+s)}] = E[(-1)^{N(s)}]$ (by independence and stationarity of Poisson increments). Since $(-1)^{N(s)} = \sum_{k\ge 0}(-1)^k e^{-\lambda s}(\lambda s)^k/k! = e^{-\lambda s} \cdot e^{-\lambda s} = e^{-2\lambda s}$. $\square$
Brownian motion has stationary increments but is NOT a stationary process — $\text{Var}(X(t)) = t$ grows with $t$. Stationarity and stationary increments are distinct properties. The Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process is the canonical stationary Gaussian Markov process.

Which of the following is true about standard Brownian motion $X(t)$?

A
$X(t)$ is a stationary process
B
$X(t)$ has stationary increments but is NOT a stationary process
C
$X(t)$ is differentiable at every $t$ with probability 1
D
$X(t)$ has stationary and independent increments, is continuous, but is nowhere differentiable a.s.
Answer: D

BM has stationary increments ($X(t+s)-X(t) \sim N(0,s)$ regardless of $t$), independent increments, and continuous paths. But $\text{Var}(X(t)) = t$ grows, so it is NOT a stationary process — the joint distribution changes with time shifts.
