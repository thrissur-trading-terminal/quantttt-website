Microprice & Volume Imbalance

The midprice treats both sides of the book as equal. They are not. Microprice and volume imbalance are the first two calculations that tell you which way the book is leaning — before price moves.

2.1 Why the Midprice Is Not Enough

In Chapter 1 we defined the midprice as $\frac{1}{2}(P_t^a + P_t^b)$. It is simple and useful, but it carries a hidden assumption: that the quantity at the best bid and the quantity at the best ask are equal, and therefore neither side is exerting more pressure than the other.

In practice, this is almost never true. At any given tick, one side of the book almost always has more volume at the touch than the other. When there are significantly more lots resting on the bid than on the ask, buyers outnumber sellers at the best price — and the "true" price is closer to the ask than the arithmetic mid would suggest.

Imagine two buyers and one seller standing at a market stall negotiating over a kilogram of mangoes. The arithmetic average of their offers is the midprice. But the microprice asks: with two buyers competing for one seller's stock, where does the transaction actually happen? Closer to the seller's ask — because buyers have more pressure to transact. The microprice captures this asymmetry mathematically.

2.2 The Microprice — Equation (3.3)

The microprice $S_t^*$ is the volume-weighted average of the best bid and ask prices, where each price is weighted by the quantity on the opposite side:

$$S_t^* = \frac{V_t^b}{V_t^b + V_t^a}\,P_t^a + \frac{V_t^a}{V_t^b + V_t^a}\,P_t^b$$
Cartea et al. (2015), Eq. (3.3), p. 46

Where:

  • $V_t^b$ = quantity (lots) resting at the best bid at time $t$
  • $V_t^a$ = quantity (lots) resting at the best ask at time $t$
  • $P_t^b$ = best bid price at time $t$
  • $P_t^a$ = best ask price at time $t$
  • $S_t^*$ = microprice — also called the weighted midprice
First introduced conceptually in Chapter 1, p. 18: "The brown solid line depicts a variation of the asset known as the microprice... used as a more subtle proxy for the asset's transaction cost-free price, as it measures the tendency that the price has to move either towards the bid or ask side as captured by number of shares posted." Formalised as Equation (3.3) in Chapter 3, Section 3.2: Asset Prices and Returns Intraday, p. 46.

Notice that the bid quantity $V_t^b$ is used to weight the ask price, and the ask quantity $V_t^a$ is used to weight the bid price. This is the key insight: a large bid queue pulls the microprice toward the ask, reflecting upward pressure. A large ask queue pulls it toward the bid, reflecting downward pressure.

Think of the microprice as a see-saw balanced at the midprice. A heavy bid side (many buyers waiting) tips the see-saw upward — price is likely to move toward the ask. A heavy ask side tips it downward. The microprice is the exact tipping point given the current weights.
WORKED EXAMPLE — MICROPRICE NSE:NIFTY26MAYFUT · 29 Apr 2026 · 12:34:20 IST

From the live snapshot used in Chapter 1:

Best Bid: $P_t^b = \text{Rs.}24{,}442.80$,   $V_t^b = 130$ lots
Best Ask: $P_t^a = \text{Rs.}24{,}444.90$,   $V_t^a = 195$ lots

Step 1 — Total touch volume

$$V_t^b + V_t^a = 130 + 195 = 325 \text{ lots}$$

Step 2 — Apply Equation (3.3)

$$S_t^* = \frac{130}{325} \times 24{,}444.90 + \frac{195}{325} \times 24{,}442.80$$ $$= 0.4 \times 24{,}444.90 + 0.6 \times 24{,}442.80$$ $$= 9{,}777.96 + 14{,}665.68 = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.64$$

Step 3 — Compare to midprice

$$\text{Midprice} = \frac{24{,}442.80 + 24{,}444.90}{2} = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.85$$

Microprice = Rs.24,443.64  ·  Midprice = Rs.24,443.85
The microprice is below the midprice by Rs.0.21. Why? Because the ask side has more volume (195 lots) than the bid (130 lots) at the touch. The heavier ask queue pulls the microprice downward — suggesting mild selling pressure at this tick. Despite the large aggregate imbalance favouring buyers (TBQ 5,28,125 vs TSQ 2,20,415), the touch is momentarily ask-heavy.

2.3 Spread in Basis Points

In Chapter 1 we computed the quoted spread in rupees: Rs.2.10. But rupee spreads are not comparable across instruments — a Rs.2.10 spread on a Rs.24,000 Futures contract is very different from a Rs.2.10 spread on a Rs.100 option. The solution is to normalise by the midprice and express in basis points (bps).

$$\text{Spread}_{bps} = \frac{P_t^a - P_t^b}{P_{mid}} \times 10{,}000$$
Cartea et al. (2015), Ch. 4, §4.3.1 — Spreads, p. 71

Where:

  • $P_t^a - P_t^b$ = quoted spread in rupees
  • $P_{mid} = \frac{1}{2}(P_t^a + P_t^b)$ = midprice
  • $10{,}000$ = conversion factor from decimal fraction to basis points (1 bps = 0.01%)
WORKED EXAMPLE — SPREAD IN BASIS POINTS NSE:NIFTY26MAYFUT · 29 Apr 2026

Using the same snapshot: Spread = Rs.2.10, Midprice = Rs.24,443.85

Computation

$$\text{Spread}_{bps} = \frac{2.10}{24{,}443.85} \times 10{,}000 = 0.0000859 \times 10{,}000 = \mathbf{0.86 \text{ bps}}$$

Spread = 0.86 bps
NIFTY Futures at 0.86 bps is extremely tight by any global standard — the US S&P 500 Futures (ES) typically trade at 0.1–0.2 bps, while NSE single-stock Futures can be 5–20 bps. Comparing 0.86 bps (Futures) vs a typical NIFTY ATM option spread of 50–150 bps immediately shows why institutional desks prefer to hedge delta in Futures rather than options.

2.4 Volume Imbalance — The Full 50-Level Signal

The microprice uses only Level 1 quantities. But we have 50 levels. Volume imbalance $\rho_t$ aggregates the entire visible book into a single directional signal.

$$\rho_t = \frac{V_t^b - V_t^a}{V_t^b + V_t^a} \in [-1, +1]$$
Cartea et al. (2015), Ch. 12, §12.2 — Intraday Features, p. 295

Where:

  • $V_t^b$ = total bid quantity across all observed price levels (TBQ)
  • $V_t^a$ = total ask quantity across all observed price levels (TSQ)
  • $\rho_t = +1$ means the entire visible book is on the bid side — maximum buy pressure
  • $\rho_t = -1$ means the entire visible book is on the ask side — maximum sell pressure
  • $\rho_t = 0$ means a perfectly balanced book
Cartea et al. (2015), Chapter 12 — Order Imbalance, Section 12.2 — Intraday Features, p. 295: "We define limit order imbalance $\rho_t$ at time $t$ as the ratio of the quoted volume imbalance to the total quoted volume... This is a measure of the buy versus sell pressure on an asset and, as we will see, it contains predictive power on both the arrival rates of MOs, and the direction and size of future price movements."

The volumes $V_t^b$ and $V_t^a$ in Cartea's formulation can be computed at-the-touch (Level 1 only), across the best $n$ levels, or across the full visible book. In our dashboard we use TBQ and TSQ — the full 50-level aggregates provided directly by the Fyers TBT WebSocket.

BID 5,28,125
ASK 2,20,415
← Sell pressure $\rho_t = \mathbf{+0.413}$  |  +41.3% imbalance Buy pressure →
WORKED EXAMPLE — VOLUME IMBALANCE NSE:NIFTY26MAYFUT · 29 Apr 2026

From the live snapshot: TBQ = 5,28,125 lots, TSQ = 2,20,415 lots

Apply the formula

$$\rho_t = \frac{5{,}28{,}125 - 2{,}20{,}415}{5{,}28{,}125 + 2{,}20{,}415} = \frac{3{,}07{,}710}{7{,}48{,}540} = +0.411$$

Interpretation

$\rho_t = +0.411$ means the bid side carries 41.1% more net volume than a neutral book would. Stated differently, the bid side has $\mathbf{2.39\times}$ more lots than the ask side: $$\frac{\text{TBQ}}{\text{TSQ}} = \frac{5{,}28{,}125}{2{,}20{,}415} \approx 2.39$$

Volume Imbalance = +0.411 (+41.1%)
This is a strongly bullish book at this snapshot. Cartea et al. (2015) demonstrate that imbalance has predictive power over both the direction of future price moves and the rate at which market orders arrive. A sustained positive imbalance of this magnitude on NIFTY Futures is consistent with the market expecting a further upside move within the next few minutes.

2.5 Level Imbalance — Per-Level Decomposition

The aggregate imbalance $\rho_t$ tells us the overall book direction. But which specific price levels are driving it? The level imbalance decomposes the signal level by level:

$$I_k = \frac{V_{bid,k} - V_{ask,k}}{V_{bid,k} + V_{ask,k}}, \qquad k = 1, 2, \ldots, 50$$

Where:

  • $V_{bid,k}$ = bid quantity at the $k$-th level
  • $V_{ask,k}$ = ask quantity at the $k$-th level
  • $I_k = +1$ means only bid volume at this level (no ask competition)
  • $I_k = -1$ means only ask volume at this level (no bid competition)
  • $I_k \approx 0$ means the level is evenly contested
Level Imbalance $I_k$ — Top 10 Levels · NIFTY26MAYFUT · 29 Apr 2026
Level 8 bid (Rs.24,440.00) has 7,475 lots against only 65 lots on the ask at that exact level — giving $I_8 = \frac{7475 - 65}{7475 + 65} = \frac{7410}{7540} \approx +0.983$. This near-perfect positive imbalance at a single level is the "support wall" pattern. A market maker seeing this will shade their bid quotes upward, expecting that level to absorb selling and provide a price floor.

2.6 Putting It Together — What Changes Between Midprice and Microprice?

The table below summarises the three price estimates for the same snapshot and what each one tells you:

Quantity Value What It Tells You
Midprice Rs.24,443.85 Neutral geometric centre — no information about volume asymmetry
Microprice (L1) Rs.24,443.64 Ask-heavy touch (195 vs 130 lots) — mild downward lean at the best prices
Volume Imbalance $\rho_t$ +0.411 Full 50-level book is bid-heavy — strong bullish aggregate signal
Level Imbalance L8 $I_8$ +0.983 Near-total bid dominance at Rs.24,440 — institutional support wall
Spread 0.86 bps Extremely tight — crossing cost is negligible relative to any real edge
On NSE, weekly NIFTY option expiries (Thursday) create a structural pattern where volume imbalance in the underlying Futures tends to spike in the final 30 minutes. Informed desks running short-volatility strategies (straddles, strangles) watch $\rho_t$ continuously — a sudden shift from +0.4 to -0.3 in the Futures book is a leading indicator of a directional break that will blow through their delta-neutral position before they can re-hedge.
Practice Problems 4 questions
A NIFTY Futures order book shows: Best Bid Rs.24,500 with 400 lots, Best Ask Rs.24,502 with 100 lots. Which of the following correctly computes the microprice $S_t^*$?
A $\frac{400 \times 24{,}500 + 100 \times 24{,}502}{400 + 100} = \text{Rs.}24{,}500.40$
B $\frac{400 \times 24{,}502 + 100 \times 24{,}500}{400 + 100} = \text{Rs.}24{,}501.60$
C $\frac{24{,}500 + 24{,}502}{2} = \text{Rs.}24{,}501$
D $\frac{100 \times 24{,}500 + 400 \times 24{,}502}{500} = \text{Rs.}24{,}501.60$
Explanation: The microprice formula weights each price by the quantity on the opposite side: $S_t^* = \frac{V^b}{V^b + V^a} P^a + \frac{V^a}{V^b + V^a} P^b$. With $V^b = 400$, $V^a = 100$: $S_t^* = \frac{400}{500} \times 24502 + \frac{100}{500} \times 24500 = 0.8 \times 24502 + 0.2 \times 24500 = 19601.6 + 4900 = \text{Rs.}24{,}501.60$. Note that the bid quantity (400) is larger, which pulls the microprice toward the ask (Rs.24,502). Option A uses the wrong weighting — bid qty weighting bid price (same side, not opposite). Option D is numerically identical to B but conceptually wrong in its description.
For the same book (Bid Rs.24,500 / 400 lots, Ask Rs.24,502 / 100 lots), compute the volume imbalance $\rho_t$ using only the touch quantities. What does the sign tell you?
A $\rho_t = -0.60$ — the ask is heavier, suggesting downward pressure
B $\rho_t = +0.60$ — the bid is heavier, suggesting upward pressure
C $\rho_t = +0.80$ — bid has 4x the ask, so imbalance is 80%
D $\rho_t = 0$ — the midprice is the fair value so the book is always balanced
Explanation: $\rho_t = \frac{400 - 100}{400 + 100} = \frac{300}{500} = +0.60$. The positive sign confirms the bid side is heavier — more buyers than sellers at the touch, consistent with upward price pressure. Option C is wrong: the ratio TBQ/TSQ = 4 does not equal the imbalance — the formula normalises to $[-1,+1]$ by dividing by the total, not by computing a ratio. Option D is wrong: the book is almost never balanced at exactly zero.
In the NIFTY26MAYFUT snapshot, the microprice (Rs.24,443.64) is below the midprice (Rs.24,443.85), yet the full-book volume imbalance is strongly positive (+41%). How can these two signals point in opposite directions?
A They cannot — one of the calculations must be wrong
B The microprice is always more reliable, so the imbalance signal should be ignored
C The microprice uses only Level 1 quantities (130 bid vs 195 ask — ask-heavy at the touch), while volume imbalance uses all 50 levels (5,28,125 bid vs 2,20,415 ask — bid-heavy in aggregate). Different windows of the book can tell different stories at the same instant
D Volume imbalance counts the number of orders, not quantities, so it measures something different
Explanation: This is one of the most important conceptual points in order book analysis. The microprice (Eq. 3.3) is a Level-1-only calculation — it only sees 130 bid lots vs 195 ask lots at the best prices. At that narrow window, the ask side is heavier, pulling the microprice below the midprice. But deeper in the book, from Level 2 onwards, the bid side dominates massively (the 7,475-lot wall at Level 8, for example), producing a strongly positive aggregate imbalance. Both signals are correct — they are just measuring different horizons of the book. A sophisticated trader uses both: microprice for short-term tick direction, full-book imbalance for the medium-term directional lean.
A NIFTY ATM straddle desk is delta-neutral. The NIFTY26MAYFUT book suddenly shifts from $\rho_t = +0.41$ to $\rho_t = -0.38$ within 30 seconds, while the futures price has not yet moved. What should the desk do, and why?
A Do nothing — the futures price hasn't moved so delta is still neutral
B Buy more straddles to increase gamma exposure
C Prepare to re-hedge delta immediately — the sudden shift to negative imbalance is a leading indicator that a downward price move is imminent, before it appears in the futures price
D Reduce position size since high imbalance always means a crash is coming
Explanation: Cartea et al. (2015) Ch. 12 establish that volume imbalance $\rho_t$ has predictive power over future price movements — it leads the price, not follows it. A rapid shift from +0.41 to -0.38 means ask-side volume has flooded the book — sellers are building up in front of the current price. The futures price will likely follow downward within seconds to minutes. A straddle desk delta-neutral at the current price will become delta-positive (net long delta) once price moves down, because the short put gains negative delta faster than the short call loses positive delta. The correct action is to pre-position the hedge. Option A is the classic mistake: waiting for price confirmation means re-hedging at worse levels.
