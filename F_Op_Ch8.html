Heston Calibration

You have a pricing engine. You have market data. Now you need to find the five parameters — $\kappa, \theta, \xi, \rho, v_0$ — that make the model match what the market is actually saying. This is calibration: the bridge between beautiful mathematics and messy reality. We will do it properly on clean synthetic data, and then we will do it on real NIFTY prices from today and show you what happens when reality refuses to cooperate.

The Inverse Problem

Everything we have built so far has been a forward problem: given parameters, compute a price. Calibration is the reverse. Given market prices — or equivalently, implied volatilities — find the parameters that reproduce them. Formally:

Calibration Objective
$$\hat{\Theta} = \underset{\Theta}{\arg\min}\; \mathcal{L}(\Theta), \qquad \Theta = (\kappa,\, \theta,\, \xi,\, \rho,\, v_0)$$

where $\mathcal{L}$ measures the distance between model-implied volatilities and market-implied volatilities across all available strikes and maturities.

The market gives you a discrete grid: $N_K$ strikes times $N_T$ maturities, so $N = N_K \times N_T$ data points. You want five numbers that collectively explain all of them. Think of it like fitting a surface — you are asking a five-parameter family of smooth curves to pass through a cloud of noisy, discrete, market-determined points.

Each point on the implied volatility surface encodes the market's collective opinion about the distribution of future returns at that strike and maturity. Calibration is the art of finding the Heston world whose risk-neutral distribution best matches that opinion. When it works well, your model prices any exotic derivative consistently with the vanilla market. When it does not — as we will see — that tells you something deep about whether the model is even appropriate for this data.

Choosing What to Minimise

The choice of loss function is not aesthetic. It decides what the calibration prioritises, and different choices give genuinely different parameter sets.

Loss FunctionFormulaPrioritises
IV RMSE√(Σ(σ̂ᵢ − σᵢ)² / N)Uniform fit across all strikes. Interpretable: RMSE of 0.5 means off by half a vol point on average.
Relative IV Error√(Σ((σ̂ᵢ−σᵢ)/σᵢ)² / N)Proportional accuracy. Avoids over-weighting high-vol deep OTM options.
Vega-Weighted IV√(Σ wᵢ(σ̂ᵢ−σᵢ)²), wᵢ ∝ vegaᵢATM accuracy. High-vega options (near-ATM, longer-dated) count more. Industry standard.
Price RMSE√(Σ(Ĉᵢ − Cᵢ)² / N)Raw price accuracy. Naturally over-weights expensive options. Rarely used alone.

For NIFTY options, vega-weighted IV error is the right default. ATM options have the tightest bid-ask spreads and the deepest liquidity — your model must nail them. Being wrong on the ATM straddle is a hedging disaster. In this chapter we use IV RMSE throughout because it is the most interpretable.

Why Calibration Is a Slippery Fish

Calibration looks like a standard least-squares problem. It is not. Several properties of the Heston loss landscape conspire to make optimisation genuinely difficult.

Non-convexity. The map $\Theta \mapsto \mathcal{L}(\Theta)$ is not convex. There are multiple local minima — different parameter combinations that explain the data equally well or nearly so. A gradient-based method starting from the wrong point will find a local minimum and declare victory.

Parameter redundancy. The parameters $\kappa$, $\theta$, and $v_0$ interact heavily. Increasing $\kappa$ and $\theta$ simultaneously can leave the near-term smile almost unchanged, creating a flat ridge in the loss landscape where the optimizer wanders aimlessly.

Correlation instability. $\rho$ and $\xi$ jointly determine skew. The product $\rho \cdot \xi$ is often better identified than either individually — you can achieve the same skew with $(\rho=-0.8, \xi=0.5)$ or $(\rho=-0.5, \xi=0.8)$. Without rich maturity structure, the two are indistinguishable.

Short-maturity instability. For small $T$, the characteristic function integrand oscillates rapidly at large $|u|$. The GL quadrature requires a higher upper cutoff, and small numerical errors in the CF can cascade into large IV errors. One bad pricing call in the loss function sends the optimizer off a cliff.

The Feller condition $2\kappa\theta > \xi^2$ ensures the variance process stays strictly positive. Calibration regularly pushes parameters outside this region — producing formally valid fits but a model where variance can touch zero, causing the characteristic function to become numerically unstable. Always check Feller after calibration, and treat a violated Feller condition as a warning sign about parameter reliability.

The Algorithms

Nelder-Mead (downhill simplex) is a derivative-free local optimizer. It maintains a geometric shape in parameter space and iteratively moves it downhill. Fast when started near the true minimum, but gets stuck in local minima when started far away. Best as a final polish step.

Differential Evolution (DE) is a population-based global optimizer. It maintains a population of candidate parameter vectors and evolves them through mutation and crossover. Excellent at exploring the full parameter space without getting trapped locally. Slower per iteration, but far more reliable from a cold start.

Hybrid DE + Nelder-Mead is the industry standard: run DE to find a good basin of attraction, then switch to Nelder-Mead for fast, precise convergence. This combines DE's global exploration with Nelder-Mead's local precision.

Think of the loss surface as a mountain range. Nelder-Mead is a skilled climber who descends to the nearest valley — fast, but she starts from wherever you drop her. DE is a search party of 15 climbers scattered randomly across the range who communicate and gradually converge on the deepest valley. The hybrid strategy: let the search party find the right valley, then send the expert in for the final descent.

The Calibration Workflow

1
Data preparation. Collect call and put IVs at each strike. Average them to get mid-IVs — this cancels the bid-ask spread. Discard strikes with a spread wider than 1 vol point. For each maturity, identify the ATM strike closest to the forward price $F = S_0 e^{rT}$.
2
Initial guess. Set $v_0 \approx \sigma_{ATM,T_1}^2$ and $\theta \approx \sigma_{ATM,T_{long}}^2$. Estimate $\rho \cdot \xi$ from the skew slope via linear regression of IV on log-moneyness. Start $\kappa = 2$, $\xi = 0.5$.
3
Log-space reparameterisation. Optimise over $(\log\kappa,\, \log\theta,\, \log\xi,\, \operatorname{arctanh}(\rho),\, \log v_0)$. This enforces positivity constraints automatically — $e^{\text{anything}}$ is always positive, $\tanh(\text{anything})$ always lies in $(-1,1)$ — and makes the loss landscape more symmetric.
4
Global search with DE. Population size 15, 300+ iterations, mutation $(0.5, 1.0)$, recombination 0.85. Takes 30–120 seconds but produces a reliable starting basin.
5
Polish with Nelder-Mead. Take DE's best output, run Nelder-Mead with tolerances $10^{-6}$ to converge precisely. As a final check, run 3–5 additional Nelder-Mead starts from perturbed initialisations — if all converge to the same point, the minimum is trustworthy.
6
Validate. Compute the full residual table. Target: MAE < 0.5 vol pts, RMSE < 0.8 vol pts, Max < 2 vol pts, R² > 0.98. Inspect residual signs — random is fine, structured is a red flag.

This is the controlled experiment: the model is correctly specified, maturities span 30 to 90 days, and we add only ±0.05 vol pts of bid-ask rounding noise. ⚡ The Honest Truth
Now let us try it with real data.

Everything above was a controlled experiment. We built the surface from Heston parameters, then asked Heston to find those parameters back. Of course it succeeded — we were asking the model to solve its own puzzle.

Why It Fails — The Precise Mechanism

The failure has nothing to do with the optimizer running incorrectly, and nothing to do with the numerical integration. The failure is structural: Heston cannot fit this surface without distorting its parameters into economically meaningless territory.

The term structure is nearly flat. Heston explains the difference between near-term and long-run vol through the mean-reversion timescale $1/\kappa$. With all three maturities under 18 days, and $1/\kappa$ comparable to the longest maturity, mean reversion has almost no time to operate. The 5-day and 18-day ATM IVs differ by only 0.33 vol pts — the optimizer cannot use this tiny signal to pin down both $\kappa$ and $\theta$ independently. So it does the only thing it can: push $\kappa$ to the maximum allowed value and let $\theta$ float to whatever makes the level roughly right.

The identifiability problem. Statistically, a parameter is identified if the data contains enough information to determine it uniquely. With three maturities that are all in the same short-date regime, you effectively have one piece of term-structure information, not three. You need at least one longer-dated maturity — 30 days minimum, ideally 60+ — to anchor $\theta$ and let $\kappa$ be determined by the term structure gradient. Without that, the calibration is solving an underdetermined system.

Short-date skew requires extreme parameters. Heston skew at short maturities scales roughly as $\rho \xi \sqrt{T}$. With $T_1 = 5/365 = 0.0137$, matching a slope of −115 vol pts per log-moneyness requires $\rho \xi \approx -0.65$ — already large. But the optimizer must also simultaneously fit T₂ and T₃, which have slightly different slopes. The only way to do all three with a fixed $(\rho, \xi)$ is to give $\kappa$ a large value so that the maturities "decouple" slightly, allowing different effective vols at different terms. This pushes $\kappa \to 20$ and forces $\theta$ to a compensating absurdity.

The hump is structurally impossible. The real market's ATM IV goes 22.13% $\to$ 22.18% $\to$ 21.80% — up then down, a slight hump. Heston produces a monotone term structure by construction: variance mean-reverts exponentially from $v_0$ to $\theta$, always monotonically. There is no way for a single-factor mean-reverting variance process to produce a humped term structure. This is not a parameter problem — it is a model class problem.

Every quant desk in Mumbai calibrates Heston to NSE data daily. The standard practice is to use weekly, monthly, and quarterly expiries simultaneously — near-weekly (5–7 days), front monthly (25–35 days), and one quarterly (60–90 days) at minimum. With that maturity structure, the term-structure information is rich enough to identify $\kappa$ and $\theta$ separately, and calibration works cleanly with RMSE under 0.3 vol pts. Feeding only weekly expiries into Heston — as we were forced to do with today's data — is a known pathological case. Real desks have systems to detect this: if calibration returns $\kappa > 10$ or $\theta < 0.01$, a flag is raised and the weekly expiry is either dropped from the calibration, given a much lower weight, or fitted using flat-vol BSM independently.
When residuals have a systematic pattern — same sign across all strikes for a given maturity, or a monotone sign pattern across maturities — do not keep tweaking the optimizer. The model is telling you something. No amount of starting-point adjustment or algorithm tuning will fix a structural limitation. The correct responses are: (1) add longer-dated expiries to the calibration set, (2) downweight or exclude the problematic short expiries, (3) use a more flexible model — Heston with jumps, two-factor stochastic vol, or rough volatility.

Quality Metrics and Acceptance Criteria

Four numbers tell you whether to use a calibration result or throw it away. These are industry-standard thresholds for liquid equity index options.

MetricFormulaTargetIf Violated
MAE$\frac{1}{N}\sum|\hat\sigma_i-\sigma_i|$< 0.50 vol ptsAverage fit is poor — systematic misfit.
RMSE$\sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum(\hat\sigma_i-\sigma_i)^2}$< 0.80 vol ptsPenalises large outliers — check the wings.
Max Error$\max_i|\hat\sigma_i-\sigma_i|$< 2.00 vol ptsSingle strike is badly wrong — check pricing.
R²$1 - \frac{\sum(\hat\sigma_i-\sigma_i)^2}{\sum(\sigma_i-\bar\sigma)^2}$> 0.98Model explains <98% of surface variation.

Beyond these numbers, always inspect the residual table visually. Random-signed residuals below 0.3 vol pts are noise — acceptable. Structured, same-signed residuals at multiple strikes or maturities are a signal — investigate before using the calibrated parameters for anything. The synthetic example passed all four criteria. The real NSE example failed R² (0.833) and showed structured residuals. The model should not be used for exotic pricing on this dataset without modification.

Quick Check 3 questions
CH08 · Q1Medium
You calibrate Heston to NIFTY data and find residuals that are +0.8 vol pts at every strike for T₁ and −0.6 vol pts at every strike for T₂. The most likely explanation is:
A The optimizer converged to a local minimum — re-run with different starting points.
B Heston's single-factor variance structure cannot match the shape of this term structure — model misspecification.
C The loss function is wrong — switching to vega-weighted IV will fix it.
D The GL quadrature is using too few nodes and introducing pricing errors.
The key is that residuals are uniform in sign across all strikes within each maturity. Numerical errors (D) would be random across strikes. A local minimum (A) typically produces non-uniform residuals. Changing the loss function (C) does not change what the model can represent. Only B explains the pattern: Heston forces a monotone IV term structure, and the market has a shape that cannot be produced by a single exponential mean-reversion. That is structural misspecification, not a numerical or optimisation failure.
CH08 · Q2Medium
Why do we optimise in log-space — i.e. over $(\log\kappa, \log\theta, \log\xi, \operatorname{arctanh}(\rho), \log v_0)$ — rather than directly over $(\kappa, \theta, \xi, \rho, v_0)$?
A Log-space makes the loss function convex, guaranteeing convergence to the global minimum.
B It automatically enforces positivity and bounds constraints, and makes the loss landscape more symmetric so the optimizer converges faster.
C It reduces the effective dimensionality of the problem from 5 to 4.
D Log-space parameterisation is a requirement of the Feller condition.
B is correct. In native space, trial steps can generate negative values for positive-constrained parameters ($\kappa, \theta, \xi, v_0 > 0$), causing the pricing function to fail. In log-space, $e^{\text{anything}}$ is always positive — the constraint is satisfied by construction. Arctanh maps any real number to $(-1,1)$, keeping $\rho$ valid automatically. Log-space does NOT make the problem convex (A is false), does not reduce dimension (C), and has no direct relationship to the Feller condition (D).
CH08 · Q3Hard
After calibrating Heston to a dataset containing only maturities of 5, 10, and 15 days, you find $\kappa = 18$, $\theta = 0.01$ (long-run vol = 10%), and $v_0 = 0.06$ (current vol = 24.5%). The most accurate interpretation is:
A Vol is expected to mean-revert sharply from 24.5% to 10% — useful information for trading the vol term structure.
B The parameters $\kappa$ and $\theta$ are not identified by this dataset — many $(\kappa,\theta)$ pairs fit equally well, and the values have no economic meaning.
C The optimizer diverged — try wider parameter bounds and more starting points.
D The calibration is unreliable because the Feller condition $2\kappa\theta > \xi^2$ is violated.
B captures the key statistical concept: parameter identification. A parameter is identified if the data contains enough information to pin it down uniquely. With max maturity 15 days, the mean-reversion half-life $\ln(2)/18 = 14$ days is comparable to the longest maturity — the variance process barely begins to revert within the sample window. Many different $(\kappa, \theta)$ pairs produce nearly identical short-dated surfaces, so the data cannot distinguish them. Option A is dangerously wrong — these numbers look economically meaningful but are identification artefacts, not market information. Trading on them would be a mistake. Option D (Feller) is a separate concern and does not explain the specific pattern of distortion.
