Sigma-Fields, Measures & Probability Spaces

We ended Chapter 2 with a problem: for continuous sample spaces, Laplace's formula produces $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ — undefined and useless. This chapter is the solution. By the end, you will have the mathematical foundation that every serious quant works on.

How Many Events Can One Experiment Have?

We know that an event is any subset of the sample space $\Omega$. Here's a question worth pausing on: if $\Omega$ has $n$ elementary events, how many distinct events are possible?

The answer is $2^n$. Every elementary event can either be included or excluded from a subset — two choices, $n$ times. A die with 6 outcomes has $2^6 = 64$ possible events. A coin tossed 3 times has $2^8 = 256$. The number explodes fast.

This is why we need a systematic way to organise events. We don't just want any collection of subsets — we want a collection that is well-behaved under the operations we care about: complement, union, intersection. That well-behaved collection has a name: the sigma-field.

Building Events by Combining Events

Often the event we care about is not a single subset — it's the result of combining simpler events. Consider a die roll. Define:

🎲 Worked Example — Even AND Multiple of 3

Question: What is the probability that one roll of a die produces a number that is even and a multiple of 3?

Let $A_1 = $ {outcome is even} $= \{2, 4, 6\}$

Let $A_2 = $ {outcome is multiple of 3} $= \{3, 6\}$

The event $A$ = "even AND multiple of 3" is the intersection:

$A = A_1 \cap A_2 = \{6\}$

$$P(A) = \frac{1}{6}$$

The complement $A_3 = $ "outcome is odd" is just $A_1^c = \{1, 3, 5\}$. Complements give us new events for free.

The key insight: once you have a collection of events, you can manufacture new ones via union ($\cup$), intersection ($\cap$), and complement (${}^c$). But you need to be careful — not every collection of subsets closes properly under these operations. This is where the sigma-field comes in.

The Sigma-Field — The Right Collection of Events

Given a set $X$, a sigma-field (also called a $\sigma$-field or $\sigma$-algebra) $\mathcal{S}$ defined on $X$ is a collection of subsets of $X$ satisfying three axioms:
i
$X \in \mathcal{S}$ — the whole set belongs to $\mathcal{S}$. The certain event is always an event.
ii
Closed under complement: If $A \subseteq X$ and $A \in \mathcal{S}$, then $A^c \in \mathcal{S}$. If an event is in $\mathcal{S}$, so is its complement.
iii
Closed under countable union: If $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ is a finite or countably infinite collection of subsets in $\mathcal{S}$, then $\bigcup_i A_i \in \mathcal{S}$.

Three axioms. That's it. But they have powerful consequences. Let's derive the most important one immediately.

📋 Immediate Results from the Axioms

Result a) $\emptyset \in \mathcal{S}$

Proof: $X \in \mathcal{S}$ by axiom (i). By axiom (ii), $X^c \in \mathcal{S}$. But $X^c$ with respect to itself is $\emptyset$. So $\emptyset \in \mathcal{S}$. ✓

Result b) If $A, B \in \mathcal{S}$, then $A \cap B \in \mathcal{S}$

Proof: Since $A, B \in \mathcal{S}$, by axiom (ii), $A^c, B^c \in \mathcal{S}$. By axiom (iii), $A^c \cup B^c \in \mathcal{S}$. By De Morgan's law: $A^c \cup B^c = (A \cap B)^c$. So $(A \cap B)^c \in \mathcal{S}$. Apply axiom (ii) again: $A \cap B \in \mathcal{S}$. ✓

This extends to countable intersections too — the $\sigma$-field is closed under all the set operations you'll ever need.

In financial modelling, the sigma-field represents the information available at a given time. A filtration — a growing sequence of sigma-fields $\mathcal{F}_0 \subseteq \mathcal{F}_1 \subseteq \cdots \subseteq \mathcal{F}_T$ — models how information accumulates as markets evolve. This is the backbone of option pricing theory and stochastic calculus. When you see $\mathcal{F}_t$ in a derivatives paper, this is what it means.

From Sigma-Field to Measure

The sigma-field tells us which subsets qualify as events. The next question is: how do we assign sizes — probabilities — to those events? That's what a measure does.

Given a set $X$ and a sigma-field $\mathcal{S}$ on $X$, a function $\mu : \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}$ is called a measure if it satisfies three conditions:

(i) $\mu(A) \geq 0$ for all $A \in \mathcal{S}$     (non-negativity)
(ii) $\mu(\emptyset) = 0$     (the empty set has zero measure)
(iii) $\mu(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_n) = \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} \mu(A_i)$ when the $A_i$'s are mutually disjoint     (countable additivity)

A measure is simply a consistent way to assign a "size" to sets. Length is a measure. Area is a measure. Volume is a measure. Probability will be a special case of a measure — one where the total size of $\Omega$ is exactly 1.

Think of it this way. You have a pizza. Cutting it into disjoint slices (no overlaps, nothing missing) and adding up the areas of all slices should give you the total pizza area. That's countable additivity. Probability works the same way — break an event into disjoint pieces, add up their probabilities, get the probability of the whole thing.

Probability Measure — The Special Case That Rules Them All

A probability measure is a measure with one additional requirement: the total measure of the entire space is 1.

A probability measure $P$ on $(\Omega, \mathcal{S})$ is a measure satisfying $P(\Omega) = 1$. Combined with the measure axioms: $$P(\Omega) = 1, \quad P(\emptyset) = 0, \quad P(A) \geq 0 \; \forall A \in \mathcal{S}$$ $$P(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P(A_i) \quad \text{when } A_i \text{ are mutually disjoint}$$

With this in place, we can now name the full structure. The triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{S}, P)$ — sample space, sigma-field, and probability measure — is called a probability space. This is the foundation on which all of modern probability theory is built.

For an event $A$ to have a probability, $A$ must be a member of the sigma-field $\mathcal{S}$. This is not a technicality — it is load-bearing. The probability of $A$ depends entirely on the sigma-field you choose. And as we're about to see, choosing different sigma-fields for the same problem gives you different probabilities. All of them correct.

Bertrand's Paradox — Same Question, Three Correct Answers

A paradox is a statement that seems absurd or contradictory but proves to be well-founded upon investigation. In mathematics, a paradox emerges when the same problem yields multiple solutions that are all internally consistent yet mutually contradictory. That should not happen — and when it does, it means something about the problem is underspecified.

Here is the problem. Read it carefully.

🔵 Bertrand's Problem

Consider a unit circle with an equilateral triangle inscribed inside it. A chord of the circle is chosen at random. What is the probability that the length of the chord is greater than the side length of the equilateral triangle?

The green chord in your notes is longer than the triangle's side. The red chord is shorter. The question: what's the probability that a random chord is "long"?

Seems like a well-posed question. One circle, one triangle, one chord. There should be one answer. Here are three — all mathematically correct.

Solution 1
Random Endpoint
Fix one end of the chord at a vertex of the triangle. The other end is chosen uniformly on the circumference. The chord is longer than the triangle's side when the other end falls on the arc between the other two vertices — which is $\frac{1}{3}$ of the circumference.
1/3
Solution 2
Random Midpoint
Every chord is uniquely determined by its midpoint (draw a perpendicular from the centre). The chord is longer than the triangle's side when its midpoint falls within the inner circle of radius $\frac{1}{2}$. That inner circle has half the radius, so its area is $\frac{1}{4}$ of the unit circle — wait, let's compute properly.
1/2
Solution 3
Random Midpoint (Area)
Same as Solution 2, but now the midpoint is chosen uniformly over the full disc. The chord is longer than the triangle's side iff its midpoint falls inside the inscribed circle of the triangle (radius $= \frac{1}{2}$). Area ratio: $\frac{\pi(1/2)^2}{\pi(1)^2} = \frac{1}{4}$.
1/4

Three methods. Three answers: $\frac{1}{3}$, $\frac{1}{2}$, $\frac{1}{4}$. All are mathematically correct. How?

Because each solution implicitly uses a different sample space and a different sigma-field to define "random chord." The phrase "chosen at random" hides an assumption about the probability measure — and different assumptions produce different, equally valid answers.

This is the lesson Bertrand's Paradox teaches: "random" is not a complete specification. You must explicitly state your probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{S}, P)$. The moment you say "chosen at random," you are making an implicit choice of measure. Make it explicit — or you will get paradoxes.

In quantitative finance, this is why model specification matters so much. "Assume the stock moves randomly" is not a model. Black-Scholes says: the stock follows geometric Brownian motion under a specific probability measure. That is a model.

One More Example — Area as Measure

Here's a clean illustration of probability measure at work. Suppose we choose a random point from the unit square $[0,1] \times [0,1]$.

📐 Problem 1 — Same x and y coordinates

Question: What is the probability that the x-coordinate equals the y-coordinate?

The event $A$ = {points where $x = y$} is the diagonal line from $(0,0)$ to $(1,1)$.

The unit square is 2-dimensional — its natural measure is area. The diagonal line is 1-dimensional — its area is zero.

$$P(A) = \frac{\text{area of line } AC}{\text{area of square } ABCD} = \frac{0}{1} = 0$$

A line has zero area. Even though infinitely many points satisfy $x = y$, the event has probability zero. This would be nonsensical under Laplace — but perfectly coherent under measure theory.

📐 Problem 2 — x-coordinate greater than y-coordinate

Question: What is the probability that the random point has $x > y$?

The event $B$ = {points where $x > y$} is the lower triangle of the unit square — below the diagonal.

$$P(B) = \frac{\text{area of triangle}}{\text{area of square}} = \frac{1/2}{1} = \frac{1}{2}$$
Notice what we just did. We computed probability as a ratio of areas — not a ratio of counts. For continuous sample spaces, counting is meaningless (there are infinitely many points), but area is well-defined. This is probability measure in action: area is the measure, and probability is its normalised version where the total space has measure 1.

Summary — The Complete Picture

Let's consolidate everything from Chapters 1 through 3 into the full framework:

📋 The Complete Probability Framework

• Let $T$ be a random experiment with $\Omega$ as its sample space. Any subset of $\Omega$ is an event.

• $\Omega$ may be discrete (finite or countably infinite) or continuous (uncountably infinite).

• For discrete $\Omega$: probability of an event = (# favourable outcomes) / (# total outcomes) — Laplace's formula.

• For continuous $\Omega$: we need a suitable measure $\mu$ to compute probabilities as $P(A) = \frac{\mu(A)}{\mu(\Omega)}$.

• $(\Omega, \mathcal{S})$ where $\mathcal{S}$ is a sigma-field over $\Omega$ is called a measurable space.

• $(\Omega, \mathcal{S}, P)$ where $P$ is a probability measure is called a probability space.

• To obtain the probability of an event $A$, the event must be a member of $\mathcal{S}$.

• The sigma-field allows us to compute probabilities for events constructed by complement, countable union, and countable intersection of more basic events.

A sigma-field $\mathcal{S}$ on a set $X$ must be closed under which of the following operations?

A
Complement only
B
Countable union only
C
Complement and finite union only
D
Complement and countable union (from which countable intersection also follows)
Answer: D

A sigma-field requires closure under complement (axiom ii) and countable union (axiom iii). Crucially, closure under countable intersection is not an independent axiom — it follows from the two: if $A, B \in \mathcal{S}$, then $A^c, B^c \in \mathcal{S}$ (by complement), so $A^c \cup B^c \in \mathcal{S}$ (by union), and $(A^c \cup B^c) = (A \cap B)^c \in \mathcal{S}$, so $A \cap B \in \mathcal{S}$ (by complement again). Three axioms, more consequences.

probability / ch03 / q03 ★★ Medium

A point is chosen uniformly at random from the unit square $[0,1] \times [0,1]$. What is the probability that the point lies on the diagonal line $x = y$?

A
$\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ — the length of the diagonal divided by the perimeter
B
$\dfrac{1}{2}$ — the diagonal splits the square into two equal triangles
C
$\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{4}$ — the length of the diagonal divided by the area
D
$0$ — a line has zero area, so its measure in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is zero
Answer: D — probability is 0

The natural measure on $[0,1]^2$ is area (2-dimensional Lebesgue measure). The diagonal $\{(x,y): x=y\}$ is a 1-dimensional object — a line — embedded in a 2-dimensional space. Its area is exactly zero.

$$P(x = y) = \frac{\text{area of diagonal}}{\text{area of square}} = \frac{0}{1} = 0$$
This is perfectly consistent — and would be impossible under Laplace's counting framework, which cannot handle uncountably infinite sample spaces. Zero probability does not mean impossible — it means the event has zero measure. This is a crucial distinction in continuous probability.
