The Limit Order Book

The order book is the operating system of every modern exchange. Before microprice, before market making, before any calculation — you must understand the structure that generates all market data.

1.1 What Is the Limit Order Book?

Every trade on NSE, NASDAQ, or any modern electronic exchange is governed by a Limit Order Book (LOB) — a continuously updated record of all outstanding buy and sell orders, organised by price and time.

When you place a limit order (LO), you specify a price. Your order joins a queue at that price, waiting for a counterparty. When you place a market order (MO), you demand immediate execution — and you pay whatever price the resting limit orders are willing to offer.

The LOB is defined on a fixed discrete grid of prices called price levels. The distance between adjacent price levels is the tick size. In India, NIFTY Futures have a tick size of Rs.0.05. The LOB keeps track of all limit orders and applies a matching algorithm to pair incoming market orders with resting limit orders.
Cartea, Jaimungal & Penalva (2015), Chapter 1, Section 1.4 — The Limit Order Book, pp. 14–16.

1.2 Price-Time Priority

Orders at the same price level are executed in time priority — first in, first out. An incoming limit order joins the queue at its price level at the back. An incoming market order is matched against the front of the queue at the best available price. If the market order is larger than the available quantity at the best price, it walks the book — consuming level after level until it is fully filled or cancelled.

Think of each price level as a checkout queue at a supermarket. The best bid is the queue where buyers are waiting to be served. The best ask is where sellers wait. A market buy order goes straight to the seller's queue and takes from the front. If that queue runs out, it moves to the next queue (next price level), paying a slightly higher price. This is "walking the book."

1.3 The Quoted Spread and Midprice

Two of the most fundamental quantities in any LOB are defined by the best bid and best ask prices alone.

The quoted spread is the cost of a round trip — buying at the ask and selling at the bid immediately:

$$\text{Quoted Spread}_t = P_t^a - P_t^b$$

where $P_t^b$ is the best bid price and $P_t^a$ is the best ask price at time $t$.

The midprice is the arithmetic average of the best bid and ask, used as a proxy for the "true" underlying price of the asset — the price that would prevail in a frictionless market with no spread:

$$\text{Midprice}_t = \frac{1}{2}\left(P_t^a + P_t^b\right)$$
Cartea et al. (2015), p. 16 — both equations appear on this page, unnumbered. The midprice is described as a proxy for "the true underlying price of the asset — the price for the asset if there were no explicit or implicit trading costs."
On NSE, the minimum quoted spread for NIFTY Futures is one tick = Rs.0.05. However, at any given moment the actual spread may be several ticks wide, especially during low-liquidity periods (first 15 minutes of session, or post-news). The midprice is used as the settlement reference price in most intraday P&L calculations.
WORKED EXAMPLE — QUOTED SPREAD & MIDPRICE NSE:NIFTY26MAYFUT · 29 Apr 2026

From the live snapshot at 12:34:20 IST:

Best Bid $P_t^b = \text{Rs.}24{,}442.80$  ·  Best Ask $P_t^a = \text{Rs.}24{,}444.90$

Step 1 — Quoted Spread

$$\text{Quoted Spread} = 24{,}444.90 - 24{,}442.80 = \text{Rs.}2.10$$

That is $2.10 / 0.05 = \mathbf{42\ \text{ticks}}$ — a wider-than-minimum spread, indicating a moment of moderate liquidity at the touch.

Step 2 — Midprice

$$\text{Midprice} = \frac{1}{2}(24{,}444.90 + 24{,}442.80) = \frac{49{,}887.70}{2} = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.85$$

Quoted Spread = Rs.2.10 (42 ticks)  ·  Midprice = Rs.24,443.85
The midprice of Rs.24,443.85 is the neutral reference — it does not tell us whether price will go up or down. That requires the microprice, which we derive in Chapter 2 using the volumes at each side.

1.5 What You Are Looking At — A Field Guide

Each row in the order book contains three pieces of information per side:

Price — the level at which these orders are resting. Bid prices decrease as you go down (level 1 is the best, most expensive bid); ask prices increase (level 1 is the cheapest ask).

Quantity — the total number of lots (contracts) resting at this price level. For NIFTY Futures, each lot = 25 units. 7,475 lots = 1,86,875 units of notional exposure.

Order Count — how many individual orders make up that quantity. This is unique to the Fyers TBT feed and most platforms do not display it. It is crucial for detecting iceberg orders and estimating the resilience of each level.

The order book is a snapshot, not a promise. Any resting limit order can be cancelled in microseconds. Large visible quantity does not guarantee that it will absorb an incoming market order — it might evaporate before your order reaches it. This phenomenon, called quote stuffing or layering, is one reason why the midprice alone is an insufficient guide to execution.
NSE uses price-time priority with no hidden order types at the exchange level (unlike some US venues). This means the order count you see from the Fyers TBT feed is the true count of visible orders — there are no exchange-native icebergs in the NSE LOB. Any "iceberg" pattern you detect (high qty, few orders) is a single large participant posting a big block, not a hidden order type.
Practice Problems 4 questions
From the NIFTY26MAYFUT snapshot above: Best Bid = Rs.24,442.80, Best Ask = Rs.24,444.90. Which of the following correctly computes the midprice?
A $\frac{24{,}444.90}{2} = \text{Rs.}12{,}222.45$
B $\frac{24{,}442.80 + 24{,}444.90}{2} = \text{Rs.}24{,}443.85$
C $24{,}444.90 - 24{,}442.80 = \text{Rs.}2.10$
D $\frac{24{,}444.90 - 24{,}442.80}{2} = \text{Rs.}1.05$
Explanation: The midprice is $\frac{1}{2}(P_t^a + P_t^b) = \frac{1}{2}(24{,}444.90 + 24{,}442.80) = Rs.24{,}443.85$. Option C computes the spread (Rs.2.10), and Option D is half the spread (Rs.1.05). Option A incorrectly divides only the ask price.
The quoted spread in the snapshot is Rs.2.10. NIFTY Futures have a tick size of Rs.0.05. How many ticks wide is this spread, and what does it imply?
A 21 ticks — this is the minimum possible spread for NIFTY Futures
B 2 ticks — spreads are always measured in whole rupees
C 42 ticks — wider than minimum, indicating moderate spread at this moment
D 42 ticks — this is always the minimum spread for NIFTY Futures
Explanation: $2.10 \div 0.05 = 42$ ticks. The minimum possible spread is 1 tick = Rs.0.05. A 42-tick spread means there is a gap of 42 price levels between the best bid and the best ask — this is wider than minimum and suggests that at this moment, there is no liquidity between Rs.24,442.80 and Rs.24,444.90. The minimum spread of 1 tick only occurs when the book is extremely tight (very liquid conditions).
A market sell order for 600 lots arrives. Looking at the bid side of the book above, the top levels are: L1 = 130 lots at Rs.24,442.80, L2 = 195 lots at Rs.24,442.50, L3 = 65 lots at Rs.24,441.20. What happens?
A The entire order fills at Rs.24,442.80 since the MO gets the best bid
B The order walks the book: 130 lots at Rs.24,442.80, 195 lots at Rs.24,442.50, 65 lots at Rs.24,441.20, then continues to deeper levels for the remaining 210 lots
C The order is rejected because 600 lots exceeds the visible depth
D The order fills 390 lots (130+195+65) and the remaining 210 lots are cancelled
Explanation: Market orders walk the book by price-time priority. The MO consumes L1 (130 lots at Rs.24,442.80), then L2 (195 lots at Rs.24,442.50), then L3 (65 lots at Rs.24,441.20) — 390 lots consumed so far. The remaining 210 lots continue walking to L4, L5, and so on until fully filled. The order is not cancelled — a standard market order on NSE will fill completely, accepting whatever prices are available. (Only an IOC — Immediate or Cancel — order would cancel the unfilled portion.)
Bid Level 8 shows: price Rs.24,440.00 · qty 7,475 lots · orders 22. Ask Level 18 shows: price Rs.24,450.00 · qty 12,415 lots · orders 116. Which statement best describes the structural difference between these two levels?
A The ask level is stronger because it has more total lots
B The bid level is more concentrated (340 lots/order) suggesting a single institutional position; the ask level is fragmented (107 lots/order) suggesting many retail participants at a round number
C Both levels are equivalent since the order count doesn't affect price impact
D The bid level is more fragile because fewer orders means less commitment
Explanation: Average size per order at Bid L8: $7475 \div 22 = 340$ lots/order. At Ask L18: $12415 \div 116 = 107$ lots/order. The bid concentration (340 lots per order) across only 22 orders points to a small number of large participants — likely a systematic strategy or institutional desk providing support at Rs.24,440. The ask fragmentation at Rs.24,450 (the round number) across 116 orders is typical retail behaviour. Crucially, Option D is wrong: fewer orders with high quantity per order is more resilient, not less — it takes a very large incoming MO to consume a single 340-lot order. In contrast, the ask side's 116 small orders can begin cancelling rapidly under pressure, making that level fragile despite the higher total quantity.
