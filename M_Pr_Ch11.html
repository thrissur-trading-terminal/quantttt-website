Named Continuous Distributions

Gamma, Beta, Laplace, Pareto, Cauchy, Weibull, Normal — the full zoo of continuous distributions that appear throughout probability, statistics, and quantitative finance. Each has a distinct shape, a distinct domain, and a reason to exist.

The Gamma Distribution

Gamma integral (essential background): $$\Gamma(\alpha) = \int_0^\infty e^{-y}\, y^{\alpha-1}\, dy, \quad \alpha > 0$$ Key facts: $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$ when $n$ is a positive integer. $\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \sqrt{\pi}$.
$X \sim \Gamma(\lambda, \alpha)$ has PDF: $$f(x) = \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\, e^{-\lambda x}\, x^{\alpha-1}, \quad x > 0,\quad \lambda > 0,\quad \alpha > 0$$ Validity: Substitute $y = \lambda x$: the integral becomes $\frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\int_0^\infty e^{-y} y^{\alpha-1} dy = 1$. $\checkmark$

Special case: Exp$(\lambda)$ is $\Gamma(\lambda, 1)$ — set $\alpha=1$ and $\Gamma(1)=1$.
Reproductive Property: If $X_1 \sim \Gamma(\lambda,\alpha)$ and $X_2 \sim \Gamma(\lambda,\beta)$ are independent (same $\lambda$!): $$X_1 + X_2 \sim \Gamma(\lambda,\, \alpha+\beta)$$
Let $Z = X_1 + X_2$. The convolution integral: $$f_Z(z) = \int_0^z \frac{\lambda^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)}e^{-\lambda x}x^{\alpha-1} \cdot \frac{\lambda^\beta}{\Gamma(\beta)}e^{-\lambda(z-x)}(z-x)^{\beta-1}\,dx = \frac{\lambda^{\alpha+\beta}}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}\,e^{-\lambda z}\int_0^z x^{\alpha-1}(z-x)^{\beta-1}\,dx$$ Substitute $y = x/z$, so $x = zy$, $dx = z\,dy$, limits $0 \to 1$: $$= \frac{\lambda^{\alpha+\beta}}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}\,e^{-\lambda z}\cdot z^{\alpha+\beta-1}\int_0^1 y^{\alpha-1}(1-y)^{\beta-1}\,dy = \frac{\lambda^{\alpha+\beta}}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}\,e^{-\lambda z}\cdot z^{\alpha+\beta-1}\cdot B(\alpha,\beta)$$ Since $B(\alpha,\beta) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}$: $$f_Z(z) = \frac{\lambda^{\alpha+\beta}}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}\,e^{-\lambda z}\,z^{\alpha+\beta-1} \qquad \square$$ This is the $\Gamma(\lambda,\alpha+\beta)$ PDF. The $\alpha$-parameters add; $\lambda$ stays fixed.
In risk modelling: if claim sizes on an insurance portfolio follow independent Exp$(\lambda)$ distributions, the total of $n$ claims follows $\Gamma(\lambda, n)$. This is used directly in reinsurance pricing and VaR computation for loss aggregation.

The Beta Distribution

The Beta function is the glue between Gamma distributions:

$$B(\alpha,\beta) = \int_0^1 x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}\,dx = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)\,\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}$$
Beta Type 1 — $X \sim \beta_1(\alpha,\beta)$, support $[0,1]$: $$f(x) = \frac{1}{B(\alpha,\beta)}\,x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}, \quad 0 \leq x \leq 1$$ Special case: $\beta_1(1,1)$ gives $f(x)=1$ on $[0,1]$ — the Uniform distribution. Proof: $B(1,1) = \frac{\Gamma(1)\Gamma(1)}{\Gamma(2)} = \frac{1\cdot 1}{1!} = 1$.
Beta Type 2 — $X \sim \beta_2(m,n)$, support $(0,\infty)$: $$f(x) = \frac{1}{B(m,n)}\,\frac{x^{m-1}}{(1+x)^{m+n}}, \quad x > 0$$ Validity: substitute $y = \frac{1}{1+x}$; the integral transforms to $B(m,n)$. $\checkmark$
$\beta_1$ lives on $[0,1]$ — natural for modelling probabilities, proportions, and option deltas. $\beta_2$ lives on $(0,\infty)$ with a heavy tail — natural for ratios and odds. Together they cover the two most common support structures after the real line itself.

Laplace Distribution (Double Exponential)

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{2}e^{-|x|}, \quad -\infty < x < \infty$$ Validity: $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{1}{2}e^{-|x|}dx = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2}\int_0^\infty e^{-x}dx = 1$. $\checkmark$

Note: $F(0) = \frac{1}{2}$ by symmetry. The generalised form with location $\theta$ and scale $\lambda$: $f(x) = \frac{\lambda}{2}e^{-\lambda|x-\theta|}$.
The Laplace is what you get when you stitch two exponential tails together at the origin. It has heavier tails than the Normal but lighter than the Cauchy. In finance it appears in models for daily returns — it captures the excess kurtosis (fat tails) that the Normal misses, without the pathological infinite-variance problem of the Cauchy.

Pareto Distribution

Parameters $\alpha > 0$, $\sigma > 0$. Support $x > \sigma$: $$f(x) = \alpha\,\frac{\sigma^\alpha}{x^{\alpha+1}}, \quad x > \sigma$$ $$F(x) = 1 - \left(\frac{\sigma}{x}\right)^\alpha, \quad x > \sigma$$ Validity: $\int_\sigma^\infty \alpha\sigma^\alpha x^{-(\alpha+1)}dx = \alpha\sigma^\alpha\left[\frac{x^{-\alpha}}{-\alpha}\right]_\sigma^\infty = \sigma^\alpha \cdot \sigma^{-\alpha} = 1$. $\checkmark$
The Pareto distribution is the mathematical expression of the 80/20 rule. In Indian equity markets: 20% of stocks account for 80% of total market cap movement. In options: the Pareto tail describes extreme loss events better than the Normal. The parameter $\alpha$ controls tail heaviness — smaller $\alpha$ means fatter tails and more extreme events.

Cauchy Distribution

$X \sim C(\mu, \theta)$, support $\mathbb{R}$: $$f(x) = \frac{\mu}{\pi}\,\frac{1}{\mu^2 + (x-\theta)^2}, \quad -\infty < x < \infty$$ Standard form $C(1,0)$: $f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi}\frac{1}{1+x^2}$.

Validity: $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{1}{\pi}\frac{1}{1+x^2}dx = \frac{1}{\pi}[\arctan x]_{-\infty}^\infty = \frac{1}{\pi}\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-\left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\right) = 1$. $\checkmark$
Reproductive Property: If $X, Y \sim C(1,0)$ are independent, then $X+Y \sim C(2,0)$ with $f(z) = \frac{2}{\pi}\frac{1}{4+z^2}$.
The Cauchy has no finite mean and no finite variance. The usual law of large numbers fails — the sample mean of Cauchy variables does not converge. This makes the Cauchy the canonical "pathological" distribution in probability theory. It appears in physics as the Lorentz distribution describing spectral line shapes.

Weibull Distribution

Parameter $c > 0$, support $x > 0$: $$f(x) = c\,x^{c-1}\,e^{-x^c}$$ Validity: Substitute $y = x^c$, $dy = cx^{c-1}dx$: $\int_0^\infty cx^{c-1}e^{-x^c}dx = \int_0^\infty e^{-y}dy = 1$. $\checkmark$
When $c=1$: reduces to Exp(1). Belongs to the exponential family.

The Normal Distribution

$X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, parameters $\mu \in \mathbb{R}$, $\sigma^2 > 0$: $$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\sigma}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \quad -\infty < x < \infty$$ Standard Normal: $N(0,1)$ with $\varphi(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2}$.
Validity of $N(0,1)$: Need $\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/2}dx = \sqrt{2\pi}$.

Since $e^{-x^2/2}$ is even: $= 2\int_0^\infty e^{-x^2/2}dx$. Substitute $y = x^2$, $dy = 2x\,dx$, $dx = \frac{dy}{2\sqrt{y}}$: $$= 2\int_0^\infty e^{-y/2}\,\frac{dy}{2\sqrt{y}} = \int_0^\infty e^{-y/2}\,y^{-1/2}\,dy$$ This is a Gamma integral with $\lambda = \frac{1}{2}$, $\alpha = \frac{1}{2}$: $$= \frac{\Gamma(1/2)}{(1/2)^{1/2}} = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{1/\sqrt{2}} = \sqrt{2\pi} \qquad \square$$ For general $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$: substitute $z = \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}$, $dx = \sigma\,dz$, integral reduces to the standard normal case $= 1$. $\square$

A Beautiful Connection: Normal $\to$ Chi-Squared $\to$ Gamma

If $X \sim N(0,1)$, then $X^2 \sim \Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \chi^2_{(1)}$.

If $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ are independent $N(0,1)$, then: $$X_1^2 + X_2^2 + \cdots + X_n^2 \sim \Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2},\,\tfrac{n}{2}\right) = \chi^2_{(n)}$$
Let $Y = X^2$. The CDF: $F_Y(y) = P(X^2 \leq y) = P(-\sqrt{y} \leq X \leq \sqrt{y}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\sqrt{y}}^{\sqrt{y}} e^{-x^2/2}dx$.

Differentiate via Leibniz rule: $$f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\left[e^{-y/2}\cdot\frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} - e^{-y/2}\cdot\left(-\frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}}\right)\right] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,e^{-y/2}\,y^{-1/2}$$ Since $\sqrt{\pi} = \Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)$: $$f_Y(y) = \frac{(1/2)^{1/2}}{\Gamma(1/2)}\,e^{-y/2}\,y^{1/2-1} \qquad (0 < y < \infty)$$ This is exactly $\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}\right)$. By the Gamma reproductive property applied $n$ times, the sum of $n$ such terms is $\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{n}{2}\right) = \chi^2_{(n)}$. $\square$
The chi-squared distribution is the workhorse of statistical testing in quantitative finance. The Jarque-Bera test for normality of returns follows $\chi^2_{(2)}$. The Ljung-Box test for autocorrelation in residuals follows $\chi^2_{(m)}$. Every time a quant runs a model diagnostic on NSE data, they are implicitly using the fact that $X \sim N(0,1) \Rightarrow X^2 \sim \chi^2_{(1)}$.

$X \sim \beta_1(1,1)$. What is the distribution of $X$?

A
Exponential(1)
B
Uniform$[0,1]$ — $B(1,1)=1$ so $f(x)=1$
C
Normal$(0,1)$
D
Gamma$(1,1)$
Answer: B

$f(x) = \frac{1}{B(1,1)}x^0(1-x)^0 = \frac{1}{B(1,1)}$. Now $B(1,1) = \frac{\Gamma(1)\Gamma(1)}{\Gamma(2)} = \frac{1 \cdot 1}{1!} = 1$. So $f(x) = 1$ for $0 \leq x \leq 1$ — exactly the Uniform$[0,1]$ distribution. The Beta family contains the Uniform as its simplest special case.
probability / ch11 / q02★★ Medium

$X_1 \sim \Gamma(\lambda, 3)$ and $X_2 \sim \Gamma(\lambda, 5)$ are independent. What is the distribution of $X_1 + X_2$?

A
$\Gamma(\lambda, 15)$ — multiply the shape parameters
B
$\Gamma(\lambda, 8)$ — add the shape parameters
C
$\Gamma(2\lambda, 8)$ — both parameters add
D
Not Gamma — different shape parameters prevent combination
Answer: B — $\Gamma(\lambda, 8)$

The Gamma reproductive property: same $\lambda$, shape parameters add: $3 + 5 = 8$. $\lambda$ stays fixed; only $\alpha$ accumulates. This is the continuous analogue of the Binomial reproductive property requiring the same $p$. Option D is wrong — the shape parameters can differ; what must match is $\lambda$.
probability / ch11 / q03★★ Medium

Which distribution has no finite mean and no finite variance?

A
Laplace — its heavy tails make moments infinite
B
Pareto with small $\alpha$ — the tail exponent determines moment existence
C
Cauchy — even the first moment (mean) does not exist
D
Weibull — the exponential tail kills all moments
Answer: C — Cauchy

The Cauchy PDF $\frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}$ decays so slowly that $\int_{-\infty}^\infty |x| \cdot \frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}dx = \infty$. The mean literally does not exist as a finite number. Averaging Cauchy samples never converges — the Law of Large Numbers fails. Laplace and Weibull have all moments finite. Pareto with $\alpha > 1$ has a finite mean.
probability / ch11 / q04★★★ Hard

$X \sim N(0,1)$. The distribution of $X^2$ is:

A
$N(0,1)$ — squaring a standard normal gives another standard normal
B
$\Gamma(1, \frac{1}{2})$ — squaring doubles the rate
C
$\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \chi^2_{(1)}$ — derived via CDF differentiation
D
Uniform$(0,1)$ — since $X$ is symmetric around 0
Answer: C — $\chi^2_{(1)}$

From the proof: $F_{X^2}(y) = P(-\sqrt{y} \leq X \leq \sqrt{y})$. Differentiating gives $f_{X^2}(y) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-y/2}y^{-1/2} = \frac{(1/2)^{1/2}}{\Gamma(1/2)}e^{-y/2}y^{1/2-1}$, which is $\Gamma(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2})$, called $\chi^2_{(1)}$. This connection is the foundation of classical hypothesis testing.
probability / ch11 / q05★★★ Hard

A quant runs 5 independent factor regressions and collects standardised residuals $Z_1, \ldots, Z_5$, each $\sim N(0,1)$. The Jarque-Bera test statistic $T = Z_1^2 + \cdots + Z_5^2$ follows which distribution?

A
$N(0,5)$ — sum of normals is normal
B
$\Gamma(1, 5)$ — Gamma with rate 1 and shape 5
C
$\chi^2_{(5)} = \Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{5}{2}\right)$ — sum of 5 independent $\chi^2_{(1)}$
D
$\Gamma\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}\right)$ — same as a single squared normal
Answer: C — $\chi^2_{(5)}$

Each $Z_i^2 \sim \chi^2_{(1)} = \Gamma(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2})$. By the Gamma reproductive property (same $\lambda = \frac{1}{2}$, shape parameters add): $\sum_{i=1}^5 Z_i^2 \sim \Gamma\!\left(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{5}{2}\right) = \chi^2_{(5)}$. This is why chi-squared tests have degrees of freedom equal to the number of squared standard normals summed. Option A: sum of $N(0,1)$ is $N(0,n)$, not $\chi^2$ — you're summing the squares, not the variables themselves.
