CTMC II — Kolmogorov Equations

The generator matrix $Q$ encodes everything about a CTMC. Kolmogorov's forward and backward equations turn $Q$ into a differential equation for the transition probability matrix $P(t) = e^{Qt}$ — the central result of CTMC analysis.

The Generator Matrix $Q$

For a CTMC with state space $\mathcal{S} = \{1, 2, \ldots, m\}$, the generator matrix (or rate matrix) $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ is defined by:

$$q_{ij} \ge 0 \text{ for } i \neq j \quad \text{(transition rate from } i \text{ to } j\text{)}$$ $$q_{ii} = -\nu_i = -\sum_{j \neq i} q_{ij} \quad \text{(each row sums to zero)}$$
The diagonal entries of $Q$ are negative — they capture the rate at which probability "leaks out" of each state. The off-diagonal entries are non-negative — they capture the rate probability flows in. Every row summing to zero is the continuous-time analogue of every row of a DTMC transition matrix summing to one.

Infinitesimal Behaviour

For small $\Delta > 0$, the transition probabilities behave as:

$$P_{ij}(\Delta) = \begin{cases} q_{ij}\Delta + o(\Delta) & i \neq j \\ 1 - \nu_i\Delta + o(\Delta) & i = j \end{cases}$$

This says: in a short window $\Delta$, the probability of transitioning from $i$ to $j \neq i$ is approximately $q_{ij}\Delta$ — linear in $\Delta$. This is the continuous-time version of the BRP arrival probability $\lambda \Delta t$ from Chapter 6.

Kolmogorov Forward Equation

Let $P(t) = [P_{ij}(t)]$ be the transition probability matrix, where $P_{ij}(t) = P(X(t)=j \mid X(0)=i)$. Using Chapman-Kolmogorov ($P(t+\Delta) = P(t)P(\Delta)$) and taking $\Delta \to 0$:

$$\frac{P(t+\Delta) - P(t)}{\Delta} = P(t)\cdot\frac{P(\Delta) - I}{\Delta}$$ Taking $\Delta \to 0$: $\lim_{\Delta\to 0} \frac{P(\Delta)-I}{\Delta} = Q$ (by the infinitesimal behaviour above). Therefore: $$\boxed{\frac{dP(t)}{dt} = P(t)\,Q, \quad P(0) = I}$$ This is the Kolmogorov Forward Equation.

Kolmogorov Backward Equation

Using $P(t+\Delta) = P(\Delta)P(t)$ instead (expanding at the beginning of the interval):

$$\frac{dP(t)}{dt} = Q\,P(t), \quad P(0) = I$$

Since both forward and backward equations have the same solution, $P(t)$ and $Q$ commute: $P(t)Q = QP(t)$.

Solution: The Matrix Exponential

Both equations have the same elegant solution — the matrix exponential:

$$P(t) = e^{Qt} = I + Qt + \frac{Q^2t^2}{2!} + \frac{Q^3t^3}{3!} + \cdots$$

When $Q$ is diagonalisable as $Q = V\Lambda V^{-1}$ (where $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_m)$):

$$e^{Qt} = V\,e^{\Lambda t}\,V^{-1} \qquad e^{\Lambda t} = \text{diag}(e^{\lambda_1 t}, \ldots, e^{\lambda_m t})$$
This is the matrix analogue of solving $\dot{y} = cy \implies y(t) = e^{ct}$. The eigenvalues of $Q$ determine how fast the CTMC converges. Since each row of $Q$ sums to zero, $\lambda = 0$ is always an eigenvalue — corresponding to the steady-state. All other eigenvalues have negative real part (for irreducible chains), so $e^{Qt}$ converges as $t \to \infty$.

State Distribution Evolution

Let $\underline{p}(t) = [P(X_t=1), \ldots, P(X_t=m)]$ be the row vector of state probabilities. Then:

$$\underline{p}(t) = \underline{p}(0)\,P(t) = \underline{p}(0)\,e^{Qt} \qquad \frac{d\underline{p}(t)}{dt} = \underline{p}(t)\,Q$$
🔄 Worked Example — Two-State Server

States: 0 = Idle, 1 = Busy. Rate $\lambda$ from 0→1, rate $\mu$ from 1→0.

Generator: $Q = \begin{pmatrix} -\lambda & \lambda \\ \mu & -\mu \end{pmatrix}$

The Kolmogorov forward equations for $\underline{p}(t) = [p_0(t),\; p_1(t)]$:

$$\dot{p}_0 = -\lambda p_0 + \mu p_1 \qquad \dot{p}_1 = \lambda p_0 - \mu p_1$$

These are coupled ODEs. "Flow into state 1" = $\lambda p_0$, "Flow out of state 1" = $\mu p_1$. The rate of change of $p_1$ equals (flow in) minus (flow out) — the flow balance intuition.

Solution starting from $p_0(0)=1$ (start idle):

$$p_1(t) = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda+\mu}\left(1 - e^{-(\lambda+\mu)t}\right) \xrightarrow{t\to\infty} \frac{\lambda}{\lambda+\mu}$$

Steady-State Distribution

A probability vector $\underline{\pi} = [\pi_1, \ldots, \pi_m]$ is the steady-state distribution of a CTMC if: $$\underline{\pi}\,Q = \underline{0} \qquad \text{(balance equations)} \qquad \sum_i \pi_i = 1, \quad \pi_i \ge 0$$ This is the condition $\frac{d\underline{p}}{dt} = 0$ — the distribution is stationary.

The balance equations written out for each state $i$:

$$\underbrace{\sum_{j \neq i} \pi_j q_{ji}}_{\text{flow into } i} = \underbrace{\pi_i \sum_{j \neq i} q_{ij}}_{\text{flow out of } i} \quad \forall i \in \mathcal{S}$$
The two-state server is a model for a trading desk's risk state: either within limits (Idle=0) or breaching limits (Busy=1). The rate $\lambda$ is how fast positions move to breach, $\mu$ is how fast risk managers resolve breaches. The steady-state fraction of time in breach is $\lambda/(\lambda+\mu)$ — directly useful for setting risk limit thresholds.

Reversible CTMCs

A CTMC is reversible (or satisfies detailed balance) if the flow between every pair of states is balanced individually — not just globally:

$$\pi_i\, q_{ij} = \pi_j\, q_{ji} \quad \forall i, j \in \mathcal{S}$$

Reversibility is stronger than just having a stationary distribution. It means the process looks the same forwards and backwards in time. Birth-Death chains are always reversible — the detailed balance equations are precisely the local balance equations from Chapter 7.

Not all CTMCs with stationary distributions are reversible. A 3-state chain with cyclic transitions $1 \to 2 \to 3 \to 1$ has a stationary distribution but is not reversible — you can tell the direction of time by watching which direction the cycle flows. Checking $\pi_i q_{ij} = \pi_j q_{ji}$ for all pairs is a quick test.
🔢 Worked Example — Checking Reversibility

3-state CTMC with $Q = \begin{pmatrix} -3 & 2 & 1 \\ 1 & -4 & 3 \\ 2 & 1 & -3 \end{pmatrix}$.

Solve $\underline{\pi}Q = \underline{0}$ with $\sum \pi_i = 1$. The system gives $\pi = [5/18,\; 5/18,\; 8/18]$.

Check reversibility: $\pi_1 q_{12} = \frac{5}{18} \times 2 = \frac{10}{18}$, but $\pi_2 q_{21} = \frac{5}{18} \times 1 = \frac{5}{18}$.

Since $\pi_1 q_{12} \neq \pi_2 q_{21}$, this chain is NOT reversible — despite having a stationary distribution.

Irreducibility and Aperiodicity for CTMCs

A CTMC is irreducible if every state is reachable from every other state (via $P_{ij}(t) > 0$ for some $t > 0$). Unlike DTMCs, CTMCs with exponential holding times are almost always aperiodic — the exponential distribution is continuous, so return times form a continuous set rather than a lattice.

Ergodic CTMC Theorem: An irreducible CTMC with finite state space has a unique stationary distribution $\underline{\pi}$, and: $$\lim_{t\to\infty} P_{ij}(t) = \pi_j \quad \forall i, j \in \mathcal{S}$$ The chain converges to stationarity from any starting state.

A CTMC has generator $Q = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 2 \\ 3 & -3 \end{pmatrix}$. What is the steady-state probability $\pi_0$ of being in state 0?

A
$2/5$
B
$3/5$
C
$1/2$
D
$2/3$
Answer: B — $3/5$

Solve $\underline{\pi}Q = 0$ with $\pi_0 + \pi_1 = 1$. From the first column: $-2\pi_0 + 3\pi_1 = 0 \implies \pi_1 = \frac{2}{3}\pi_0$.
Normalising: $\pi_0 + \frac{2}{3}\pi_0 = 1 \implies \pi_0 = \frac{3}{5}$, $\pi_1 = \frac{2}{5}$.

For a two-state chain: $\pi_0 = \frac{\mu}{\lambda+\mu} = \frac{3}{2+3} = \frac{3}{5}$. The state with the higher exit rate from the other state has higher steady-state probability.

The Kolmogorov Forward Equation for a CTMC's transition matrix $P(t)$ is:

A
$\dfrac{dP}{dt} = QP(t)$
B
$\dfrac{dP}{dt} = P(t)Q$
C
$P(t) = QP(t)$
D
$\dfrac{dP}{dt} = P(t) + Q$
Answer: B — $\frac{dP}{dt} = P(t)Q$

The Forward Equation comes from expanding $P(t+\Delta) = P(t)P(\Delta)$ at the end of the interval. Option A is the Backward Equation, which expands at the beginning: $P(t+\Delta) = P(\Delta)P(t)$. Both have the same solution $P(t) = e^{Qt}$ because $P(t)$ and $Q$ commute.

Forward: $\dot{P} = PQ$ (post-multiply by Q). Backward: $\dot{P} = QP$ (pre-multiply by Q). Same solution, different derivations.

A 2-state CTMC has $\lambda = 1$ (rate 0→1) and $\mu = 4$ (rate 1→0). In the long run, what fraction of time is the system in state 1 (busy)?

A
$4/5$
B
$1/5$
C
$1/4$
D
$1/2$

For the two-state chain: $\pi_1 = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda+\mu} = \frac{1}{1+4} = \frac{1}{5}$.

The system is busy only 20% of the time despite $\mu$ being just 4× larger than $\lambda$ — because when it enters state 1 (busy), it exits quickly at rate $\mu=4$. $\pi_1 = \lambda/(\lambda+\mu)$ is also the server utilisation formula in M/M/1 queuing — a preview of Chapter 10.
stochastic / ch08 / q04 ★★★ Hard

A CTMC has stationary distribution $\pi = [0.4, 0.6]$ and $q_{01} = 3$. For detailed balance (reversibility) to hold, what must $q_{10}$ be?

A
$3$
B
$4.5$
C
$2$
D
$1.8$
Answer: C — $2$

Detailed balance requires $\pi_0 q_{01} = \pi_1 q_{10}$: $$0.4 \times 3 = 0.6 \times q_{10} \implies q_{10} = \frac{1.2}{0.6} = 2$$ This makes intuitive sense: state 1 has higher stationary probability (0.6 vs 0.4), so it needs a lower exit rate to maintain balance — fewer transitions out compensate for more time spent there. Detailed balance: the flow $0 \to 1$ (probability × rate) equals the flow $1 \to 0$. Both equal $0.4 \times 3 = 1.2$ per unit time.
