Renewal Theory

The BRP had geometric inter-arrivals. The Poisson process had exponential. Now we go fully general — arbitrary iid inter-arrival times. The surprising result: even without knowing the distribution, we can characterise long-run behaviour exactly. That's the power of renewal theory.

The Generalisation

Both BRP and PCP are special cases where inter-arrival times $\{T_i\}$ are iid with arbitrary distribution $F_T$ and $\mu = E[T] > 0$.

BRP
$T_k \sim$ Geom$(p)$
Discrete, $N(t) \sim$ Binomial
PCP
$T_k \sim$ Exp$(\lambda)$
Continuous, $N(t) \sim$ Poisson
Renewal
$T_k \sim F_T$ (any)
General, $N(t)$ via convolutions
A Renewal Process is a counting process $N(t)$ with iid inter-arrivals $T_1, T_2, \ldots \sim F_T$, $\mu = E[T] > 0$: $$S_0 = 0, \quad S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n T_i, \quad N(t) = \max\{n \ge 0 : S_n \le t\}$$
A machine replaced each time it fails. Each lifetime $T_i$ is drawn from the same distribution. $N(t)$ = total replacements by time $t$. Renewal theory gives the long-run replacement rate without specifying the distribution.

$N(t)$ Is Finite, $N(\infty) = \infty$

$N(t) < \infty$: SLLN gives $S_n/n \to \mu > 0$, so $S_n \to \infty$. Only finitely many $n$ satisfy $S_n \le t$. $\square$

$N(\infty) = \infty$ a.s.: $P(N(\infty) < \infty) \le \sum_{n} P(T_n = \infty) = 0$. $\square$

Distribution of $N(t)$ and the Renewal Function

Using $\{N(t) \ge n\} = \{S_n \le t\}$:

$$P(N(t) = n) = F_T^{(n)}(t) - F_T^{(n+1)}(t)$$ $$m(t) = E[N(t)] = \sum_{n=1}^\infty F_T^{(n)}(t)$$

where $F_T^{(n)}$ is the $n$-fold convolution. Conditioning on $X_1$ gives the renewal equation:

$$m(t) = F_T(t) + \int_0^t m(t-x)\,f_T(x)\,dx$$
$m(t)$ is the expected number of component replacements by time $t$. In bond markets, if each defaulted bond is replaced, $m(t)$ counts expected defaults. It also drives warranty cost modelling — each replacement restarts the renewal cycle.

Elementary Renewal Theorem

As $t \to \infty$:   $\dfrac{N(t)}{t} \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} \dfrac{1}{\mu}$   and   $\dfrac{m(t)}{t} \to \dfrac{1}{\mu}$
Sandwich: $\dfrac{S_{N(t)}}{N(t)} \le \dfrac{t}{N(t)} \le \dfrac{S_{N(t)+1}}{N(t)}$

Left $\to \mu$ by SLLN. Right $= \dfrac{S_{N(t)+1}}{N(t)+1} \cdot \dfrac{N(t)+1}{N(t)} \to \mu \cdot 1$. Squeeze gives $t/N(t) \to \mu$. $\square$
$N(t)/t \to 1/\mu$ a.s. does NOT automatically give $E[N(t)/t] \to 1/\mu$. The mean convergence is proved separately using uniform integrability.

CLT for Renewal Processes

With $\mu = E[T]$ and $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}(T)$: $$\frac{N(t) - t/\mu}{\sqrt{t\sigma^2/\mu^3}} \xrightarrow{D} N(0,1) \qquad N(t) \approx N\!\left(\frac{t}{\mu},\; \frac{t\sigma^2}{\mu^3}\right)$$
The variance formula $t\sigma^2/\mu^3$ involves all three parameters. When $\sigma^2 = 0$ (perfectly regular arrivals), zero fluctuation. High inter-arrival variance inflates uncertainty in the cumulative count — quantified exactly.

Renewal Reward Theorem

Attach reward $R_n$ to the $n$-th renewal cycle. Total reward $R(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{N(t)} R_n$.

If $E[R] < \infty$ and $E[T] < \infty$: $$\lim_{t\to\infty} \frac{R(t)}{t} = \frac{E[R]}{E[T]} \quad \text{(a.s.)} \qquad \lim_{t\to\infty} \frac{E[R(t)]}{t} = \frac{E[R]}{E[T]}$$
$\dfrac{R(t)}{t} = \underbrace{\dfrac{\sum_{n=1}^{N(t)} R_n}{N(t)}}_{\to E[R] \text{ (SLLN)}} \times \underbrace{\dfrac{N(t)}{t}}_{\to 1/E[T]} \to \dfrac{E[R]}{E[T]}$ $\square$

Age, Excess, and the Inspection Paradox

At time $t$: Age $A(t) = t - S_{N(t)}$, Excess $Y(t) = S_{N(t)+1} - t$.

Average Age via Renewal Reward

During a cycle of length $X$, age grows from 0 to $X$. Reward $= \int_0^X t\,dt = X^2/2$. By the Renewal Reward Theorem:

$$\lim_{s\to\infty} \frac{1}{s}\int_0^s A(t)\,dt = \frac{E[X^2/2]}{E[X]} = \frac{E[X^2]}{2E[X]}$$

Same result for average excess life (reward $= \int_0^X(X-t)\,dt = X^2/2$). For Poisson: average age $= 1/\lambda$ — the memoryless property confirmed.

Inspection Paradox: Average age $= E[X^2]/(2E[X]) \ge E[X]/2$ by Jensen. Arriving at a random time, your expected wait exceeds half the average headway. High variance makes this worse — the classic bus paradox.

Alternating Renewal Processes

Alternates between ON ($Z_n$) and OFF ($Y_n$) periods, $\{(Z_n, Y_n)\}$ iid. Cycle length $T_n = Z_n + Y_n$:

$$\text{Long-run fraction ON} = \frac{E[Z]}{E[Z] + E[Y]}$$
A market maker ON (quoting) with mean $E[Z]$ and OFF (risk-off) with mean $E[Y]$. Long-run quoting fraction $= E[Z]/(E[Z]+E[Y])$ — distribution-free, only means matter. This drives capacity planning for HFT desks and circuit breaker design.

stochastic / ch09 / q01★ Easy

A machine fails every $\mu = 5$ days on average (arbitrary distribution). The long-run rate of replacements per day is:

A
$5$ per day
B
$1/5$ per day
C
Depends on the full distribution of failure times
D
$\sqrt{5}$ per day
Answer: B — $1/5$ per day

By the Elementary Renewal Theorem: $N(t)/t \to 1/\mu = 1/5$. Option C is the key misconception — the long-run rate depends only on $\mu$, not the full distribution. Distribution-free results for long-run behaviour: that is the power of renewal theory.
stochastic / ch09 / q02★★ Medium

A renewal process has $E[T] = 4$ and $E[T^2] = 20$. What is the long-run average age $\lim_{s\to\infty} \frac{1}{s}\int_0^s A(t)\,dt$?

A
$4$
B
$2$
C
$2.5$
D
$5$
Answer: C — $2.5$

Average age $= E[T^2]/(2E[T]) = 20/(8) = 2.5$. Note $E[T]/2 = 2$, but the average age is $2.5 > 2$ — the inspection paradox. Var$(T) = 20-16 = 4 > 0$ inflates the average above $E[T]/2$. Higher variance means higher average age and longer expected wait at a random arrival time.
stochastic / ch09 / q03★★ Medium

A trading desk is ON for an average of 3 hours and OFF for an average of 1 hour. What fraction of time is it ON in the long run?

A
$3/4$ only if ON and OFF times are exponential
B
$3/4$ regardless of the distribution
C
$1/4$
D
Cannot be determined without the full distributions
Answer: B — $3/4$ regardless of distribution

Fraction ON $= E[Z]/(E[Z]+E[Y]) = 3/4$. This holds for any distributions with finite means. Only the means matter for long-run fractions — the Renewal Reward Theorem gives distribution-free results.
stochastic / ch09 / q04★★★ Hard

With $E[T]=2$ and $\text{Var}(T)=4$, what is the approximate standard deviation of $N(100)$ by the CLT for renewal processes?

A
$\sqrt{200} \approx 14.1$
B
$\sqrt{50} \approx 7.07$
C
$\sqrt{50}$ only if $T$ is exponential
D
$50$ (the mean of $N(100)$)
Answer: B — $\sqrt{50} \approx 7.07$

Var$(N(100)) = t\sigma^2/\mu^3 = 100 \times 4/8 = 50$. SD $= \sqrt{50} \approx 7.07$.

Option A uses $\mu^2$ not $\mu^3$ in the denominator. Option D is $E[N(100)] = t/\mu = 50$ — the mean, not the SD. CLT formula: Var$(N(t)) = t\sigma^2/\mu^3$. All three quantities — $t$, $\sigma^2$, $\mu^3$ — appear.
